Eight papers form the literature backbone for the uncertainty work in this collection — the question of how the brain represents and computes with *uncertainty* rather than point estimates. They split into four roles: *coding* — the two competing schemes for how a neural population could carry a whole probability distribution (probabilistic population codes vs sampling); *sampling dynamics* — what the sampling hypothesis predicts for cortical activity and how a circuit could implement it; *readout* — how an agent's own confidence (metacognition) is measured; and *cost* — the metabolic price of coding precision, which turns uncertainty into a budget constraint. Each entry below summarises the paper in a sentence or two.

## Summaries

**Pouget, Dayan & Zemel (2003).** Reviews how a population of neurons can encode not just a point estimate of a stimulus but a whole probability distribution over it, framing neural computation as Bayesian inference rather than function approximation. The foundational statement of the "population codes for inference" program, and the common ancestor of both schemes below.

**Ma, Beck, Latham & Pouget (2006).** Introduces *probabilistic population codes* (PPCs). Shows that the Poisson-like variability of cortical neurons is exactly what's needed for a population to represent a probability distribution over the stimulus, and that Bayes-optimal cue combination then reduces to a simple linear sum of population activities. Neural variability becomes a feature, not a bug.

**Fiser, Berkes, Orbán & Lengyel (2010).** Argues that perception *and* learning are both statistical inference and should be treated jointly, and reviews the case for the main alternative to PPCs: a *sampling-based* representation, in which evoked and spontaneous neural activity are samples drawn from a probability distribution rather than parameters of one.

**Orbán, Berkes, Fiser & Lengyel (2016).** Tests the sampling hypothesis quantitatively in primary visual cortex. Linking perceptual uncertainty to neural response variability accounts for independent changes in the strength and variability of responses and predicts a formal relationship between signal, noise, and spontaneous correlations.

**Aitchison & Lengyel (2016).** Shows that Hamiltonian Monte Carlo — an efficient sampling algorithm — maps naturally onto excitatory-inhibitory circuit dynamics. This gives a functional reason for two ubiquitous cortical features, the E/I split and oscillations: they let the network span state space quickly, making sampling-based inference an order of magnitude faster than a non-oscillatory variant.

**Echeveste, Aitchison, Hennequin & Lengyel (2020).** Trains a biologically-constrained recurrent E/I circuit to perform sampling-based inference and finds it spontaneously reproduces a suite of cortical dynamical signatures — divisive normalization, stimulus-quenched noise variability, inhibition-dominated onset transients, and strong gamma oscillations — as consequences of implementing fast sampling efficiently.

**Fleming & Lau (2014).** Reviews how to quantify *metacognition*, an agent's ability to judge the accuracy of its own decisions. Separates metacognitive *bias* (overall confidence), *sensitivity* (how well confidence tracks correctness), and *efficiency* (sensitivity relative to task performance), using bias-free signal-detection / ROC measures. The behavioural-readout side of uncertainty.

**Padamsey, Katsanevaki, Dupuy & Rochefort (2022).** Makes the cost of precision concrete. Under food restriction, mouse visual-cortex neurons save ATP by reducing excitatory currents; compensatory increases in input resistance and resting potential preserve mean spike rate but amplify variability and broaden tuning — i.e. lower coding precision. Supplementing the fat-regulated hormone leptin restores it. Uncertainty cast as an energy-budget trade-off.
