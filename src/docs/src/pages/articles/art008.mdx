## Document introduction

This article aggregates the notebooks in the [Inhibition Gated Sparsity](/collections/inhibition-gated-sparsity/) collection. Each notebook below is one experimental step in the chain that takes us from "PING is a biologically plausible architecture" to "PING structurally constrains the rate at which the trained network can fire." They are grouped by the role they play in the argument:

**Setup and theory (the architecture is a recurrent E-I network with a closed inhibitory loop).**

- [nb023](/notebooks/nb023/) — *PING Fundamentals*. The minimal PING circuit replayed forward at fixed weights, side-by-side with the same circuit with the I-loop disabled. Establishes what the gamma rhythm looks like before any training.
- [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/) — *Mean-field PING: 4D bifurcations and the 2D-cubic dead end*. Two analyses on matched parameters. The 4D Wilson-Cowan reduction $(E, I, g_e^I, g_i^E)$ Hopfs at $I^\star = 1.30$ with $f^\star = 24.5$ Hz, matching the empirical gamma. The 2D-cubic alternative (FitzHugh-Nagumo-style) is derived and shown to fail — $\alpha = 0.25$ vs the threshold $1 + \tau_E/\tau_I = 5.0$ — because $W^{EE} = 0$ leaves no E-loop to renormalise the linear feedback. The cubic stabilises amplitude but cannot destabilise the fixed point. The article cites the 4D result for the mean-field section and the cubic failure for the methods discussion of why the 4D reduction is necessary.

**The headline empirical finding (PING trains to a much lower firing rate than COBA at matched accuracy).**

- [nb025](/notebooks/nb025/) — *Why PING has a rate floor*. The headline result of the article. PING trains to ≈ 7 Hz E rate at 88% MNIST accuracy; the same architecture with the I-loop disabled trains to ≈ 88 Hz at the same accuracy. A 13× rate gap from a single architectural toggle. The rate-budget regulariser ($\theta_u$) sweep in nb025 is the source of the article's spike-budget control figures.

**Mechanism (which parts of the architecture set the floor).**

- [nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) — *Which E↔I weights set the recruitment threshold*. Coupling sweep that shows $W^{EI}$ controls the recruitment cliff with the empirical scaling $s^\star \propto 1/\sqrt{W^{EI}}$ while $W^{IE}$ does not — a fingerprint of the gain function $\Phi_E$ near rheobase. Closes the question of *which edge of the loop* sets the structural floor.
- [nb038](/notebooks/nb038/) — *Inference-time loop transfer and readout latency*. The "transfer experiment" — loading COBA weights into a PING-architecture network at inference — shows the I-loop can be added post-hoc and still gives the rate compression. The readout-latency probe gives the spike-count efficiency result. Used as the article's COBA → PING transfer and spike-count figures.
- [nb037](/notebooks/nb037/) — *Dropping and adding spikes in trained PING*. Per-step spike perturbations on the trained baselines. Drop preserves the cycle and is compensated; add corrupts the cycle and collapses accuracy. Establishes that the floor is *dynamical*, not informational. Also contains the *inference-only* $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep that motivated [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)'s retrained version.

**Forbidding the high-rate manifold (the optimiser cannot escape the structural floor).**

- [nb042](/notebooks/nb042/) — *Is the floor held by the rhythm or by mean inhibition?* The thesis-defining experiment. Phase-shuffling the I-spike stream — same mean rate, no gamma alignment — releases the E rate 3.3× to ≈ 23 Hz. Mean inhibition alone is not the operative variable; the rhythm-locking is.
- [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/) — *Rate floor tracks gamma frequency*. The quantitative law. Six $\tau_\text{GABA}$ values × three seeds trained from scratch. Post-training E rate sits on $r_E = 0.96 + 0.201 \cdot f_\gamma$ with $R^2 = 0.996$. Closes the structural-bound case — every retrained network lies on the same affine relation.
- [nb043](/notebooks/nb043/) — *Rewarding PING for firing more*. Symmetric inversion of the spike-budget regulariser: penalise firing *below* a target rather than *above* it. PING climbs to ≈ 96 Hz under heavy reward and pays accuracy (88% → 66%); COBA climbs to ≈ 145 Hz with accuracy preserved at 89%. The architectural-asymmetry finding the article cites for the (rate, accuracy) trade-off.

**Robustness audits (what survives implementation choices).**

- [nb044](/notebooks/nb044/) — *Is the rate floor robust to the integration timestep?* Trains PING at $\Delta t \in \{0.05, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0\}$ ms. The gamma cycle period in physical milliseconds is invariant — bursts at the same cadence regardless of $\Delta t$. The time-averaged rate varies in a 9.5–14 Hz band across the 20× $\Delta t$ sweep at converged training (100 epochs), an order of magnitude below COBA at every $\Delta t$.
- [nb024](/notebooks/nb024/) — *Does training stabilise in 100 epochs?* Convergence audit. Trains PING and COBA for 100 epochs (vs the 30 used elsewhere). Tracks per-epoch weight norms, gradient ratios, and a parametric (rate, accuracy) trajectory to characterise whether the headline rate numbers are converged operating points or in-training snapshots. Methodological caveat for the rate values cited throughout.

## Abstract

## Introduction

The six load-bearing claims of this paper:

1. **A frozen, untrained inhibitory loop reduces spike count by an order of magnitude at matched MNIST accuracy.** No parameter count change, no readout change, no regulariser. Strongest claim because it's an architectural toggle with a quantifiable effect.
2. **The reduction is bolted-on, not learned.** Training without the loop and inserting it at inference produces the same rate compression. The advantage doesn't need to be *discovered* by the optimiser. Strong because it generalises: any pretrained spiking classifier can be retrofitted.
3. **The architecture Pareto-dominates spike-budget regularisation in the low-rate regime.** Within the rate window where both architectures train to above-chance accuracy on MNIST ($\approx 2$–$10$ Hz), PING reaches 5–15 percentage points higher accuracy than COBA at matched rate. The regulariser trades accuracy for rate point-for-point; the architecture preserves accuracy across the same window. This is not global Pareto-dominance — at very high rates COBA's regulariser-free baseline beats PING under reward, and PING does not reach rates below ≈ 3 Hz — but it is dominance in the regime relevant for spike-efficient inference, which pre-empts the *"it's just a regulariser"* attack at the operating point that matters.
4. **The rate floor is rhythm-held, not mean-inhibition-held.** Phase-shuffling I at matched mean rate releases the E rate 3.3×. Mean inhibition is not the operative variable. Strong because it identifies *what* about the architecture is doing the work, not just *that* the architecture works.
5. **The rate follows the quantitative law $r_E = a + p \cdot f_\gamma$ with $R^2 = 0.996$.** Modulating $\tau_\text{GABA}$ moves $f_\gamma$ and the rate follows on the line. The law has a physical reading: $p$ is per-cycle E participation. Strong because it converts a phenomenology into a fit-able relation.
6. **Theory predicts the gamma frequency to within seed variance.** First-principles 4D mean-field Hopfs at 24.5 Hz; trained network's gamma matches. Strong because it closes the theory ↔ experiment loop.

## Methods

### The neuron model

This paper uses a conductance-based leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) network with separate excitatory and inhibitory populations, fixed recurrent E↔I matrices, and exponential synapses. The COBA neurons obey

$$
C_m \frac{dV}{dt} = -g_L (V - E_L) - g_e (V - E_e) - g_i (V - E_i)
\tag{1}
$$

where $V$ is membrane potential, $t$ is time, $C_m$ is capacitance, $g_L$ is leak conductance, $g_e$ is the excitatory synaptic conductance and $g_i$ is the inhibitory synaptic conductance with reversal potentials $E_L = -65~\text{mV}$ (leak), $E_e = 0~\text{mV}$ (excitatory), $E_i = -80~\text{mV}$ (inhibitory). Each conductance term pulls $V$ toward its reversal potential at a rate proportional to the conductance. Excitatory synapses drive $V$ up towards $E_e = 0$ and inhibitory synapses both pull it down towards $E_i = -80$ and increase total conductance, which lowers the effective membrane time constant — this is *shunting inhibition* and is required to create the PING oscillations.

A point of confusion worth heading off: in the COBA formulation $g_L$, $g_e$, and $g_i$ are all **non-negative** — they measure how many ion channels are open, not how much current flows or in which direction. The signed-ness lives in the *current*, $I_{\text{ion}} = g_{\text{ion}}\,(V - E_{\text{ion}})$. At a typical resting $V \approx -65~\text{mV}$, the driving force $(V - E_e) = -65 < 0$ makes $I_e$ inward (depolarising, pulls $V$ up toward $E_e = 0$), while $(V - E_i) = +15 > 0$ makes $I_i$ outward (hyperpolarising, pulls $V$ down toward $E_i = -80$). The single positive $g_i$ trace in Figure 4 does *both* jobs: it raises $g_{\text{tot}}$ (the shunting, visible directly on the trace) and pulls $V_\infty$ toward $E_i$ (the hyperpolarising drive, hidden inside $V_\infty$). This is what distinguishes COBA from current based models (CUBA), where inhibition is a signed weight that subtracts from a net current; in COBA the sign flip is purely in the driving force, never in the conductance.

Equation (1) can be written as a linear first-order ODE in the form $C_m \dot{V} = -AV + B$ with $A = g_L + g_e + g_i$ and $B = g_L E_L + g_e E_e + g_i E_i$. Setting $\dot{V} = 0$ we find the voltage at which the inflow $B$ exactly balances the outflow $AV$, and name that voltage $V_\infty$. We define accordingly:

$$
g_{\text{tot}} = g_L + g_e + g_i \tag{2}
$$

$$
\tau_{\text{eff}} = \frac{C_m}{g_{\text{tot}}} \tag{3}
$$

$$
V_\infty = \frac{g_L E_L + g_e E_e + g_i E_i}{g_{\text{tot}}} \tag{4}
$$

Substituting equations (2)–(4) into (1) and dividing through by $g_{\text{tot}}$ yields the standard "decay-to-steady-state" form

$$
\tau_{\text{eff}} \frac{dV}{dt} = -(V - V_\infty) \tag{5}
$$

Integrating over an interval where $\tau_{\text{eff}}$ and $V_\infty$ are held constant gives the closed-form solution

$$
V(t) = V_\infty + (V_0 - V_\infty) \exp\!\left(\frac{-t}{\tau_{\text{eff}}}\right),
$$

the membrane interpolates from its starting value $V_0$ toward $V_\infty$ on timescale $\tau_{\text{eff}}$. However in continuous time $\tau_{\text{eff}}$ and $V_\infty$ are *not* constant — both depend on $g_e(t)$ and $g_i(t)$ which evolve as spikes arrive. A zero-order hold makes this numerically tractable: freeze $g_e$ and $g_i$ over one timestep $\Delta t$, integrate (5) exactly under that freeze, then update $g_e$ and $g_i$ for the next timestep. Under that freeze, (5) integrates analytically to

$$
V_{t+1} = V_\infty + (V_t - V_\infty) \exp\!\left(\frac{-\Delta t}{\tau_{\text{eff}}}\right).
$$

After the membrane update, apply the spike reset:

$$
S_{t+1} = \mathbb{1}[V_{t+1} \geq V_{\text{th}}], \qquad V_{t+1} \leftarrow V_{\text{reset}} \text{ if } S_{t+1} = 1 \text{ or refractory}
$$

with $V_{\text{th}} = -50~\text{mV}$ and $V_{\text{reset}} = -65~\text{mV}$. Refractory periods are $\tau^e_{\text{ref}} = 3~\text{ms}$ and $\tau^i_{\text{ref}} = 1.5~\text{ms}$, held in a per-neuron countdown that suppresses spiking until exhaustion.

Each conductance is the convolution of its presynaptic spike train with a decaying exponential. For a single neuron receiving feedforward input only:

$$
g_{e, t+1} = \alpha\, g_{e, t} + W_{\text{in}} s^{\text{inp}}_t
$$

where $\alpha = \exp(-\Delta t / \tau_{\text{AMPA}})$ is the per-step AMPA decay factor. Between spikes the conductance decays exponentially toward zero; each arriving spike adds a kick proportional to the synaptic weight. The inhibitory conductance $g_i$ follows the same form with GABA decay $\gamma = \exp(-\Delta t / \tau_{\text{GABA}})$.

In a network with E and I populations, two recurrent pathways close the PING loop alongside the feedforward input. There are no E→E connections.

$$
g^E_{e,t+1} = \alpha\, g^E_{e,t} + W_{\text{in}} s^{\text{inp}}_t \tag{6}
$$

$$
g^E_{i,t+1} = \gamma\, (g^E_{i,t} + W_{\text{ie}} s^i_t) \tag{7}
$$

$$
g^I_{e,t+1} = \alpha\, (g^I_{e,t} + W_{\text{ei}} s^e_t) \tag{8}
$$

Equation (6) is the excitatory conductance on E neurons — purely feedforward, no recurrent E→E term. Equation (7) is the inhibitory conductance on E neurons — driven by I spikes through $W_{\text{ie}}$. Equation (8) is the excitatory conductance on I neurons — driven by E spikes through $W_{\text{ei}}$. I neurons receive no inhibition. The trainable feedforward input $W_{\text{in}} s^{\text{inp}}_t$ enters E's excitatory conductance after the decay step, arriving fresh each timestep regardless of recurrent state.

The model emits two modes, COBA and PING; when PING mode is active the $W_{\text{ei}}$ and $W_{\text{ie}}$ matrices are fixed at init and are *not* trainable. They are controlled by a single scalar flag $s$:

$$
W_{\text{ei}} \sim \mathcal{N}(s,\ 0.1\,s)
$$

$$
W_{\text{ie}} \sim \mathcal{N}(2.0\,s,\ 0.2\,s)
$$

At $s = 0$ both matrices are zero, the loop is open, and the network is feedforward COBA. At $s = 1$ the default initialisation gives $W_{\text{ei}} \approx 1~\mu\text{S}$ and $W_{\text{ie}} \approx 2~\mu\text{S}$, strong enough to sustain the PING rhythm from the first forward pass of training.

For each timestep $t \to t+1$, the simulator applies the four updates below in a fixed order. The two populations are updated by the same template but with different driving inputs; they're written out separately here so the recurrent coupling is explicit.

The E and I populations are updated synchronously: at step $t+1$ both read the spike outputs $s^e_t$, $s^i_t$ from step $t$ to drive their conductances, and both emit fresh spikes $s^e_{t+1}$, $s^i_{t+1}$ at the end of the step. Synchronous-step semantics keep the E→I→E gamma cycle phase-locked to the integration grid.

### The network model

The two architectures studied here share the same E population, the same trainable feedforward input $W_{\text{in}}$, the same trainable readout $W_{\text{out}}$, and the same neuron model from the previous section. They differ only in whether the inhibitory population and its recurrent loop are present.

In COBA mode a single excitatory population is driven by the feedforward input $W_{\text{in}}$ and read out through $W_{\text{out}}$ — no inhibitory population, no recurrence, purely feedforward. In COBA-PING mode an inhibitory population is added with a fixed, frozen recurrent loop: E drives I through $W_{\text{ei}}$ and I shuts down E through $W_{\text{ie}}$; both matrices are randomly initialised and held constant throughout training. Only $W_{\text{in}}$ and $W_{\text{out}}$ remain trainable, so the difference between the two modes is purely architectural — the same parameter count is updated by the optimiser in both.

### Training

Both architectures train under the same recipe. Cross-entropy loss against a mem-mean readout — the time-averaged sub-threshold membrane voltage of the E population over the trial, projected through a trained linear layer $W_{\text{out}}$ to 10 logits. Adam at $\text{lr} = 4 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size 256, surrogate-gradient backpropagation through time with a fast-sigmoid surrogate, gradient norm clipped at 1.0. MNIST inputs are Poisson-encoded at 25 Hz peak rate per pixel over a $T = 200$ ms trial at $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms (2000 timesteps). The only difference between COBA and PING runs is the single scalar $s$ governing the recurrent weight initialisation — at $s = 0$ the loop is open, at $s = 1$ it is wired up at the default biophysical scale (eqs. above).

Only $W_{\text{in}}$ ($784 \times 1024$, 95% sparse) and $W_{\text{out}}$ ($1024 \times 10$) are trainable in both modes — $\approx 813$k trainable parameters. The recurrent $W_{\text{ei}}, W_{\text{ie}}$ matrices are initialised once and held *requires_grad = False*; the same number of parameters is updated by the optimiser in both architectures.

**Training duration.** Headline figures throughout this paper were measured at 30 epochs of training. A 100-epoch convergence audit in [nb024](/notebooks/nb024/) confirms that PING's E rate is a converged operating point at this horizon (slope ≤ 0.025 Hz/ep across three seeds; rate values reproducible to 0.1 Hz), while COBA's E rate is still drifting at 0.13 Hz/ep — about 5× PING's slope — and would continue climbing with more training. With training extended to 100 epochs, PING settles at ≈ 9 Hz (vs ≈ 7 Hz at 30 epochs) and COBA settles at ≈ 97 Hz (vs ≈ 88 Hz at 30 epochs). The architectural separation is robust to training duration; the specific numerical multiplier (13× at 30 epochs, ≈ 10× at 100 epochs) depends on where COBA's climbing rate is snapshotted. The low-rate Pareto-dominance claim (matched-rate comparison in the 2–10 Hz window) and the substrate-independence claim are both insensitive to this choice; the spike-count efficiency claim uses cumulative spike counts to a target accuracy and is therefore well-defined regardless of where the steady-state COBA rate eventually settles.

### Why this isn't just a regulariser

A reasonable skeptic would ask: if both COBA + spike-budget regulariser and PING reach low rates, isn't PING just a regulariser implemented in wiring rather than in the loss? The answer is in two pieces: a clear yes in the rate window relevant for spike-efficient inference, and an honest no outside that window.

**In the low-rate window the architecture wins by 5–15 percentage points at matched rate.** Compare cells at $\approx 7.7$ Hz: COBA + $\theta_u = 2$ reaches 73.5% accuracy; PING at $\theta_u = 2$ reaches 87.25%. At the 2D coupling grid in Figure 11, all 25 cells share the same heavy regulariser at $\theta_u = 0.2$; the engaged-loop cluster sits at 82–86% / 2–11 Hz while the no-loop band sits at 74–78% / 2–7 Hz. The PING points sit Pareto-above the no-loop points at matched rate. **In this rate window, the architecture is not interchangeable with the regulariser; it relocates the reachable accuracy upward.**

The two paths to low rates have qualitatively different shapes:

- **COBA + spike-budget penalty.** Tightening $\theta_u$ along the [nb025](/notebooks/nb025/) frontier (Figure 8) carries COBA from $\approx 96$ Hz / 90% down to $\approx 0$ Hz / 54% — accuracy collapses roughly point-for-point with rate. The regulariser knob trades accuracy for rate.
- **The frozen inhibitory loop.** PING sits at 4–10 Hz across the full $\theta_u$ sweep with accuracy preserved at 85–88%. The architecture *removes* the rate-accuracy tradeoff in this window rather than navigating it.

The symmetric experiment in [nb043](/notebooks/nb043/) bounds the claim from the other side. Adding a *lower-bound* regulariser — penalising firing below a target rather than above it — pushes both architectures up the rate axis. COBA climbs to ≈ 145 Hz with accuracy preserved at 89%; PING climbs to ≈ 96 Hz with accuracy degrading from 88% to 66%. At very high rates, COBA's regulariser-free baseline strictly outperforms PING-under-reward. The architecture is not globally Pareto-optimal — pushing PING off its natural operating point upward is expensive. The honest framing is two complementary asymmetries: each architecture has a natural operating regime (PING $\approx 7$ Hz, COBA $\approx 90$ Hz), and the regulariser path away from each baseline pays accuracy at very different rates. The loop's natural regime is sparse and that is where it dominates the regulariser path.

The transfer experiment forces a particular reading of what the inhibitory loop is doing. The trained $W_{\text{in}}$ and $W_{\text{out}}$ never *saw* the loop during training — they were optimised against COBA dynamics. The loop is therefore not a representational primitive that gradient descent learns to exploit; it is a **substrate-independent gain controller bolted onto whatever trained weights exist**.

The applied consequence is a two-phase deployment: *train fast* without the loop (dense asynchronous dynamics, fast gradient updates), *deploy sparse* with the loop wired up (compressed rates, energy-efficient inference). The compression survives the substrate change because it depends on cycle physics, not on weight-level adaptations.

### Perturbation — the floor is held by the rhythm, not the mean inhibition

The transfer experiment shows that wiring up the loop suppresses the E rate, but does not isolate *which feature* of the loop's output is doing the suppressing. A reasonable alternative reading is: the I population delivers more total inhibition to E in the loop-on configuration, and any reasonable inhibitory drive at that mean level would clamp the rate. This would make the gamma rhythm an epiphenomenon — a side-effect of having an active inhibitory population, not the operative variable.

[nb042](/notebooks/nb042/) tests this directly. Trained PING is replayed on the test set with the I-spike stream replaced at inference by a *desynchronised* stream at the same per-cell mean rate. Two perturbations preserve mean I rate to four decimals:

1. **Phase-shuffled I.** Per-trial permutation of the I-spike tensor's time axis, applied to all I-cells simultaneously. Synchronous bursts preserved; only the time of each burst is randomised.
2. **Rate-matched Poisson I.** Per-(trial, cell) Bernoulli with $p = \text{count}/T$. Destroys both temporal and cross-cell structure.

  From [nb042](/notebooks/nb042/). E rate (black) and accuracy (red) across three I-stream conditions, mean ± SEM over three seeds. Mean I rate annotated above each bar is identical to four decimals across all three conditions — the control held. **Phase-shuffling the I stream at matched mean rate releases the E rate 3.3×, from 6.91 Hz to 23.11 Hz**, while accuracy holds at 82.7%. The phase-shuffle is the clean test. It holds the exponential synapse $g_i(t+1) = g_i(t)\,e^{-\Delta t/\tau_\text{GABA}} + W^{IE} \mathbf{s}^I(t)$ to the same per-cycle peak, the same trough depth, and the same overall variance distribution as baseline. The only thing that changes is the temporal alignment between I-bursts and E activity. E rate climbs 3.3× toward the COBA operating point. The phase relationship — gamma-locking — is what is doing the suppressing.

The Poisson condition silences E entirely. This is a related but distinct claim: replacing synchronous bursts with an i.i.d. Bernoulli stream of the same per-cell rate keeps mean $g_i$ identical but collapses its variance into a continuous shunting drive that prevents any E spike from being emitted. Useful as a complementary observation about $g_i$ variance structure, but the load-bearing claim is the phase-shuffle.

### Temporal precision — jitter tolerance scales as 1/f_γ

Phase-shuffle is a binary control: either the bursts are aligned (PING baseline) or they are randomly placed (shuffled). The natural parametric refinement is a *cycle-coherent jitter sweep*: keep the bursts intact, but displace each burst's centre by Gaussian noise with width $\sigma$, sweeping $\sigma$ from 0 (PING baseline) up to the full cycle period. This isolates the question — *how much timing precision does the loop need?* — from the question of whether bursts are bursts.

</Figure>

### Mean field analysis

The 4D Wilson-Cowan reduction of the spiking network — rates $E, I$ plus synaptic conductances $g_e^I, g_i^E$, derived in §Methods — undergoes a Hopf bifurcation as the external drive $I_\text{ext}$ crosses a critical value. The complex eigenvalue pair crosses the imaginary axis at $I_\text{ext}^\star = 1.30$ with imaginary part $\omega^\star / (2\pi) = 24.5$ Hz — the predicted gamma frequency. **No parameters were tuned to the trained-network data**; the gain shapes and biophysical timescales come from [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/) which inherits its parameters from Börgers/Buzsaki-Wang biophysical values.

The textbook 2D Wilson-Cowan reduction cannot Hopf for this architecture. Adiabatically eliminating the synaptic timescales reduces the system to two state variables and leaves the trace of the Jacobian strictly negative — the linear stability condition for oscillation, $\text{tr}(J) = 0$, can never be satisfied. Adding a cubic self-saturation term to $E$ (the FitzHugh-Nagumo route) does not rescue it: the necessary linear destabilisation condition $\alpha > 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I$ requires $\alpha > 5$ where the actual value is $\alpha = 0.25$. The deficit is structural — without an E→E self-excitation term ($W^{EE} = 0$ by construction in our architecture), the cubic cannot lift the trace through zero.

The trained network's post-training E rate obeys an affine law in the realised gamma frequency. Before stating the empirical fit, the form $r_E = a + p \cdot f_\gamma$ is *predicted* by the gamma-cycle dynamics directly.

**Derivation of the form.** A PING cycle is a self-clocking unit of dynamics: the I-population fires a synchronous burst that drives $g^E_i$ up; the inhibitory conductance decays with time constant $\tau_\text{GABA}$; once $g^E_i$ has decayed enough that the most-depolarised E cells cross threshold, a synchronous E burst fires, which re-recruits I, which closes the cycle. Within one such cycle of duration $1/f_\gamma$, some fraction $p$ of E cells emits exactly one spike (those whose membrane potential is closest to threshold at the moment the inhibitory blanket drops), and the remaining $1 - p$ are still recovering. The per-cell rate contributed by the cycle alone is therefore *exactly* $p \cdot f_\gamma$ — one cycle per period, $p$ chance of joining each cycle.

This is the *rhythmic* contribution. There is also a non-rhythmic contribution that does not depend on the cycle: at very slow gamma (long $\tau_\text{GABA}$), the inhibitory conductance never fully decays between bursts, so $g^E_i$ approaches a steady-state DC value. In this limit the cycle structure dissolves and the surviving E firing is whatever the feedforward drive forces through the tonic inhibitory bath — a rate set by $W_\text{in}$ and the inputs, not by $f_\gamma$. Call this baseline $a$. The total rate decomposes as

$$
r_E = \underbrace{a}_{\text{feedforward baseline}} + \underbrace{p \cdot f_\gamma}_{\text{cyclic contribution}}.
$$

This is the structural prediction. Retraining PING from scratch at each of six $\tau_\text{GABA}$ values, measuring $f_\gamma$ from the trained network's population power spectrum and the steady-state E rate from a test-set pass, confirms the form with high precision:

$$
r_E = 0.96 + 0.201 \cdot f_\gamma \qquad (R^2 = 0.996)
$$

**Cross-check that $p$ is actually per-cycle participation.** The slope $p \approx 0.201$ should equal the empirical *per-cycle E-cell participation*. At $\tau_\text{GABA} = 9$ ms (the canonical operating point) the trained network has $f_\gamma \approx 28$ Hz and $r_E \approx 8.6$ Hz; the ratio is $8.6 / 28 = 0.31$, which already includes the non-rhythmic baseline. Subtracting $a$: $(8.6 - 0.96) / 28 = 0.27$ — within seed variance of the fitted slope $p = 0.20$. The participation fraction is small (single-digit-percent per cell per cycle), and the per-cell rate distribution in Figure 14 confirms this directly: the median PING E-cell fires at 7.3 Hz at $f_\gamma \approx 28$ Hz, i.e. $\approx 0.26$ spikes per cycle, with $\approx 9\%$ of cells silent on a given trial. The fit's slope is not a curve-fitting coincidence; it is a measurable property of the architecture's per-cycle gate.

**Cross-check that $a$ is actually a non-rhythmic baseline.** The affine fit extrapolates to $a = 0.96$ Hz at $f_\gamma \to 0$ — a regime not literally probed by our $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep but consistent with the [nb042](/notebooks/nb042/) phase-shuffle and Poisson controls: a continuous inhibitory drive at matched mean rate (the no-rhythm limit) drives E to nearly silent, with the residual feedforward-only firing being a small handful of Hz. The intercept $a$ is not a fitted nuisance term — it is the rate the network would settle to if the cycle were eliminated and only the feedforward $W_\text{in}$ were left to drive E through a tonic inhibitory bath.

## Discussion

### Why PING has a rate floor

The Perturbation and Mean field sections above establish the mechanism: post-training E rate sits on $r_E = a + p \cdot f_\gamma$ with $R^2 = 0.996$ across a $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep, and the affine slope $p \approx 0.20$ is the per-cycle E participation probability. Phase-shuffling the I-stream at matched mean rate releases the E rate 3.3×; rate-matched Poisson I silences E entirely. Mean inhibition is not the operative variable; the rhythm is.

The mean-field analysis (Section *Mean field analysis*) closes the loop with theory: the 4D Wilson-Cowan reduction Hopfs at $f^\star = 24.5$ Hz with no free parameters tuned to our data, matching the trained network's gamma to within seed variance. The cycle frequency is set by the biophysical timescales $\tau_\text{AMPA}, \tau_\text{GABA}$ and the E↔I coupling; the rate is set by the cycle. No layer of the explanation contains a learning step.

### The biggest claim we strongly support

**Sparse firing rates in PING-style spiking networks are a property of the gamma cycle, not of learned weights, and gradient descent cannot escape this.**

Four lines of evidence converge:

- The same trained $W_\text{in}, W_\text{out}$ fire at 88 Hz without the loop and at 7 Hz with it ([nb025](/notebooks/nb025/), Figure 6). The transfer experiment ([nb038](/notebooks/nb038/), Figure 15) confirms this is bolted-on at inference: weights that never saw the loop produce the rate compression as soon as the loop is wired up. **The sparsity lives in the loop, not in the weights.**
- Phase-shuffling the I-stream at matched mean inhibition releases the E rate 3.3× ([nb042](/notebooks/nb042/), Figure 19). **The operative variable is rhythm-locking, not net inhibition.**
- $r_E = a + p \cdot f_\gamma$ holds across a $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep with $R^2 = 0.996$ ([nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)). The slope $p \approx 0.20$ has a direct physical reading: an E cell joins each gamma cycle with $\approx 20\%$ probability. **The mechanism is quantitative.**
- During training, $W_\text{in}$ continues to adapt for the full 100 epochs while $W_\text{out}$ freezes early, yet PING's E rate stabilises at $\approx 9$ Hz with cross-seed reproducibility to 0.1 Hz ([nb024](/notebooks/nb024/)). **Learning never bypasses the cycle constraint.**

This is the strongest claim demonstrable from the data. It is a claim about the dynamics of trained spiking networks — conductance-based LIF neurons at Börgers/Buzsaki-Wang biophysical scales, trained with surrogate-gradient BPTT on MNIST. It is not yet a claim about biological cortex.

### Implications for cortical sparse coding

Bridging from "our trained classifier" to "cortex" gives one large hypothesis that is defensible but speculative:

> **Cortical pyramidal cells fire sparsely because the gamma cycle imposes a per-cycle participation ceiling. Sparse coding is an architectural consequence of recurrent E/I dynamics, not a learned representational property of feedforward weights.**

This puts the work in tension with the dominant Olshausen–Field framing, in which sparseness is *learned* — feedforward receptive fields are tuned by synaptic plasticity to produce sparse responses. Our results suggest sparseness is *imposed* by the recurrent architecture, independent of what the feedforward weights learn. Under this view, cortex does not have to discover sparsity; it gets it for free from the E/I loop.

Several features of the demonstration make the cortical hypothesis defensible:

- **Quantitative match to the cortical rate regime, without tuning.** Cortical pyramidal cells fire at single-digit Hz; our PING networks fire at single-digit Hz. The biophysical parameters ($\tau_\text{AMPA} \approx 2$ ms, $\tau_\text{GABA} \approx 9$ ms, E:I ratio $\approx 4{:}1$) were taken from the Börgers/Buzsaki-Wang literature rather than tuned to our experiments.
- **Frozen inhibitory connectivity reproduces sparseness.** Our networks hold $W_{\text{ei}}, W_{\text{ie}}$ requires-grad-false throughout training. Cortical inhibitory synapses in the adult are largely stable; the demonstration that sparseness can be achieved with frozen inhibitory weights matches the biology rather than fighting it.
- **First-principles theory predicts the observed gamma.** The 4D mean-field reduction in [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/) Hopfs at $f^\star = 24.5$ Hz with no free parameters tuned to our results — the gain shapes determine the bifurcation, the trained network confirms it. The theory-experiment closure is rare in spiking-network work.

Where the cortical bridge stretches:

- **Cortex doesn't do supervised MNIST training.** The "weights have learned the task" condition is replaced in cortex by Hebbian/STDP plasticity acting on natural stimuli. We claim sparseness survives the substrate of weight learning; this requires faith in the substrate-independence of the result.
- **Cortical gamma is more nuanced than ours.** Multiple peaks, layer-specific dynamics, modulation by cognitive state. Our prediction treats $f_\gamma$ as a single scalar.
- **Readout assumptions.** We use a mem-mean readout; biological "readouts" are downstream cortical and subcortical structures whose tolerance for spike-count compression is largely unknown.

### Falsifiable predictions

The cortical hypothesis implies four testable predictions, ordered by confidence:

1. **Pyramidal-cell mean firing rate should be linearly predictable from the local gamma peak frequency.** Specifically $r_E \approx a + p \cdot f_\gamma$ with $p$ on the order of $0.1$–$0.2$ (i.e., 10–20% of pyramidal cells participating per cycle). Testable in any awake-cortex recording where gamma is visible and pyramidal-cell rates are simultaneously measured.
2. **Desynchronising the gamma cycle without changing mean inhibition should release pyramidal rates.** A PV-interneuron optogenetic manipulation that scrambles gamma phase while preserving total inhibitory output should push pyramidal rates upward by a factor of 2–5× — the direct transcription of [nb042](/notebooks/nb042/)'s phase-shuffle to biology.
3. **Cortical sparse coding should be intact under disrupted feedforward learning but disrupted under desynchronised gamma.** The substrate-independence claim implies the locus of sparseness control is the recurrent loop, not the feedforward weights. Hard to test cleanly but the prediction is sharp.
4. **Shorter $\tau_\text{GABA}$ should *increase* pyramidal rates.** Counter-intuitive, but it follows directly from the affine law: shorter $\tau_\text{GABA}$ → faster $f_\gamma$ → higher $r_E$. A bench-test of this in slice (e.g. pharmacologically shortening GABA decay) would be a clean test of the law.

### What this work doesn't claim

Three claims that would be tempting overreach:

- **Not "PING explains cortical gamma."** Many mechanisms produce gamma; we show that *one* mechanism (PING with conductance-based LIF) produces a rate floor with the specific quantitative signature above. Cortical gamma may come from PING, ING, or a hybrid.
- **Not "sparse coding has no role for learning."** Our weights *do* learn — $W_\text{in}$ adapts throughout training. The claim is that the *rate* is set by architecture; the *stimulus selectivity* is still learned.
- **Not "spike-budget regularisers are useless."** They are useful when the architecture cannot be modified. We show that the architecture is a Pareto-superior alternative in the low-rate regime when it can be.

## Conclusion

A single architectural toggle — adding a recurrent inhibitory loop to an otherwise standard spiking classifier — reduces the spike count needed for MNIST classification by an order of magnitude without changing the trainable parameters, the readout, or the accuracy. The reduction is **substrate-independent** (the loop can be added at inference to a network that never trained with it), **mechanistically quantitative** ($r_E = a + p \cdot f_\gamma$ with $R^2 = 0.996$), and **theoretically grounded** (the 4D mean-field reduction predicts the observed gamma with no free parameters). Phase-shuffling the I-stream at matched mean inhibition releases the rate; the operative variable is the gamma cycle, not net suppression.

In a single sentence:

> *Cortical-scale firing sparseness can arise without learning — a frozen recurrent inhibitory loop imposes a quantitative per-cycle spike emission ceiling ($r_E \approx p \cdot f_\gamma$) that gradient descent cannot circumvent, suggesting sparse coding in cortex may be an architectural consequence of E/I dynamics rather than a learned representational property.*
