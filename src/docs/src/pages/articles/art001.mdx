### PING basics

PING (pyramidal–interneuron gamma) is the gamma-band oscillation produced when an excitatory population $E$ drives an inhibitory population $I$ which feeds back onto $E$. The mechanism is mechanical:

1. $E$ starts firing.
2. After an AMPA delay ($\tau_{\text{AMPA}} \approx 2$ ms), $I$ spikes.
3. GABA-A inhibition silences $E$ for the GABA-decay time ($\tau_{\text{GABA}} \approx 9$ ms).
4. Inhibition decays; $E$ refires.

The interspike interval is $\tau_{\text{AMPA}} + \tau_{\text{GABA}} \sim 11$ ms, giving a fundamental near $30$ Hz — the gamma band.

Figures 1–4 strip the network down to this loop and characterise it *in isolation from any task*. Two cells are compared: same 1024 E + 256 I conductance-based LIF network, same MNIST digit-0 Poisson input at 50 Hz, same 400 ms integration window. The only difference is *--ei-strength*: 0 (loop disabled, "PING off") versus 1.5 (loop active, "PING on"). Full setup in [023 — PING fundamentals](/notebooks/nb023/).

<Figure id="Figure 1" title="COBA — recurrent loop off" src="/figures/notebooks/nb023/raster__coba.png" alt="Spike raster: E population fires asynchronously across 400 ms, I population silent.">
  Baseline: *--ei-strength 0*. With the I→E loop disabled, the network is feedforward-only conductance-based LIF. The E raster is asynchronous across the full window; I is silent. There is no rhythm to read off. From [023 — PING fundamentals](/notebooks/nb023/), Figure 1.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 2" title="PING — recurrent loop on" src="/figures/notebooks/nb023/raster__ping.png" alt="Spike raster: E population shows vertical bands every ~45 ms, I population shows matching bursts trailing each E-burst.">
  Same network, *--ei-strength 1.5*. The E raster now shows vertical bands and the I population fires synchronous bursts trailing each E-burst by the AMPA delay. The gamma rhythm is visible at the population level without any training. From [023 — PING fundamentals](/notebooks/nb023/), Figure 2.

Figures 3 and 4 zoom in on a single representative neuron from each population to show *why* the rhythm emerges. The membrane voltage $V$ and excitatory / inhibitory conductances $g_E$, $g_I$ are recorded from the most active E neuron (and, in the PING case, the most active I neuron).

<Figure id="Figure 3" title="COBA — single-neuron traces" src="/figures/notebooks/nb023/traces__coba.png" alt="Two panels: membrane voltage V_E for one E neuron, and its excitatory conductance g_E. No inhibitory conductance — the loop is off.">
  V_E reaches the threshold $V_{\text{th}} = -50$ mV repeatedly and hard-resets back to $V_{\text{reset}}$. g_E is the only synaptic drive — the loop being off means g_I is identically zero, so we omit the panel. The cell is *tonically* firing: drive comes purely from the Poisson input and ISIs are set by the recovery time. From [023 — PING fundamentals](/notebooks/nb023/), Figure 3.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 4" title="PING — single-neuron traces" src="/figures/notebooks/nb023/traces__ping.png" alt="Four panels: V_E and conductances for one E neuron, then V_I and its excitatory conductance for one I neuron.">
  Same plot for the PING cell, with the I neuron added below. The E neuron's V_E is held *below* threshold for most of each cycle by the rhythmic g_I bursts (panel 2, red); g_E (cyan) is small in comparison. The I neuron's g_E (panel 4) ramps as the E population fires together, V_I crosses threshold, and the resulting I spike injects g_I back onto the E population — silencing it until g_I decays. This is the PING loop in cross-section. From [023 — PING fundamentals](/notebooks/nb023/), Figure 4.
</Figure>

The point of this section is that PING is not a property the network learns — it is a property of the architecture. The rest of this article asks what happens when you train on top of it.

### PING training

PING has been modelled but not trained on a task. We train it here on MNIST classification.

The network is a conductance-based E/I model with a fixed E→I→E loop. Feedforward and E→E weights are learned; the inhibitory loop is held at its initialised strength. Training uses surrogate-gradient backprop with a cumulative-membrane readout (Figure 5).

### dt stability vs other models

Six models are trained on MNIST at two *dt* regimes (0.1 ms and 1 ms), each with the per-model recipe from its calibration sibling ([nb007](/notebooks/nb007/)–[nb012](/notebooks/nb012/)). Weights are then frozen and accuracy is re-evaluated across a sweep of eval-*dt* values under three input-transport rules ([upsample / downsample / resample](#eval-time-dt-transport)). Identity transport (eval-*dt* = train-*dt*) anchors each sweep, so any change downstream is *integrator-side*.

**Models** (see [the ladder](/docs/models/)):

- [*standard-snn*](/docs/models/#standard-snn) — pinglab reimplementation of snnTorch's *snn.Leaky*. The Tutorial-3 simplification ($\Delta t = 1$, $R = 1$, $(1-\beta)$ absorbed into $W$) drops $\Delta t$ from the update, which is what produces the *dt*-leakage analysed below.
- [*standard-snn-exp*](/docs/models/#standard-snn) — *standard-snn* with an exponential synapse ($\tau_\text{syn} \approx 2$ ms) on the feedforward inputs.
- [*snntorch-library*](/docs/models/#snntorch-library) — snnTorch's *snn.Leaky* directly. Parity probe against *standard-snn*.
- [*cuba*](/docs/models/#cuba) — same LIF, exp-Euler + ZOH integration restoring the $(1-\beta)/dt$ and $(1-\beta)$ prefactors. *dt* is back in the update.
- [*coba*](/docs/models/#coba) — conductance-based LIF with exponential synapses, hard reset, refractory. COBANet with *--ei-strength 0* (feedforward-only).
- [*ping*](/docs/models/#ping) — COBA plus the frozen E→I→E recurrent loop that produces gamma.


All six models reach comparable accuracy at their training dt (Figure 8); differences appear once dt is perturbed at inference.

### Spike Penalty

Unpenalised, the three models settle at different rates (Figure 16). Adding an upper-bound regulariser $\lambda_u \cdot \sum \text{ReLU}(\bar s - \theta_u)^2$ — with strength $\lambda_u = 10^{-3}$ held fixed — and sweeping the budget $\theta_u$ (mean spikes per trial per unit) traces each model's accuracy–rate frontier.

<Figure id="Figure 17" title="Accuracy / rate frontier" src="/figures/notebooks/nb020/frontier.png" alt="Pareto frontier: best test accuracy versus achieved hidden E rate, one line per model. Each point labelled with its θ_u value.">
  Test accuracy vs achieved hidden-E firing rate, one curve per model; each point is one *(model, $\theta_u$)* configuration. From [020 — Spike-Penalty Sweep Across the Ladder](/notebooks/nb020/), Figure 1.
</Figure>

PING's frontier is flat: its unpenalised rate is already biological, and accuracy is insensitive to $\theta_u$. CUBA loses accuracy when forced down toward PING's rate.
