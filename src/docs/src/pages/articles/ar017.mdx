*Eoin Murray.*

van Vreeswijk, C. & Sompolinsky, H. (1996). *Chaos in neuronal networks with balanced excitatory and inhibitory activity.* Science 274, 1724–1726. [doi:10.1126/science.274.5293.1724](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.274.5293.1724).

This is the paper that explains why cortex can fire as irregularly as it does without being either silent or saturated, and why it nonetheless responds to its inputs in a simple, almost linear way. It is the substrate the rest of this project's gamma work is imposed on: the *balanced state* is the off-rhythm operating point, and everything PING does is measured against it. It is catalogued in the gamma-gated-sparsity backbone ([ar008](/articles/ar008/)) and reproduced directly in [nb050](/notebooks/nb050/).

## The puzzle

Cortical neurons fire irregularly — interspike intervals look close to Poisson, with a coefficient of variation near 1. That is strange. Each cortical neuron receives thousands of synaptic inputs; if those inputs were independent and each contributed a small push, the law of large numbers would average them into a smooth, near-constant drive, and the neuron would fire like clockwork (low CV). Regular firing is what a naive integrator *should* do. So where does the irregularity come from?

The pre-1996 escape hatches were unsatisfying. You could add an external noise source by hand — but then irregularity is assumed, not explained. You could fine-tune the mean input to sit exactly at threshold so the neuron lives off fluctuations — but fine-tuning is fragile and not biological. van Vreeswijk and Sompolinsky show that a generic recurrent network organises *itself* into the fluctuation-driven, irregular regime, with no tuning and no injected noise.

## The idea: strong synapses and $\sqrt{K}$ balance

Take a sparse random network: $N$ neurons split into excitatory and inhibitory populations, each neuron receiving $K$ inputs, with $1 \ll K \ll N$. The decisive modelling choice is the synaptic scale. Instead of weak synapses ($J \propto 1/K$, which would make the summed input smooth and $O(1)$), take **strong synapses**:

$$
J \;\propto\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{K}} .
$$

Now count orders of magnitude in the total synaptic input a neuron receives from a presynaptic population firing at rate $m$:

- **Mean input** $\;\propto\; \underbrace{K}_{\text{inputs}} \times \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{K}}}_{\text{efficacy}} \times \, m \;=\; \sqrt{K}\, m$ — it *grows* with $\sqrt{K}$, and is therefore enormous for large $K$.
- **Variance of input** $\;\propto\; K \times \big(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{K}}\big)^2 \times m \;=\; m \;=\; O(1)$ — the fluctuations stay finite.

The external drive is scaled the same way, $\propto \sqrt{K}$. The mean excitatory and mean inhibitory currents are each $O(\sqrt{K})$ and huge — so the only way a neuron can fire at a moderate rate (neither silent nor saturated) is if **excitation and inhibition cancel to leading order**, leaving a net mean input of order one. That cancellation is the balanced state:

$$
\underbrace{\sqrt{K}\,(\text{huge mean E}) - \sqrt{K}\,(\text{huge mean I})}_{\text{must cancel to } O(1)} \; + \; \underbrace{O(1)\ \text{fluctuations}}_{\text{drive the spikes}} .
$$

where:

- $K$ — number of synaptic inputs per neuron;
- $J$ — synaptic efficacy, scaled as $1/\sqrt{K}$ (strong coupling);
- $m$ — population firing rate (fraction of the population active per unit time).

The payoff: after the $O(\sqrt{K})$ means cancel, the residual net input is the *same order* as the $O(1)$ fluctuations. The neuron sits near threshold and is pushed across it by fluctuations, not by a steady suprathreshold drive — exactly the fluctuation-driven regime that produces high-CV, Poisson-like firing. And nothing was tuned: the cancellation is forced by the network having to find a self-consistent moderate rate.

## Balance equations and linear response

How does the network *find* the cancellation? The rates self-adjust until the leading $O(\sqrt{K})$ part of every neuron's mean input vanishes. Writing $m_E$, $m_I$ for the population rates and $m_0$ for the external drive, that requirement is a pair of linear equations — one per population:

$$
J_{EE}\,m_E + J_{EI}\,m_I + J_{E0}\,m_0 = 0 ,
\qquad
J_{IE}\,m_E + J_{II}\,m_I + J_{I0}\,m_0 = 0 ,
$$

where:

- $m_E, m_I$ — excitatory and inhibitory population rates (the unknowns);
- $m_0$ — the external input rate;
- $J_{ab}$ — the (order-one) effective coupling from population $b$ to population $a$, with the inhibitory entries $J_{EI}, J_{II} < 0$;
- $J_{a0}$ — coupling of population $a$ to the external drive.

Two consequences fall straight out, and they are the heart of the paper:

1. **The rates are a *linear* function of the external input.** Solving the $2\times2$ system gives $m_E, m_I \propto m_0$. The network responds linearly to its drive — even though every single neuron has a sharply nonlinear (threshold) input–output relation. The nonlinearity is washed out at the population level by the balance constraint.
2. **The single-neuron transfer function drops out of the mean rates.** The balance equations contain only the connectivity $J_{ab}$ and the drive, not the f–I curve. The detailed biophysics sets the *fluctuation* statistics (the subleading $O(1)$ part), but the mean rates are fixed by connectivity alone.

The state exists only when the $J_{ab}$ satisfy ordering inequalities (inhibition-dominated, with the external drive to E and I ordered correctly) so that the solution has positive, stable rates. When those *balance conditions* fail, the network leaves the balanced regime — saturating, falling silent, or oscillating instead.

## Why "chaos"

The irregularity is not injected — it is generated. The microscopic dynamics here are **deterministic**: no noise term anywhere. Yet the activity is temporally irregular, aperiodic, and exquisitely sensitive to initial conditions — the signature of deterministic chaos, which van Vreeswijk and Sompolinsky establish through a dynamic mean-field analysis and direct simulation (the largest Lyapunov exponent is positive). The fluctuations that drive each neuron across threshold *are* the chaotic recurrent activity of the rest of the network feeding back on itself.

This is the conceptual punchline: a balanced recurrent network manufactures its own noise. The Poisson-like irregularity of cortex need not be imported from outside; it can be an intrinsic, self-sustaining property of strong, inhibition-dominated recurrence.

## Why it endures

The balanced state has become the default account of the cortical "ground state" because it explains four observations at once, with no tuning:

- **Irregular (Poisson-like) firing** — fluctuation-driven crossings give $\mathrm{CV} \approx 1$.
- **Low rates** — the $O(\sqrt{K})$ means cancel, leaving moderate net drive.
- **Fast, linear population response** — the balance constraint linearises the network and lets it track inputs on the timescale of the fluctuations rather than the membrane time constant.
- **Robustness** — the cancellation is self-organising, not hand-set.

It is also the natural null model: an *asynchronous-irregular* state with no rhythm. That is precisely why it matters here.

## Relation to this project

The balanced state is the substrate the gamma-gated-sparsity story is told against. Three concrete connections:

- **It defines "sparse compared to what."** The project's claim that PING-gated firing is more economical only means something relative to a baseline. The balanced state is that baseline — the irregular, asynchronous regime the same architecture occupies when the rhythm is off. The COBA baselines in the collection are balanced-state networks; PING imposes a gamma envelope on top.
- **[nb050](/notebooks/nb050/) reproduces it directly.** That notebook drives the project's network into the asynchronous-irregular regime, confirms strong E/I cancellation and fluctuation-driven Poisson firing, and runs a perturbation (chaos) probe of the kind this paper predicts. The conductance-based extension — near-zero pairwise correlations surviving heavy shared input — is the Renart et al. (2010) follow-up, also catalogued in [ar008](/articles/ar008/).
- **It is the regime real gamma resembles.** As the skeptical V1 literature in [ar008](/articles/ar008/) argues, cortical gamma looks more like noisy, damped-oscillator activity riding on a balanced background than like a clean limit cycle. The balanced state is the honest description of what the network is doing between volleys.

The one tension worth keeping in view: this model is current-based and binary-rate in its original form, and its "linear response" is a large-$K$ leading-order statement. The project's networks are conductance-based and finite, so the cancellation is approximate and the linearity is a tendency, not a law — which is exactly the kind of deviation [nb050](/notebooks/nb050/) is set up to measure.
