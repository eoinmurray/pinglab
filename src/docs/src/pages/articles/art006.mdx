# Training

This page is the shared training recipe every model on the ladder uses — the gradient framework, the loss and optimiser, the tasks the networks are exercised against, and the pitfalls that bite across models.

## Backpropagation through time

Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) is how gradients are computed in any network whose output at time $t$ depends on state from earlier timesteps. The idea is to *unroll* the recurrent computation into a deep feedforward graph and then run standard backpropagation through it.

Consider a recurrent system with hidden state $h^t$ that evolves according to:

$$
h^t = f(h^{t-1}, x^t; \theta), \quad y^t = g(h^t; \theta)
$$

where $x^t$ is the input, $y^t$ is the output, and $\theta$ are the parameters (shared across time). Iterating for $T$ steps produces a chain $h^0 \to h^1 \to \cdots \to h^T$. For gradient computation, we treat this chain as a deep feedforward network of depth $T$ where layer $t$'s weights are tied to layer $t-1$'s.

The gradient of the loss with respect to a parameter $\theta_k$ is a sum over all timesteps at which $\theta$ influenced the computation:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_k} = \sum_{t=1}^T \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial h^t} \cdot \frac{\partial h^t}{\partial \theta_k}
$$

Each $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial h^t$ is itself recursive:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial h^t} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial h^{t+1}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{t+1}}{\partial h^t} + \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial y^t} \cdot \frac{\partial y^t}{\partial h^t}
$$

The backward pass marches from $t = T$ down to $t = 1$, accumulating gradient contributions at each step.

The recursion contains a product of Jacobians $\partial h^{t+1}/\partial h^t$ across every timestep:

$$
\frac{\partial h^T}{\partial h^1} = \prod_{t=1}^{T-1} \frac{\partial h^{t+1}}{\partial h^t}
$$

If the Jacobian norms are consistently greater than 1, this product explodes exponentially in $T$; if consistently less than 1, it vanishes. Standard mitigations include gradient clipping, architectural choices that stabilise the Jacobian, and in the SNN setting, surrogate gradients that smooth the otherwise-discontinuous spike function.

SNNs are a natural fit for BPTT: each timestep of the simulation is one step of the recursion, and the "hidden state" includes membrane potentials, synaptic conductances, and refractory counters. Simulating a 200 ms input at $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms gives $T = 2000$ unrolled steps — a very deep computation graph. Because SNNs have biophysical state variables with physical units ($V$ in mV, $g$ in μS), the Jacobians can be wildly scaled — voltage updates involve tiny factors like $\Delta t / C_m$, while surrogate gradients through spikes are $O(1)$. This is the origin of the gradient scale mismatch addressed by the dampening trick below.

## Surrogate gradients

The spike function $S = \mathbf{1}[U \geq \theta]$ has zero gradient almost everywhere, so backward passes use a surrogate. Pinglab uses fast-sigmoid (matching [snntorch.surrogate.fast_sigmoid](https://snntorch.readthedocs.io/en/latest/snntorch.surrogate.html); motivation and alternatives discussed in [Tutorial 5 § The Surrogate Gradient Approach](https://snntorch.readthedocs.io/en/latest/tutorials/tutorial_5.html)) with pseudo-derivative

$$
\frac{\partial \tilde S}{\partial U}\bigg|_{U} = \frac{k}{(1 + k\,|U - \theta|)^2}
$$

with slope $k = 1$ on both the biophysical path (mV-scale membrane) and the standard-snn path (dimensionless membrane). The snnTorch-library path uses the library default $k = 25$ — the asymmetry is deliberate and is part of what the parity test exposes. This keeps the forward dynamics exact while giving BPTT a usable gradient through the otherwise-discontinuous spike function.

## Gradient stabilisation for biophysical models

The biophysical models (COBA, PING) face a gradient-scale problem. Naive backprop through a 2000-step trial produces NaN within a few batches. Pinglab handles this with the costate-control framework of [Burghi, Pugliese Carratelli & Rule 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.16627) ("Costate control for nonlinear system identification with applications to excitable systems"). The framework is general; the paper develops it for parameter identification of Hodgkin–Huxley-type models. We use it for SNN training, which is the same mathematical problem (BPTT through an excitable forward model) wearing different clothes.

This section builds up to two controllers, $K^{\text{prop}}$ and $K^{\text{gat}}$, both of which are exposed as CLI flags. The theory takes a few steps; the controllers themselves are one-line code changes.

### Why the Jacobian blows up in excitable systems

The forward dynamics of a conductance-based neuron, in continuous time, are

$$
\dot x = f_\theta(t, x, u), \qquad x = (V, g_e, g_i, \ldots),
$$

with $u(t)$ the input and $\theta$ the parameters (weights, time constants, etc.). The Jacobian $\nabla_x f_\theta$ describes how a small perturbation in any state variable propagates into all the others over one infinitesimal step. For a LIF neuron with conductance synapses, $\nabla_x f_\theta$ has the block structure

$$
\nabla_x f_\theta = \begin{pmatrix}
\partial \dot V / \partial V & \partial \dot V / \partial g \\
\partial \dot g / \partial V & \partial \dot g / \partial g
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The diagonal blocks decay (the membrane and conductance both have leak terms). The **off-diagonal** blocks are the cross-coupling: $\partial \dot V / \partial g$ is the synaptic drive into the membrane (large during spikes), $\partial \dot g / \partial V$ is the voltage's feedback into the conductance (small but nonzero through reversal-potential terms). It's the off-diagonal block that has the destabilising spectrum — large transient eigenvalue magnitudes during firing events, which is the *very feature* that lets the neuron spike fast.

For training, we don't just need $\nabla_x f_\theta$ at one moment; we need to chain it across every timestep in the trial. The product $\prod_{t=1}^T \nabla_x f_\theta(t)$ accumulates the off-diagonal explosion exponentially in $T$. For a $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms simulation of a 200 ms trial, $T = 2000$ — the product blows up by many orders of magnitude.

### The costate (adjoint) equation

BPTT computes parameter gradients by running an adjoint equation backward in time. Define the *costate* $\lambda(t) \in \mathbb{R}^d$, initialised at the loss's gradient with respect to the terminal state:

$$
\lambda(T) = \nabla_{x(T)} \mathcal{L}, \qquad
\dot \lambda(t) = -\nabla_x f_\theta(t, x, u)^\top \lambda(t).
$$

The parameter gradient is then the integral $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} = -\int_0^T \nabla_\theta f_\theta^\top \lambda \, dt$. This is exactly what PyTorch's autograd computes when you call $\mathcal{L}.\mathrm{backward}()$ on a network unrolled in time, just stated as a continuous ODE rather than a discrete chain rule.

The costate equation is *linear in $\lambda$*, with time-varying coefficient $\nabla_x f_\theta$. Its solution norm therefore tracks the integrated exponent of the Jacobian:

$$
\|\lambda(0)\| \leq \|\lambda(T)\| \cdot \exp\!\left(\int_0^T \|\nabla_x f_\theta(t)\|\,dt\right).
$$

If $\nabla_x f_\theta$ has any large transient eigenvalues — as it does during spikes — the costate blows up between $t = T$ and $t = 0$, and the parameter gradient comes out either NaN or astronomically large.

### Costate control

The fix is to modify the costate equation — and *only* the costate equation, not the forward pass — by adding a controller $K_\theta(t, x)$:

$$
\dot \lambda = -[\nabla_x f_\theta + K_\theta(t, x)]^\top \lambda.
$$

If we choose $K_\theta$ so that the operator $\nabla_x f_\theta + K_\theta$ has eigenvalues with sufficiently negative real parts (*exponentially contracting*), the costate $\lambda(t)$ stays bounded by a $K_\theta$-controlled exponential as $t$ marches backward — independent of how badly $\nabla_x f_\theta$ alone would have blown up.

The forward dynamics are unchanged. The forward state $x(t)$ is identical with or without the controller. Only the *gradient flow* is modified — backward propagation now respects a different effective Jacobian.

This breaks any naïve identification of "the gradient" with "the true gradient": we're computing a *surrogate* gradient. The interesting question is whether descending on the surrogate still converges to the right answer.

### Proposition 1 — same fixed points

The paper's key theoretical result is that controlled costate descent has the same critical points as true gradient descent. Let $\theta^\star$ be a stationary point of the original loss, i.e. $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta^\star) = 0$. Burghi et al. prove: the controlled gradient (computed by the modified costate) also vanishes at $\theta^\star$, provided $K_\theta$ is chosen so the modified costate is uniformly bounded.

The intuition: at $\theta^\star$ the true costate $\lambda$ produces a parameter integral that integrates to zero. The controller $K_\theta$ deforms $\lambda$ along the trial, but doesn't add any new sources of bias — at the global optimum the modified $\lambda$ also integrates to zero through $\nabla_\theta f_\theta$. The path through $\theta$-space gets reshaped; the destination doesn't.

This is what makes costate control different from blunt tricks like aggressive gradient clipping. Clipping changes where the optimiser converges to (a clipped gradient is a different vector field). $K_\theta$ converges to the same place — it just takes a different route there. In particular, no fictitious local minima.

### Two controller designs

Burghi et al. propose two specific choices for $K_\theta$:

**$K^{\text{prop}}$** — proportional high-gain. Pick a scalar $\gamma > 0$ and set $K_\theta(t, x) = -\gamma I$. The controller is constant; it scales every component of the costate equation uniformly. The resulting modified Jacobian has eigenvalues shifted by $-\gamma$, which guarantees exponential contraction as long as $\gamma > \max_t \mathrm{Re}(\text{eig}(\nabla_x f_\theta))$.

Pinglab's *--v-grad-dampen* flag is the discrete-time analogue: scale the backward voltage gradient by $1/\gamma$ at every timestep, with $\gamma$ around 80 for unitless models and 1000 for COBA/PING:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial V_i^t} \leftarrow \frac{1}{\gamma}\,\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial V_i^t}.
$$

This works — it eliminates the NaN — but it's *uniform*. It attenuates the diagonal voltage gradient (which we wanted to keep) by the same factor as the destabilising off-diagonal block (which we wanted to suppress). The loss landscape becomes uniformly smoother. Empirically, large $\gamma$ silently caps achievable accuracy; the controller's gain is so high that useful first-order information is washed out along with the explosion.

**$K^{\text{gat}}$** — gating-based. Pick $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ and define $K_\theta$ to act *only* on the off-diagonal block of $\nabla_x f_\theta$, scaling it by $(1 - \alpha)$ and leaving the diagonal alone:

$$
\nabla_x f_\theta + K^{\text{gat}}_\theta =
\begin{pmatrix}
\partial \dot V / \partial V & \alpha\,\partial \dot V / \partial g \\
\alpha\,\partial \dot g / \partial V & \partial \dot g / \partial g
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

At $\alpha = 1$ the controller is identity (no modification — bit-exact plain BPTT). At $\alpha = 0$ the cross-coupling is fully blocked and only the diagonal contributes. Useful values sit around $\alpha \in [0.3, 0.7]$ — enough to tame the off-diagonal explosion, not so much that the optimiser loses the cross-coupling signal entirely.

Two properties make $K^{\text{gat}}$ work in practice. First, the diagonal gradient through voltage (the leak / refractory pathway) is *untouched* — Adam still sees a clean signal there. Second, the parameter gradient $\nabla_\theta f_\theta$ is also untouched — the controller acts on the costate dynamics, not on the integral that produces $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$. Both are how $K^{\text{gat}}$ retains landscape structure that $K^{\text{prop}}$ flattens.

### Implementing $K^{\text{gat}}$ as a one-line scale-grad

The discrete-time implementation drops out of the structure of LIF + COBA. At each timestep the LIF step takes $(V^t, g_e^t, g_i^t)$ as input and emits $(V^{t+1}, g_e^{t+1}, g_i^{t+1}, S^{t+1})$. The off-diagonal block of $\nabla_x f_\theta$ is precisely the gradient that flows from $V^{t+1}$ *back into* $g_e^t$ and $g_i^t$. So scaling only the gradient that the conductance receives from downstream voltage achieves the $K^{\text{gat}}$ effect.

In PyTorch this is a custom autograd op: $\mathrm{ScaleGrad}(x, \alpha)$ is identity in the forward pass and multiplies its gradient by $\alpha$ in the backward pass. Wrap the combined conductance term immediately before it enters the LIF step:

$$
g_e^{\text{step}} = \mathrm{ScaleGrad}(g_e, \alpha), \qquad
V^{t+1}, S^{t+1} = \mathrm{LIF\,step}(V^t, g_e^{\text{step}}, g_i^{\text{step}}).
$$

Forward: $g_e^{\text{step}} = g_e$. Backward: $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial g_e \leftarrow \alpha \cdot \partial \mathcal{L} / \partial g_e$. That's the entire implementation. Pinglab exposes the flag pair *--sgcc / --sgcc-alpha*. Bit-exact parity with plain COBA at $\alpha = 1$ is verified in the smoke tests.

### Empirical validation

A head-to-head comparison — same network, same recipe, same seed, *--v-grad-dampen 1000* replaced with *--sgcc --sgcc-alpha 0.5* — tests the prediction directly. Through the first ~10 epochs the two training curves are visually indistinguishable: both descend from CE loss $\sim 2.3$ to $\sim 0.55$, both reach $\sim 84\%$ test accuracy. This matches Proposition 1's prediction: same optimum, similar path early.

Past epoch 10 the V_GRAD_DAMPEN baseline plateaus; SGCC continues to descend and reaches 87.75% by epoch 20. The 3.5 pp gap is the cost of $K^{\text{prop}}$'s uniformity — the global dampener was silently throttling the diagonal pathway too. $K^{\text{gat}}$ keeps the diagonal sharp while taming only what needed taming.

The trained-network rasters corroborate the dynamical equivalence: the SGCC and V_GRAD_DAMPEN baselines arrive at indistinguishable operating points (~85 Hz E, asynchronous-dense, silent I) via different optimisation paths.

### Choosing between them

*--v-grad-dampen* is the simpler instrument — one scalar, no autograd subclass — and is what to use when you just need the network not to NaN. *--sgcc* is what to reach for when V_GRAD_DAMPEN is silently capping accuracy, on longer-trial tasks where preserving cross-coupling gradient matters (working memory, sequence problems), or whenever you want the gradient to behave the way the underlying optimisation problem wants it to. Both are flag-level changes; nothing else in the recipe needs to move.

## The training loop

The network output (spike-count readout, unified across all models) is treated as logits and passed to cross-entropy loss:

$$
L_\text{CE} = -\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \log \frac{\exp(\hat y_{b,\,c_b})}{\sum_k \exp(\hat y_{b,k})}
$$

where $B$ is the batch size, $\hat y_b$ is the logit vector for sample $b$, and $c_b$ is the true class. Chance-level loss on a 10-class problem is $\ln 10 \approx 2.30$.

The optimiser is Adam with an optional ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler, or the --adaptive-lr schedule which divides the learning rate by 10 after epoch 20. Gradients are clipped to unit norm before each step.

The best model state (by test accuracy) is saved at each epoch. Training terminates early if accuracy has not improved for a configurable number of epochs (default patience: 15). The saved weights.pth is the *best-epoch* state, not the final-epoch state.

## Linear readout

Every model in the ladder uses the same output head: accumulate last-hidden-layer spikes over the trial and project through a trained linear layer.

$$
\hat y_t = \Bigl(\sum_{s \leq t} s^{\text{hid}}_s\Bigr) W_{\text{out}} + b_{\text{out}}
$$

No output-layer spiking dynamics, no softmax — logits are read out at each step and the final $\hat y_{T}$ is used for cross-entropy loss. Shared readout means any accuracy gap across the ladder cannot localise to the output.

## Weight init

Feedforward weights are sampled from a fan-in-normalised half-normal (Dale's law) or normal (signed):

$$
W \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2), \qquad W \leftarrow W / N_{\text{pre}}
$$

with optional sparsity $s \in [0, 1)$: a fraction $s$ of entries are zeroed, and surviving entries are rescaled by $1/(1-s)$ so that total expected synaptic input per post-neuron is preserved. Under Dale's law ($\mu \geq 0$, clamp $< 0$ to $0$), signs are fixed at init and the forward pass re-clamps on every step.

## Dale's law under Adam

When Dale's law is enforced, weight matrices are clamped to $W \geq 0$ in the forward pass. Gradients still flow through the unclamped parameters, so Adam can push a weight below zero — but it will be clipped back to zero on the next forward pass. The optimiser explores the full real line; the network only ever sees non-negative weights.

## Tasks and inputs

The models are exercised against three classes of input: synthetic drive for probing PING dynamics in isolation, synthetic spike trains for a spiking input with no spatial structure, and real image datasets for classification.

### Synthetic conductance

Direct conductance injection into layer-1 E neurons, used for baseline oscillation studies where we want to study PING dynamics in isolation from any encoding stage. Drive is generated Börgers-style as a step function with per-neuron heterogeneity $X_i$ plus an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck noise process:

$$
g_i^{\text{ext}}(t) = T_E(t)(1 + \sigma_e X_i) + \eta_i(t)
$$

where $T_E$ switches between async and PING-regime values during a stimulus window, $X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ is per-neuron heterogeneity, and $\eta$ is a discrete Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process with $\tau_\eta = 3$ ms. Drive is calibrated at $\Delta t_{\text{cal}} = 0.1$ ms and rescaled at runtime so that the steady-state AMPA conductance is invariant across $\Delta t$.

### Synthetic spikes

Poisson spike trains over $N_{\text{in}}$ input neurons with a rate that steps between a baseline and a stimulus level during a fixed window. Used when we want a spiking input with no spatial structure.

### Image datasets

Supported datasets are scikit-digits ($8 \times 8$, 10 classes), MNIST ($28 \times 28 = 784$), and sequential MNIST. Pixels become Poisson spike trains. For a pixel with normalised intensity $x \in [0, 1]$, input neuron $i$ fires a Bernoulli spike at each step with probability

$$
p_i(t) = x_i \cdot r_{\max} \cdot \Delta t / 1000, \qquad r_{\max} = 25 \text{ Hz}
$$

so the per-neuron firing rate is $x_i \cdot r_{\max}$ Hz, independent of $\Delta t$. The resulting $(T, B, N_{\text{in}})$ binary tensor is the model's only input; stimulus duration is $T_{\text{ms}} = 1000$ ms for image tasks. Because encoding is stochastic, the network sees a different spike realisation of the same image every epoch — a form of data augmentation intrinsic to the rate code. At evaluation time, a seeded torch.Generator is threaded through the encoder so train-time eval and standalone infer on the same weights produce identical spike trains.

Sequential MNIST (sMNIST) presents each $28 \times 28$ image row-by-row: at each moment only the 28 pixels of the current row are presented, for $t_{\text{row}} = 10$ ms, giving a $T = 280$ ms sample with $N_{\text{in}} = 28$. This is the standard ML benchmark for temporal credit assignment in SNNs. Because the 28-neuron bottleneck is too narrow to classify from, sMNIST runs require $\geq 2$ hidden layers and recurrence on at least one of them.
