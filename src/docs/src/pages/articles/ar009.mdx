## Results

### Claim 0. The model: PING is a COBA network plus one recurrent E↔I loop

<Figure id="Figure 0" title="PING network architecture" src="/figures/general/network.png" alt="Schematic of the PING network: an input layer projects through W_in to the excitatory (E) population; E projects to the inhibitory (I) population through W_ei, and I projects back to E through W_ie, forming the recurrent E→I→E loop. There is no I→I synapse.">
  The architecture. An input layer drives the excitatory population through $W_\text{in}$; the recurrent loop is $W_{ei}$ (E→I) and $W_{ie}$ (I→E), with no I→I synapse. Disabling the loop ($W_{ei} = W_{ie} = 0$) recovers the COBA reference; engaging it produces the gamma rhythm. Full equations in the Methods.
</Figure>

### Claim 1. The loop makes gamma and compresses the E rate

<Figure id="Figure 1" title="COBA vs PING — rasters, spectra, and f–I curves" src="/figures/notebooks/nb023/raster_compound.png" alt="Two-column figure; each condition has one combined spike raster on top (E neurons black in the lower portion, I neurons red in the upper portion, same axes) and a bottom row of two panels: a Welch PSD beside an f–I curve. Left A (COBA, loop off): E-only raster firing asynchronously, labelled 'no I population'; PSD with scattered power and no gamma peak; f–I where E (black) climbs and saturates near 270 Hz and I (red) stays at zero. Right B (PING, loop active): combined raster with E in vertical bands and I bursting synchronously once per cycle above; PSD with a sharp peak at f_gamma ≈ 25 Hz; f–I on the same y-scale where E stays ≈ 2–9 Hz and I climbs to ≈ 64 Hz.">
  The recurrent loop, off and on, on the same network ([nb023](/notebooks/nb023/)). Each column shows one combined raster (E black, I red above) over a Welch PSD and a free-running f–I curve. **A — COBA** (loop off): asynchronous E, no gamma peak, an E f–I curve saturating toward ≈ 270 Hz. **B — PING** (loop on): synchronous I bursts, E gamma bands, a clean PSD peak at $f_\gamma \approx 25$ Hz, and E held ≈ 10× below COBA across two decades of drive. The single E→I→E loop converts asynchronous firing into a gamma rhythm *and* clamps the E rate.
</Figure>

### Claim 2. Rhythmicity turns on smoothly across the $W_{ei} \times W_{ie}$ plane

<Figure id="Figure 2" title="Gamma turns on across the W_EI × W_IE plane" src="/figures/notebooks/nb054/turnon_compound.png" alt="Left: a greyscale 11×11 heatmap of lobe–trough contrast over W_EI mean (x) and W_IE mean (y), each cell labelled, three points marked A, B, C; pale at the weak-coupling edges, dark toward the strong corner.   Gamma switches on smoothly across the recurrent-weight plane ([nb054](/notebooks/nb054/)). **Left** — the lobe–trough contrast (the rhythm scored 0–1) over $W_{ei} \times W_{ie}$: exactly 0 along both COBA edges, rising smoothly to ≈ 0.9 toward strong coupling. **Right** — example rasters at the marked points: **A** loop-off (asynchronous), **B** intermediate (clear volleys), **C** strong (sharp volleys).
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 3" title="E rate, I rate, and contrast over the W_EI × W_IE plane" src="/figures/notebooks/nb054/grid_maps.png" alt="Three square 11×11 greyscale heatmaps, every cell labelled.   The loop engaging, read three ways across the same plane ([nb054](/notebooks/nb054/); greyscale, every cell labelled). **E rate** falls, **I rate** rises, and **contrast** rises together — three readings of one loop engaging, with both COBA edges anchoring the baseline at E ≈ 95 Hz / contrast 0.
</Figure>

### Claim 3. The onset is a predictable bifurcation

<Figure id="Figure 4" title="The recruitment cliff is a predictable Hopf bifurcation" src="/figures/notebooks/nb033/bifurcation_compound.png" alt="Three panels. C: gamma frequency vs τ_GABA, calibrated mean-field f* (black) tracking the nb041 spiking f_gamma (red), both falling with slower inhibition.">
  The recruitment cliff of Claim 4, explained by a 4D conductance mean-field calibrated from the biophysics — no fitted scale ([nb033](/notebooks/nb033/)). **A** — the loop's eigenvalues: one complex pair crosses into the right half-plane at $I^\star = 0.59$ nA (cyan), a Hopf bifurcation whose imaginary part sets a gamma rhythm at $f^\star \approx 24.5$ Hz. **B** — the hysteresis sweep: amplitude rises continuously from $I^\star$ and the up/down branches coincide, so the onset is **supercritical and reversible**, not a hard switch. **C** — the predicted gamma frequency falls with $\tau_\text{GABA}$ in step with the spiking measurement ([nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)). The turn-on of Claim 2 is a textbook bifurcation set by the synaptic time constants. *(The empirical counterpart — $W_{ei}$ sets the cliff, $W_{ie}$ shapes the basin, [nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) — is a planned second panel.)*
</Figure>

### Claim 4. Trained, PING matches accuracy on $\approx$10× fewer spikes, and the floor is a stable attractor

<Figure id="Figure 5" title="PING matches COBA accuracy at a fraction of the spikes" src="/figures/notebooks/nb025/results_compound.png" alt="Two-by-two figure. Top row, two trained-baseline single-trial rasters: COBA (loop off) dense and asynchronous with I silent; PING (loop on) gamma bands with synchronous I bursts (red) above E (black). Bottom left, test accuracy per epoch for both models rising to ≈83%. Bottom right, accuracy–rate frontier across the θ_u sweep: PING (black) up-and-left of COBA (red), operating points starred and labelled (PING ≈83% at ≈3.8 Hz, COBA ≈90% at ≈56 Hz).">
  Trained on MNIST with surrogate-gradient descent ([nb025](/notebooks/nb025/)): both architectures learn the task, but PING does it on ≈ 10× fewer spikes. **Top** — rasters: COBA dense and asynchronous, PING gamma-banded with synchronous I (red) above E (black). **Bottom left** — test accuracy per epoch: both reach ≈ 83%. **Bottom right** — the accuracy–rate frontier across the spike-budget penalty $\theta_u$: PING sits up-and-left of COBA — the same accuracy at a fraction of the hidden-E rate — with the $\theta_u$-off operating points starred and labelled. The structural floor (it cannot be trained away) and the attractor result (the rate locks while COBA's drifts) are in [nb025](/notebooks/nb025/) and [nb024](/notebooks/nb024/). The collection's headline: gamma gating buys an order-of-magnitude per-spike economy at matched accuracy.
</Figure>

### Claim 5. The sparsity is supplied by the architecture, not learned

<Figure id="Figure 6" title="The I-loop is a post-hoc sparsity knob" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/loop_transfer_compound.png" alt="Two-by-two figure. Top: single-trial rasters of a trained COBA replayed at ei=0 (dense asynchronous, I silent) and ei=1 (gamma bands, synchronous I bursts in red above E). Bottom left: mean E (black) and I (red) firing rate vs inference E→I strength — E falls from ≈60 to ≈5 Hz, I rises to ≈28 Hz. Bottom right: test accuracy vs ei strength, falling from the ≈87% COBA baseline to ≈63% at ei=1.">
  Switch the recurrent loop on *at inference* on a trained COBA, with no retraining ([nb038](/notebooks/nb038/)). **Top** — the same feedforward weights fire densely/asynchronously at $ei=0$ and in gamma bands at $ei=1$. **Bottom left** — E rate falls ≈ 10× (≈ 60 → 5 Hz) as the loop engages. **Bottom right** — accuracy *degrades* without retraining (≈ 87% → 63%, a ≈ 24 pp cost). The reading: the rate-gating is **architectural** (it appears the instant the loop is wired in), but the accuracy must be **learned** by training with the loop (Claim 4). Together with the converse — unfreeze the recurrent weights and gradient descent *prunes* the loop away rather than discovering it ([nb049](/notebooks/nb049/), Dale's-law clamp; Figure 7) — the loop is a structural prior the architecture imposes, not one training finds.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 7" title="Gradient descent prunes the loop — it does not discover PING" src="/figures/notebooks/nb049/attractor_ei.png" alt="Scatter of I firing rate (y, 0–40 Hz) vs E firing rate (x, 0–50 Hz). Frozen-PING control (grey) sits in the upper-left PING corner (E≈8, I≈38 Hz, 86% accuracy); all trainable conditions — PING init (black), zero init (red), 0.1× canonical init (orange), three seeds each — collapse to the lower-right loop-free corner (E≈37–48, I≈0 Hz, 88–89% accuracy), the inhibitory population silent.">
  The converse test ([nb049](/notebooks/nb049/)): unfreeze the recurrent weights $W^{EI}, W^{IE}$ and let Adam train them. From *any* initialisation — canonical PING, zero, or 0.1× canonical — training prunes most $W^{EI}$ entries below zero, the Dale's-law forward-pass clamp turns them into structural zeros, and the network collapses to the **loop-free COBA corner** (I silent, dense E firing) at ≈ 88–89% accuracy — slightly *above* the frozen-PING control (grey). Gradient descent does not rediscover PING; the loop is a structural prior the **freeze imposes**. Read with Figure 6, the two halves close the claim: the architecture supplies the gating, and training neither needs it nor finds it.
</Figure>

### Claim 6. The E rate is set by the gamma clock: one spike per cell per cycle

<Figure id="Figure 8" title="The E rate is set by the gamma clock: r_E = a + p·f_γ" src="/figures/notebooks/nb041/rate_vs_fgamma.png" alt="Top panel: scatter of mean post-training E rate (Hz) vs measured gamma frequency f_γ (Hz), six clusters one per τ_GABA value (4.5 to 27 ms), three seeds each, error bars in both axes; an affine fit r_E = 0.76 + 0.216·f_γ passes through every error bar with R² = 0.997. Bottom panel: test accuracy vs the same x-axis, flat at 81–89% across the whole f_γ range.">
  Retrain the network at each $\tau_\text{GABA}$ ([nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)) to sweep the gamma frequency $f_\gamma$, and the post-training E rate tracks it **affinely**: $r_E = 0.76 + 0.216\,f_\gamma$ ($R^2 = 0.997$, three seeds per point). The slope $p \approx 0.2$ is the per-cycle E-cell participation; the intercept is a small non-rhythmic baseline. **Bottom** — accuracy holds at 81–89% across the whole range, so the cycle clock constrains the rate without costing accuracy. The E rate is not a free parameter: it is set by the rhythm — roughly one spike per cell every few gamma cycles.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 9" title="One spike per cell per cycle — the rate law's microscopic cause" src="/figures/notebooks/nb046/spikes_per_cycle_distribution.png" alt="Six bar-chart panels, one per τ_GABA value. Each panel shows the fraction of (E cell, gamma cycle) pairs emitting 0, 1, 2, or ≥3 spikes within the cycle window. Across all panels P(0) ≈ 77%, P(1) ≈ 22%, P(2) ≈ 0.5%, P(≥3) ≈ 0.04%; the ≥2 fraction grows mildly with τ_GABA but stays below 3%.">
  Why is the rate affine in $f_\gamma$? Because each E cell fires **at most once per gamma cycle** ([nb046](/notebooks/nb046/)). Resolving the spikes into (cell, cycle) pairs, $99.45\%$ emit $\leq 1$ spike: across the whole $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep $P(0) \approx 77\%$, $P(1) \approx 22\%$, and multi-spike events ($\geq 2$) stay below $\sim 3\%$. A cell either skips the cycle or contributes a single spike — the I-volley resets the population before a second can fire. So the E rate is just the per-cycle participation fraction times the cycle frequency, which is exactly the affine law of Figure 8.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 10" title="The rate knob is the per-synapse weight W^IE, not the I pool size" src="/figures/notebooks/nb047/rate_vs_w_ie.png" alt="Two side-by-side panels of per-cell firing rate versus the per-synapse I-to-E weight W^IE in microsiemens, from 0.5 to 16. Left: E rate. Right: I rate. Each panel has three lines, one per pool size N_I = 16, 64, 256, and the three lines lie on top of one another. The E rate falls from ≈ 14 Hz at W^IE = 0.5 to ≈ 9.6 Hz at W^IE = 16; the I rate falls from ≈ 42 Hz to ≈ 27 Hz. A dotted vertical line marks the default W^IE = 2.">
  What sets the suppression strength? The *per-synapse* I→E weight $W^{IE}$, not how many interneurons there are ([nb047](/notebooks/nb047/)). Sweeping $W^{IE}$ across three I-pool sizes ($N_I = 16, 64, 256$), the E and I rate curves **collapse onto a single line** — the three pool sizes are indistinguishable. The rate is governed by the strength of each inhibitory contact, so the loop scales by synaptic gain rather than headcount: a handful of strong interneurons gate the E population as effectively as a large pool.
</Figure>

### Claim 7. The gating is dynamical and robust

<Figure id="Figure 11" title="The gating is robust to dropped spikes but fragile to added ones" src="/figures/notebooks/nb037/perturbation_curves.png" alt="Two-panel accuracy plot, both axes in percentage units. Left: accuracy vs % of emitted spikes dropped (0–100%). PING (black) holds ≈ 82% through 80% drop then collapses to chance by 100%; COBA (red) tracks slightly below, collapsing similarly. Right: accuracy vs added Poisson noise as % of each population's baseline rate (0–100%). PING falls off a sharp cliff between 15% and 50% relative noise, reaching chance by 65%. COBA holds 82–88% through 50% then drifts down to 75% at 100% noise.">
  Perturbing a trained PING's spikes at inference reveals a sharp **drop/add asymmetry** ([nb037](/notebooks/nb037/)). **Left** — dropping emitted spikes is benign: PING holds ≈ 82% accuracy through an *80%* drop, only collapsing as the network goes fully silent. The code is robust to deletion because the surviving gamma cycles still carry the digit. **Right** — added Poisson spikes are corrosive: PING falls off a cliff by ≈ 15% relative noise and reaches chance by 65%, because off-phase spikes drive the I-pool at arbitrary phase and dissolve the rhythm. COBA, having no rhythm to protect, is the mirror image — it tolerates added noise but has no structure to exploit. The gating lives in spike *timing*: silence is information, but noise breaks the clock.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 12" title="The suppression is the rhythm, not mean inhibition" src="/figures/notebooks/nb042/rhythm_compound.png" alt="Two-by-two figure. Top row, two single-trial PING rasters, E black below and I red above. Left: per-I-cell jitter σ = 5 ms, I smeared into continuous scatter, E silent (E ≈ 0 Hz, I ≈ 40 Hz). Right: cycle-coherent jitter σ = 100 ms, bursts displaced but crisp, E firing densely (E ≈ 44 Hz, I ≈ 35 Hz). Bottom row, E rate (black) and accuracy (red) vs σ. Bottom left: per-I-cell jitter, E rate falls to ≈ 0 Hz, accuracy to chance. Bottom right: cycle-coherent jitter, E rate rises from ≈ 8 to ≈ 50 Hz, accuracy ≈ 80%.">
  The rate gap cannot be explained away as "the I-loop just delivers more inhibition." Two inference-time perturbations of the trained PING I-stream — **both holding mean per-cell I rate fixed** ([nb042](/notebooks/nb042/)) — drive the E rate in *opposite* directions. **Left** — per-I-cell jitter smears each burst into a continuous shunt at the same mean rate; the E rate *falls to zero* and accuracy collapses to chance. **Right** — cycle-coherent jitter displaces each gamma burst while preserving its within-burst synchrony; gaps open and the E rate *rises* from ≈ 8 Hz toward ≈ 50 Hz, accuracy holding near 80%. Identical mean inhibition, opposite outcome: the gate is the *timing* of inhibition, not its average.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 13" title="The rate is a physical-Hz property, invariant across a 20× Δt sweep" src="/figures/notebooks/nb044/dt_sweep.png" alt="Two-axis plot. Log x-axis: integration timestep Δt from 0.05 to 1.0 ms. Left y-axis (black diamonds, 0–50 Hz scale): post-training hidden E rate sits in an 8.4–12 Hz band across the whole sweep, non-monotonic in Δt. Right y-axis (red squares): test accuracy holds essentially flat at 86.8–89.5% across the entire sweep, a span of 2.7 percentage points.">
  Is the sparse rate a real biophysical quantity or a discretisation artefact? Retraining the PING across a **20× span of integration timestep** ($\Delta t = 0.05 \to 1.0$ ms) leaves it intact ([nb044](/notebooks/nb044/)): the post-training E rate stays in a tight $8.4$–$12$ Hz band — non-monotonic in $\Delta t$, so finer stepping does not buy a lower rate — and accuracy holds flat within $2.7$ pp. The bursts keep the same physical cadence (≈ 30 ms period) regardless of $\Delta t$. The rate is measured in spikes per second, not spikes per step: a property of the dynamics, not of the solver.
</Figure>

### Claim 8. The economy is usable: real-time streaming classification

The economy would be academic if it only worked on static, one-digit-at-a-time presentations. It does not: a PING trained on single MNIST digits classifies a *continuous stream* with no retraining and no segmentation cues ([nb048](/notebooks/nb048/)). The readout is a sliding leaky integrator over the same trained $W_\text{out}$; the network re-identifies each new digit within ≈ 1 gamma cycle of the switch.

<Figure id="Figure 14" title="Robust to per-digit changes in duration and drive within one stream" src="/figures/notebooks/nb048/varying_headline_stream.png" alt="Four-panel column figure. Top: five MNIST digits across 450 ms, each segment labelled with its own (τ, rate) and a prediction badge; thumbnail opacity scales with input rate. Segments: (200 ms, 10 Hz)→5; (50 ms, 100 Hz)→3; (100 ms, 25 Hz)→4; (25 ms, 200 Hz)→1; (75 ms, 15 Hz)→7. All five correct. Hidden E and I rasters show gamma cycles throughout, sparser under weak drive and denser under strong drive; readout traces identify each segment cleanly.">
  A harder test: each digit gets its *own* duration (25–200 ms) and input rate (10–200 Hz) within a single stream, the sliding window using each segment's own τ. The gamma clock persists under both weak and strong drive, firing sparsely or densely to match, and every digit is still read out cleanly. **5/5 correct** — the streaming economy is not tuned to one presentation rate.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 15" title="The gamma cycle is the temporal quantum of classification" src="/figures/notebooks/nb048/acc_grid_tau_rate.png" alt="Heatmap of per-segment accuracy across τ ∈ {10, 15, 25, 40, 50, 75, 100, 200} ms (x-axis) and input rate ∈ {5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 200} Hz (y-axis), 48 cells, averaged over 3 seeds × 1200 segments per cell, magma colormap 0–100%. Lower-left (10 ms, 5 Hz): 14%. Upper-right (200 ms, 200 Hz): 91%. The high-accuracy region is broad with roughly hyperbolic τ × rate contours. Trained baseline (200 ms, 25 Hz): 88%. A sub-cycle failure regime at τ ≤ 15 ms where even the strongest input stays below 80%.">
  Per-segment accuracy over the full (τ, input-rate) grid (3 seeds × 1200 segments per cell). Three readings. **(1) Sub-cycle failure below τ ≈ 15 ms** — at τ = 10 ms even 200 Hz input reaches only 59%; the network cannot classify in less than one cycle whatever the drive, so the **gamma cycle is the temporal quantum** of its ability. **(2) Above one cycle, accuracy ≈ f(τ · rate)** — iso-accuracy contours run diagonal, so duration and drive substitute for one another. **(3) The trained operating point** (200 ms, 25 Hz) sits at 88%, interior to a broad plateau — the corners add only ≈ 3 pp, so there is headroom in both directions. The economy is usable across a wide envelope of stream timings, bounded below only by the cycle itself.
</Figure>
