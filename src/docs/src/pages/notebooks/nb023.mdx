## Introduction

PING (pyramidal-interneuron gamma) is the gamma-band oscillation produced by an excitatory population $E$ feeding an inhibitory population $I$ which in turn feeds back onto $E$. The standard story:

1. The E population starts firing.
2. After an AMPA-delay ($\tau_{\text{AMPA}} \approx 2$ ms), the I population spikes.
3. GABA-A inhibition silences E for the GABA-decay time ($\tau_{\text{GABA}} \approx 9$ ms).
4. Inhibition decays away; E refires.

The interspike-interval comes out around $\tau_{\text{GABA}} + \tau_{\text{AMPA}} \sim 11$ ms, giving a fundamental near $\sim 30$ Hz — the gamma band.

This entry strips PING down to its biophysical fundamentals and characterises it *in isolation from any task*. No training, no readout, no loss — just a free-running network driven by Poisson input. Goal: see the rhythm emerge, measure its frequency, and trace how each piece of the recipe (E-I coupling strength, AMPA / GABA decay constants, refractory period) shapes the gamma peak.

The full conductance-based model that produces PING is documented in [COBANet](/articles/art003/); this notebook is the empirical companion piece.

### Input-rate sweep

The rasters and traces above are at a single working point. Sweeping the Poisson input rate across the trained PING baseline ([nb025](/notebooks/nb025/), seed 42, $\theta_u =$ off) shows how the network responds to drive: silent below a recruitment threshold, gamma-locked above it, with cycle period invariant to further drive once the loop engages.

<Figure id="Figure 5" title="Input-rate sweep on trained PING" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/rate_rasters__ping.png" alt="Ten stacked raster panels showing trained PING on MNIST digit 0 at input rates from 0 to about 23 Hz; E spikes in black, I spikes in red. Each panel is labelled with the input rate, the per-cell E rate, and the per-cell I rate. At low input (0–5 Hz) the network is silent (E and I rates both 0). At ≈8 Hz input the I-loop engages (I rate jumps from 0 to ≈10 Hz). Above engagement the E rate saturates near 4–5 Hz while the I rate climbs from ≈10 to ≈25 Hz.">
  Trained PING replayed on MNIST digit 0 across ten input-rate values; E (black) above I (red) per panel.
</Figure>

At rate = 0 the network is silent. Above a recruitment threshold a few E units fire, the I loop catches, and the gamma cadence appears. Once the loop is locked the cycle period is invariant to further increases in input rate.

A subtlety in the just-engaged panels (e.g. input $\approx 5$ Hz, E = 1.7 Hz, I = 0): the E spikes look mildly clustered even though the I-loop hasn't fired. This is feedforward — not PING. The Poisson input stream has natural temporal fluctuations, the digit-0 input is spatially correlated, and the trained $W_\text{in}$ has learned to be selective for digit-relevant pixels. So the cells that fire are the input-tuned subset, and they fire together during input-rich windows. The participation pattern is partially preserved from training; the gamma timing is not, because the loop is genuinely off.

Extending the sweep over 0–100 Hz and plotting per-cell mean firing rate against input rate gives the population f-I curve.

<Figure id="Figure 6" title="Trained PING f-I curve (MNIST digit 0)" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/fi_curve__ping.png" alt="Single panel: input Poisson rate (Hz, per channel) on x-axis vs per-cell mean firing rate on y-axis. Two curves — E (black circles) and I (red squares). The network is silent below 5 Hz input; at 5 Hz input E fires at 1.65 Hz but I is still silent. At 10 Hz input I engages (jumps to 15 Hz) and E rises to 3.78 Hz. Above engagement E climbs slowly from ~4 Hz at 10 Hz input to ~10 Hz at 100 Hz input; I rises from 15 Hz to ~50 Hz across the same range.">
  Trained PING replayed on MNIST digit 0 with the Poisson input rate swept 0–100 Hz in 5 Hz steps. Per-cell mean E (black) and I (red) firing rates over the 200 ms trial.
</Figure>

Three features. (1) **Recruitment threshold between 5 and 10 Hz input**: I jumps from 0 to 15 Hz across that interval, marking the cliff in classical f-I form. (2) **A narrow sub-recruitment regime** at 5 Hz input where E fires (1.65 Hz) but I does not — the "E spikes, I silent" state from the rate-floor argument made directly visible. (3) **Saturation**: above $\approx 30$ Hz input the I-loop bounds both populations — E plateaus around 8–11 Hz, I around 35–50 Hz, despite 3× more drive. This is gamma physics: cycle period set by $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA}$ limits spikes per cell per trial regardless of how hard the input pushes.

Figure 5 conflates two effects: the network's response to drive, and the readout's selectivity for digit-0-shaped input through trained $W_\text{in}$. To isolate the bare drive-response, repeat the sweep with a **spatially uniform input** — every input channel firing at the same Poisson rate, with no digit structure — and compare PING against COBA on the same axes.

<Figure id="Figure 7" title="Population f-I curves under uniform Poisson input" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/fi_curve_uniform.png" alt="Four-panel figure (2x2). Top row: full input range 0–100 Hz. Top-left (PING): E rate in black, I rate in red. PING engages at 2 Hz input — I jumps from 0 to 13 Hz, E settles near 3-5 Hz. At 100 Hz input E reaches ~27 Hz, I reaches ~125 Hz. Top-right (COBA): E rate in black, I rate flat at zero (no I-loop). E climbs steeply from 0 to ~300 Hz, saturating near the refractory ceiling. Bottom row: 0–10 Hz zoom at 0.1 Hz resolution. Bottom-left (PING): the recruitment cliff resolves into a sharp step at ~1.6–1.8 Hz where I lifts off zero. Bottom-right (COBA): E rises smoothly from 0 to ~130 Hz with no cliff feature.">
  Population f-I curves for trained PING (left) and trained COBA (right) under spatially uniform Poisson input — all 784 input channels firing at the same rate, no MNIST structure. Top row: full range 0–100 Hz. Bottom row: 0–10 Hz zoom at 0.1 Hz resolution, exposing the recruitment cliff. All points averaged over 32 trials. COBA's I curve sits at zero because *ei-strength = 0* disables the loop.
</Figure>

Two qualitative responses to the same drive. COBA (top-right) rises sharply across 1–10 Hz input then saturates toward its refractory ceiling ($\approx 300$ Hz at 100 Hz input — every E cell firing essentially as fast as it can); the I rate stays at zero because the loop is off. PING (top-left) shows the recruitment cliff at 1–2 Hz input (I jumps from 0 to 13 Hz) and then holds E roughly 10–14× lower than COBA across the entire post-engagement range: at 5 Hz input PING fires at 4.9 Hz vs COBA's 69 Hz; at 100 Hz input PING fires at 26.5 Hz vs COBA's 297 Hz. The I curve sitting above the E curve at every input rate is the visual signature of the loop — I has to fire harder than E to suppress it. The I-loop is doing dynamic-range compression — pulling drive that would saturate the E population into a bounded gamma cadence.

The bottom row zooms into 0–10 Hz at 0.1 Hz resolution. PING (bottom-left) resolves the recruitment cliff into a sharp step: the I rate is identically zero up to $\approx 1.6$ Hz input, then lifts off to $\approx 12$ Hz over a narrow $\approx 0.5$ Hz interval. This is the empirical signature of the saddle-node bifurcation in $f$-space, projected onto the input-rate axis. COBA (bottom-right) shows no cliff feature — its E curve is monotone-smooth across the same range. The recruitment threshold here ($\approx 1.6$ Hz input) is lower than in Figure 5 (5–10 Hz) because uniform input drives all channels simultaneously, while MNIST drives only digit-relevant pixels.
