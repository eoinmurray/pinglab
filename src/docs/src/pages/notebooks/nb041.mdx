## Introduction

[nb040](/notebooks/nb040/) trained a CUBA-PING network with a mem-mean readout and reached **84.3% test accuracy on 10 000 MNIST samples** with hidden firing rates of **E ≈ 0.02 Hz, I ≈ 0.25 Hz** — extraordinarily low, well below the COBA-PING baselines from [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb038](/notebooks/nb038/) which sit at 5–7 Hz.

The interesting question is *why*. Two candidate explanations:

1. **The I-loop clamps the rate.** The inhibitory population suppresses E firing whenever it gets recruited; under the training pressure of cross-entropy + mem-mean readout, the optimiser finds a solution where most of the work is done sub-threshold and the spikes are rare. The PING architecture is *necessary* for the sub-Hz rate.
2. **The mem-mean readout doesn't need spikes.** A continuous membrane readout can classify from $V^E$ patterns directly; the spiking machinery is incidental. The PING architecture is *not necessary* — any CUBA LIF stack with mem-mean would do the same thing.

This entry runs the control: a CUBA *no-PING* network (E-only, no $W^{EI}$, no $W^{IE}$, no I population) with the same recipe as nb040 (10 000 train, 30 epochs, mem-mean readout, TBPTT $K = 10$, batch 64, Adam lr $5 \times 10^{-4}$).

## Architecture

Identical to [nb040](/notebooks/nb040/) except:

- **No I population** — $W^{EI}$, $W^{IE}$ removed entirely.
- **No I-loop** in the V update — the E-cell current is just $I^E_t = x_t W_\text{in}$ with no inhibitory subtraction.

In one line:

$$
V^E_{t+1} \;=\; V^E_t \;+\; \tfrac{\Delta t}{\tau_m}\!\left(-(V^E_t - V_\text{rest}) \;+\; R_m\, x_t W_\text{in}\right), \quad s^E_t = \mathbb{1}\!\left[V^E_t \geq V_\text{th}\right]
$$

with the same output-LIF mem-mean readout as nb040 — an output LIF layer integrates hidden E spikes through $W_\text{out}$ ($\tau_\text{out} = 20$ ms) and the logits are its time-averaged membrane. Everything else — $\tau_m$, $V_\text{th}$, $\Delta t$, surrogate slope, $W_\text{in}$ init, TBPTT, gradient clipping, optimiser — matches nb040 byte-for-byte. The only difference is whether the I population is present.

## Interpretation

Headline numbers, both runs with the same output-LIF mem-mean readout and TBPTT $K=10$:

| | Test accuracy | E rate | I rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| CUBA-PING ([nb040](/notebooks/nb040/)) | 79.60% | 1.13 Hz | 28.0 Hz |
| CUBA-no-PING (this notebook) | **91.60%** | **41.83 Hz** | — |

The comparison is now clean. **Both effects of the I-loop show up**:

1. **The rate-clamp is real and strong.** Removing the I-loop lets the E rate climb to ≈42 Hz — biologically high but within the snnTorch / rate-coded SNN regime. With the I-loop in place, that 42 Hz drops to ≈1.1 Hz: a **38× sparsification** purely from the inhibitory architecture, no $L_\text{rate}$ regulariser needed.

2. **The sparsification has a real but bounded accuracy cost.** Accuracy drops from 91.6% to 79.6% — a 12 pp gap, much smaller than the catastrophic 49 pp gap an earlier version of nb040 saw with an unbounded $V$ floor and an over-strong $W^{IE}$. The output-LIF readout integrates spike events, so suppressing E spikes throttles the output membrane response; the network still has enough integrated signal to classify, just less than the dense-spike control.

So the I-loop is doing exactly the work the PING story claims for it, at a price that's now competitive with the COBA-PING baselines:

- **no-PING**: high-rate dense-spike regime, ≈42 Hz. The output LIF integrates many spike events into a clean signal. Accuracy is good (92%) but the rate is well above cortical pyramidal background firing.
- **PING**: low-rate sparse-spike regime, ≈1 Hz. The I-loop ensures sparse firing without any explicit regulariser, and the output LIF still gets enough integrated spike signal to push accuracy past 80%.

This is the *same* tradeoff the COBA-PING baselines in [nb035 Figure 5](/notebooks/nb035/) walk via the $\theta_u$ spike-budget term — except COBA-PING walks it by tightening an explicit penalty, while CUBA-PING walks it by the architectural inhibitory clamp. Both end up on similar frontiers; only the knob differs. And the closeness is meaningful: nb040 sits at 1.1 Hz / 79.6%, COBA-PING at $\theta_u = $ off sits at ≈7 Hz / 88%, COBA-PING at $\theta_u = 0.2$ sits at ≈3 Hz / 85%. CUBA-PING's architectural clamp is *tighter* than COBA-PING's tightest regulariser setting.

Practical implications:

- **CUBA-PING's accuracy is now competitive** — the 12 pp gap to the no-PING control should close further with more data (the training loss is still decreasing at epoch 30) or with a tuned $\tau_\text{out}$ that lets each rare spike contribute longer to the output integral.
- **CUBA-no-PING at 42 Hz isn't biologically realistic.** That's about the level of a fast-spiking interneuron in vitro, not a cortical pyramidal cell. So while it wins on raw accuracy, the "neuron-like" interpretation requires either a spike-budget term, a longer trial, or — most natural — the inhibitory loop that PING provides.
- **The remaining accuracy gap is the cost of being biological at this scale** (10 k samples, 30 epochs, MNIST). Whether that gap closes with full MNIST and a tuned recipe is the natural next experiment.
