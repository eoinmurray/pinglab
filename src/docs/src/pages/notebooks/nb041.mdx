## Abstract

**Training recipe (canonical / `medium` tier):**

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Integration timestep $\Delta t$ | 0.1 ms |
| Trial duration $T$ | 200 ms |
| MNIST samples (80/20 stratified split of 2000) | 1600 train / 400 test ($\approx$ 2.9% of the 70k-sample MNIST corpus) |
| Epochs | 100 |

[nb037](/notebooks/nb037/) swept $\tau_\text{GABA}$ at inference on a network trained at 9 ms; the rate followed the rhythm. This entry does the *retrained* version: one PING trained from scratch at each $\tau_\text{GABA}$, three seeds. The "cannot escape the cycle" claim only earns its verb if the optimiser had a free hand at each timescale.

## Methods

Six $\tau_\text{GABA} \in \{4.5, 6, 9, 12, 18, 27\}$ ms × three seeds = 18 cells. Same recipe as the [nb025](/notebooks/nb025/) PING baseline (100 epochs medium tier, batch 256, Adam $4 \times 10^{-4}$, mem-mean readout, $\theta_u$ off); only $\tau_\text{GABA}$ varies, exposed as `--tau-gaba`. For each trained cell, $f_\gamma$ is the parabolic-interpolated peak of the Welch PSD on the per-trial population E spike trace. Fit the affine relation $r_E = a + p \cdot f_\gamma$ across cells.

### Derivation of the form

The shape $r_E = a + p \cdot f_\gamma$ is predicted by cycle dynamics, not curve-fitted. Within one cycle of duration $1/f_\gamma$, a fraction $p$ of E cells emits exactly one spike (those nearest threshold when the inhibitory blanket drops); the rest are recovering. The cyclic contribution to per-cell rate is therefore $p \cdot f_\gamma$. At long $\tau_\text{GABA}$ the inhibitory conductance never fully decays between bursts and the cycle structure dissolves into a tonic bath; the surviving E firing is whatever $W_\text{in}$ forces through it — a baseline $a$ independent of $f_\gamma$. Total:

$$
r_E = \underbrace{a}_{\text{feedforward baseline}} + \underbrace{p \cdot f_\gamma}_{\text{cyclic contribution}}.
$$

### Fit and cross-checks

Affine fit: $a = 0.76$ Hz, $p = 0.216$ Hz/Hz, $R^2 = 0.997$ (refit on per-trial peak medians, see Figure 4). The earlier trial-mean PSD peak fit gave $a = 0.96$, $p = 0.201$, $R^2 = 0.996$ — the per-trial-median refit shifts $a$ down by ≈ 0.2 Hz and $p$ up by 7% because the trial-mean PSD peak is biased upward at high $f_\gamma$ by ≈ 3 Hz.

**$p$ is per-cycle participation.** At $\tau_\text{GABA} = 9$ ms the canonical cell has $f_\gamma \approx 35$ Hz and $r_E \approx 8.6$ Hz; $(r_E - a)/f_\gamma = 7.8/35 = 0.22$, matching the fitted slope. [nb024](/notebooks/nb024/)'s per-cell distribution at the same operating point gives the same fraction from a different direction: median 7.3 Hz, i.e. $\approx 0.21$ spikes per cell per cycle, with $\approx 9\%$ of cells silent.

**$a$ is a non-rhythmic baseline.** The fit extrapolates to $a \approx 0.8$ Hz at $f_\gamma \to 0$ — the no-rhythm regime that [nb042](/notebooks/nb042/)'s rate-matched Poisson condition probes directly (continuous I drive at matched mean drives E nearly silent). The intercept is a prediction about a no-rhythm limit, not a nuisance term.

The accuracy panel of Figure 1 stays at 80.5–90% across the whole $f_\gamma$ sweep — the rate change is not paid in classification.

The law is visible in single-trial rasters: the cycle stretches monotonically with $\tau_\text{GABA}$ and per-cell rates follow.

#### How the PSDs are computed

Each curve in Figure 3 is the *trial-mean* of single-trial Welch periodograms at one $(\tau_\text{GABA}, \text{seed})$ cell. Pipeline:

1. **Population trace per trial.** Run the trained network forward on the test set. For trial $n$, the population E trace is
   $$
   x_n(t) \;=\; \frac{1}{N_E}\sum_{j=1}^{N_E} s_{j,n}^E(t),
   $$
   the mean across $N_E = 1024$ hidden cells at every $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms timestep, giving $T = 2000$ samples at $f_s = 1000/\Delta t = 10$ kHz.

2. **Single-trial PSD.** Mean-subtract (`x_n − x̄_n`, no detrending) and pass to `scipy.signal.welch` with `nperseg = T` (one window per trial, no overlap, density scaling). One window means $\Delta f = f_s / T = 5$ Hz frequency resolution:
   $$
   P_n(f) \;=\; \mathrm{Welch}\!\big(x_n - \overline{x_n};\; n_\text{perseg} = T,\; f_s\big).
   $$

3. **Trial average.** With $N_c$ test trials in the cell, the displayed curve is
   $$
   \overline{P_c}(f) \;=\; \frac{1}{N_c} \sum_{n=1}^{N_c} P_n(f).
   $$
   Averaging *after* the periodogram (not on the time series) suppresses single-trial variance by $1/\sqrt{N_c}$ without smearing the peak. Trial-to-trial peak *location* is identical (same trained network, same gamma cycle), so trial-averaging sharpens it; only amplitude jitter gets averaged out.

4. **Peak extraction with parabolic interpolation.** Find the peak bin of $\overline{P_c}(f)$ inside the 5–150 Hz gamma band. With $(y_0, y_1, y_2)$ the PSD values at the peak bin and its two neighbours, the sub-bin offset is
   $$
   \delta \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\frac{y_0 - y_2}{y_0 - 2y_1 + y_2}, \qquad \delta \in [-\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}],
   $$
   clamped to $\pm\tfrac{1}{2}$. The reported frequency is $f_\gamma = \text{freq}[\text{peak}] + \delta \cdot \Delta f$. Parabolic interpolation is necessary because $\Delta f = 5$ Hz would otherwise quantise $f_\gamma$ coarsely across the six $\tau_\text{GABA}$ values; on a well-isolated peak its error is $O((\Delta f)^3)$.

The seed averaging happens *later* — when the six $(\tau, s)$ curves feed Figure 1's affine fit, the three seeds at each $\tau_\text{GABA}$ contribute three independent $f_\gamma$ estimates that become the horizontal error bars.

#### Sanity check: do all trials produce the same peak?

The trial-average above only sharpens the peak if every trial *has the same peak* — otherwise it produces a centroid-like quantity biased toward modal trials. Different MNIST digits drive different E subnetworks through $W_\text{in}$, so in principle $f_\gamma$ could be digit-dependent. Three reasons it probably isn't, plus one empirical check:

1. **MNIST digits drive similar mean rates.** Active-pixel counts are comparable across digits, so the effective $I_\text{ext}$ projected onto the hidden layer is roughly digit-independent.
2. **The cycle clock cares about the aggregate.** $f_\gamma$ is set by the I-loop responding to *population-mean* E activity (the Hopf in [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/)), so digit-dependent heterogeneity that preserves the mean is invisible to $f_\gamma$.
3. **The participation gate $p$ is architecturally clamped.** [nb046](/notebooks/nb046/) shows $p \approx 0.20$ across the full $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep; this buffers the per-cycle E output against drive fluctuations.

The empirical check: instead of finding the peak of the trial-mean PSD, find the peak of each *individual-trial* PSD and look at the distribution.

So the trial-mean PSD peak — and therefore Figure 1's $f_\gamma$ — overestimates the typical-trial cycle frequency by ≈ 3 Hz at small $\tau_\text{GABA}$ and ≈ 0 Hz at large $\tau_\text{GABA}$. That's small relative to the 22-Hz inter-$\tau$ spacing but real. The affine-law fit is robust because the bias is monotonic in $\tau_\text{GABA}$ and small compared to the dynamic range, but the more rigorous quantity would be the per-trial peak median. Refit on the per-trial medians is a candidate next-step.

The histograms are also clearly unimodal at every $\tau_\text{GABA}$, which rules out catastrophic digit-dependent bimodality — the spread comes from continuous trial-to-trial variation, not from digit-class-specific operating points.

### Convergence

Accuracy and rate converge on different timescales. Accuracy plateaus at 80.5–90% by epoch 15 across all $\tau_\text{GABA}$ values. Rate climbs steadily for the first 30 epochs, slows by epoch 50, and stabilises by epoch 70–100. The convergence audit in [nb024](/notebooks/nb024/) showed the same pattern at the $\theta_u$ = off baseline: PING's rate is a converged operating point at 100 epochs, not at 30.

The fit numbers shift accordingly with training duration:

| training | $a$ (Hz) | $p$ (Hz/Hz) | $R^2$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 30 epochs | 1.14 | 0.166 | 0.990 |
| 100 epochs | 0.76 | 0.216 | 0.997 |

(100-epoch row updated to the per-trial-median refit; trial-mean PSD peak gave 0.96 / 0.201 / 0.996.)

The *shape* of the law is robust — a clean affine relation in $f_\gamma$ at every training duration. The *slope* is duration-conditional and tightens toward its fixed point as training continues. Reporting the converged 100-epoch values is the right framing for any claim about per-cycle participation $p$; reporting them at 30 epochs is honest but understates the participation fraction.

## Discussion

The retrained complement to [nb037](/notebooks/nb037/) Figure 6. nb037 mutated $\tau_\text{GABA}$ at inference; nb041 retrains at each value and shows the optimiser cannot find a different operating point. Every retrained network sits on the same affine line, with both constants physically interpretable.

### Cortical prediction

The result is in silico — a conductance-based PING network trained on MNIST. But the derivation does not depend on the task or on the training substrate; it depends only on the cycle structure produced by the recurrent E↔I loop. To the extent that cortical pyramidal-interneuron circuits implement the same kind of self-clocking cycle, the same law should hold *in vivo*:

> **Pyramidal-cell mean firing rate should be linearly predictable from the local gamma peak frequency: $r_E \approx a + p \cdot f_\gamma$, with $p$ on the order of 0.1–0.2 (10–20% of pyramidal cells participating per cycle) and a small non-rhythmic baseline $a$.**

Testable in any awake-cortex recording where gamma is visible and pyramidal-cell rates are simultaneously measured. The prediction is falsifiable in three directions:

- **Slope.** If $p$ is consistently far outside 0.05–0.3 across cortical areas, the per-cycle-participation interpretation is wrong.
- **Linearity.** If pyramidal rates correlate with gamma power but not with gamma *frequency* in an affine way, the cycle-counting picture is the wrong model.
- **Direction.** Pharmacologically shortening $\tau_\text{GABA}$ (e.g. some benzodiazepine washouts or specific GABA-A subunit manipulations) should *raise* pyramidal rates, not lower them — counter-intuitive but a direct consequence of the law.

The architectural ingredient that makes the law hold here is a recurrent I-loop with fixed (not plastic) connectivity, on adult-cortex-like biophysical parameter ranges ($\tau_\text{AMPA} = 2$ ms, $\tau_\text{GABA} \in 5$–$30$ ms, E:I ratio 4:1). If cortical gamma is generated by a fundamentally different mechanism (e.g. interneuron-only ING, or pacemaker-driven), the law need not transfer; the prediction is therefore also a test of *whether the cortical gamma mechanism is PING-like*.
