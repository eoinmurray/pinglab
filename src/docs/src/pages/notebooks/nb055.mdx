## Abstract

Every entry drives the network with an input and skips the step in between: how presynaptic *spike trains* become the excitatory *conductance field* $g_e(i,t)$ the cells actually feel. That step is a single linear operation — each spike adds a fixed weight that then decays, so the field is the spike train convolved with an exponential kernel (decay-then-add). We generate independent Poisson spike trains, apply the filter, and look at the input and the resulting field side by side, checking the field's statistics against Campbell's theorem. They match exactly. The same algebra carries a warning for everything downstream: with a fixed synapse and Poisson input, three input properties — strength, reliability, and fluctuation timescale — collapse onto a single knob, the rate.

## The synaptic filter

Each E cell $i$ receives a private input spike train, a sum of impulses at the spike times $t_{i,k}$. Each spike injects a quantum $W$ into the cell's excitatory conductance, which then decays with $\tau = \tau_\text{AMPA}$. So the conductance is the spike train convolved with a causal exponential kernel:

$$
g_e(i,t) = W\sum_k \kappa(t - t_{i,k}), \qquad \kappa(s) = e^{-s/\tau}\,\Theta(s) ,
$$

where:

- $W$ — the per-synapse weight: peak conductance injected per spike (μS);
- $\tau$ — the AMPA decay, the kernel's time constant (≈ 2 ms);
- $\kappa(s)$ — the synaptic kernel; $\Theta(s)$ the step function making it causal (zero before the spike).

This is the diagonal $W_\text{in}$ pathway of [ar003](/articles/ar003/) (its eq. 7) in isolation; the discrete decay-then-add recurrence used below is the *exact* one-step integral of that delta-driven ODE, not an approximation.

The field is a *filtered Poisson process* (shot noise), so its low-order statistics follow from Campbell's theorem. For any process $g(t) = \sum_k h(t - t_k)$ whose events $t_k$ are Poisson at rate $\rho$ and whose single-event response is $h$,

$$
\langle g\rangle = \rho\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\! h(s)\,ds, \quad \mathrm{Var}(g) = \rho\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\! h(s)^2\,ds, \quad C(u) = \rho\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\! h(s)\,h(s+u)\,ds,
$$

where:

- $h(s) = W\kappa(s) = W\,e^{-s/\tau}\,\Theta(s)$ — one spike's contribution (weight × kernel);
- $\rho$ — the per-cell event rate;
- $C(u)$ — the autocovariance at lag $u$.

The kernel integrals are elementary:

$$
\int_0^\infty\! e^{-s/\tau}\,ds = \tau, \qquad \int_0^\infty\! e^{-2s/\tau}\,ds = \frac{\tau}{2}, \qquad \int_0^\infty\! e^{-s/\tau}e^{-(s+u)/\tau}\,ds = \frac{\tau}{2}\,e^{-u/\tau}\ \ (u \ge 0).
$$

Substituting $h = W\kappa$ gives the strength, the variance, and the autocovariance,

$$
\mu = W\rho\tau, \qquad \sigma^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}W^2\rho\tau, \qquad C(u) = \sigma^2\,e^{-|u|/\tau},
$$

and the reliability follows by dividing,

$$
\mathrm{SNR} = \frac{\mu}{\sigma} = \frac{W\rho\tau}{W\sqrt{\rho\tau/2}} = \sqrt{2\rho\tau},
$$

with $\mu$ the mean drive (strength), $\sigma = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}}$ the temporal standard deviation, and SNR the reliability. Three things to read off:

- **The weight cancels in SNR.** Reliability is set by rate and the kernel, never by $W$ — scaling the weight scales $\mu$ and $\sigma$ together.
- **SNR counts events per kernel window.** In general $\mathrm{SNR}^2 = \rho\,(\int h)^2/\!\int h^2$; for the exponential $(\int\kappa)^2/\!\int\kappa^2 = \tau^2/(\tau/2) = 2\tau$, so $\mathrm{SNR}^2 = 2\rho\tau$. The $\approx \rho\tau$ spikes overlapping within one kernel width average the relative fluctuation down as $1/\sqrt{\rho\tau}$; the factor 2 is the exponential's shape.
- **The correlation time is the kernel's.** $C(u)\propto e^{-|u|/\tau}$, so the drive decorrelates on $\tau$ regardless of rate.

## Methods

1. Generate $N$ independent per-cell Poisson spike trains at rate $\rho$ over a fixed window, on a grid of step $\Delta t$ (Bernoulli per step, $p = \rho\,\Delta t$).
2. Filter each train with the decay-then-add recurrence $g[t] = e^{-\Delta t/\tau}\,g[t-1] + W\,s[t]$ to build the field $g_e(i,t)$.
3. Plot the input raster, the field as a heatmap, and one cell's spikes with the conductance they build — together as one compound figure on a shared time axis.
4. Compare the field's empirical mean and per-cell SNR against the Campbell predictions $W\rho\tau$ and $\sqrt{2\rho\tau}$.
5. For Figure 2, swap the Poisson generator for a Gamma renewal process with shape $k$ and scale $1/(k\rho)$ — same mean rate, ISI CV $= 1/\sqrt{k}$, so $k = 1$ is Poisson and $k \to \infty$ is periodic. Sweep $k$ on a log-2 ladder, filter, and read $\mu$, $\sigma$, $\mathrm{SNR}$ off the field.

Hardcoded recipe: $N = 60$ cells, $\rho = 80$ Hz, $W = 0.05$ μS, $\tau = \tau_\text{AMPA} = 2$ ms, $\Delta t = 0.25$ ms, 300 ms window. No network, no recurrence — only the input pathway. The Figure 2 sweep uses the same $\rho$, $W$, $\Delta t$ on a longer 2.4 s window with a slower kernel $\tau = 50$ ms (NMDA-like) — the AMPA value sits at $\rho\tau \approx 0.16$, where the kernel sees at most one spike at a time and the dissociation below is invisible.

Switching the Poisson generator for a Gamma renewal process at the same rate $\rho$ is the cleanest way to break the rate-only collapse: ISI CV $= 1/\sqrt{k}$, so $k = 1$ is the Poisson floor and large $k$ tightens toward a periodic train. Across the sweep the field's mean is dead flat at $\mu = W\rho\tau = 0.20$ μS — strength only sees the rate, and the rate is matched by construction. The temporal SD falls and the SNR rises by the same factor; the dashed asymptotes $\sigma = W\sqrt{\rho\tau/(2k)}$ and $\mathrm{SNR} = \sqrt{2\rho\tau\,k}$ are the long-window Fano-factor predictions ($F = 1/k$) and track the data for moderate $k$. At large $k$ they overshoot because the train is no longer "renewal with reduced count variance" but essentially periodic at $1/\rho$ — the residual fluctuation is a deterministic sawtooth at the input frequency, not Fano-reduced shot noise, and the kernel can only smooth it so far. The left panels show that endgame directly: the $k = 64$ trace is a tight sawtooth riding on the same mean the Poisson trace careens around.

This is the first genuine dissociation — reliability moved while strength stayed put — but it required leaving the AMPA regime to see. At the article's own parameters ($\tau = 2$ ms, $\rho = 80$ Hz, so $\rho\tau \approx 0.16$), the kernel covers at most one spike at a time, so the input's ISI distribution leaves no fingerprint on $\sigma$ — regularising single per-cell trains at AMPA timescale is effectively invisible. Here we run the sweep at $\tau = 50$ ms (NMDA-like, or stand-in for a slower downstream integration window) so $\rho\tau = 4$ and the kernel averages several ISIs. The takeaway is regime-flavoured: input regularity moves reliability *only* when the synapse's effective window covers many input spikes — which biologically means slow kernels, or many converging fast ones.

## Discussion

The transform is linear and low-pass, and that fixes what the input can and cannot say. With a *fixed synapse and independent Poisson input*, three of the field's properties — strength $\mu = W\rho\tau$, reliability $\mathrm{SNR} = \sqrt{2\rho\tau}$, and the correlation time $\tau$ — are all functions of just the rate $\rho$ and the kernel. The synapse hands over the mean and a Poisson-floor reliability for free, both riding on rate; the timescale is simply the kernel's. So a single knob moves three "different" input statistics together — they are not independent until the input departs from independent Poisson (regularised, bursty, correlated across cells, or slowly modulated). The synapse gives strength and the Poisson floor; everything else must be engineered into the spike trains and then filtered.

Regularising the input at matched rate (Figure 2) is the first such departure, and it carries a sting: the dissociation only appears in the slow-kernel regime $\rho\tau \gtrsim 1$. In the AMPA regime that drives most of the rest of this site ($\rho\tau \approx 0.16$ for one input at 80 Hz), the synapse is too fast to feel the ISI distribution — so input regularity is a silent variable until it's amplified by either a slower kernel or convergence from many presynaptic cells. The filter's bandwidth, not just its time constant, is what decides which fluctuations make it through.
