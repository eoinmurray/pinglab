## The idea

**Hypothesis:** the tightness of a PING network's gamma bands encodes the *precision* (reliability, or inverse uncertainty) of its feedforward input — a candidate precision signal — not the input's strength (contrast). Tighter, more vertical gamma volleys would signal a more reliable stimulus; smeared bands a less reliable one.

**What "precision of the input" means.** The feedforward drive each E cell receives is a noisy signal, not a clean number. Independent excitatory events arrive and are low-pass filtered by the AMPA conductance into a drive $g_e(t)$ that fluctuates around a mean level. *Precision* is the reliability of that signal — how tightly $g_e(t)$ hugs its mean from moment to moment, i.e. inverse uncertainty about the instantaneous drive. Operationally it is the signal-to-noise ratio of the drive conductance,

$$
\mathrm{SNR} = \frac{\mu}{\sigma_{g_e}} ,
$$

where:

- $\mu = \langle g_e \rangle$ — the mean drive conductance (μS), what we call *contrast*;
- $\sigma_{g_e}$ — the standard deviation of $g_e(t)$ over time, the size of its fluctuation (μS).

A high-precision input is a dense stream of small events that average into a smooth conductance (small $\sigma_{g_e}/\mu$); a low-precision input is a sparse stream of large events felt as a few big kicks (large $\sigma_{g_e}/\mu$), even though, counter-intuitively, that jumpier signal looks calmer on average. This is precision in the Bayesian sense — inverse variance, the weight a signal earns by being reliable — which is why this entry sits in the Bayesian collection: the hypothesis is that the recurrent loop reads out that reliability and reports it as band tightness. For the shot-noise drive here the ratio has a closed form, $\sigma_{g_e}/\mu = 1/\sqrt{2\rho\tau}$, so $\mathrm{SNR} = \sqrt{2\rho\tau}$ (next section).

This entry is **theory first**. Before touching the simulator we ask whether the dependence can be *derived* from a mean-field argument, so the experiment has a concrete, falsifiable shape to test rather than an open-ended sweep. The plan below (then the code) exists to validate the prediction in §6.

Two knobs define the input, and they must be kept distinct:

- **Contrast** $\mu = W\rho\tau$ — the mean drive (μS): each E cell receives an independent Poisson stream of rate $\rho$, each spike injecting a weight $W$ (the per-synapse peak conductance, μS) into the AMPA conductance with decay $\tau = \tau_\text{AMPA}$.
- **Precision** $\mathrm{SNR} = \sqrt{2\rho\tau}$ — the inverse relative fluctuation of the drive ($\sigma/\mu = 1/\mathrm{SNR}$), set by the event rate $\rho$.

Because $\mu$ and SNR scale differently, raising $\rho$ while lowering $W$ sharpens precision at fixed contrast — the dissociation the experiment must exploit.

The band-tightness metric is the coefficient of variation of the E population rate $r(t)$ (total E spikes per 1 ms bin):

$$
\mathrm{CV}_r = \frac{\mathrm{std}(r)}{\mathrm{mean}(r)} ,
$$

where spiky, synchronous volleys give high $\mathrm{CV}_r$ and smeared bands give low $\mathrm{CV}_r$. (This is the CV of $r$ over *time*, distinct from the per-neuron ISI CV.)

## Mean-field analysis

The reduction that makes this tractable: band tightness is a statement about the *width of one gamma volley*, and a volley's width is a first-passage-time problem.

### 1. Tightness is inverse volley width

In the gamma regime $r(t)$ is a train of brief bursts, one per period $T_\gamma$. Model each burst as a Gaussian of temporal width $\sigma_t$ carrying $q$ spikes:

$$
r(t) = \frac{q}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_t}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{2\sigma_t^2}\right), \qquad \text{repeating every } T_\gamma .
$$

Averaging over one period, $\langle r\rangle = q/T_\gamma$ and $\langle r^2\rangle = q^2/(2\sqrt{\pi}\,\sigma_t\,T_\gamma)$, so

$$
\mathrm{CV}_r^2 = \frac{\langle r^2\rangle}{\langle r\rangle^2} - 1 = \frac{T_\gamma}{2\sqrt{\pi}\,\sigma_t} - 1 .
\tag{1}
$$

The spike count $q$ cancels — tightness depends on the *width* of the volley relative to the cycle, not on how many cells fire, so the sparse participation of E cells does not enter at leading order. For a tight volley ($\sigma_t \ll T_\gamma$), $\mathrm{CV}_r \approx \sqrt{T_\gamma/(2\sqrt{\pi}\,\sigma_t)} \propto \sigma_t^{-1/2}$: everything hinges on $\sigma_t(\mathrm{SNR})$.

### 2. Volley width is threshold-crossing jitter

A gamma cycle is a race. After the inhibitory pulse decays, every E cell's membrane potential $V$ climbs toward threshold $V_\text{th}$ on the feedforward drive and fires the moment it crosses; the volley width is the spread in crossing times. For a roughly linear ramp through threshold with slope $\dot V = dV/dt$ and voltage noise $\sigma_V$, the level-crossing result is

$$
\sigma_t = \frac{\sigma_V}{|\dot V|} .
\tag{2}
$$

The ramp slope $\dot V$ is set by the deterministic drive, so at a fixed operating point it is constant; the width therefore tracks the membrane voltage noise $\sigma_V$.

### 3. Voltage noise inherits the input's precision

In the high-conductance state of a volley the membrane is fast, so $V$ tracks its instantaneous steady state and fluctuations in the drive conductance $g_e$ pass through with gain $(E_e - V)/g_\text{tot}$. The drive's *relative* fluctuation is exactly the inverse SNR — for the shot-noise drive, $\sigma_{g_e}/\langle g_e\rangle = 1/\mathrm{SNR}$ — so

$$
\sigma_V \;\approx\; \frac{\langle g_e\rangle (E_e - V)\,\kappa}{g_\text{tot}}\,\frac{1}{\mathrm{SNR}} \;=\; \frac{b}{\mathrm{SNR}} ,
\tag{3}
$$

with $\kappa \le 1$ a membrane low-pass factor. The constant $b$ holds *only* if the mean drive $\langle g_e\rangle$, the operating point ($V$, $g_\text{tot}$), and the time constants are fixed — which is exactly what the experiment must control.

### 4. The heterogeneity floor

Even with a perfectly smooth input ($\mathrm{SNR}\to\infty$) the volley has nonzero width: cells differ in threshold, leak, and drive, so they cross at slightly different times regardless of noise. Call that quenched, SNR-independent jitter $\sigma_t^\text{het}$. The noise jitter ($\sigma_t^\text{noise} = a/\mathrm{SNR}$, from §2–3) and the heterogeneity jitter are independent and add in quadrature:

$$
\sigma_t(\mathrm{SNR}) = \sqrt{\left(\frac{a}{\mathrm{SNR}}\right)^2 + \big(\sigma_t^\text{het}\big)^2 } .
\tag{4}
$$

### 5. The prediction

Substituting (4) into (1) gives the full curve:

$$
\mathrm{CV}_r(\mathrm{SNR}) = \sqrt{\,\frac{T_\gamma}{2\sqrt{\pi}\,\sqrt{(a/\mathrm{SNR})^2 + (\sigma_t^\text{het})^2}} - 1\,} .
\tag{5}
$$

Three free constants — $T_\gamma$ (gamma period), $a$ (noise-jitter coefficient), $\sigma_t^\text{het}$ (heterogeneity floor) — set the curve; the *shape* is fixed by the argument and has two regimes:

- **Low SNR, noise-limited.** When $a/\mathrm{SNR} \gg \sigma_t^\text{het}$, $\sigma_t \approx a/\mathrm{SNR}$ and $\mathrm{CV}_r \approx \sqrt{T_\gamma\,\mathrm{SNR}/(2\sqrt\pi\,a)} \propto \sqrt{\mathrm{SNR}}$ — a decelerating rise.
- **High SNR, heterogeneity-limited.** When SNR is large, $\sigma_t \to \sigma_t^\text{het}$ and $\mathrm{CV}_r$ saturates at $\mathrm{CV}_r^{\text{max}} = \sqrt{T_\gamma/(2\sqrt{\pi}\,\sigma_t^\text{het}) - 1}$ — a **ceiling**.

The ceiling is the sharpest claim: tightness saturates not because the input stops improving but because **cell-to-cell heterogeneity, not input noise, becomes the limiting source of jitter**. The plateau is therefore a readout of $\sigma_t^\text{het}$, an intrinsic network property, not of the stimulus.

## 6. The prediction to test

The theory makes three claims the simulation must confront:

1. **Monotone rise with precision.** $\mathrm{CV}_r$ increases with SNR, as $\sqrt{\mathrm{SNR}}$ in the noise-limited regime.
2. **Saturation at a heterogeneity ceiling.** $\mathrm{CV}_r$ plateaus at high SNR; the plateau measures $\sigma_t^\text{het}$, independent of the stimulus.
3. **Contrast-invariance.** At fixed SNR, varying contrast $\mu$ leaves $\mathrm{CV}_r$ unchanged (eq. 3 depends on $\sigma_{g_e}/\langle g_e\rangle$, not on $\langle g_e\rangle$).

The form (5) is directly falsifiable: measure the volley width $\sigma_t$ at each SNR; (1) predicts $\mathrm{CV}_r^2 + 1$ is linear in $1/\sigma_t$, and (4) predicts $\sigma_t$ follows $\sqrt{(a/\mathrm{SNR})^2 + (\sigma_t^\text{het})^2}$. If both hold, the constants returned ($T_\gamma$ from the period, $\sigma_t^\text{het}$ from the ceiling) should match the network independently.

## Planned validation

*(No code yet — this is the experiment to build once the theory above is settled.)*

**Network.** $N_E = 1024$ E and $N_I = 256$ I cells in a recurrent PING loop (dense E↔I, no $W^{II}$), $\Delta t = 0.25$ ms, $T = 800$ ms, inference only. $\tau_\text{GABA} = 9$ ms fixes the ≈ 30 ms cycle. The only drive is the per-cell Poisson stream; the precision knob is the event rate $\rho$ traded against the per-spike weight $W$ so the mean drive $\mu = W\rho\tau$ stays fixed while SNR varies — contrast and precision moved independently.

**Input delivery — conductance injection is a diagonal $W_\text{in}$.** The drive is injected directly as a conductance, but this is *exactly* equivalent to a diagonal feedforward matrix $W_\text{in} = \mathrm{diag}(W_1, \dots, W_{N_E})$ — one private input line of weight $W$ per E cell (uniform $W\cdot I$ in the sweeps here; the diagonal entries vary when the input encodes an image) — because a kick of weight $W$ decaying at $\tau$ is precisely a single spike through a weight-$W$ AMPA synapse. Two rules keep the equivalence honest: $W_\text{in}$ stays diagonal so the drive is independent across cells (a dense $W_\text{in}$ shares spikes and manufactures synchrony — a confound), and the input synapse carries its own decay $\tau_\text{in}$ so precision can be varied at fixed rate (a single shared $\tau$ locks SNR to $\rho$). Beyond being more biological, the explicit diagonal-$W_\text{in}$ form is the bridge to image input: pixels → per-cell input rates → $W_\text{in}$ → cells.

The figure set is built mechanism-first, following the *sprinters and a starting gun* picture: each E cell is a sprinter, one gamma cycle is a race from reset to threshold, and the input is the gun. Precision is how crisp the gun is; tightness is how tightly the pack crosses the line.

**Figure 1 — The gun (what precision is).** Per-cell drive conductance $g_e(t)$ for two inputs at the *same mean* $\mu$: reliable (dense small events → smooth trace hugging the mean, small $\sigma_{g_e}/\mu$) vs unreliable (sparse big kicks → jumpy trace, large $\sigma_{g_e}/\mu$). The raw event trains sit above each trace. This is the crisp bang vs the staticky one.

**Figure 2 — The race (the mechanism).** Membrane voltage $V(t)$ of ≈ 25 E cells across one gamma cycle, climbing from reset toward the threshold line, reliable (left) vs unreliable (right). A reliable gun gives a tight bundle crossing threshold in a narrow window; an unreliable one gives frayed traces crossing over a wide window. A histogram of crossing times sits underneath — that histogram is the gamma volley, and its width is $\sigma_t$. This visualises $\sigma_t = \sigma_V/|\dot V|$ (eq. 2) directly: the figure that makes the mechanism legible.

**Figure 3 — The pack (the consequence).** E spike raster (tight vs loose vertical stripes) with the population rate $r(t)$ stacked below it, at the same mean drive and near-equal firing rate $r_E$ (reported on each panel), $\mathrm{CV}_r$ annotated. Shows the visible bands and defines the metric in one frame: $\mathrm{CV}_r$ is just how spiky $r(t)$ is.

**Figure 4 — The headline (clarity, not volume).** $\mathrm{CV}_r$ vs input SNR, one line per contrast $\mu$, expected to lie on top of each other — tightness set by the gun's clarity (SNR), not its volume ($\mu$). The whole claim in one plot, and the test of claims 1 (rise) and 3 (contrast-invariance).

**Figure 5 — Theory meets data (the payoff of going theory-first).** Measured $\mathrm{CV}_r$ over a wide SNR range with the mean-field prediction (eq. 5) overlaid — the $\sqrt{\mathrm{SNR}}$ rise and the heterogeneity ceiling, with $r_E$ tracked to mark where the controlled regime holds. Twin panel: the directly-measured volley width $\sigma_t$ against the quadrature form (eq. 4). The twin is the strong-form test — does the *width* obey the theory, not just the summary $\mathrm{CV}_r$ — and it reads off $\sigma_t^\text{het}$ from the ceiling (claim 2).

**Controls (rigor, not narrative).**

- *No-loop control.* Repeat with the recurrent E↔I coupling zeroed. The input is independent across cells, so any tightness surviving without the loop is inherited from the input, not generated by it; the expectation is a flat line at the asynchronous floor.
- *Precision-vs-rate control.* Reach each SNR from several event rates $\rho$ by smoothing the drive with its own $\tau_\text{drive}$ (decoupled from $\tau_\text{AMPA}$ so the loop and $r_E$ are unperturbed). If $\mathrm{CV}_r$ collapses onto SNR across an order-of-magnitude range of $\rho$, tightness tracks precision, not the bare event rate.

## Success criteria

- $\mathrm{CV}_r$ rises monotonically with SNR and is flat across contrast $\mu$ (claims 1, 3).
- A ceiling appears at high SNR (claim 2), and the directly-measured $\sigma_t(\mathrm{SNR})$ obeys the quadrature form (4) with a heterogeneity floor matching the ceiling.
- The no-loop control sits at the asynchronous floor, confirming the loop generates the tightness.
- $\mathrm{CV}_r$ collapses onto SNR across an order-of-magnitude range of $\rho$ at fixed $r_E$.
