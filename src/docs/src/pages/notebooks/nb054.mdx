## Abstract

A single calibrated scalar for how rhythmic a network is — so "rhythmic / not rhythmic" can sit on one axis instead of being read three different ways (PSD peak in [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/), spikes-per-cycle in [nb046](/notebooks/nb046/), visible banding in [nb023](/notebooks/nb023/)). The periodogram locates the gamma frequency but is not, on its own, a usable scalar; the metric here reads rhythmicity off the **spike-time autocorrelation**, quantifying the central-lobe-versus-trough "Mexican-hat" shape that marks a rhythm, with the inter-event-interval histogram used to locate the lag at which to read it.

Calibrated on synthetic non-homogeneous Poisson references, the **lobe-to-trough ratio** flags both a stationary and a frequency-drifting rhythm near-equally ({fmt(ref.stationary?.lobe_to_trough)} and {fmt(ref.drifting?.lobe_to_trough)}) while sitting at the baseline value {fmt(ref.flat?.lobe_to_trough)} for asynchronous firing — where the periodogram peak collapses from {fmt0(ref.stationary?.psd_snr)} to {fmt0(ref.drifting?.psd_snr)} between the two rhythms it cannot tell apart from a weak one.

## Background: periodogram vs autocorrelation

A periodogram peak gives the frequency $f_\gamma$ but not a bounded measure of how *concentrated* the rhythm is — to become a scalar it needs a cutoff, or a quantification of the mass in the central lobe. The autocorrelation is more direct. Two reference cases (non-homogeneous Poisson processes, the diagnostic suggested by T. O'Leary) make the target shape concrete:

- **Stationary sinusoidal rate (a "pure" rhythm).** The inter-event-interval (IEI) histogram has a tall short-interval peak plus a clear *second mode* at the rhythm period; the autocorrelation is a clean periodic comb of lobes under a triangular envelope. Even here the central lobe has real width and the surrounding trough is not empty.
- **Non-stationary sinusoidal (drifting frequency).** The IEI second mode broadens and the periodic comb washes out, leaving a sharp central lobe ringed by a trough — the **Mexican hat** — on a broadband floor.

The robust, shared signature across both is that Mexican-hat profile: a central lobe surrounded by a trough. The metric should quantify *that*, not the height of a spectral peak (which collapses on the drifting case it cannot distinguish from a weak rhythm).

## Proposed metric

From the population E spike train, build two objects:

- the **IEI histogram** — relative timing of consecutive spikes; its peak and second mode set characteristic lags; and
- the **empirical autocorrelation** $A(\ell)$ — formed by binning the timing of every spike relative to every other spike, normalised so rate-matched independent firing sits at $A = 1$ (central lobe near $\ell = 0$, surrounded by a trough).

Three candidate scalars, compared on the references below:

1. **IEI-anchored lag.** Take the peak of the IEI histogram as a characteristic lag $\ell^\star$, and report $A(\ell^\star)$.
2. **Lobe-to-trough ratio.** The height of the central lobe (*excluding* the zero-lag value) relative to the first trough — located directly as the autocorrelation's first local minimum (the IEI second mode is an unreliable bracket for dense populations, where near-simultaneous spikes swamp it). A ratio, floored against an empty trough — high for a clean rhythm, near 1 for none.
3. **Biophysical lag.** Use a known network timescale (the total E→I→E transmission delay) to set the critical lag, and report $A$ at that lag.

## Desiderata

- Bounded and calibrated — near a baseline value for asynchronous (Poisson) firing, high for clean volleys.
- Robust to the central-lobe spread that even a pure rhythm shows.
- Discriminates rhythmic from non-rhythmic *regardless of stationarity*, where a periodogram peak conflates a frequency-drifting rhythm with a weak one.
- Rate-comparable, so sparse PING (≈5 Hz) and dense COBA can sit on the same axis.

## Calibration on synthetic references

The metric primitives live in *metrics.py* (*iei_histogram*, *spike_autocorrelogram*, *rhythmicity_metrics*); the runner at *src/notebooks/nb054.py* calibrates them across a continuous spectrum rather than a few samples. Each reference is a population of independent inhomogeneous-Poisson cells sharing a common rate $\lambda(t) = \lambda_0\,(1 + m\sin\phi(t))$, swept over the modulation depth $m$ from 0 (flat, asynchronous) to 1 (a deep rhythm), in two flavours: **stationary** (fixed frequency $f$) and **drifting** ($f$ swept as a chirp). $m = 0$ is the shared flat baseline; the two $m = 1$ endpoints are the stationary and drifting cases. Synthetic and measurement-only — no training, local CPU, seconds to run.

**Result.** Across the sweep the lobe-to-trough ratio climbs from {fmt(ref.flat?.lobe_to_trough)} at $m = 0$ (flat) to {fmt(ref.stationary?.lobe_to_trough)} (stationary) and {fmt(ref.drifting?.lobe_to_trough)} (drifting) at $m = 1$, the two curves tracking closely — rhythmicity read off independent of stationarity, exactly the wanted behaviour. The periodogram peak SNR (endpoints {fmt0(ref.flat?.psd_snr)} / {fmt0(ref.stationary?.psd_snr)} / {fmt0(ref.drifting?.psd_snr)}) ranks the drifting rhythm with the flat baseline across the whole sweep, confirming the failure mode. The IEI-anchored scalar ({fmt(ref.flat?.iei_anchored)} / {fmt(ref.stationary?.iei_anchored)} / {fmt(ref.drifting?.iei_anchored)} at the endpoints) separates rhythm from baseline too, but with less dynamic range.

**Chosen scalar: lobe-to-trough.** Bounded, calibrated at 1 for Poisson, stationarity-robust, and the widest separation of the three. Candidate 3 (biophysical lag) is candidate 1 with the lag fixed from circuit delay instead of the IEI; it is only testable where that delay is known, so it is deferred to the trained-network runner.
