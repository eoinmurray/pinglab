## Introduction

[025 — Slow synapse on trained coba and ping](/notebooks/nb025/) wired the NMDA-like slow synaptic channel ($\tau_{NMDA} = 100$ ms) into COBANet and showed it's a usable substrate. The structural follow-up question: with that channel in place, can a network produce *non-seizure sustained activity post-stimulus* — i.e. a bump that survives the input cutting out without either dying or running away to saturation?

This entry concretely answers **no, not in coba** (*ei_strength* = 0). The slow channel + recurrent E→E that, with inhibition, gives ping a stable attractor admits only **two** outcomes in coba: the network either decays to silence (no recurrence, or recurrence too weak), or it locks at refractory saturation. There is no intermediate $W_{ee}$ that produces a stable attractor at a moderate firing rate.

## Method

**Setup.** Untrained COBANet at $\text{ei\_strength} = 0$ — only excitatory cells effectively drive each other; no I→E feedback loop. $N_E = ${cfg.n_e}, $N_{IN} = ${cfg.n_in}, $dt = ${cfg.dt} ms. The slow channel is on at gain {cfg.slow_syn_gain ?? "?"} ($\tau_{NMDA} = 100$ ms). $W_{in}$ is set to coba's lighter init ($0.3 \pm 0.09$, 95% sparse) so the network isn't NMDA-charged into saturation by input alone — we want the dynamics to be determined by $W_{ee}$, not $W_{in}$.

**Protocol.** Each trial is {cfg.t_total_ms} ms:

1. **Stim** $[0, ${cfg.t_stim_ms}]$ ms — Poisson input spikes at rate $R$ on all input channels.
2. **Silence** $[${cfg.t_stim_ms}, ${cfg.t_total_ms}]$ ms — input is identically zero.

**2D sweep.** Grid over $W_{ee}$ mean $\in \{0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0\}$ μS (pre-fan-in) and stim input rate $R \in \{5, 25, 50, 100, 150\}$ Hz.

**Outcome classification.** Per trial we compute two post-stim rate windows:

- $r_1$ — mean E rate over $[t_{stim}, t_{stim} + 100]$ ms (early post-stim).
- $r_2$ — mean E rate over $[t_{total} - 100, t_{total}]$ ms (late post-stim).

Then assign:

- **seizure** — $r_2 \geq 120$ Hz (refractory-saturation territory; $1/\tau_{\text{ref},E} = 333$ Hz is the ceiling, anything past ~120 Hz is locked).
- **decay** — $r_2 < 1$ Hz, OR $r_2 / r_1 < 0.5$ (still actively dying — a passive NMDA decay tail is not a stable attractor).
- **sustained** — anywhere else: $r_2$ stable relative to $r_1$, sitting in $[1, 120]$ Hz. *This is the class we want to find.*

## Discussion

The grid is bistable: the only two outcomes are "decays to silence" and "locks at refractory saturation." Both stable states are degenerate from a working-memory perspective — one stores nothing, the other stores nothing distinguishable. The cells in the bottom row ($W_{ee} = 0$) that have non-zero late-window rate (9–116 Hz) aren't sustained either; they're caught mid-decay through the NMDA tail. Slope-aware classification correctly puts them in the *decay* class — $r_2 / r_1 < 0.5$ for all of them, meaning activity is still cutting in half over the post-stim window.

**Why this is structural, not a tuning failure.** Imagine the mean-field fixed-point equation for a one-population network with positive recurrence:

$$
r^{\star} = f\!\left(W_{ee}\,r^{\star} + I\right)
$$

where $f$ is the I-V (or rather f-I) curve — monotonic and saturating at $1/\tau_{\text{ref}}$. With the slow channel on, $I$ has a long memory of past spikes that effectively raises the steady-state input. For any non-zero $W_{ee}$, this equation has at most two stable solutions: $r^{\star} = 0$ (sub-threshold), and $r^{\star} \approx 1/\tau_{\text{ref}}$ (saturation). Intermediate fixed points exist but are *unstable* — small perturbations push you to one of the two stable points. Adding an NMDA channel doesn't change the topology of this equation; it just changes the rate at which the unstable fixed point is approached.

A stable intermediate attractor requires a second equation — an inhibitory population whose firing rate also depends on $r^{\star}$ and feeds back negatively. That's what *ei_strength > 0* gives ping, and it's what nb030 strips away on purpose.

## Implication

If you want sustained post-stimulus activity in a network with the pinglab COBANet stack, you need *either* inhibition (ping config, *ei_strength > 0*) *or* an alternative slow negative-feedback primitive (e.g. spike-rate adaptation via ALIF). Coba + NMDA alone is structurally incapable, and the result is robust to choice of $W_{ee}$ across two orders of magnitude. This rules out a whole family of "just add NMDA to coba" approaches for working memory work on this codebase.

## Next steps

- **Add ALIF as the brake.** ALIF (per-neuron threshold adaptation, $\tau = 700$ ms) provides slow self-quenching that could substitute for I-cell inhibition. The interesting question: does the bistability dissolve when ALIF is added on top, or is the structural problem deeper?
- **Repeat with ping** (*ei_strength = 1*) for visual comparison — the same $(W_{ee}, R)$ phase map at ping should have a green band that's *empty* in coba, making the I-cell role concrete.
- **slow_syn_gain sweep.** Does any gain produce sustained cells in coba? Likely no — the structural argument doesn't care about gain — but worth confirming.
