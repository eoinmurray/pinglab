## Introduction

How quickly does a trained spiking network commit to a classification? cuba, coba, and ping all reach $\sim$80% on a single 200 ms MNIST trial, but they get there along very different timelines. This entry asks two related questions of the same trained weights:

1. **Single-window latency** — when one digit fills the full 200 ms, how does the running argmax accuracy rise with elapsed time, and with cumulative input or hidden spikes?
2. **How many ping cycles?** — ping's E→I→E loop produces gamma-band activity. Is the natural unit of accumulation the millisecond, or the *ping cycle*? We look directly at single-trial rates and the trial-averaged autocorrelation rather than relying on a stimulus-locked PSTH.

The companion entry [nb016](/notebooks/nb016/) reuses the same weights to ask the dual question — classification accuracy when *many* digits share the trial — for the multi-window tracking story.

## Method

cuba, coba, ping trained on standard single-MNIST per [nb010](/notebooks/nb010/), [nb011](/notebooks/nb011/), [nb012](/notebooks/nb012/), then frozen. The runner that produces every artifact in this entry is [src/pinglab/notebooks/nb016.py](/docs/the-notebooks/) — it trains once and emits artifacts for both nb015 (here) and nb016 (sequential tracking). Two probes against the frozen weights:

1. **Single-window latency.** One digit fills the 200 ms window; the running cumulative-mem-mean argmax is scored at sixteen fractions of the window. 800 trials per fraction. Mirrors [nb013's latency protocol](/notebooks/nb013/#latency-to-correct-answer-protocol).
2. **Per-trial dynamics.** Same trial layout but we record the smoothed hidden firing rate per trial (50 trials, $\sigma$ = 1 ms Gaussian smoothing) and compute the trial-averaged autocorrelation of the post-onset rate (drop the first 20 ms onset transient, z-score per trial, FFT-based AC).

### How many ping cycles?

ping's E→I→E loop produces gamma activity, so the natural unit of accumulation might be the *ping cycle* rather than the millisecond. A trial-averaged PSTH only sees stimulus-locked rhythm; phase-varying rhythm is invisible. We look directly: per-trial rate heatmap (top) and trial-averaged autocorrelation post-onset (bottom).

<Figure id="Figure 4" title="ping single-trial rates and trial-averaged autocorrelation" src="/figures/notebooks/nb015/per_trial_dynamics.png" alt="Top: heatmap of single-trial hidden firing rate for ping, 50 trials × 200 ms. Bottom: trial-averaged autocorrelation of the post-onset hidden rate for cuba, coba, and ping over lags 0–60 ms.">
  Top: ping's per-trial smoothed hidden rate (first 50 trials). Bursts are visible but land at different times across trials. Bottom: trial-averaged autocorrelation after dropping the 20 ms onset transient. cuba and coba decay smoothly; ping shows one small peak at lag $\sim$29 ms (height $\sim$0.06 vs lag-0 = 1.0).
</Figure>

- **One real cycle.** The onset burst at $\sim$9 ms is stimulus-locked and lifts running accuracy to {latencyAt("ping", 10)?.accuracy_pct?.toFixed(1) ?? "?"}% by 10 ms.
- **Sustained rhythm, but weak and phase-incoherent.** The $\sim$29 ms ($\sim$35 Hz) autocorrelation peak is barely above noise.
- **Past cycle 1 it's integration, not counting.** The mem-mean readout averages out the dephased oscillation. Plateau is one locked cycle plus $\sim$60 ms of drift integration.

## Discussion

The ladder splits two ways. *Per-spike* (Figure 3, x-axis = cumulative hidden spikes), ping is the clear winner — the I-loop's dynamic sparsity means each E spike carries more readout-relevant information. *Per-millisecond* (Figure 1), cuba wins below $\sim$15 ms because its dense intrinsic activity keeps the running average above chance even with very few input spikes. The crossover happens around 15–30 ms, where ping briefly leads before all three plateau.

The "ping cycles" question resolves negatively: there's exactly one stimulus-locked cycle (the onset burst), and the sustained gamma rhythm is real but phase-incoherent across trials, so it averages out under the mem-mean readout. The accuracy plateau past $\sim$10 ms is integration, not counting cycles. If we want the readout to *count* cycles, we'd need either a phase-locked drive or a phase-sensitive decoder.

## Success criteria

- ✅ All three reach $\geq$80% within 80 ms of a single 200 ms digit (cuba {latencyAt("cuba", 80)?.accuracy_pct?.toFixed(1) ?? "?"}%, coba {latencyAt("coba", 80)?.accuracy_pct?.toFixed(1) ?? "?"}%, ping {latencyAt("ping", 80)?.accuracy_pct?.toFixed(1) ?? "?"}%); the rest just sharpens the answer.
- ⚠ ping has one stimulus-locked cycle (the $\sim$9 ms onset burst → {latencyAt("ping", 10)?.accuracy_pct?.toFixed(1) ?? "?"}% by 10 ms); the sustained $\sim$29 ms rhythm is phase-incoherent. cuba and coba have no rhythm.
- ✅ Per cumulative hidden spike, ping is $\sim$8× more efficient than cuba at 200 ms ({Math.round(latencyAt("cuba", 200)?.mean_hidden_spikes ?? 0)} vs {Math.round(latencyAt("ping", 200)?.mean_hidden_spikes ?? 0)} hidden spikes for the same accuracy plateau).

## Next steps

- Phase-lock the input to ping's gamma so the sustained rhythm becomes coherent across trials and the autocorrelation peak rises above noise — does the readout then benefit from a cycle-counting decoder?
- Try a readout that takes ping's instantaneous rate envelope (not just the mem-mean) — see whether accuracy plateaus earlier when the decoder reads bursts rather than running averages.
- Push to dt = 1 ms and ask whether the latency curve shifts in proportion (would imply the decoder is doing time-step counting) or stays anchored in milliseconds (would imply biophysical-time integration).
