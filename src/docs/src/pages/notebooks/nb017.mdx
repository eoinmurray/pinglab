## Introduction

Forked from [nb016](/notebooks/nb016/) to swap the raster-based Bayes factor for a **rate-based** one. The raster BF saturated bimodally in the PING regime (log-BF on real data hovered near zero, the sigmoid amplified small fluctuations into 0/1 jumps), and the diagnosis was that real PING data carries population-coordinated information in the rate amplitude, not in individual spike times. Working on the smoothed E rate directly should give substantially more evidence per bin and clean post-threshold saturation. Same ei sweep, same population-rate / autocorrelation views as nb016 — only the BF likelihood changes.

### Population rate

Replay the same Poisson input through a fresh ping network at each frame's *ei* value, record the E spike train $s_{i,t}$, and bin at $\Delta_\text{bin} = 2$ ms. We use the **participation fraction** — the same dimensionless normalisation the oscilloscope video panel shows — i.e. total spikes per bin divided by $N_E$:

$$
r_k \;=\; \frac{1}{N_E} \sum_{i=1}^{N_E} \sum_{t \in \mathrm{bin}_k} s_{i, t}.
$$

Each $r_k \in [0, 1]$ when no neuron fires more than once per bin (the regime we operate in for *ei* $\gtrsim 0.2$). This equals the rate in Hz divided by the maximum possible rate $1000/\Delta_\text{bin} = 500$ Hz.

### Generative models

Two recipes, one per regime, picked by an *ei* threshold (currently 0.2). The participation trace $r_t$ is at 2 ms bins on the post-onset window ($N \approx 490$ samples).

Both models share a count-based noise structure: at each bin, the participation $r_t = k_t / N_E$ where $k_t \sim \mathrm{Bin}(N_E, p_t)$. This puts the variance in the peaks (where it's invisible against the signal) and zero variance in the trough — so the cadence reads cleanly instead of being washed out by additive Gaussian noise that doesn't know the trace is bounded below by zero.

**Async** — constant Bernoulli rate per bin:

$$
k_t \sim \mathrm{Bin}(N_E,\, \mu_a),\qquad r_t = k_t / N_E.
$$

$\hat\mu_a$ is the sample mean of the real trace, clipped to $[0, 1]$. No frequency, no phase.

**Ping** — Gaussian-pulse-train modulating the rate:

$$
p_t = \mathrm{clip}\!\left[\mu_0 + A \sum_{n} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(t - t_0 - n/f)^2}{2\sigma_b^2}\right),\; 0,\; 1\right],\qquad k_t \sim \mathrm{Bin}(N_E,\, p_t),\quad A \ge 0.
$$

Five mean-curve parameters: baseline $\mu_0$, pulse amplitude $A$, fundamental frequency $f$, phase offset $t_0$, and burst width $\sigma_b$. Fitting is an outer grid search over $(f, t_0, \sigma_b)$ — gamma-band $f$ at 0.5 Hz spacing, eight phase positions per period, $\sigma_b \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ ms — and at each grid point $(\mu_0, A)$ are closed-form least-squares against the real rate trace. We pick the triple that minimises the LS residual.

Pulse-train rather than Fourier harmonics because PING is *physically* a periodic train of population bursts: $\sigma_b$ directly parameterises burst width (instead of being smeared across multiple harmonic coefficients), and the troughs go genuinely flat between pulses. Tight Gaussian over Lorentzian — Gaussians are local, so the trough quiets between pulses.

The async model is the $A = 0$ limit of ping, so the split is conceptual: we use it to keep parsimony in the low-*ei* regime where fitting a pulse train to a flat trace would just chase noise.

Working on the rate rather than the raster trades per-spike resolution for population-synchronous amplitude — at burst peaks $\sim$10–25% of E neurons fire together within a single 2 ms bin, and that swing is what we want the model to reproduce.

## Next steps

The current model matches the *ei* = 0.8 burst train credibly but falls short at *ei* = 0 (real trace has regular tonic ripple that the constant-mean limit treats as noise). Open work:

1. **Capture the tonic ripple**: at *ei* $\lesssim 0.1$ the real participation trace has a fast oscillation outside the 25–75 Hz gamma grid. Either widen the $f$ grid to include it, or add a non-rhythmic component to the model (e.g. an AR(1) noise structure on $\varepsilon_t$).
2. **Quantify fit quality**: report per-frame $\hat\sigma_p / \hat\mu_0$ (residual / signal) or $|R(\hat f)|^2 / \hat\sigma_a^2$ (variance-explained fraction) along the sweep so improvements to the model show up as a measurable goodness curve, not just visual judgement.
