## Summary

Same MNIST accuracy (82-86%), ~11× fewer spikes. Coba (no I-loop) settles at ~52 Hz hidden firing; ping (E↔I loop wired up, frozen) settles at ~5 Hz with comparable accuracy. A spike-budget regulariser drags coba toward ping-like rates at an accuracy cost; ping's frontier is flat — it's already at its floor. **The floor is dynamical, not informational**: hidden-spike drop shows the readout works at ~1 Hz, but the gamma loop's preferred cycle period (τ_AMPA + τ_GABA ≈ 11 ms) keeps the network from going there spontaneously.

## Method

Two COBANet recipes, identical except for *ei_strength*:

- **coba** — *--ei-strength 0*, *--w-in 0.3*, *--readout-w-out-scale 100*.
- **ping** — *--ei-strength 1*, *--w-in 1.2*, *--readout-w-out-scale 500*.

E↔I matrices in ping are wired at init and held *requires_grad=False*. Same surrogate, lr, dt, seed everywhere. Spike-budget regulariser θ_u sweeps over six values (off, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2 spikes/trial) — 12 cells total.

**Hidden-spike perturbation.** A per-step callback rewrites the emitted E and I spike vectors *inside* the forward loop, so the I-feedback and readout both react within the same trial. Two modes:

- *drop* — each emitted spike kept w.p. $1 - p_\text{drop}$.
- *add* — each silent slot turns to a spike w.p. $r_\text{add} \cdot \Delta t / 1000$.

Both populations get the same statistics. Full MNIST test set per (model, mode, level).

## Discussion

The drop/add asymmetry separates two candidate explanations of ping's rate floor:

1. **Information-bound** — the readout needs ~3–5 Hz of spikes to classify. Drop refutes this: ping still classifies at ~73% with 90% of its emitted spikes thrown away.
2. **Dynamics-bound** — the gamma loop has a preferred cycle period of $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA} \approx 11$ ms; the readout is phase-locked to it. Add confirms this: extra spikes at the wrong phase corrupt the readout's signal, even though they *raise* the E rate. Coba has no phase to be off of, so add degrades smoothly.

**Figure 5 closes the loop on the dynamics-bound argument.** A clean inference-only sweep of $\tau_\text{GABA}$ across [4.5, 27] ms moves the rate floor monotonically and proportionally: 7 Hz at 4.5 ms → 5 Hz at the trained 9 ms → 2 Hz at 27 ms. Accuracy holds 80–86% throughout, so the readout adapts (or was already invariant) to changes in cycle period. This is the experiment the original notebook only predicted; it now confirms that the floor is *literally* the cycle period, not something coincidentally near it.

**Figure 6 separates rate from structure.** Raising the firing threshold uniformly across E and I is a structure-preserving rate manipulation — it suppresses spikes without injecting noise at the wrong phase. Accuracy holds 80–86%, in sharp contrast to Figure 4's add curve which dropped ping to chance at 10 Hz of added Poisson. The interesting wrinkle is that E *rate* doesn't fall monotonically: I cells fire higher than E at baseline (23 vs 5 Hz), so a uniform threshold offset suppresses I first, releasing the inhibitory grip on E and *raising* the E rate up to ~7 Hz before everything finally collapses around +15 mV.

Together: ping's rate floor is the loop's natural cadence; the readout is robust to anything that preserves the gamma structure and brittle only to corruptions of it. "Inhibition does sparsity" here is a physical consequence of the cycle period, not an optimisation preference.

## Limitations

Medium tier (max_samples = 2000, epochs = 10); absolute accuracies (82-86%) are below state-of-the-art. MNIST only. Sweep cells single-seed; baselines 3-seed. Drop/add applies the same statistic to E and I — a population-resolved version would isolate which population's timing the readout uses.
