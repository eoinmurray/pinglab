## Method

Two configurations of the same COBANet architecture, differing only in whether the E→I→E inhibitory loop is active. **COBA** (*--ei-strength 0*) disables the loop; excitatory cells drive each other but receive no structured inhibition. **PING** (*--ei-strength 1*) enables the loop, producing pyramidal-interneuron gamma (PING) oscillations at a cadence set by $\tau_\text{AMPA}$ and $\tau_\text{GABA}$.

**Architecture.** $N_E = 1024$ excitatory cells, $N_I = 256$ inhibitory cells, single hidden layer. Input: 784 channels (MNIST pixels), Poisson-encoded at 25 Hz peak rate. Readout: mem-mean (time-averaged E spike vector projected through a trained linear layer $W_\text{out}$; see [art006](/articles/art006/) for the full readout specification). Dale's law enforced.

**What is trainable.** Only the input weights $W_\text{in}$ ($784 \times 1024$, 95% sparse) and the readout $W_\text{out}$ ($1024 \times 10$). The recurrent weights $W_{ee}$ (fixed at zero — Börgers-style PING needs no E→E coupling), $W_{ei}$, and $W_{ie}$ are initialised once and held *requires_grad = False*. The synaptic time constants $\tau_\text{AMPA}$, $\tau_\text{GABA}$ are module-level constants. 813k trainable parameters out of 2.4M total.

**Training.** Adam, lr = $4 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size 256, gradient norm clipped to 1.0. Cross-entropy loss on 10-class MNIST (definition in [art006](/articles/art006/)). Gradient stabiliser: *--v-grad-dampen 1000* (uniform scaling of per-step voltage gradients; see [nb027](/notebooks/nb027/) for a principled alternative). Trial duration $T = 200$ ms at $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms (2000 timesteps).

**Tier.** Medium: 2000 training samples, 10 epochs, three seeds (42, 43, 44) for baselines, one seed (42) for sweep cells.

**Recipe difference.** The only parameter that differs between COBA and PING besides *--ei-strength* is the $W_\text{in}$ initialisation: COBA uses mean 0.3 (std 0.03), PING uses mean 1.2 (std 0.12). PING needs stronger input drive to reliably recruit the I-loop at init.

## The spike-budget control

To probe the rate axis we add a spike-budget regulariser: a soft upper bound on per-trial spike count. For each E cell $n$, let $\bar z_n$ be the mean spike count per trial. The penalty is

$$
L_\text{rate} \;=\; \lambda \sum_n \mathrm{ReLU}(\bar z_n - \theta_u)^2,
$$

with $\theta_u$ the per-cell budget in spikes/trial and $\lambda = 10^{-3}$ the strength. Only cells whose mean output exceeds $\theta_u$ contribute; cells under budget are free. Total training loss is cross-entropy plus $L_\text{rate}$.

We sweep six target levels (off, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2 spikes/trial; at $T = 200$ ms that's no penalty, 25, 10, 5, 2.5, 1 Hz). Twelve cells, one point per (model, $\theta_u$) in (rate, accuracy) space:

<Figure id="Figure 5" title="Accuracy / rate frontier" src="/figures/notebooks/nb024/frontier.png" alt="Test accuracy vs hidden-E firing rate, one curve per model; each point one (model, θ_u).">
  Test accuracy vs achieved hidden-E rate; one point per cell. Gray point labels indicate the spike penalty.
</Figure>

COBA traces a curve from ≈85 Hz down to ≈0.2 Hz, costing ≈24 pp accuracy. PING spans 3.2–5.1 Hz across every $\theta_u$ — the penalty has measurable but small leverage (5.1 → 3.2 Hz from $\theta_u =$ off to $\theta_u = 0.2$), and never pushes the network into COBA's territory.

## Why PING has a rate floor

Figure 5 shows COBA spanning three decades under $\theta_u$ (≈85 → ≈0.2 Hz) while PING barely moves (5.1 → 3.2 Hz). Three cases, by where the budget sits relative to PING's ≈5 Hz trained rate. (A) **No budget**: network sits at the gamma-locked attractor. (B) **Budget above 5 Hz**: penalty inactive at the operating point, equivalent to (A). (C) **Budget below 5 Hz**: the interesting case — the penalty wants a rate PING cannot deliver, and PING with the loop disengaged is structurally COBA, so why not slide down COBA's curve to 0.2 Hz?

Let $f$ be the **recruitment fraction** (share of E cells crossing threshold per cycle) and $f^\star$ the critical value at which the I-population fires reliably: $f > f^\star$ engaged PING, $f < f^\star$ a low-rate COBA-like regime. Figure 6 trains four PING networks across this threshold, $W_\text{in}$ initialised at 0.05, 0.1, 0.3, and 1.2 (the standard init).

Three pieces hold the floor in place. First, **PING and COBA are different statistical regimes**: PING fires in gamma-coordinated bursts at ≈25 Hz with ≈20% of E cells per cycle (≈5 Hz mean); COBA fires asynchronously at ≈85 Hz with most cells participating. $W_\text{out}$ specialises on whichever regime training produced. Second, **the recruitment cliff at $f^\star$** (Figure 7, dotted line at $s \approx 0.42$): below it the loop disengages and CE rises to chance — PING's readout can't decode the sparse uncoordinated pattern. Third, **above the cliff the rate is set by gamma physics**: cycle period scales with $\tau_\text{GABA}$, and at the trained value (9 ms) the natural per-cell rate is ≈4.5–5 Hz. The penalty can nudge down within the basin, but the engaged loop sets a soft minimum. The total loss has a broad minimum at $s = 1$: below, the cliff kicks CE up; above, the penalty grows. The ≈3.2 Hz floor is where the two pressures cross.

PING cannot slide down COBA's curve to 0.2 Hz for two reasons. **Different readouts:** Figure 7's COBA curve shows what 0.2 Hz looks like with a COBA-trained readout (rate ≈1 Hz, CE 2.27, accuracy 53.75%) — its readout was trained on sparse asynchronous firing, PING's on gamma-coordinated participation; abandoning the cycle breaks the readout. **The architecture forbids it:** only $W_\text{in}$ and $W_\text{out}$ are trainable; $W^{EI}$ and $W^{IE}$ are fixed at construction, $W^{EE} = 0$. No setting of the trainable weights suppresses the I-loop. The COBA-low solution lives in the parameter space *--ei-strength = 0* opens.

This also explains Figure 6's universality: at $W_\text{in} = 0.05$ the network starts silent, not COBA-like — too little drive to fire. The only gradient pulls $W_\text{in}$ up; the moment drive crosses $f^\star$, the fixed E↔I wiring self-assembles the gamma cycle. The optimiser never chose PING; the architecture forced it.

One further observation: PING's CE drops much more steeply across the recruitment threshold than COBA's (≈2.30 → ≈0.91 across a factor of 2 in $s$, vs COBA 2.30 → 2.04 over the same range). The gamma cycle concentrates the class signal — each burst is a structured class-discriminative event, so 2–3 bursts per trial produce a clean readout contribution. COBA's asynchronous spikes each add a noisy single-cell increment; the signal only emerges after many accumulate. PING does not just use fewer spikes, it uses them more efficiently.

**In one sentence:** the rate floor is the equilibrium of gamma-cycle dynamics (lower bound set by $\tau_\text{GABA}$) and the rate$^2$ penalty (upper pressure), confined to the basin in $W_\text{in}$-space where $W_\text{out}$ still decodes the participation pattern.

## Coupling sweep: shifting the recruitment cliff

If $f^\star$ is set by the E↔I coupling, altering $W^{EI}$ or $W^{IE}$ at inference should move it. On the trained PING baseline ($\theta_u =$ off), we multiply either $W^{EI}$ or $W^{IE}$ by a scalar in $\{0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ and re-run the W_in scale sweep on top. All other weights frozen.

</Figure>

Figure 9 cleanly separates the two roles. $W^{EI}$ moves the cliff (left column): halving E→I coupling shifts $s^\star$ from 0.25 to 0.35, quartering it to 0.50. Doubling does not push it further left because below $s \approx 0.2$ the feedforward drive itself is too weak to make E fire — the bottleneck has moved from I-cell recruitment to E-cell firing, and stronger $W^{EI}$ cannot rescue silent E spikes. $W^{IE}$ does not move the cliff (right column): all four curves lift off at $s \approx 0.25$, because $W^{IE}$ only matters once I has fired, and at the cliff I has not. Above the cliff the right-column I-rate curves separate visibly — stronger inhibition per I spike suppresses the steady-state I rate. So the recruitment threshold is set by the E→I drive; inhibitory feedback shapes the basin only above engagement.

## Hidden-spike perturbation

The mechanism is a per-step callback installed on the COBANet's *_hidden_perturb_fn* slot.

**When the callback fires.** Every timestep of every trial. No warm-up, no schedule, no exclusion. Trials are $T = 200$ ms at $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms → 2000 fires per trial. Test set ≈400 trials → ≈800 000 fires per (model, mode, level) sweep point against the same trained network. Level is constant across all timesteps and trials at a given sweep point.

**Where in the step body.** At each timestep $t$:

1. *update conductances* from the previous step's spikes: $g_e^{(t)} \leftarrow g_e^{(t-1)} \cdot d_\text{AMPA} + \mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t-1)} W_{ee} + \text{input}^{(t)} W_\text{in}$, with the analogous update for $g_i^{(t)}$ from $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{I,(t-1)} W_{ie}$ and the E→I conductance from $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t-1)} W_{ei}$;
2. *LIF step* integrates $V^{(t)}$ from $g_e^{(t)}, g_i^{(t)}$ and emits raw spike vectors $\mathbf{s}^{E,(t)} \in \{0,1\}^{B \times N_E}$, $\mathbf{s}^{I,(t)} \in \{0,1\}^{B \times N_I}$;
3. *perturbation callback* rewrites the raw vectors → $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t)}, \mathbf{\tilde s}^{I,(t)}$;
4. *record* the perturbed vectors into the spike buffer;
5. *readout accumulator* adds $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t)} W_\text{out}$ to the mem-mean integrator.

Step 1 of timestep $t+1$ consumes $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t)}$ and $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{I,(t)}$ through $W_{ee}$, $W_{ei}$, $W_{ie}$. A dropped E spike at $t$ fails to drive I at $t+1$ and contributes nothing to the readout at $t$. An injected I spike at $t$ adds inhibition at $t+1$ and counts in the rate metric. E and I get the same mode and level with independent draws.

In **drop** mode, for each (batch, neuron, timestep) slot $i$ draw $u_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$ i.i.d.:

$$
\tilde s_i \;=\; s_i \cdot \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i \geq p_\text{drop}\right].
$$

Each emitted spike is kept with probability $1 - p_\text{drop}$; silent slots stay silent. Drop reduces the spike count fed to both the readout and the next-step conductance update, while leaving the E→I→E feedback loop intact. Sweep $p_\text{drop} \in \{0.0, 0.1, \dots, 1.0\}$, 11 levels.

In **add** mode, for each slot draw $u_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$ i.i.d.:

$$
\tilde s_i \;=\; \min\!\left(s_i + \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i < r_\text{add} \cdot \Delta t / 1000\right],\; 1\right).
$$

At $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms, $r_\text{add} = 10$ Hz gives a per-step extra-spike probability of $10^{-3}$. The clamp is for binary cleanliness — the process is Bernoulli, not additive integer. Silent slots become 1 with probability $r_\text{add} \Delta t / 1000$. Add injects spikes at Poisson statistics independent of cycle phase. Sweep $r_\text{add} \in \{0, 2, \dots, 40\}$ Hz, 21 levels.

The per-step RNG is a CPU torch.Generator seeded at *EVAL_SEED + 1*, separate from the input encoder so the Poisson input stream matches the unperturbed baseline. Total: 2 models × (11 drop + 21 add) = 64 forward passes.

COBA stays at 83–84% under both perturbation modes — without a cycle to compensate or corrupt, dropped spikes thin a uniform mean and added spikes raise it.

This suggests the rate floor is dynamical, not informational. Test: lengthening $\tau_\text{GABA}$ should stretch the cycle and lower the floor; shortening should raise it.

At rate = 0 the network is silent. Above a recruitment threshold a few E units fire, the I loop catches, and the gamma cadence appears. Once the loop is locked the cycle period is invariant to further increases in input rate across the rest of the sweep.

A subtlety in the just-engaged panels (e.g. input ≈ 5 Hz, E = 1.7 Hz, I = 0): the E spikes look mildly clustered even though the I-loop hasn't fired. This is feedforward — not PING. The Poisson input stream has natural temporal fluctuations, the digit-0 input is spatially correlated, and the trained $W_\text{in}$ has learned to be selective for digit-relevant pixels. So the cells that fire are the input-tuned subset, and they fire together during input-rich windows. The participation pattern is partially preserved from training; the gamma timing is not, because the loop is genuinely off.

Three features. (1) **Recruitment threshold between 5 and 10 Hz input**: I jumps from 0 to 15 Hz across that interval, marking the cliff in classical f-I form. (2) **A narrow sub-recruitment regime** at 5 Hz input where E fires (1.65 Hz) but I does not — the "E spikes, I silent" state from the rate-floor argument made directly visible. (3) **Saturation**: above ≈30 Hz input the I-loop bounds both populations — E plateaus around 8–11 Hz, I around 35–50 Hz, despite 3× more drive. This is gamma physics: cycle period set by $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA}$ limits spikes per cell per trial regardless of how hard the input pushes.

Figure 18 conflates two effects: the network's response to drive, and the readout's selectivity for digit-0-shaped input through trained $W_\text{in}$. To isolate the bare drive-response, repeat the sweep with a **spatially uniform input** — every input channel firing at the same Poisson rate, with no digit structure — and compare PING against COBA on the same axes.

The left panel shows COBA reaching higher accuracy faster in wall-clock time: 73.5% at 20 ms vs PING's 68.5%. The curves cross near 100 ms and PING finishes ahead (85.0% vs 83.0%). COBA fires asynchronously from timestep 1 at ≈85 Hz, so by 20 ms the readout has ≈1.7 spikes/cell to average over. PING needs the gamma cycle to self-assemble — the first structured burst appears at ≈10-15 ms — so the readout sees little useful signal before then.

The right panel reframes the same data with cumulative E spikes on the x-axis. At the crossover accuracy (≈81%), PING has used ≈530 spikes vs COBA's ≈5,700 — roughly 11× fewer. Each gamma burst is a concentrated class-discriminative event, so the readout converges faster per spike than COBA's noisy per-spike average. COBA wins on absolute latency (more spikes available earlier). PING wins on spike efficiency (fewer spikes to reach the same accuracy). If the relevant metric is minimum spikes to decision rather than minimum time to decision, PING is strictly better.

## Summary

PING reaches COBA's accuracy at an order of magnitude lower rate. The rate floor is the loop's preferred cycle period, governed by $\tau_\text{GABA}$, not by what the task requires. The readout phase-locks to that cycle; perturbations that leave the E→I→E loop intact (drop, $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep) are compensated dynamically, while those that inject phase-incoherent activity (add) corrupt the cycle and collapse accuracy. $\theta_u$ has no leverage below the cycle's cadence — the floor is physical, not optimised. Within its operating regime, PING uses each spike more efficiently than COBA: the gamma cycle concentrates the class signal into coordinated bursts that the readout can decode at lower total count, though this efficiency comes with a startup latency cost.
