## Abstract

Spiking neural networks trained on MNIST converge to high accuracy across the biophysical ladder, but the firing rates they operate at differ by more than an order of magnitude. COBA — a conductance-based LIF network without recurrent inhibition — settles at $\sim 80$ Hz hidden firing rate after training; PING — the same network with an excitatory–inhibitory loop wired up at initialisation and frozen — settles at $\sim 5$ Hz with comparable accuracy. Adding an upper-bound spike-budget regulariser drags COBA's rate down to PING-like levels at a measurable accuracy cost, while PING's regularised frontier is flat: the network already sits inside the budget. This entry asks what sets PING's rate floor and whether it is bound by what the readout *needs* or by what the gamma loop *produces*. Test-time hidden-spike perturbation — random Bernoulli drop or Poisson add applied per timestep, inside the forward loop so the inhibitory feedback and the readout both react — separates the two. PING tolerates dropping up to $\sim 70$% of its emitted hidden spikes without losing accuracy, so the readout is happy at much lower rates than PING naturally produces. But PING collapses to chance at just 10 Hz of added Poisson noise per neuron, while COBA degrades smoothly across the full add sweep. The asymmetry says PING's rate floor is *dynamical*, not informational: the gamma loop has a preferred period set by $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA}$, and the readout has learned to phase-lock to it; the spike-budget regulariser cannot push the rate below the loop's natural cadence without dismantling the very structure the readout is reading.

## 1. Introduction

A persistent puzzle in trained spiking neural networks (SNNs) is the gap between the firing rates the models settle at after gradient-based training and the rates seen in biology. Standard rate-coded SNNs reach high MNIST accuracy at firing rates an order of magnitude above mammalian cortex; biologically-motivated architectures with recurrent inhibition fire orders of magnitude lower at the same task accuracy. The folk explanation is "inhibition does sparsity"; the precise mechanism is less often pinned down. This notebook isolates one piece of that mechanism: the rate floor set by the gamma oscillation in a pyramidal–interneuron-gamma (PING) network.

The contribution is empirical:

1. We train COBA and PING — two rungs of the conductance-based LIF ladder differing only by whether the recurrent E↔I matrices are non-zero — to comparable MNIST accuracy and quantify the resulting firing-rate gap (Figure 1).
2. We apply an upper-bound spike-budget regulariser $\theta_u$ across a six-point grid and trace the accuracy/rate Pareto frontier (Figure 3). COBA's frontier slopes; PING's is flat because the unregularised network is already at a firing-rate floor.
3. We characterise PING's rate floor with two perturbation protocols. An input-rate sweep (Figures 6–7) shows the gamma loop engages above a recruitment threshold and locks across a wide input range. A hidden-spike perturbation (Figures 8–12) — random Bernoulli drop and Poisson add, applied inside the forward loop so the I-loop and readout both react — separates *information-bound* from *dynamics-bound* explanations of the floor.
4. We replay the trained COBA baseline with a freshly-initialised PING I-loop wired up at increasing strengths (Figures 13–15) to confirm the gamma cadence and the rate suppression both emerge from the loop, not from training.

The headline finding is that PING's rate floor is dynamical. The readout works fine at $\sim 1$ Hz when the floor is bypassed by drop; the gamma loop's preferred cycle period prevents the rate from going there spontaneously. This subsumes the now-retired *nb020*.

## 2. Background

[The LIF Neuron](/articles/art001/) derives the leaky integrate-and-fire neuron from a membrane circuit and the corresponding linear ODE. [CUBANet](/articles/art002/) discretises that ODE for GPU evaluation; [COBANet](/articles/art003/) extends it to conductance-based synapses, E/I partition, hard reset, refractory periods, and recurrent E↔I matrices. The two models studied here are both COBANet instances; they differ only in *--ei-strength*:

- COBA — *--ei-strength = 0*. The E↔I matrices exist but are silenced; the network is structurally a feedforward conductance-based LIF.
- PING — *--ei-strength = 1*. The E↔I matrices are wired up at initialisation and held *requires_grad=False*. Only the feedforward weights train; the recurrent inhibitory loop is a fixed dynamical substrate the optimiser learns to read out.

[023 — PING fundamentals](/notebooks/nb023/) characterises that loop in isolation from any task. Untrained PING produces gamma-band ($\sim 30$ Hz) population oscillations from a Poisson input. The interspike-interval is set by $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA} \approx 11$ ms — a fundamental empirical anchor for the rate-floor analysis here.

### 3.1 Models and training

Two cells per model, six $\theta_u$ values each (12 cells total):

- *coba* — [coba](/articles/art003/) recipe (*--ei-strength=0*, *--w-in=0.3*, *--lr=0.0004*, *--readout-w-out-scale=100*).

Each unpenalised baseline is trained on three seeds (42, 43, 44) for variance estimates; sweep cells run on seed 42 only. Training uses the [oscilloscope](/docs/the-oscilloscope/) directly via [BatchDispatcher](/docs/the-oscilloscope/#modal-dispatch).

### 3.2 Test-time hidden-spike perturbation

The mechanism is a per-step callback installed on the live COBANet instance — *src/pinglab/models.py* exposes a *_hidden_perturb_fn* slot that, when non-None, is invoked inside the forward loop at every timestep, right after each layer's E (and I) spike vector is emitted by the LIF step and *before* those spikes are read by anything downstream. The same instance forward loop is used; only the spike tensor is rewritten in-place.

**Exactly when and on what.** For each layer $\ell$ at each timestep $t$ the LIF emits

$$
\mathbf{s}^E_t \in \{0,1\}^{B \times N_E}, \qquad \mathbf{s}^I_t \in \{0,1\}^{B \times N_I}
$$

(one binary entry per neuron per batch element). The callback replaces these with $\mathbf{\tilde s}^E_t$ and $\mathbf{\tilde s}^I_t$. The replaced tensors then feed *all three* downstream consumers within the same step:

1. The output readout — *prev_spk @ W_ff[−1]* uses the perturbed $\mathbf{\tilde s}^E_t$.
2. The next step's E→E and E→I recurrence — *state["s_e"][ℓ] @ W_ee*, *@ W_ei* use $\mathbf{\tilde s}^E_t$.
3. The next step's I→E feedback — *state["s_i"][ℓ] @ W_ie* uses $\mathbf{\tilde s}^I_t$.

So the loop dynamics react to the perturbation: an injected I-spike actually inhibits E next step; a dropped E-spike actually fails to drive I. There is no "shadow" un-perturbed pathway. Membrane voltages, conductances, and refractory counters all update from the perturbed spikes onwards. Both E and I populations get the same mode and level (independent random draws, identical statistics).

**Drop mode.** For each (batch element, neuron, timestep) slot $i$,

$$
\mathbf{\tilde s}_i = \mathbf{s}_i \cdot \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i \ge p_\text{drop}\right], \qquad u_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0,1)
$$

i.i.d. across slots. Each emitted spike is kept with probability $1 - p_\text{drop}$; a slot that was 0 stays 0. So drop *thins* the spike train without creating new spikes. Levels: $p_\text{drop} \in \{0.0, 0.1, 0.2, ..., 1.0\}$ (11 values).

**Add mode.** For each (batch element, neuron, timestep) slot $i$,

$$
\mathbf{\tilde s}_i = \min\!\left(\mathbf{s}_i + \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i < p\right],\; 1\right), \qquad p = r_\text{add} \cdot \Delta t / 1000
$$

with $u_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0,1)$ i.i.d. across slots. The probability $p$ is the per-step probability of an extra spike for a single neuron such that the expected added rate equals $r_\text{add}$ Hz. Slots that already had a spike stay at 1 (the clamp is just for binary cleanliness — the noise process is Bernoulli, not additive integer). With this notebook's $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms, $r_\text{add} = 10$ Hz corresponds to $p = 0.001$ per step per neuron. Levels: $r_\text{add} \in \{0, 10, 20, ..., 100\}$ Hz (11 values).

**Per-step cadence.** $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms and trial length $T = 200$ ms gives 2000 timesteps per trial; the callback fires twice per layer per step (once on $\mathbf{s}^E$, once on $\mathbf{s}^I$). Random draws come from a CPU/MPS *torch.Generator* seeded as *EVAL_SEED + 1* and *not* reset between batches, so the noise is independent across the full test set. The encoding generator for the input Poisson stream is seeded with *EVAL_SEED* exactly as in the unperturbed baseline, so the only thing that differs across the sweep is the hidden-layer perturbation.

**Evaluation.** Full MNIST test set (size matches *cfg.max_samples*), batch size 64, one forward pass per (model, mode, level) — $2 \times 2 \times 11 = 44$ passes total. Accuracy is the argmax of the mem-mean readout vs ground-truth labels. Mean hidden-E firing rate is computed post-hoc from the recorded last-layer raster.

Rasters in Figures 9–12 use the same trained network, same MNIST digit 0 sample 0, but display only six perturbation levels per (model, mode) instead of the full 11-point grid: drop ∈ {0.0, 0.3, 0.6, 0.8, 0.9, 1.0} and add ∈ {0, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100} Hz.

### 3.3 Input-rate sweep

To characterise the trained PING network as a dynamical system, we replay it on a single MNIST trial (digit 0 sample 0) while sweeping the input Poisson rate from 0 Hz to 100 Hz in 40 linearly-spaced steps. Each frame is a 400 ms forward pass — longer than the 200 ms training window so the gamma rhythm has room to develop visibly. The output is rendered as both an oscilloscope-style scope-frame video (Figure 6) and a stacked-raster figure for the first ten frames (Figure 7).

### 3.4 COBA → PING weight transfer

The *coba__off__seed42* baseline (trained with the E↔I loop silenced) is re-evaluated with a fresh PING-architecture I-loop at 11 inference-time values of *--ei-strength* from 0.0 to 1.0. *W_ff* and *W_ee* load from the trained COBA checkpoint; *W_ei* and *W_ie* are *re-initialised* at the inference-time ei_strength so each sweep point has a genuinely active I-loop (the trained-COBA checkpoint has *W_ei* = *W_ie* = 0, which would otherwise nullify the loop at any strength). Single forward pass over the test set per ei_strength value; same digit (sample 0) used for the raster snapshots.

## 5. Discussion

The headline contrast — same task, same accuracy, COBA at $\sim 80$ Hz and PING at $\sim 5$ Hz — is reproducible and economically meaningful: matched accuracy at $\sim$16× fewer spikes is a significant energy / bandwidth saving in any neuromorphic deployment. The spike-budget regulariser experiment in §4.2 reframes that gap. COBA's frontier slopes — push it toward PING-like rates with $\theta_u$ and pay $\sim 10$ pp in accuracy. PING's frontier is flat: $\theta_u$ has nothing to do because the network is already inside the budget. The asymmetry suggests the architecture is doing what the regulariser was meant to do.

### 5.1 The rate floor is dynamical, not informational

A natural question from Figure 3: PING's natural rate is $\sim 5$ Hz and tightening $\theta_u$ to 0.2 spikes/trial (1 Hz target) still leaves it firing at $\sim 3$ Hz. The penalty has no leverage. *Why?*

Two candidate explanations:

1. **Information-bound.** The readout *needs* $\sim 3$–$5$ Hz of spikes to classify MNIST. Push the rate lower and accuracy must fall with it.
2. **Dynamics-bound.** The gamma loop has a preferred cycle period — set by $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA} \approx 11$ ms ($\sim 90$ Hz population rhythm, divided across the population, gives a few-Hz single-neuron rate). The dynamics *want* to fire that often; the rate floor is the loop's stable operating point and the penalty can't push the network off it without breaking the loop.

The hidden-spike perturbation in §4.5 separates these:

- **Drop.** PING holds $\sim$84% accuracy through $p_\text{drop} = 0.7$ and is still at 73% at $p_\text{drop} = 0.9$. The readout works fine on a small fraction of PING's natural spike count, so the rate floor is *not* information-bound. PING could in principle classify at $\sim 1$ Hz; the penalty isn't blocked by what the task needs.
- **Add.** PING collapses to chance at just 10 Hz of added Poisson noise, even though the noise *raises* the E rate (where you'd expect more drive, not less, to help). The readout is locked to the gamma *phase*, not the rate — extra spikes at the wrong phase corrupt the signal. By contrast COBA degrades smoothly because it has no phase to be off of.

Together these say: PING's rate floor is dynamical. The penalty $\theta_u$ can't push the rate below the floor because doing so would require the loop to oscillate slower than $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA}$ permits — and the loop physically can't. The drop experiment shows the *readout* is happy at much lower rates; it's the *generator* (the gamma loop) that's stuck.

### 5.2 Implications

The "loop sets the floor" framing makes specific predictions:

- *Lengthening $\tau_\text{GABA}$ at inference* should lower the floor — the cycle period stretches, the network fires less often per cycle, and the readout's gamma phase moves with it.
- *Weakening --ei-strength* should raise the floor — less inhibitory authority means more E spikes per cycle. The COBA → PING transfer in §4.6 already varies this; replotted as E rate vs ei_strength on the existing data (Figure 14 already has both rates), the floor moves continuously.
- *Architectures that decouple the readout from the gamma phase* (e.g. mem-mean over many cycles, instead of phase-locked spike count) should be more robust to additive noise but lose the sparsity advantage.

The wider implication: "inhibition does sparsity" is true here, but the mechanism is not regularisation in the optimisation sense. The inhibitory loop forces the network into an attractor with a specific cycle period, and that period sets a non-negotiable rate floor. Spike-budget regularisation is the wrong tool for this floor — it's bounded by physics, not by a loss-function preference.

## 6. Limitations

- **Small tier.** Each cell trained at *max_samples = 500, epochs = 5*, near MNIST's small-sample regime. Absolute accuracies (84–86%) are below state-of-the-art and the headline contrast may compress at larger tiers — see Next steps.
- **Single dataset, single task.** Findings are MNIST-only. PING's gamma phase-locking may serve digit classification well but not all tasks; tasks that require temporal precision in the input (SHD, sequential MNIST) would test this differently.
- **Frontier seed coverage.** Baselines (θ_u = off) are averaged across 3 seeds; sweep cells (θ_u ≠ off) are single-seed. The frontier shape is robust at the qualitative level but per-point error bars are missing for the sweep.
- **Perturbation is uniform across populations.** Drop / add apply the same statistic to E and I. A separate sweep perturbing only E (or only I) would isolate which population's spike timing the readout depends on.
- **No structure-preserving counterfactual.** The add experiment confounds *spike rate* and *spike timing structure*. A threshold-shift sweep (raise the LIF threshold to suppress firing without injecting noise) would isolate the structure axis cleanly.
- **No mechanistic test of the cycle-period claim.** §5.2 predicts that varying $\tau_\text{GABA}$ moves the floor; this notebook has not run that experiment.

## 7. Conclusion

PING reaches MNIST accuracy comparable to COBA at more than an order of magnitude lower firing rate. The accuracy/rate frontier under an upper-bound spike-budget regulariser is flat for PING and sloped for COBA: the recurrent inhibitory loop already operates inside the budget. Hidden-spike perturbation at inference demonstrates that PING's rate floor is dynamical, not informational — the gamma loop's preferred cycle period, set by $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA}$, forces the network into a phase-locked attractor that the readout has learned to read. Tolerance to drop and brittleness to add together pin the mechanism: the readout uses far fewer spikes than the loop produces, and the spikes it uses are the ones at the right phase. Inhibition-gated sparsity, in this architecture, is a physical consequence of the loop's natural cadence rather than an optimisation preference.

## 8. Next steps

- **Plot E rate vs ei_strength from the existing ei_sweep** to make the "loop sets the floor" story directly visible.
- **Vary $\tau_\text{GABA}$ at inference** on the trained PING baseline. Predicts: longer $\tau_\text{GABA}$ → slower cycle → lower floor; shorter → higher floor. Cleanest test that the floor is set by the gamma period.
- **Threshold-shift sweep** as a structure-preserving alternative to random add — isolates rate from spike-timing structure.
- Extend the $\theta_u$ grid downward (e.g. 0.1, 0.05) to find where every model breaks.
- Add a *coba-with-loop* cell (active recurrent inhibition but trained as coba) to disentangle "the loop on" from "the loop being trained against".
- Repeat at *large* tier on Modal to tighten the per-point variance and check the headline contrast survives at higher absolute accuracy.
