## The spike-budget frontier

To probe the rate axis we add a spike-budget regulariser $\theta_u$ — a soft upper bound on per-trial spike count — and sweep six target levels (off, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2 spikes/trial). Twelve cells, one point per (model, $\theta_u$) in (rate, accuracy) space:

COBA traces a Pareto curve from ≈85 Hz down to ≈0.1 Hz, costing ≈24 pp accuracy. PING clusters tight at 3–5 Hz across every $\theta_u$ — the penalty has measurable but small leverage (5.3 → 2.8 Hz from $\theta_u =$ off to $\theta_u = 0.2$), and never pushes the network into COBA's territory.

## Why PING has a rate floor

Figure 5 shows COBA spanning nearly three decades under $\theta_u$ (≈85 → ≈0.1 Hz) while PING barely moves (5.3 → 2.8 Hz). Three cases, by where the budget sits relative to PING's natural ≈5 Hz.

__(A) No budget.__ The network sits at PING's gamma-locked attractor; if E ever dips low enough to disengage the I-loop, dynamics relax toward COBA's ≈85 Hz fixed point, recruitment crosses the I-cell threshold, and the loop re-engages.

__(B) Budget above 5 Hz.__ Penalty inactive at the operating point; equivalent to (A).

__(C) Budget below 5 Hz.__ The interesting case: the penalty wants a rate PING's cadence cannot deliver, and PING with the loop disengaged is structurally COBA — so why not slide down COBA's curve to 0.1 Hz?

Two pieces of structure. Let $f$ be the **recruitment fraction** (share of E cells crossing threshold per cycle) and $f^\star$ the **critical value** at which the I-population fires reliably. The boundary at $f^\star$ is a saddle-node bifurcation — there is no intermediate stable state with E firing sparsely and I silent. The PING readout $W_\text{ff}^{(-1)}$ is a linear projection of the time-averaged E spike vector and during training specialises on PING's sparse participation pattern at the ≈5 Hz scale: each digit recruits a distinct subset of E cells through $W_\text{in}$. The downward exit from this basin destroys the readout's input distribution: shrinking $W_\text{in}$ pushes $f$ below $f^\star$, the loop disengages, residual drive cannot sustain firing on its own, and the network goes near-silent. The upward direction is asymmetric: raising $W_\text{in}$ scales the I-loop in lockstep with E recruitment, which bounds E from above rather than exposing a COBA-like high-rate fixed point. So the only real exit is downward, and that route runs to chance. Local gradient at the boundary points back into the basin, and training settles at the PING floor (≈2.8 Hz under $\theta_u = 0.2$). The 0.1 Hz solution exists but requires a readout trained against asynchronous statistics, which sits across a non-convex barrier.

The argument is *path-dependent*: it assumes the readout has already specialised on PING activity by the time $\theta_u$ has leverage. So we tested the obvious alternate schedule — initialise $W_\text{in}$ below $f^\star$ with $\theta_u$ on from epoch 0. Three PING trainings at $\theta_u = 0.2$ (heaviest budget), MNIST, medium tier (2000 samples × 10 epochs, seed 42), $W_\text{in}$ ∈ {0.1, 0.3, 1.2}.

All three converge to PING. Final accuracies 83.25% / 82.75% / 84.25%; final I rates 22.2 / 16.6 / 16.7 Hz. The traces show the trajectory: at $W_\text{in} = 0.1$ the I-loop is dormant for two epochs (network firing sparsely, no gamma), then engages at epoch 3 and locks. The 0.1 Hz COBA-frontier solution is not reachable from any PING-config training tested here — it lives in COBA's basin and requires *--ei-strength 0*.

A more direct probe of the loss landscape: scale each trained network's $W_\text{in}$ by a multiplicative factor $s$ at inference time and measure loss across $s \in [0.1, 5]$. No retraining; the readout is frozen at its trained-PING (or trained-COBA) values. If a bifurcation is shaping the landscape, PING should show a sharp loss feature where the I-loop engages.

<Figure id="Figure 7" title="W_in scale sweep on trained networks" src="/figures/notebooks/nb024/w_in_scale_sweep.png" alt="Four panels showing test loss, accuracy, E rate, and I rate vs W_in scale s.   Inference-time $W_\text{in}$ scale sweep on trained PING (black) and trained COBA (red). Each network's $W_\text{in}$ is multiplied by $s$ at evaluation only; no weights are retrained. Dashed line marks the trained operating point $s = 1$.
</Figure>

Both networks have a recruitment threshold below which the readout sees chance: at $s = 0.15$ both produce loss ≈ $\ln 10 \approx 2.30$ and accuracy 10%. The thresholds differ, and that is where the bifurcation signature actually lives. PING engages between $s = 0.22$ and $s = 0.33$ — loss drops from 2.09 to 1.20, accuracy from 42% to 79%, I rate from 0.22 to 4 Hz. COBA at the same $s$ is still near chance (loss 2.19 at $s = 0.33$) and only reaches working accuracy at $s \geq 0.75$. The I-loop lets PING sustain at lower drive: once E recruits I, the GABA pulse stretches each cycle and the next volley needs only weak feedforward input. Above engagement PING's loss is flat in 0.47–0.53 across $s = 0.5$ to $s = 5$ because the I-loop scales with recruitment (I rate 12 → 56 Hz, holding E to 3–12 Hz); COBA's loss has a narrower minimum near $s = 1.5$ before climbing as rates saturate.

Two refinements to the earlier prediction, both in the direction of *weaker* claims than I made above. (a) The argument anticipated a localised loss peak at the crossing. The data shows no peak in either network — loss rises monotonically to chance as $s$ shrinks. The honest statement is "PING has a sharper-and-earlier recruitment edge than COBA", not "PING's loss has a barrier feature absent in COBA". Gradient still points back into the basin from outside (loss is higher outside), but the shape is a threshold, not a barrier. (b) The predicted runaway route — *growing* $W_\text{in}$ past PING to expose COBA's ≈85 Hz attractor — does not materialise within tested $s$. At $s = 5$ the I-loop has scaled to 56 Hz while E is bounded at 12 Hz. The loop is self-stabilising in $s$, and raising $W_\text{in}$ within the optimiser's reach does not provide an exit upward.

The two empirical pictures combine into a simple story. PING has a rate floor because the I-loop creates a wide, asymmetric basin in $W_\text{in}$-space. Below the recruitment threshold (Figure 7, $s \lesssim 0.22$) the network goes silent and the loss reverts to chance — no useful gradient toward sub-PING firing. Above engagement (Figure 7, $s \geq 0.5$) the I-loop scales with recruitment and bounds E in the 3–12 Hz band across an order of magnitude in $W_\text{in}$ — no upward exit either. The basin is flat near 5 Hz, which is the exact rate $\theta_u$ runs into when it tries to push the network below PING's cadence. The alternate-schedule test (Figure 6) shows the basin is also attractive from outside: a network initialised below recruitment with $\theta_u$ already on still crosses into PING within a few epochs. So the 0.1 Hz COBA-frontier solution is unreachable from any PING-config training: there is no continuous path within the loop-architecture to a regime without it. Reaching that solution requires leaving PING-config entirely — *--ei-strength = 0*.

## Hidden-spike perturbation

The mechanism is a per-step callback installed on the COBANet's *_hidden_perturb_fn* slot.

**When the callback fires.** Every timestep of every trial. No warm-up, no schedule, no exclusion. Trials are $T = 200$ ms at $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms → 2000 fires per trial. Test set ≈400 trials → ≈800 000 fires per (model, mode, level) sweep point against the same trained network. Level is constant across all timesteps and trials at a given sweep point.

**Where in the step body.** At each timestep $t$:

1. *update conductances* from the previous step's spikes: $g_e^{(t)} \leftarrow g_e^{(t-1)} \cdot d_\text{AMPA} + \mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t-1)} W_{ee} + \text{input}^{(t)} W_\text{in}$, with the analogous update for $g_i^{(t)}$ from $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{I,(t-1)} W_{ie}$ and the E→I conductance from $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t-1)} W_{ei}$;
2. *LIF step* integrates $V^{(t)}$ from $g_e^{(t)}, g_i^{(t)}$ and emits raw spike vectors $\mathbf{s}^{E,(t)} \in \{0,1\}^{B \times N_E}$, $\mathbf{s}^{I,(t)} \in \{0,1\}^{B \times N_I}$;
3. *perturbation callback* rewrites the raw vectors → $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t)}, \mathbf{\tilde s}^{I,(t)}$;
4. *record* the perturbed vectors into the spike buffer;
5. *readout accumulator* adds $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t)} W_\text{ff}^{(-1)}$ to the mem-mean integrator.

Step 1 of timestep $t+1$ consumes $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{E,(t)}$ and $\mathbf{\tilde s}^{I,(t)}$ through $W_{ee}$, $W_{ei}$, $W_{ie}$. A dropped E spike at $t$ fails to drive I at $t+1$ and contributes nothing to the readout at $t$. An injected I spike at $t$ adds inhibition at $t+1$ and counts in the rate metric. E and I get the same mode and level with independent draws.

In **drop** mode, for each (batch, neuron, timestep) slot $i$ draw $u_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$ i.i.d.:

$$
\tilde s_i \;=\; s_i \cdot \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i \geq p_\text{drop}\right].
$$

Each emitted spike is kept with probability $1 - p_\text{drop}$; silent slots stay silent. Drop thins the spike train without injecting new spikes — gamma phase is preserved, only amplitude reduced. Sweep $p_\text{drop} \in \{0.0, 0.1, \dots, 1.0\}$, 11 levels.

In **add** mode, for each slot draw $u_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, 1)$ i.i.d.:

$$
\tilde s_i \;=\; \min\!\left(s_i + \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i < r_\text{add} \cdot \Delta t / 1000\right],\; 1\right).
$$

At $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms, $r_\text{add} = 10$ Hz gives a per-step extra-spike probability of $10^{-3}$. The clamp is for binary cleanliness — the process is Bernoulli, not additive integer. Silent slots become 1 with probability $r_\text{add} \Delta t / 1000$. Add injects spikes at Poisson statistics independent of cycle phase. Sweep $r_\text{add} \in \{0, 2, \dots, 40\}$ Hz, 21 levels.

The per-step RNG is a CPU torch.Generator seeded at *EVAL_SEED + 1*, separate from the input encoder so the Poisson input stream matches the unperturbed baseline. Total: 2 models × (11 drop + 21 add) = 64 forward passes.

<Figure id="Figure 8" title="Hidden-spike perturbation" src="/figures/notebooks/nb024/perturbation_curves.png" alt="Test accuracy vs p(drop) (left) and vs Poisson-add rate (right) per model.">
  Accuracy under spike drop (left) and Poisson add (right).
</Figure>

Drop and add are asymmetric. Under drop, PING holds 73% at $p_\text{drop} = 0.9$; the readout works at a tenth of PING's natural rate because drop preserves the cycle. Under add, PING's accuracy falls across a sharp 4–10 Hz cliff: 77% at 4 Hz, 52% at 6 Hz, 29% at 8 Hz, 14% at 10 Hz, chance from 12 Hz on. COBA stays at 82–84% across the same range — without a cycle to corrupt, extra spikes just add to a uniform mean.

This suggests the rate floor is dynamical, not informational. Test: lengthening $\tau_\text{GABA}$ should stretch the cycle and lower the floor; shortening should raise it.

## Summary

PING reaches COBA's accuracy at an order of magnitude lower rate. The rate floor is the loop's preferred cycle period, $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA}$, not what the task requires. The readout phase-locks to that cycle, tolerates rate changes that preserve phase (drop, $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep, threshold shift) and collapses on changes that corrupt it (add). $\theta_u$ has no leverage below the cycle's cadence — the floor is physical, not optimised.
