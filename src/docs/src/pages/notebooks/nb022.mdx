## Introduction

**Dale's law**, in this codebase, is the constraint that the *trainable* feedforward weight matrix $W_{\text{ff}}$ stays non-negative: an "excitatory" input projection genuinely excites the post-synaptic cell rather than being free to switch sign mid-training. It is the default for the *COBA* and *ping* models — and it is the biological assumption that distinguishes them from a "vanilla" SNN where every weight is freely signed.

This entry trains the [COBA](/notebooks/nb011/) recipe twice — once with the default *--dales-law*, once with *--no-dales-law* — and compares accuracy, firing rate, and the resulting distribution of trained weights. Every other knob (lr, w_in, surrogate slope, readout, batch size, seed) is held fixed at the calibrated [nb011](/notebooks/nb011/) recipe, so any difference is attributable to the constraint alone.

The recurrent E-I matrices ($W^{EE}$, $W^{EI}$, $W^{IE}$ inside COBANet) are fixed buffers either way — Dale's law on them is just the initial-condition sign convention. This experiment therefore isolates the *trainable* feedforward effect.

## Method

Two cells, both running the [COBA](/articles/art003/) recipe:

- *COBA/dales* — `--dales-law` (default). The trainable $W_{\text{ff}}$ is clamped non-negative at every forward pass.
- *COBA/no_dales* — `--no-dales-law`. Signed weights allowed everywhere.

## Discussion

*To fill in after first run.* Two things to look for:

1. **Accuracy gap.** Does relaxing Dale's law buy any task performance? If the constraint is just "free parameters thrown away", we'd expect signed weights to do slightly better; if the COBA dynamics actually *use* the sign-fixing (e.g. the cumulative-membrane readout interacts with E vs I), the gap could go the other way.
2. **Weight-distribution shape.** Under Dale's law, the histogram should be a hard right-half of a distribution. Without it, the distribution should be roughly symmetric — if it isn't, that tells us the optimiser has discovered an effectively-Dale solution on its own.
