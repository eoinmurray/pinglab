## Abstract

Trains [COBA](/articles/ar003/) twice — once with Dale's law on the trainable $W_\text{ff}$ matrix, once off — every other knob held fixed, so any difference is attributable to the constraint alone. The recurrent E–I matrices are fixed buffers either way, so the experiment isolates the trainable-feedforward effect of forcing input projections non-negative.

## Methods

Two cells, both running the [COBA](/articles/ar003/) recipe:

- *COBA/dales* — `--dales-law` (default). The trainable $W_{\text{ff}}$ is clamped non-negative at every forward pass.
- *COBA/no_dales* — `--no-dales-law`. Signed weights allowed everywhere.

- **Recipe.** *--ei-strength=0* (no active I-loop), *--readout=mem-mean*, *--readout-w-out-scale=100*, *--lr=0.0004*, *--w-in=0.3*, *--surrogate-slope=1*, *--batch-size=256*.
- **Same seed (42).** The only difference between the two runs is the flag.

## Discussion

*To fill in after first run.* Two things to look for:

1. **Accuracy gap.** Does relaxing Dale's law buy any task performance? If the constraint is just "free parameters thrown away", we'd expect signed weights to do slightly better; if the COBA dynamics actually *use* the sign-fixing (e.g. the cumulative-membrane readout interacts with E vs I), the gap could go the other way.
2. **Weight-distribution shape.** Under Dale's law, the histogram should be a hard right-half of a distribution. Without it, the distribution should be roughly symmetric — if it isn't, that tells us the optimiser has discovered an effectively-Dale solution on its own.
