## Introduction

Pinglab notebooks pull from a small palette of chart types — line, bar, scatter, histogram with theoretical overlay, heatmap, raster. This entry generates one canonical example of each on synthetic data, so the *style* (typography, spacing, colors, axis treatment, gridlines) can be iterated on independently of the science. Whatever this page settles on becomes the brand-aligned baseline that every other notebook inherits.

## Method

Six plots, one per chart type, written with raw matplotlib defaults as the starting point. Data is synthetic and seeded so the plots are reproducible across runs. The renderer is *src/pinglab/notebooks/nb001.py*; figures land in *src/docs/public/figures/notebooks/nb001/*.

## Results

<Figure id="Figure 1" title="Line — training curves" src="/figures/notebooks/nb001/line.png" alt="Two overlaid line plots showing accuracy climbing then plateauing across 40 epochs">
  Two-series line plot with a legend. Used for training curves, sweep curves, time-series of any scalar metric. The 5-color categorical cycle handles up to ~5 distinct series before legibility falls apart.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 2" title="Line — many series (sweep)" src="/figures/notebooks/nb001/line_many.png" alt="Twelve overlaid line plots colored by a sequential brand colormap, with a colorbar encoding learning rate">
  When the number of series exceeds the categorical cycle (here, 12 learning rates across a sweep), drop the cycle and use the brand colormap as a sequential scale. Brightness encodes the parameter, a colorbar replaces the legend, and the visual story becomes "how does this metric vary along the swept axis" rather than "which series is which."
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 3" title="Bar — grouped categorical" src="/figures/notebooks/nb001/bar.png" alt="Grouped bar chart with three series across four categorical bins, dashed chance line">
  Three-series grouped bars over four categorical positions. Used for per-window or per-condition comparisons across a small set of models.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 4" title="Scatter — two continuous variables" src="/figures/notebooks/nb001/scatter.png" alt="Scatter plot with size-encoded points showing a linear correlation">
  Scatter with a third variable encoded by marker size. Used for correlations, latency-vs-accuracy, rate-vs-accuracy.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 5" title="Histogram — data + theoretical overlay" src="/figures/notebooks/nb001/histogram.png" alt="Histogram of speeds with a smooth theoretical curve overlaid">
  Histogram with the analytical reference curve overlaid. Used whenever a measured distribution should be compared to its equilibrium shape (Maxwell–Boltzmann, Poisson, exponential ISI, etc.).
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 6" title="Heatmap — 2D parameter sweep" src="/figures/notebooks/nb001/heatmap.png" alt="Color-coded matrix showing accuracy across train-dt and eval-dt combinations">
  Heatmap of a scalar over two parameter axes, with a colorbar. Used for dt-stability matrices, hyperparameter sweeps, confusion-style summaries.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 7" title="Raster — spike trains" src="/figures/notebooks/nb001/raster.png" alt="Sparse scatter raster of spike events across 200 neurons over 1 second">
  Scatter raster of spike events over time. Used for input encoders, hidden-layer responses, every dynamics-level visualisation across the codebase.
</Figure>
