## Abstract

One network ([COBANet](/articles/ar003/)) can run as the gamma rhythm **PING** or as the [Vreeswijk-Sompolinsky](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.274.5293.1724) **asynchronous-irregular** state. Harmonising the parameters one at a time leaves a single control: the **correlation of the input** across cells. Gamma turns on continuously as that correlation rises — at matched firing rate — with the recurrent connectivity only modulating how much is needed.

## Methods

### Model differences

#### Connectivity

The sparsity applies to the recurrent E↔I matrices — $W^{EI}$ (E→I) and $W^{IE}$ (I→E), the only recurrent couplings present here ($W^{EE} = 0$, and $W^{II}$ is off after harmonisation). Each is either **dense** or **sparse with fixed fan-in**: every postsynaptic cell draws exactly $K$ presynaptic partners at random from its $N$ candidates, at connection probability

$$
p = \frac{K}{N} = 1 - s,
$$

where:

- $s$ — the sparsity (fraction of entries zeroed); $s = 0.99$ for the V&S cell;
- $N$ — number of candidate presynaptic cells (the source population);
- $K = pN$ — presynaptic inputs per postsynaptic cell (fixed fan-in);
- $p$ — connection probability.

Note that the sparsity fixes $p$, not $K$: at $s = 0.99$ the fan-in is $K \approx 10$ onto each I cell (from $N_E = 1024$ excitatory sources) but $K \approx 3$ onto each E cell (from $N_I = 256$). What is common across both matrices is $p \approx 0.01$.

What decides whether a population can synchronise is how much *recurrent* input two cells hold in common. For two postsynaptic cells each drawing $K$ of the same $N$ sources, the number of shared presynaptic partners is hypergeometric, with mean and shared fraction

$$
\langle n_\text{shared}\rangle = \frac{K^2}{N},
\qquad
\frac{\langle n_\text{shared}\rangle}{K} = \frac{K}{N} = p .
$$

So the fraction of its recurrent drive that any cell holds in common with another is exactly $p$, and the pairwise correlation of the recurrent input scales with it:

- **Dense** ($p = O(1)$): two cells receive almost the same recurrent input. That shared fluctuation is what lets the E↔I loop *phase-lock* — cells rise and fall together and settle on one rhythm.
- **Sparse fixed-$K$** ($K \approx 10$, $N \approx 10^3 \Rightarrow p \approx 0.01$): cells share essentially no presynaptic partners. Each cell's recurrent input is nearly private, the pairwise correlation is of order $p \approx 1\%$, and there is no common signal to cohere on.

This makes connectivity the *internal* counterpart of the input knob: sparse $K$ decorrelates the **recurrent** input exactly as per-cell independent streams decorrelate the **external** input. Both starve the population of the shared drive it needs to synchronise — which is why relaxing *either* one is enough to leave the gamma state. (V&S keep the *balance* intact under this dilution by scaling the weights $\propto 1/\sqrt{K}$, so the mean recurrent drive stays $O(\sqrt{K})$ while its fluctuations stay $O(1)$.)

#### Input

Both regimes are driven by Poisson spike trains converted to a decaying AMPA conductance — the same *kind* of input. They differ in the **correlation of that drive across cells**. The external excitatory conductance onto E cell $j$ obeys

$$
\tau_E \,\dot g^{\text{ext}}_j(t) = -\,g^{\text{ext}}_j(t) \;+\;
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{N_\text{in}} W^{\text{in}}_{jk}\, s_k(t) & \text{(PING)} \\[1.4em]
g_0\, \xi_j(t) & \text{(V\&S AI)}
\end{cases}
$$

where:

- $g^{\text{ext}}_j(t)$ — external excitatory conductance onto E cell $j$;
- $\tau_E$ — AMPA decay time constant (2 ms), common to both;
- $s_k(t) = \sum_m \delta(t - t_k^m)$ — spike train of **shared** input neuron $k$ (Poisson, 20 Hz), read by every E cell;
- $W^{\text{in}}_{jk}$ — dense input weight from input neuron $k$ to E cell $j$; $N_\text{in} = 1024$;
- $\xi_j(t) = \sum_m \delta(t - t_j^m)$ — a **private** Poisson train (45 Hz) unique to cell $j$;
- $g_0$ — conductance increment per private spike (0.38 μS).

The consequence is the cross-cell correlation of the drive:

$$
\operatorname{Corr}\!\big(g^{\text{ext}}_j,\, g^{\text{ext}}_{j'}\big)
\;>\; 0 \ \ \text{(PING, shared layer)},
\qquad
\operatorname{Corr}\!\big(g^{\text{ext}}_j,\, g^{\text{ext}}_{j'}\big)
\;=\; 0 \ \ \text{(V\&S AI, private streams)},
\qquad j \neq j'.
$$

The shared layer is a *common reference* the loop can lock onto; the private streams kick each cell independently, with no common phase. (The drive *rate*, 20 vs 45 Hz, is an incidental magnitude — it sets the operating rates, not the qualitative state.) What both share: neither input carries any rhythm. Both are featureless, steady Poisson (Figure 1), so PING's gamma is generated by the network, not inherited from the drive.

### Harmonising connectivity

The two endpoints above differ in *both* connectivity and input, so neither is yet pinned as the switch. To separate them we sweep the recurrent connection probability $p$ on the shared, harmonised weights (no $W^{II}$) and, at each $p$, drive the network *both* ways — shared correlated input and per-cell independent input — scoring each with the lobe–trough contrast of the E autocorrelogram ([nb054](/notebooks/nb054/)'s rhythmicity metric, 0 = asynchronous, → 1 = sharp volleys).

Red squares, per-cell independent input: flat near zero (0.004 to 0.023) across the whole range.">
  Lobe–trough contrast against connection probability $p$, for shared (black) and per-cell independent (red) input on the harmonised weights. Independent input never gammas at any $p$ (contrast ≤ 0.02). Shared input is asynchronous below $p \approx 0.05$, crosses at $p \approx 0.1$ (contrast 0.50), and saturates by $p \approx 0.2$ (0.98). E firing rate stays ≈ 6–13 Hz across the whole sweep, so the contrast reads rhythm, not rate.

So there is a **critical connection probability $p^* \approx 0.1$**: above it, correlated input produces gamma and independent input does not — the **input correlation alone switches the state**. The original V&S cell ($K \approx 10$, $p \approx 0.01$) sits *below* $p^*$, which is why it stays asynchronous whatever the input. This **harmonises connectivity**: fix $p$ anywhere above $p^*$ — say a cortically realistic $p \approx 0.2$ — and both regimes share it, leaving **input correlation as the single control parameter**. The earlier "gamma needs both" was an artefact of placing the V&S endpoint at $p = 0.01$, below the threshold; once connectivity clears $p^*$ it drops out of the switch entirely.

### Correlation, not rate

One objection remains: the shared and independent conditions also differ in drive *rate* (20 vs 45 Hz), so perhaps the rhythm tracks rate, not correlation. It does not. Fixing $p = 0.2$ and sweeping the drive level for both inputs so their firing rates overlap, the contrast separates cleanly by *correlation* at every matched rate (Figure 4): shared input holds contrast ≥ 0.70 across E rates of 7.7–10.3 Hz, while independent input stays ≤ 0.04 across the same 6.3–9.1 Hz band. Same firing rate, opposite rhythm — the switch is the input correlation, not the rate.

### The phase diagram

The binary sweep (Figure 3) used the dense shared *layer* as its correlated input — a fixed, moderate correlation. To map the full landscape we replace it with a clean correlation knob $c$: split each cell's drive into a shared fraction $c$ (one common stream broadcast to all cells) and a private fraction $1-c$ at the same per-spike conductance, so the mean *and* variance of the drive are held fixed and only the cross-cell correlation varies ($c = 0$ independent, $c = 1$ fully shared). Sweeping the $p \times c$ plane and scoring each cell with the lobe–trough contrast gives the phase diagram (Figure 5).

<Figure id="Figure 5" title="Phase diagram over connectivity × input correlation" src="/figures/notebooks/nb050/phase_diagram.png" alt="Heatmap of lobe–trough contrast (magma, 0 to 1) over a 4×5 grid: connection probability p (y, 0.05 / 0.10 / 0.20 / 0.50) versus input correlation c (x, 0.00 / 0.25 / 0.50 / 0.75 / 1.00), every cell labelled. The c = 0 column is ≈ 0.01–0.02 (black) at all p. Contrast rises steadily left to right along c, reaching 1.00 (pale) in the c = 1 column at every p. Variation down each column (with p) is small: e.g. at c = 0.25 the values are 0.36, 0.42, 0.50, 0.56 for p = 0.05 to 0.50.">
  Lobe–trough contrast over the connectivity ($p$) × input-correlation ($c$) plane on the harmonised weights. The gradient is almost entirely horizontal: contrast is set by $c$, with $p$ only weakly modulating the intermediate columns.
</Figure>

The gradient is overwhelmingly horizontal — **input correlation $c$ is the dominant control**. At every $p$ the contrast climbs monotonically from ≈ 0 ($c = 0$, independent) to 1 ($c = 1$, fully shared), while connectivity $p$ only nudges the intermediate columns (at $c = 0.25$, contrast rises from 0.36 at $p = 0.05$ to 0.56 at $p = 0.5$). So there is no hard connectivity threshold: gamma turns on *continuously* with correlation, and $p$ merely sets how much correlation is needed. The apparent "critical $p$" of Figure 3 was a slice at the shared layer's fixed, moderate correlation; once the correlation itself is free to vary, it is $c$ that carries the network across — at any $p$ in this range. (E rate holds at ≈ 8–11 Hz for $c \le 0.75$, so the rise is not a rate effect; only the fully coherent $c = 1$ column lifts the rate to ≈ 17 Hz, as synchrony feeds back on firing.)

## Next steps

The connectivity sweep, the rate-matched control, and the phase diagram settle the headline: the input correlation $c$ is the single control parameter — gamma turns on continuously with it, at matched rate, with connectivity only modulating the gain. What remains stress-tests it:

1. **Re-check with a stronger PING.** This harmonised loop is a weak rhythm (cross-correlation 0.047); the position of the $c$-gradient (and any residual $p$-dependence) may shift for a more strongly coupled loop, so repeat the $p \times c$ sweep against the original $\alpha_{EI} = 1.5$ PING.
2. **Rate-match the $c = 1$ corner.** The contrast gradient holds at fixed rate up to $c = 0.75$, but the fully coherent $c = 1$ column also lifts the firing rate (≈ 17 Hz); a drive-level control along $c$ would confirm the saturation there is correlation, not the rate rise it induces.

The throughline is the transient, damped-oscillator reading: cortex most likely *amplifies* a gamma resonance for a few cycles rather than switching attractors, so — with connectivity fixed above $p^*$ — the boundary is the line a stimulus-driven burst of correlated input carries the network across, and back.
