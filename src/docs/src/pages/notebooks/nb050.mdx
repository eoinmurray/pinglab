## Abstract

Cortical neurons fire irregularly — their spike trains look almost like random (Poisson) processes, even though a single neuron driven by steady current fires like clockwork. Vreeswijk & Sompolinsky's 1996 *Science* paper explained why: in a recurrent network where strong excitation and inhibition roughly cancel, what's left over is *fluctuations*, and cells fire when those fluctuations randomly push them over threshold. This entry is a companion to that paper. It states the balance argument quantitatively, then reproduces and tests each of its predictions on a conductance-based PING network — the same network this collection uses to make gamma — using PING itself as the rhythmic foil. We confirm the cancellation of large opposing currents, the *linear* dependence of rates on drive (the paper's signature paradox), fluctuation-driven Poisson firing, asynchrony, deterministic chaos, and invariance under the $1/\sqrt K$ synaptic scaling. The same architecture gives two completely different regimes depending only on how it's wired and driven.

## The balance argument

A leaky integrate-and-fire neuron given a constant supra-threshold current is a metronome: it charges to threshold, fires, resets, repeats — perfectly regular, CV (coefficient of variation of inter-spike intervals) = 0. Real cortical neurons have CV ≈ 1, the value you'd get from a Poisson process. Where does the randomness come from? V&S's answer is the **balance of strong recurrent excitation and inhibition**. Here is the argument made quantitative — the part the simulations below put to the test.

**The scaling.** Put $N$ neurons in a sparse recurrent network where each cell receives $K$ excitatory and $K$ inhibitory inputs, $1 \ll K \ll N$. The crucial choice is that each synapse has strength of order $J/\sqrt K$ with $J = O(1)$. A cell's recurrent input is then a sum of $\approx K$ terms each $O(1/\sqrt K)$, so

$$
\text{mean} \;=\; K \cdot \tfrac{J}{\sqrt K}\, r \;=\; J\sqrt K\, r \;=\; O(\sqrt K),
\qquad
\text{std} \;=\; \sqrt K \cdot \tfrac{J}{\sqrt K}\,\sqrt r \;=\; O(1).
$$

The mean recurrent drive is *huge* ($O(\sqrt K)$); the fluctuation around it is merely *order one*.

**The cancellation.** A neuron's firing rate is $O(1)$ — it is neither silent nor saturated — so its *net* mean input must also be $O(1)$. But the excitatory and inhibitory means are each $O(\sqrt K)$. The only way their difference can be small is if they **cancel to leading order**:

$$
\underbrace{(\text{excitatory mean})}_{O(\sqrt K)} \;-\; \underbrace{(\text{inhibitory mean})}_{O(\sqrt K)} \;=\; O(1).
$$

No tuning is required: if excitation gets ahead, rates rise, inhibition grows to match, and the surplus is quenched — the balance is a *stable fixed point* of the rate dynamics. (It exists only when inhibition can dominate; a network without strong enough recurrent inhibition saturates or falls silent instead.)

**The rate law — the surprising part.** Imposing the leading-order cancellation on *both* populations gives two linear *balance equations* for the mean rates, here with external drive $r_0$ and the network's couplings $J_{\cdot\cdot}$ (ours has no E→E, $J_{EE}=0$):

$$
J_{E0}\,r_0 \;-\; J_{EI}\,r_I \;=\; 0,
\qquad
J_{I0}\,r_0 \;+\; J_{IE}\,r_E \;-\; J_{II}\,r_I \;=\; 0.
$$

This $2\times2$ linear system solves to rates that are a **linear function of the external input**, even though every unit is a hard nonlinear threshold device. The balanced network turns nonlinear neurons into a linear input–output map — V&S's signature result, tested directly in [§2](#2-rates-are-linear-in-the-drive).

**Where the irregularity comes from.** After the means cancel, what a cell actually feels is the $O(1)$ residue: a mean parked near threshold plus an $O(1)$ Gaussian fluctuation. Every spike is a chance upward excursion of that noise across threshold. Firing becomes threshold-crossing of noise — approximately Poisson, CV → 1. The same cancellation that linearises the network in the mean makes it *irregular* in time.

**What the theory predicts** — and what the rest of this entry checks, one section each:

1. **Cancellation** — the excitatory and inhibitory mean currents are individually large and nearly equal.
2. **Linear response** — population rates are linear in the external drive.
3. **Fluctuation-driven** — the fluctuation amplitude is comparable to or larger than the gap from mean drive to threshold.
4. **Irregular & asynchronous** — ISI CV → 1; pairwise correlations → 0 (the shared input is cancelled by tracking E/I; Renart et al. 2010).
5. **Chaotic** — a tiny perturbation grows (positive Lyapunov exponent).
6. **Scale-invariant** — because the scaling is $J/\sqrt K$, the state survives $K \to \infty$; its statistics are $K$-invariant.

**From binary to spiking.** V&S used *binary* (0/1) neurons with a stochastic threshold update, but the argument above is only about means and variances of inputs and is indifferent to that choice. Brunel (2000) carried the regime to spiking *LIF* neurons and Renart et al. (2010) to *conductance-based* synapses. We use COBANet — conductance-based LIF — so the per-spike weights play the role of $J/\sqrt K$ and the f-I curve plays the role of V&S's gain function. The question is whether the same balanced, irregular, chaotic state appears on an architecture built for something else.

## Building it

We run two cells of the same network ([COBANet](/articles/ar003/), $N_E = 1024$, $N_I = 256$, $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms, $T = 1000$ ms): a **PING control** (the gamma-rhythmic regime, our foil) and a **V&S AI** target. They differ only in connectivity and drive:

| | PING (control) | V&S AI |
|---|---|---|
| Recurrent connectivity | dense | fixed fan-in: exactly $K \approx 10$ inputs per cell |
| $I \to I$ coupling ($W^{II}$) | none | $\mathcal{N}(0.4, 0.12)$ μS |
| $W^{EI}$ / $W^{IE}$ | biophysical PING defaults | $\mathcal{N}(0.6, 0.18)$ / $\mathcal{N}(3.0, 0.9)$ μS |
| External drive | 20 Hz Poisson through $W_\text{in}$ | per-cell *independent* Poisson on E (45 Hz) and I (8 Hz), bypassing $W_\text{in}$ |

Three ingredients are what the PING runs never use, each mapping directly onto a V&S assumption:

- **An $I \to I$ matrix.** PING has no inhibitory self-coupling; V&S needs it so the inhibitory population doesn't fire in synchronous bursts (which would re-establish a rhythm).
- **Fixed-$K$ connectivity.** Every cell draws *exactly* $K$ presynaptic inputs (V&S's assumption that all cells are statistically equivalent). The obvious alternative — zeroing each weight independently with probability $1 - K/N$ — gives a *binomial* fan-in that varies cell to cell (mean 10, but range 2–21), which broadens the rate distribution. Switching to fixed-$K$ cut the spread of per-cell rates from CV $0.79$ to $0.33$.
- **Per-cell independent drive on both populations.** Each cell gets its own private Poisson noise stream into its excitatory conductance — the uncorrelated external input V&S's cells receive. (Driving only E and letting I inherit noise through $W^{EI}$ leaves the I cells too correlated; both populations need their own.)

The two regimes at a glance, before the diagnostics:

| | PING | V&S AI |
|---|---|---|
| $\langle r_E\rangle, \langle r_I\rangle$ | 3.6, 31 Hz | 15, 23 Hz |
| Raster | gamma bands | scattered |
| Welch PSD | sharp 31 Hz peak | broadband, no peak |

## Testing V&S's predictions

Each subsection takes one prediction from the balance argument and checks it against the PING foil.

| Prediction | PING | V&S AI | V&S says |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cancellation** — net / excitatory mean current | ≈ 0 (clock) | **0.24** (balance) | large opposing means |
| **Linear response** — $r_{E,I}$ vs drive, fit $R^2$ | — | **0.995 / 0.992** | linear |
| **Fluctuation-driven** — median $\eta$ | 5.5 (a clock) | **20** | $\gtrsim 1$ (noise) |
| **Irregular** — median ISI CV | 0.61 / 0.02 (E/I) | **1.02 / 1.12** | → 1 |
| **Asynchronous** — peak \|pairwise C(τ)\| | 0.047 (gamma comb) | **0.009** (flat) | → 0 |
| **Chaotic** — divergence after ε kick | returns to 0 each cycle | **sustained ≈ 6 cells** | grows ($\lambda > 0$) |
| **$\sqrt K$-invariant** — median CV across $K$ | — | **0.96–1.02** ($K = 10$–40) | $K$-invariant |

### 1. Excitation and inhibition cancel

The mechanism, made visible. We decompose each E cell's membrane current $I_\text{tot}(t) = g_E(E_e - V) + g_I(E_i - V) + g_L(E_L - V)$ into its excitatory part $I_E = g_E(E_e - V)$ and inhibitory part $I_I = g_I(E_i - V)$. The balance argument predicts $\langle I_E\rangle$ and $|\langle I_I\rangle|$ are each large and nearly equal, leaving a small net that sits near the threshold current $I_\text{th} = g_L(V_\text{th} - E_L) = 0.75$ nA (Figure 3). They are: $\langle I_E\rangle \approx 2.5$ nA, $|\langle I_I\rangle| \approx 2.2$ nA, net $\approx 0.6$ nA — the two big opposing currents cancel to a residue a quarter their size. With $K \approx 10$ the cancellation is partial (V&S's is exact as $K \to \infty$; see [§6](#6-invariance-under-k-scaling)), but the structure is unmistakable.

A caution this figure makes honest: cancellation of the *time-mean* is not by itself the balanced state. PING's currents also average to a small net (its mean drive is near zero too) — what differs is the *character of the leftover*. In PING the residue is the deterministic gamma clock; in the AI cell it is genuinely random noise. The remaining sections are what separate the two.

### 2. Rates are linear in the drive

The balance equations predict that population rates are a *linear* function of the external input — the counterintuitive heart of the paper, since the units are sharp nonlinear thresholds. We sweep the external Poisson drive (scaling both the E and I streams together) and read off $r_E$ and $r_I$. Both rise dead straight: linear fits give $R^2 = 0.995$ ($r_E$) and $0.992$ ($r_I$) across a near-threefold change in drive (Figure 4). The slopes are set by the weight matrix through the balance equations — $r_I$ tracks the external drive directly, $r_E$ through the inhibitory feedback loop — which is why the two lines have different gradients. Nonlinear neurons, linear network: V&S's signature result, on a spiking conductance-based substrate.

### 3. Fluctuation-driven firing

If the means cancel, firing must be driven by the *fluctuations*. We measure this per cell from the total current $I_\text{tot}(t)$: its time-mean $\mu_I$, its time-std $\sigma_I$, and the ratio

$$
\eta = \frac{\sigma_I}{|I_\text{th} - \mu_I|},
$$

the fluctuation amplitude relative to the gap from the mean to the threshold current. $\eta \gg 1$ means noise dwarfs that gap — fluctuation-driven firing. The AI cell has median $\eta \approx 20$: every cell sits with its mean drive near threshold and fluctuations many times larger (Figure 6), and here the fluctuations are genuinely random (independent Poisson), which is what makes the firing Poisson. PING also clears $\eta \gtrsim 1$ (Figure 5) — but, as §1 warned, its "fluctuations" are the deterministic gamma pump, not noise; the spectrum and correlogram are what expose the difference.

### 4. Irregular and asynchronous

These two read directly off the rasters (Figures 1 and 2). **Irregularity** is the ISI-CV histogram (panel 4): PING's E cells are regular (CV 0.61) and its I cells almost perfectly periodic (0.02, a metronome), while the AI cell's E and I CVs both sit at ≈ 1 — Poisson. **Asynchrony** is the cross-correlogram (panel 5): PING shows a comb of peaks at multiples of the gamma period (cells that fire together this cycle fire together next cycle), whereas the AI correlogram is flat noise with a 5× smaller peak (0.009 vs 0.047) — cells fire essentially independently. The near-zero correlation despite heavy *shared* recurrent input is the active-decorrelation result of Renart et al. (2010): tracking E and I currents cancel the common drive.

### 5. Chaos

The sharpest test, and the one that separates "balanced and noisy" from "balanced and chaotic." Clone the network, perturb every membrane voltage by a tiny ε = 2 mV at $t = 0$, and rerun on *identical frozen input* (same Poisson stream, same seed). Track $D(t)$ = the number of E cells whose spike differs between the two copies. A chaotic network amplifies the perturbation; a stable one forgets it (Figure 7).

<Figure id="Figure 7" title="Spike-train divergence after an ε = 2 mV perturbation" src="/figures/notebooks/nb050/lyapunov_divergence.png" alt="Line plot of spike-train divergence D(t) vs time, 0–1000 ms. Grey (PING): a periodic train of pulses that spike up to about 6 cells once per gamma cycle and return to exactly 0 at every trough between cycles. Red (V&S AI): rises within about 100 ms to a sustained fluctuating level around 6 cells and stays there for the full second, never returning to 0.">
  **PING forgets the kick; AI does not.** PING (grey) is a stable limit cycle: the perturbation flares for an instant at each gamma burst but returns to *exactly zero* at every trough between cycles — it never accumulates ($\lambda \leq 0$). The AI cell (red) saturates at a sustained ≈ 6-cell divergence and never returns to zero — the perturbation grows and persists, the signature of positive $\lambda$ (chaos).
</Figure>

The divergence saturates at a modest level (a handful of cells out of 1024) because the strong shared input partly *entrains* both copies — strongly-driven neurons fire reliably regardless of network state (the Mainen-Sejnowski effect). So our cell is "noise-entrained with chaos on top" rather than V&S's strongly-autonomous chaos. But the qualitative split is unambiguous: PING's perturbation vanishes to exactly zero every cycle; AI's persists.

### 6. Invariance under √K scaling

The balance argument hangs on one structural choice — synapses $\propto 1/\sqrt K$ — and predicts the state is therefore a well-defined *regime*, not a tuned operating point: its statistics should survive $K \to \infty$. We test this by varying $K$ (through the connection sparsity at fixed $N$) while rescaling every recurrent weight by $1/\sqrt K$. The irregularity holds: median ISI CV stays at ≈ 1 across $K = 10$–40 (0.96–1.02), and pairwise correlations stay flat and low (Figure 8). Only at $K = 5$ does it drift (CV ≈ 1.17) — the finite-$K$ edge, where too few inputs per cell undermine the central-limit argument that makes the leftover Gaussian. The state lives on the balanced manifold the $\sqrt K$ scaling defines, exactly as the theory requires.

## How faithful is it?

All six predictions of the balance argument reproduce on a conductance-based LIF network — cancellation, linear response, fluctuation-driven Poisson firing, asynchrony, chaos, and $\sqrt K$-invariance — side-by-side with a PING control that fails every one. Two honest caveats remain:

- **A near-invariant regime, not a verified $N \to \infty$ limit.** §6 shows the statistics are $K$-invariant under $1/\sqrt K$ scaling, but our external drive is a single per-cell Poisson stream, not V&S's *sparse* external input of $K_\text{ext}$ connections at strength $1/\sqrt K$. A fully $N$-invariant test (rates included, not just CV) needs that sparse external structure so the external mean is itself $O(\sqrt K)$ with $O(1)$ fluctuations.
- **Weak chaos, masked by input entrainment.** Because our cells are driven by strong shared Poisson noise, the perturbation can't grow far before the common input re-entrains both copies. The autonomous chaos of V&S's quenched-input network would show much larger divergence.

Both are sharpenings, not contradictions — the regime is V&S; the remaining work is making it *quantitatively* V&S.

## Next steps

1. **Sparse external drive for a true $N$-invariance test.** Replace the single per-cell Poisson stream with $K_\text{ext} \propto K$ sparse external connections at strength $1/\sqrt K$, so the external input scales like the recurrent one. Then sweep $N$ at fixed $K/N$ and confirm rates (not just CV) are $N$-invariant — proof of the balanced manifold rather than a single-$N$ operating point.
2. **Quenched-input chaos.** Replace the fresh per-timestep Poisson drive with a frozen DC offset per cell, so the Lyapunov probe measures autonomous network chaos without the input-entrainment mask.
3. **Predicted vs measured rates.** Compute the balance-equation coefficients from the weights and overlay the predicted $(r_E, r_I)$ on the linear-response sweep of [§2](#2-rates-are-linear-in-the-drive), turning the linearity check into a quantitative test of the balance equations themselves.
