## Introduction

[nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb038](/notebooks/nb038/) characterise PING in the *conductance-based* (COBA) regime, where every synapse has an exponential filter with timescale $\tau_\text{AMPA}$ or $\tau_\text{GABA}$. This entry asks the colleague's question: does **current-based** (CUBA) PING with *instant* synapses — no synaptic filtering at all, no $\tau_\text{AMPA}$, no $\tau_\text{GABA}$ — still produce the gamma dynamics, and can it be trained?

Four sections.

1. **Dynamics**: build the network from scratch (random fixed $W_\text{in}$, $W^{EI}$, $W^{IE}$) and run it forward under uniform Poisson input. The recruitment cliff and the inhibitory clamp survive.
2. **Training**: make $W_\text{in}$ and $W_\text{out}$ trainable, surrogate-gradient backprop on MNIST.
3. **No-PING ablation**: the same recipe minus the I population, to isolate what the I-loop actually contributes to the trained operating point.
4. **Why naive BPTT fails**: working through the Jacobian of the I-loop to show the per-cycle gradient compound factor and why TBPTT is the only configuration that actually converges.

## Architecture

LIF E and I populations with the same wiring as [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)'s COBA-PING, but with **instant synapses** instead of exponential conductances:

- $N_E = 1024$ excitatory neurons, $N_I = 256$ inhibitory neurons, $N_\text{in} = 784$ input channels.
- **Membrane**: $\tau_m = 20$ ms, $V_\text{rest} = 0$, $V_\text{th} = 1$, soft-subtract reset (subtract $V_\text{th} - V_\text{reset}$ on spike). $R_m = 1$ (dimensionless — all weights are in $V/\Delta t$ units).
- **Weights**: $W^{EI} \sim \mathcal{N}(1, 0.1)$ (E→I), $W^{IE} \sim \mathcal{N}(1, 0.1)$ (I→E), both clamped non-negative; $W_\text{in}$ is 95% sparse with mean $0$, std $0.5$ (trainable, signed).

The recurrent dynamics in one line each ($\Delta t = 1$ ms, $T = 200$ ms):

$$
V^E_{t+1} \;=\; V^E_t \;+\; \tfrac{\Delta t}{\tau_m}\!\left(-(V^E_t - V_\text{rest}) \;+\; R_m\!\left(x_t W_\text{in} - s^I_{t-1} W^{IE}\right)\right), \quad s^E_t = \mathbb{1}\!\left[V^E_t \geq V_\text{th}\right]
$$

$$
V^I_{t+1} \;=\; V^I_t \;+\; \tfrac{\Delta t}{\tau_m}\!\left(-(V^I_t - V_\text{rest}) \;+\; R_m\,s^E_{t-1} W^{EI}\right), \quad s^I_t = \mathbb{1}\!\left[V^I_t \geq V_\text{th}\right]
$$

Both $V^E$ and $V^I$ are floored at $V_\text{rest}$ after each step (biological: membrane can't fall below the dominant ion's reversal potential). There is no synaptic state — every presynaptic spike at $t-1$ contributes its weight to the post-synaptic current at $t$ and is gone by $t+1$.

The model is `cuba-ping` in the oscilloscope's registry; the no-PING ablation uses `cuba-noping`, identical except $W^{EI}$ and $W^{IE}$ are absent and the I population is dropped entirely.

## Section 1 — Dynamics

Fixed weights, no training. Spatially uniform Poisson input at 80 Hz per channel for the full 200 ms window.

E neurons fire in sparse, time-locked bursts; I neurons fire in narrow synchronous bursts immediately following each E burst.">
  Spike raster from one trial with fixed random weights and uniform 80 Hz Poisson input on every channel. E above (black), I below (red). The I-population shows narrow synchronous bursts at gamma-band cadence; E bursts immediately precede each I burst.

The dynamics reproduce the architectural signatures of PING: recruitment threshold, inhibitory clamp on the E rate, periodic E→I bursts. The cycle period is set by how long the network needs to recover from the previous inhibitory pulse — driven jointly by the membrane time constant $\tau_m = 20$ ms and by the time it takes the input drive to push enough E cells back above threshold for the next round of recruitment. With no synaptic filter, the only timescales are membrane decay and the recruitment latency itself.

## Section 2 — Training CUBA-PING

Make $W_\text{in}$ and $W_\text{out}$ trainable. Forward dynamics unchanged. Surrogate-gradient backprop through time on Poisson-encoded MNIST. Adam at lr $2 \times 10^{-3}$, batch 64, mem-mean output readout, TBPTT window $K = 10$.

Two design choices matter.

**Output-LIF mem-mean readout.** An output LIF layer with $\tau_\text{out} = 20$ ms integrates the hidden E spikes through $W_\text{out}$, and the logits are the time-average of its membrane:

$$
V^\text{out}_{t+1} \;=\; V^\text{out}_t \;+\; \frac{\Delta t}{\tau_\text{out}}\!\left(-V^\text{out}_t \;+\; s^E_t W_\text{out}\right), \qquad \text{logits} \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_t V^\text{out}_t \;+\; b_\text{out}.
$$

This matches the convention used by the COBA-PING baselines in [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb038](/notebooks/nb038/) and by `snntorch`'s standard examples. The output layer only integrates when hidden cells *spike* — silent hidden activity gives a flat output membrane and no class signal. This *forces* the hidden layer to spike in the trained network.

**Truncated BPTT.** With window $K = 10$ steps: every 10 timesteps the state variables ($V^E$, $V^I$, previous spikes) are detached so gradient only flows back within a 10-step window. Section 4 explains why this matters; for the figures here, the result is that the network actually trains.

## Section 3 — The CUBA-no-PING ablation

The interesting question is *why* CUBA-PING ends up at sub-Hz E rates. Two candidate explanations:

1. **The I-loop clamps the rate.** Inhibitory feedback suppresses E whenever it gets recruited; under the training pressure of cross-entropy + mem-mean readout, the optimiser finds a solution where the spikes are rare. The PING architecture is *necessary* for the sub-Hz rate.
2. **The mem-mean readout doesn't need spikes.** A continuous membrane readout can classify from $V^E$ patterns directly; the spiking machinery is incidental. The PING architecture is *not necessary* — any CUBA LIF stack would do the same thing.

To separate these, we run a control: a CUBA *no-PING* network (E-only, no $W^{EI}$, no $W^{IE}$, no I population) under the same recipe — same hidden size, same readout, same TBPTT, same MNIST subset, same hyperparameters.

In one line:

$$
V^E_{t+1} \;=\; V^E_t \;+\; \tfrac{\Delta t}{\tau_m}\!\left(-(V^E_t - V_\text{rest}) \;+\; R_m\, x_t W_\text{in}\right), \quad s^E_t = \mathbb{1}\!\left[V^E_t \geq V_\text{th}\right].
$$

  Training curves for the E-only baseline. Loss (left), test accuracy (middle), mean hidden-E rate (right). Same recipe as Section 2 minus the I-loop; the rate climbs immediately to tens of Hz and accuracy converges within a handful of epochs.

**The comparison is now clean.** Both effects of the I-loop show up:

1. **The rate-clamp is real and strong.** Removing the I-loop lets the E rate climb into the tens of Hz — biologically high but within the snnTorch / rate-coded SNN regime. With the I-loop in place, the rate is held at ≈1 Hz: an order-of-magnitude sparsification purely from the inhibitory architecture, no $L_\text{rate}$ regulariser needed.
2. **The sparsification has a real but bounded accuracy cost.** Accuracy drops by roughly 10 pp, much smaller than the catastrophic gap an earlier version of this notebook saw with an unbounded $V$ floor and an over-strong $W^{IE}$. The output-LIF readout integrates spike events, so suppressing E spikes throttles the output membrane response; the network still has enough integrated signal to classify, just less than the dense-spike control.

So the I-loop is doing exactly the work the PING story claims for it, at a price that's now competitive with the COBA-PING baselines:

- **no-PING**: high-rate dense-spike regime, tens of Hz. The output LIF integrates many spike events into a clean signal. Accuracy is good but the rate is well above cortical pyramidal background firing.
- **PING**: low-rate sparse-spike regime, ≈1 Hz. The I-loop ensures sparse firing without any explicit regulariser, and the output LIF still gets enough integrated spike signal to classify.

This is the *same* tradeoff the COBA-PING baselines in [nb035 Figure 5](/notebooks/nb035/) walk via the $\theta_u$ spike-budget term — except COBA-PING walks it by tightening an explicit penalty, while CUBA-PING walks it by the architectural inhibitory clamp. Both end up on similar frontiers; only the knob differs.

## Section 4 — Why naive BPTT diverges

The single design choice in the training section that takes some justification is the TBPTT window. With $K = 10$ the network trains; with $K = T = 200$ (full BPTT, no truncation) it doesn't — gradients overflow to NaN within the first batch. This section works out why.

### The recurrent Jacobian

The training loss $L$ depends on the hidden spike train $\{s^E_t\}$ via the output-LIF integrator. By the chain rule,

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_\text{in}} \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{\partial L}{\partial V^E_t} \cdot \frac{\partial V^E_t}{\partial W_\text{in}}
$$

with the direct contribution $\partial V^E_t / \partial W_\text{in} = \alpha R_m\, x_{t-1}^\top$ (where $\alpha = \Delta t / \tau_m$). The challenge is computing $\partial L / \partial V^E_t$, which carries the recurrent contributions — the readout sees the membrane directly, but the membrane *depends on past spikes* through the I-loop current, and spikes are computed from membrane via the surrogate-Heaviside. So the recurrent chain still routes through `s_E` and `s_I` even though the forward readout doesn't.

Look at one E→I→E round trip: how does $V^E_t$ influence $V^E_{t+2}$ via the I-loop?

$$
V^E_t \;\xrightarrow{\text{spike}}\; s^E_t \;\xrightarrow{V^I \text{ update}}\; V^I_{t+1} \;\xrightarrow{\text{spike}}\; s^I_{t+1} \;\xrightarrow{V^E \text{ update}}\; V^E_{t+2}
$$

The four Jacobian links in operator norm:

1. **Surrogate at E**: $\partial s^E_t / \partial V^E_t = \sigma'(V^E_t - V_\text{th})$ bounded by $\sigma$ (the surrogate slope, set to 1).
2. **E→I drive**: $\partial V^I_{t+1} / \partial s^E_t = \alpha R_m\, W^{EI}$ (matrix of shape $N_E \times N_I$, operator norm $\alpha R_m \|W^{EI}\|$).
3. **Surrogate at I**: $\partial s^I_{t+1} / \partial V^I_{t+1}$ bounded by $\sigma$.
4. **I→E suppression**: $\partial V^E_{t+2} / \partial s^I_{t+1} = -\alpha R_m\, W^{IE}$ (matrix of shape $N_I \times N_E$, operator norm $\alpha R_m \|W^{IE}\|$).

Multiplying through, the per-round-trip Jacobian has operator norm bounded by

$$
\boxed{\left\|\frac{\partial V^E_{t+2}}{\partial V^E_t}\right\|_{\text{loop}} \;\leq\; (\alpha R_m)^2 \, \sigma^2 \, \|W^{EI}\| \cdot \|W^{IE}\|}
$$

### Plugging in our numbers

For random weight matrices with entries $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma_w)$, the spectral (operator) norm is well-approximated by $\mu \sqrt{\min(N_\text{pre}, N_\text{post})} + O(\sigma_w \sqrt{\max(N_\text{pre}, N_\text{post})})$. For our weights:

- $W^{EI}$ is $1024 \times 256$ with $\mu = 1$, $\sigma_w = 0.1$: $\|W^{EI}\| \approx \sqrt{256} \cdot 1 + 0.1 \sqrt{1024} \approx 16 + 3 \approx 19$.
- $W^{IE}$ is $256 \times 1024$ with $\mu = 1$, $\sigma_w = 0.1$: $\|W^{IE}\| \approx \sqrt{256} \cdot 1 + 0.1 \sqrt{1024} \approx 16 + 3 \approx 19$.

With $\alpha = 1/20 = 0.05$, $R_m = 1$, $\sigma = 1$:

$$
(\alpha R_m)^2 \sigma^2 \|W^{EI}\|\, \|W^{IE}\| \;=\; (0.05)^2 \cdot 1 \cdot 19 \cdot 19 \;\approx\; 0.9
$$

just under unity — but with even moderate spectral slack the compound exceeds 1 and growth begins. With the earlier $W^{IE} \sim \mathcal{N}(2, 0.2)$ recipe the factor was ≈1.8, and over the full $T = 200$ steps that compound overflows float32 well before the trial ends. The current recipe halves $W^{IE}$ to soften suppression and bring the per-cycle factor to the edge of stability; even so, naive full-BPTT remains unreliable.

### Why truncated BPTT works

TBPTT with window $K$ caps the per-cycle compound to $K / 2$ round trips instead of $T / 2$. The per-step factor raised to $K/2 = 5$ is finite and well within float32. More importantly, the consistently-signed suppression signal is now summed over $K = 10$ steps instead of $T = 200$, so its magnitude is comparable to the readout signal and the optimiser sees a balanced gradient.

The cost is **bias**. TBPTT computes the gradient as if each window of $K$ steps were independent of every other window. This is wrong: $V^E$ at step $10$ really *does* depend on $V^E$ at step $1$ via the membrane decay. So the optimiser doesn't converge to the same local minimum that full BPTT would (if full BPTT were numerically stable). For our 200 ms MNIST trials this bias is negligible — there's no useful gradient signal hidden in dependencies more than 10 ms apart. For sequence tasks with genuine long-range structure (language modelling, music, sequence MNIST), TBPTT would underestimate long-range gradients and systematically miss those features.

So calling it "truncated" is a bit unfair. The truncation isn't a bug, it's the *regulariser* that lets you train at all. **Local-BPTT** or **gradient horizon limit** would be a more honest name.

## Discussion

The instant-synapse CUBA-PING:
- **Has the right dynamics** without any synaptic filtering: recruitment cliff, inhibitory clamp, periodic E→I bursts at the cadence set by $\tau_m$ and the recruitment latency (Figure 1).
- **Is trainable** with surrogate-gradient backprop, *if* (a) the readout is an output-LIF mem-mean that requires hidden spikes to flow, and (b) the BPTT horizon is limited to a few times $\tau_m$. The recurrent Jacobian compounds at ≈1 per round trip with this recipe; even small slack overflows float32 over the 200-step trial (Section 4).
- **Is ablation-checked**. Section 3 confirms the I-loop is doing real work — removing it raises the E rate by an order of magnitude. The accuracy cost is bounded and comparable to the COBA-PING $\theta_u$ frontier.

Both the PING and no-PING runs go through the same oscilloscope path (`oscilloscope train --model cuba-ping ...` and `--model cuba-noping ...`), so the only difference between Figures 2/3 and Figures 4/5 is the I population's presence — recipe, optimiser, surrogate gradient, TBPTT window, batching, and seed all match.

The natural follow-ups now sit one step away rather than several:

1. **Scale to full MNIST.** Training loss is still decreasing at the end of the run, so the operating point is not yet converged on the medium-tier subset. Full MNIST + more epochs should close the gap to the COBA-PING baselines.
2. **Add an explicit $L_\text{rate}$** like nb035's. This would expose a $\theta_u$ knob that lets you slide along the rate–accuracy frontier on top of what the architecture already gives. Combining it with the architectural clamp gives the most controllable recipe.
3. **Replace TBPTT with a principled stabiliser** — spectral normalisation of $W^{EI}$ / $W^{IE}$, or a Jacobian-norm regulariser. The current TBPTT bias is small but real (see Section 4); a stabiliser that lets full-horizon BPTT through would close the COBA-PING gap from below.
