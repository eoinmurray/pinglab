## Introduction

[nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb038](/notebooks/nb038/) characterise PING in the *conductance-based* (COBA) regime, where every synapse has an exponential filter with timescale $\tau_\text{AMPA}$ or $\tau_\text{GABA}$. This entry asks the colleague's question: does **current-based** (CUBA) PING with *instant* synapses — no synaptic filtering at all, no $\tau_\text{AMPA}$, no $\tau_\text{GABA}$ — still produce the gamma dynamics, and can it be trained?

Three sections.

1. **Dynamics**: build the network from scratch (random fixed $W_\text{in}$, $W^{EI}$, $W^{IE}$) and run it forward under uniform Poisson input. The recruitment cliff and the inhibitory clamp survive.
2. **Training**: make $W_\text{in}$ and $W_\text{out}$ trainable, surrogate-gradient backprop on MNIST. The forward dynamics are the same; the gradient path is what's interesting.
3. **Why naive BPTT fails**: working through the Jacobian of the I-loop to show the per-cycle gradient compound factor, plugging in our weights, and explaining why the only training configuration that actually converges is truncated BPTT.

## Architecture

LIF E and I populations with the same wiring as [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)'s COBA-PING, but with **instant synapses** instead of exponential conductances:

- $N_E = 1024$ excitatory neurons, $N_I = 256$ inhibitory neurons, $N_\text{in} = 784$ input channels.
- **Membrane**: $\tau_m = 20$ ms, $V_\text{rest} = 0$, $V_\text{th} = 1$, hard reset to $V_\text{reset} = 0$. $R_m = 1$ (dimensionless — all weights are in $V/\Delta t$ units).
- **Weights**: $W^{EI} \sim \mathcal{N}(1, 0.1)$ (E→I), $W^{IE} \sim \mathcal{N}(2, 0.2)$ (I→E), both clamped non-negative. $W_\text{in}$ is 95% sparse with mean $1.2$, std $1.2$ in the dynamics section and mean $0$, std $0.5$ in the training section (the trainable version starts smaller so gradient descent has room to grow it).

The recurrent dynamics in one line each ($\Delta t = 1$ ms, $T = 200$ ms):

$$
V^E_{t+1} \;=\; V^E_t \;+\; \tfrac{\Delta t}{\tau_m}\!\left(-(V^E_t - V_\text{rest}) \;+\; R_m\!\left(x_t W_\text{in} - s^I_{t-1} W^{IE}\right)\right), \quad s^E_t = \mathbb{1}\!\left[V^E_t \geq V_\text{th}\right]
$$

$$
V^I_{t+1} \;=\; V^I_t \;+\; \tfrac{\Delta t}{\tau_m}\!\left(-(V^I_t - V_\text{rest}) \;+\; R_m\,s^E_{t-1} W^{EI}\right), \quad s^I_t = \mathbb{1}\!\left[V^I_t \geq V_\text{th}\right]
$$

with hard reset $V \leftarrow V_\text{reset}$ whenever $s_t = 1$. There is no synaptic state — every presynaptic spike at $t-1$ contributes its weight to the post-synaptic current at $t$ and is gone by $t+1$.

## Section 1 — Dynamics

Fixed weights, no training. Spatially uniform Poisson input at 80 Hz per channel for the full 200 ms window.

E neurons fire in sparse, time-locked bursts at ~50 ms intervals; I neurons fire in narrow synchronous bursts immediately following each E burst.">
  Spike raster from one trial with fixed random weights and uniform 80 Hz Poisson input on every channel. E above, I below. E = 1.8 Hz, I = 45 Hz per cell. The I-population shows narrow synchronous bursts at ≈50 ms inter-burst interval (≈20 Hz); E bursts immediately precede each I burst.

The dynamics reproduce the architectural signatures of PING: recruitment threshold, inhibitory clamp on the E rate, periodic E→I bursts. The cycle period (≈50 ms, beta-band rather than canonical gamma) is set by how long the network needs to recover from the previous inhibitory pulse — driven jointly by the membrane time constant $\tau_m = 20$ ms and by the time it takes the input drive to push enough E cells back above threshold for the next round of recruitment. With no synaptic filter, the only timescales are membrane decay and the recruitment latency itself.

## Section 2 — Training

Make $W_\text{in}$ and $W_\text{out}$ trainable. Forward dynamics unchanged. Surrogate-gradient backprop through time on Poisson-encoded MNIST. Adam at lr $5 \times 10^{-4}$, batch 64, 30 epochs, 10 000 train / 1 000 test, single seed.

Two design choices matter.

**Output-LIF mem-mean readout.** An output LIF layer with $\tau_\text{out} = 20$ ms integrates the hidden E spikes through $W_\text{out}$, and the logits are the time-average of its membrane:

$$
V^\text{out}_{t+1} \;=\; V^\text{out}_t \;+\; \frac{\Delta t}{\tau_\text{out}}\!\left(-V^\text{out}_t \;+\; s^E_t W_\text{out}\right), \qquad \text{logits} \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_t V^\text{out}_t \;+\; b_\text{out}.
$$

This matches the convention used by the COBA-PING baselines in [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb038](/notebooks/nb038/) and by `snntorch`'s standard examples. Crucially, the output layer only integrates when hidden cells *spike* — so silent hidden activity gives a flat output membrane and no class signal. This *forces* the hidden layer to spike in the trained network.

An earlier draft of this notebook used a direct linear projection of $V^E$ (no output LIF), which trained to higher accuracy but at literally zero hidden firing rate — see [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/) for the documented counterexample. With output-LIF the spiking machinery is back in the gradient path and the trained network actually uses it.

**Truncated BPTT.** With window $K = 10$ steps: every 10 timesteps the state variables ($V^E$, $V^I$, previous spikes) are detached so gradient only flows back within a 10-step window. Section 3 explains why this matters; for the figure here, the result is that the network actually trains.

Two implementation details made the difference between the *broken-CUBA* operating point (42% / 0.7 Hz) and the working one above. **(1)** Floor $V^E$ and $V^I$ at $V_\text{rest}$ after every step. Without this floor, a single synchronous I-burst pushes $V^E$ to deeply negative values (≈−25 in these units), and the membrane takes ≈3 $\tau_m$ ≈ 60 ms of decay to recover above threshold. The floor is biological (membrane potential can't go below the dominant ion's reversal potential, $E_K \approx -90$ mV) and cuts the post-burst recovery time roughly in half. **(2)** Halve $W^{IE}$ from $\mathcal{N}(2, 0.2)$ to $\mathcal{N}(1, 0.1)$. Each I-spike now delivers half the suppressive kick, so $V^E$ doesn't dip as deep even with the floor in place. The I-loop still works (E rate stays well below the no-PING baseline) but the E population can recover and fire again on the next cycle.

## Section 3 — Why naive BPTT diverges

The single design choice in the training section that takes some justification is the TBPTT window. With $K = 10$ the network trains; with $K = T = 200$ (full BPTT, no truncation) it doesn't — gradients overflow to NaN within the first batch. This section works out why.

### The recurrent Jacobian

The training loss $L$ depends on the hidden spike train $\{s^E_t\}$ via the output-LIF integrator. By the chain rule,

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_\text{in}} \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{\partial L}{\partial V^E_t} \cdot \frac{\partial V^E_t}{\partial W_\text{in}}
$$

with the direct contribution $\partial V^E_t / \partial W_\text{in} = \alpha R_m\, x_{t-1}^\top$ (where $\alpha = \Delta t / \tau_m$). The challenge is computing $\partial L / \partial V^E_t$, which carries the recurrent contributions — the readout sees the membrane directly, but the membrane *depends on past spikes* through the I-loop current, and spikes are computed from membrane via the surrogate-Heaviside. So the recurrent chain still routes through `s_E` and `s_I` even though the forward readout doesn't.

Look at one E→I→E round trip: how does $V^E_t$ influence $V^E_{t+2}$ via the I-loop?

$$
V^E_t \;\xrightarrow{\text{spike}}\; s^E_t \;\xrightarrow{V^I \text{ update}}\; V^I_{t+1} \;\xrightarrow{\text{spike}}\; s^I_{t+1} \;\xrightarrow{V^E \text{ update}}\; V^E_{t+2}
$$

The four Jacobian links in operator norm:

1. **Surrogate at E**: $\partial s^E_t / \partial V^E_t = \sigma'(V^E_t - V_\text{th})$ bounded by $\sigma$ (the surrogate slope, set to 1).
2. **E→I drive**: $\partial V^I_{t+1} / \partial s^E_t = \alpha R_m\, W^{EI}$ (matrix of shape $N_E \times N_I$, operator norm $\alpha R_m \|W^{EI}\|$).
3. **Surrogate at I**: $\partial s^I_{t+1} / \partial V^I_{t+1}$ bounded by $\sigma$.
4. **I→E suppression**: $\partial V^E_{t+2} / \partial s^I_{t+1} = -\alpha R_m\, W^{IE}$ (matrix of shape $N_I \times N_E$, operator norm $\alpha R_m \|W^{IE}\|$).

Multiplying through, the per-round-trip Jacobian has operator norm bounded by

$$
\boxed{\left\|\frac{\partial V^E_{t+2}}{\partial V^E_t}\right\|_{\text{loop}} \;\leq\; (\alpha R_m)^2 \, \sigma^2 \, \|W^{EI}\| \cdot \|W^{IE}\|}
$$

### Plugging in our numbers

For random weight matrices with entries $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma_w)$, the spectral (operator) norm is well-approximated by $\mu \sqrt{\min(N_\text{pre}, N_\text{post})} + O(\sigma_w \sqrt{\max(N_\text{pre}, N_\text{post})})$. For our weights:

- $W^{EI}$ is $1024 \times 256$ with $\mu = 1$, $\sigma_w = 0.1$: $\|W^{EI}\| \approx \sqrt{256} \cdot 1 + 0.1 \sqrt{1024} \approx 16 + 3 \approx 19$.
- $W^{IE}$ is $256 \times 1024$ with $\mu = 2$, $\sigma_w = 0.2$: $\|W^{IE}\| \approx \sqrt{256} \cdot 2 + 0.2 \sqrt{1024} \approx 32 + 6 \approx 38$.

With $\alpha = 1/20 = 0.05$, $R_m = 1$, $\sigma = 1$:

$$
(\alpha R_m)^2 \sigma^2 \|W^{EI}\|\, \|W^{IE}\| \;=\; (0.05)^2 \cdot 1 \cdot 19 \cdot 38 \;\approx\; 1.8
$$

So per round trip the gradient norm multiplies by ≈1.8. Over the full $T = 200$ steps (≈100 round trips of 2 steps each, though in practice the gamma cycle is more like 25 ms = 25 steps so ≈8 cycles), the worst-case compound factor is

$$
(1.8)^{T/2} = (1.8)^{100} \approx 10^{25}
$$

— well past float32's max representable value of $\approx 3.4 \times 10^{38}$ but the multiplications in BPTT chain together many factors and overflow happens *during* the backward pass, not just at the end.

### Empirical confirmation

Running the backward at varying horizons, this is what we measure for the gradient norm at $W_\text{in}$:

| Horizon $T$ | max $\|\nabla_{W_\text{in}} L\|$ |
|---|---|
| 50 | $5 \times 10^{14}$ |
| 100 | $\inf$ (float32 overflow) |
| 150 | NaN |
| 200 | NaN |

At $T = 50$ the gradient norm is large but finite; somewhere past 100 the chain of matmuls overflows to $+\infty$, which then multiplied by zero (from a sparse Jacobian entry) gives NaN. Standard `clip_grad_norm_` rescales finite gradients but can't recover from inf/NaN — they survive the clip and contaminate every parameter.

### Why per-element clipping doesn't help either

Once the gradient is finite (say at $T = 50$ where the norm is $5 \times 10^{14}$), clipping should help, right? Empirically it doesn't, for a subtler reason. The I-loop gradient is *consistently signed* in the "decrease $W_\text{in}$" direction: every E spike triggers more I activity which suppresses future E spikes, so the chain rule says "if you want loss to decrease, fire less". Summed over 200 steps, this gives a coherent suppression signal much larger than the readout's "fire more on the right classes" signal. Clipping per-element bounds the magnitude but preserves the sign, so the network gets pushed toward silence anyway.

Concretely: with per-element clip at $\pm 1$ and 200 steps, the accumulated $W_\text{in}$ gradient from the I-loop path is $O(200)$ per element, all negative. The readout path contributes $O(10^{-2})$ per element. Optimiser steps push $W_\text{in}$ toward zero; the network goes silent within a few epochs; no signal remains.

### Why truncated BPTT works

TBPTT with window $K$ caps the per-cycle compound to $K / 2$ round trips instead of $T / 2$. The per-step factor of $\approx 1.8$ raised to $K/2 = 5$ is $\approx 19$ — finite, well within float32. More importantly, the consistently-signed suppression signal is now summed over $K = 10$ steps instead of $T = 200$, so its magnitude is comparable to the readout signal and the optimiser sees a balanced gradient.

The cost is **bias**. TBPTT computes the gradient as if each window of $K$ steps were independent of every other window. This is wrong: $V^E$ at step $10$ really *does* depend on $V^E$ at step $1$ via the membrane decay. So the optimiser doesn't converge to the same local minimum that full BPTT would (if full BPTT were numerically stable). For our 200 ms MNIST trials this bias is negligible — there's no useful gradient signal hidden in dependencies more than 10 ms apart. For sequence tasks with genuine long-range structure (language modelling, music, sequence MNIST), TBPTT would underestimate long-range gradients and systematically miss those features.

So calling it "truncated" is a bit unfair. The truncation isn't a bug, it's the *regulariser* that lets you train at all. **Local-BPTT** or **gradient horizon limit** would be a more honest name.

## Discussion

The instant-synapse CUBA-PING:
- **Has the right dynamics** without any synaptic filtering: recruitment cliff, inhibitory clamp, periodic E→I bursts at the cadence set by $\tau_m$ and the recruitment latency (Figure 1, beta-band rather than canonical gamma).
- **Is trainable** with surrogate-gradient backprop, *if* (a) the readout is an output-LIF mem-mean that requires hidden spikes to flow, and (b) the BPTT horizon is limited to a few times $\tau_m$. The recurrent Jacobian compounds at ≈1.8 per round trip; over 200 ms this overflows float32 (Section 3).
- **Reaches 79.6% test accuracy on 10 000 MNIST samples in 30 epochs** with E rate ≈ 1.1 Hz and I rate ≈ 28 Hz — biologically plausible rates with the inhibitory clamp doing 38× sparsification vs the no-PING control.

The PING dynamics from Figure 1 are preserved into the trained network (Figure 3): E fires sparsely, I fires in periodic bursts at the same beta-band cadence. Compared to the [CUBA-no-PING control in nb041](/notebooks/nb041/) (91.6% at 41.8 Hz E rate), the I-loop gives a clean rate–accuracy trade: 38× sparsification at the cost of ≈12 pp accuracy. The same shape of trade-off the COBA-PING baselines in [nb035 Figure 5](/notebooks/nb035/) walk via the $\theta_u$ spike-budget term — except CUBA-PING walks it by the architectural inhibitory clamp rather than by an explicit penalty.

The natural follow-ups now sit one step away rather than several:

1. **Scale to full MNIST.** Training loss is still decreasing at epoch 30 (0.95 and falling), so the operating point is not yet converged on the 10k subset. 60k samples + more epochs should push past 85% while keeping the rate near 1 Hz.
2. **Add an explicit $L_\text{rate}$** like nb035's. This would expose a $\theta_u$ knob that lets you slide along the rate–accuracy frontier on top of what the architecture already gives. Combining it with the architectural clamp gives the most controllable recipe.
3. **Replace TBPTT with a principled stabiliser** — spectral normalisation of $W^{EI}$ / $W^{IE}$, or a Jacobian-norm regulariser. The current TBPTT bias is small but real (see Section 3); a stabiliser that lets full-horizon BPTT through would close the COBA-PING gap from below.
