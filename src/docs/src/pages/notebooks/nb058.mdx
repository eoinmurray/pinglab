## Abstract

[Vreeswijk & Sompolinsky](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.274.5293.1724) predict that a strongly coupled, sparse, inhibition-dominated network with uncorrelated drive falls — with no tuning — into a *balanced* state whose hallmark is irregular (CV ≈ 1), asynchronous, rhythmless firing: large excitatory and inhibitory currents cancel, and the leftover fluctuations carry each cell across threshold. We push the conductance-based [COBANet](/articles/ar003/) into the full four-coupling version of this regime — fixed fan-in $K \approx 10$, per-cell independent drive, and all four recurrent matrices ($W^{EE}, W^{EI}, W^{IE}, W^{II}$) — and confirm the signature: Poisson-like ISIs (CV ≈ 1), near-zero pairwise correlations, and a broadband spectrum with no peak. The defining test is the coupling scaling: only under the V&S strong-coupling rule (synapse $\propto J/\sqrt K$) does the irregularity *survive* as the fan-in grows — weak coupling lets the recurrent fluctuations wash out and the cells relax toward the Poisson floor set by their external drive. Finally, with the input replaced by a *frozen* DC current (no noise of its own), a clone-and-perturb probe measures a positive Lyapunov exponent (λ ≈ +28 s⁻¹ for the balanced net vs ≈ 0 for a decoupled control): the irregularity is self-generated and the state is deterministically chaotic. (Gamma, the rhythmic alternative this same hardware can occupy, is the subject of the companion entry [nb050](/notebooks/nb050/).)

## Background

The whole entry rests on one picture. Every neuron is driven by two opposing crowds — a population of excitatory inputs pushing it toward threshold and a population of inhibitory inputs pulling it back — and the two are *strong* but nearly cancel. Because they oppose, a moderate firing rate is enough to balance them, parking the average drive right near threshold. What's left over is the *imbalance*: the two crowds never match instant to instant, and that leftover jitter is what actually carries each cell over threshold, at random moments. Firing is the crossing of a noisy near-tie, not a steady climb — so the spike train is irregular (Poisson-like, CV ≈ 1), and because each cell hears its own crowd the cells don't synchronise (asynchronous). The striking part, and V&S's deepest claim, is that this holds even when the input itself carries no noise: the jitter is *manufactured* by the network.

Two numbers govern it, and every experiment below is about them:

- **Fan-in $K$** — how many presynaptic cells make up a neuron's crowd (how many inputs it receives). The cancellation is a law-of-large-numbers effect over those $K$ inputs, so it only sharpens as $K$ grows.
- **Coupling $J$** — the strength of one synapse, measured in the scaling that keeps the jitter alive as the crowd grows: each synapse is worth $J/\sqrt K$. With that scaling the mean drive from $K$ synapses is $O(\sqrt K)$ — large, and cancels against the opposing crowd — while its fluctuation is $O(1)$ — small, and survives. That exact $1/\sqrt K$ is the load-bearing choice: scale the weights any weaker and the jitter washes out as $K \to \infty$ (the cells relax into clocks); the V&S regime is precisely the one where it does not.

**From theory to our knobs.** The simulator is not parameterised by $K$ and $J$ — it takes a connection *sparsity* $s$ and a synaptic *weight* $w$. The two map onto the theory as

$$K = (1 - s)\,N_\text{pre}, \qquad J = \sqrt{K}\,\frac{w}{K} = \frac{w}{\sqrt{K}} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad w = J\sqrt{K},$$

where:

- $s$ — connection sparsity (the *--ei-sparsity* flag): the fraction of synapses that are absent, so $1 - s$ is the probability any given pair is wired;
- $N_\text{pre}$ — size of the presynaptic pool a cell draws from ($N_E = 1024$ or $N_I = 256$);
- $K$ — fan-in: the number of inputs each cell actually receives from that pool;
- $w$ — the mean of the synaptic weight matrix in μS (the *--w-ei* etc. value; e.g. $W^{EI}$ has $w = 0.6$);
- $J$ — the $O(1)$ coupling strength of the theory.

The middle equality reflects the wiring rule: with fixed fan-in the simulator divides each synapse by $K$, so the conductance *per synapse* is $w/K$ and the *total* a cell receives, $K \cdot (w/K) = w$, is independent of $K$; multiplying by $\sqrt K$ recovers $J$. So **sparsity sets the crowd size, weight sets the coupling** — and the defining V&S move (Figure 2) is to grow $K$ by lowering $s$ while raising $w \propto \sqrt K$ so that $J$ stays put. At our baseline $s = 0.99$ the fan-in is $K = 0.01 \times 1024 \approx 10$ from the 1024-cell E pool, and only $\approx 3$ from the smaller 256-cell I pool — the inhibitory side is where the large-$K$ argument is thinnest.

## Methods

1. **Network and four-coupling V&S configuration.** [COBANet](/articles/ar003/), $N_E = 1024$, $N_I = 256$, $\Delta t = 0.25$ ms, $T = 1000$ ms. The ingredients that put it in the canonical balanced regime: **fixed fan-in** $K \approx 10$ (sparse exact-$K$, sparsity $s = 0.99$) so every cell draws exactly $K$ inputs and all cells are statistically equivalent; **all four recurrent matrices** present — $W^{EI} = \mathcal{N}(0.6, 0.18)$, $W^{IE} = \mathcal{N}(3.0, 0.9)$, $W^{II} = \mathcal{N}(0.4, 0.12)$, and $W^{EE} = \mathcal{N}(0.4, 0.12)$ μS — the complete Brunel/V&S E–I architecture; and **per-cell independent Poisson drive** (E: 45 Hz × 0.38 μS, I: 8 Hz × 0.25 μS) so the external input is uncorrelated across cells. Recurrent excitation $W^{EE}$ is included for canonical completeness — the full four-coupling architecture — though the rates are set by the fixed-$K$ balance, not by $W^{EE}$. (See [Background](#background) for how the fan-in $K$ and coupling $J$ map to the sparsity $s = 0.99$ and the weight means $w$ above.)

   One caveat on the fan-in: $K \approx 10$ is the count drawn from the 1024-cell E pool ($W^{EI}$, $W^{EE}$); because the I pool is 4× smaller, the inhibitory fan-in is only $K_I \approx 3$ ($W^{IE}$, $W^{II}$). V&S asks for $K_E$ and $K_I$ the *same order* — 4:1 qualifies — but $K_I \approx 3$ is theoretically marginal. It does not, however, distort the state: raising the inhibitory crowd to $K_I \approx 10$ (lowering $s$, with weights kept on the $J/\sqrt K$ scaling of Step 3) leaves the signatures essentially unchanged (CV_E 1.11 → 1.15, CV_I 1.20 → 1.14, both supra-Poisson).

2. **Diagnostics.** From the post-burn-in spike trains we read the per-neuron ISI coefficient of variation, the Welch spectrum of the population-mean E trace, and the mean pairwise spike-train cross-correlogram over 100 random E pairs.

3. **The $J/\sqrt K$ coupling test.** The defining V&S statement is about *strong* coupling: synaptic weight $\propto J/\sqrt K$, so the recurrent input keeps an $O(1)$ fluctuation as $K \to \infty$. Holding the nominal weight $w$ fixed (Step 1) makes the synapse $w/K \propto 1/K$ — the classical *weak*-coupling limit whose fluctuation $\propto 1/\sqrt K$ vanishes; using $w = J\sqrt K$ instead recovers synapse $= J/\sqrt K$. So at fixed $N_E = 1024$ we sweep the fan-in $K = 10 \to 160$ under two conventions — **strong** (all recurrent weights scaled $\propto \sqrt K$, and the external drive folded into the balance: rate $\propto K$, per-spike $g \propto 1/\sqrt K$, giving an $O(\sqrt K)$ mean with $O(1)$ fluctuations) and **weak** (everything held fixed) — and read off whether the irregularity survives. (At high $K$ the strong-coupling rate drifts low, so this sweep uses a 3 s trial to keep the ISI count high enough for an unbiased CV.)

4. **Direct Lyapunov exponent (frozen input).** To measure the chaos rather than infer it, we strip the input of all noise — replacing the per-cell Poisson drive with a **quenched DC conductance** (a per-cell offset drawn once and frozen for the trial, no per-timestep fluctuation) — then clone the network and run two copies on that *identical* frozen input, kicking every membrane voltage by a tiny $\varepsilon$ at $t = 0$ in the second copy and tracking the voltage distance $\lVert\Delta V(t)\rVert$. Because the input carries no noise of its own, any divergence is generated by the deterministic dynamics. We run the balanced four-coupling network and, as a control, a **decoupled** one (recurrent weights ≈ 0) at a matched rate. Spiking dynamics *contract* between spikes (leak) and *expand* only at spike-flips, so $\varepsilon$ must be large enough to flip spikes — below that, $\lVert\Delta V\rVert$ merely contracts and reports a spurious $\lambda < 0$; we use $\varepsilon = 0.1$ mV. The slope of $\log\lVert\Delta V\rVert$ in the post-flip, pre-saturation window is the largest Lyapunov exponent (five seeds each). This is the *initial-growth* estimate: $\lVert\Delta V\rVert$ saturates at the attractor size, and a precise asymptotic $\lambda$ would need a renormalised Benettin scheme — but the sign, positive vs ≈ 0, is the textbook criterion and is unambiguous.

## Next steps

The network is the full four-coupling Brunel/V&S architecture; Figure 1 confirms the balanced asynchronous-irregular signatures, Figure 2 shows the irregularity survives the $K \to \infty$ limit only under strong $J/\sqrt K$ coupling, and Figure 3 measures a positive Lyapunov exponent under noise-free frozen input — the state is self-generated and deterministically chaotic. Two refinements remain.

1. **Hold the rate as $K$ grows.** Figure 2's caveat: under strong coupling $r_E$ drifts down with $K$ (17.5 → 4.9 Hz) — the $O(1/\sqrt K)$ finite-$K$ correction to the balance, not a collapse, but it means the operating point is not held. The fix is to retune the external-drive mean per $K$ (a small additive $O(\sqrt K)$ offset solving the balance equations for a target rate) so the state is compared at constant $r_E$. With the rate pinned, the CV curve isolates the coupling effect cleanly and the sweep can push to larger $K$ without starving the spike count.

2. **Pin down the asymptotic $\lambda$.** Figure 3 gives the *sign* unambiguously (positive for the balanced net, ≈ 0 for the decoupled control), but the magnitude is an initial-growth estimate: $\varepsilon$ must be large enough to flip spikes, and $\lVert\Delta V\rVert$ saturates at the attractor size within ~100 ms, so the fit window is short and the value depends on $\varepsilon$. The precise asymptotic exponent needs a *renormalised* Benettin scheme — run the two copies in lockstep, periodically rescale $\Delta V$ back to a small fixed norm (keeping its direction), and average the $\log$-growth over a long trajectory. That dodges both saturation and the $\varepsilon$-dependence, and would also yield the full spectrum (and the attractor dimension) rather than just the leading exponent.
