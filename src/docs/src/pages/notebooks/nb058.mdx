## Abstract

[Vreeswijk & Sompolinsky](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.274.5293.1724) predict that a strongly coupled, sparse, inhibition-dominated network with uncorrelated drive falls — with no tuning — into a *balanced* state whose hallmark is irregular (CV ≈ 1), asynchronous, rhythmless firing: large excitatory and inhibitory currents cancel, and the leftover fluctuations carry each cell across threshold. We push the conductance-based [COBANet](/articles/ar003/) into the full four-coupling version of this regime — fixed fan-in $K \approx 10$, per-cell independent drive, and all four recurrent matrices ($W^{EE}, W^{EI}, W^{IE}, W^{II}$) — and confirm the signature: Poisson-like ISIs (CV ≈ 1), near-zero pairwise correlations, and a broadband spectrum with no peak. Growing the network 4× at fixed $K$ leaves the signatures and rates intact, and a network *without* recurrent excitation behaves identically — confirming that the fixed-$K$ balance, not $W^{EE}$, is what pins the rates. The defining test is the coupling scaling: only under the V&S strong-coupling rule (synapse $\propto J/\sqrt K$) does the irregularity *survive* as the fan-in grows — weak coupling lets the recurrent fluctuations wash out and the cells relax toward the Poisson floor set by their external drive. Finally, replacing the input with a *frozen* DC current (no noise of its own) leaves the balanced network firing irregularly (CV ≈ 2.2) while a decoupled network becomes a clock (CV ≈ 0) — the irregularity is generated by the deterministic recurrent dynamics, not inherited from the input — and a clone-and-perturb probe measures a positive Lyapunov exponent (λ ≈ +28 s⁻¹ for the balanced net vs ≈ 0 for the decoupled control): the state is deterministically chaotic. (Gamma, the rhythmic alternative this same hardware can occupy, is the subject of the companion entry [nb050](/notebooks/nb050/).)

## Methods

1. **Network and four-coupling V&S configuration.** [COBANet](/articles/ar003/), $N_E = 1024$, $N_I = 256$, $\Delta t = 0.25$ ms, $T = 1000$ ms. The ingredients that put it in the canonical balanced regime: **fixed fan-in** $K \approx 10$ (sparse exact-$K$, sparsity $s = 0.99$) so every cell draws exactly $K$ inputs and all cells are statistically equivalent; **all four recurrent matrices** present — $W^{EI} = \mathcal{N}(0.6, 0.18)$, $W^{IE} = \mathcal{N}(3.0, 0.9)$, $W^{II} = \mathcal{N}(0.4, 0.12)$, and $W^{EE} = \mathcal{N}(0.4, 0.12)$ μS — the complete Brunel/V&S E–I architecture; and **per-cell independent Poisson drive** (E: 45 Hz × 0.38 μS, I: 8 Hz × 0.25 μS) so the external input is uncorrelated across cells. Recurrent excitation $W^{EE}$ is included for canonical completeness; as Step 4 shows, it is *not* what sets the rates.

2. **Why it is irregular (the V&S mechanism).** In the V&S limit each cell sums $\approx K$ synapses of strength $\propto J/\sqrt K$ ($J = O(1)$), so the mean recurrent input is $O(\sqrt K)$ while its fluctuation is $O(1)$. A moderate firing rate forces the two large opposing means to **cancel to leading order**, parking the mean drive near threshold; the surviving $O(1)$ fluctuations then carry each cell across threshold at random times. Firing becomes threshold-crossing of noise — Poisson, CV → 1 — and, because each cell is driven by its own uncorrelated input, the population is asynchronous. (The headline run sits at finite $K \approx 10$; Step 5 sweeps $K$ under the exact $J/\sqrt K$ scaling to show the irregularity is the strong-coupling kind, not a finite-$K$ accident.)

3. **Diagnostics.** From the post-burn-in spike trains we read the per-neuron ISI coefficient of variation, the Welch spectrum of the population-mean E trace, and the mean pairwise spike-train cross-correlogram over 100 random E pairs.

4. **$N$-invariance control.** To check that the state lives on the balanced manifold and to isolate what pins the rates, we hold the fan-in fixed at $K \approx 10$ (setting sparsity $s = 1 - K/N_E$) and grow the network 4× — $N_E = 1024 \to 4096$, $N_I = N_E/4$ — running both the full four-coupling network and a *reduced* one with $W^{EE} = 0$. Per-cell input statistics are $N$-independent at fixed $K$, so the balance equations predict $N$-invariant rates regardless of $W^{EE}$. Three seeds per point give the spread.

5. **The $J/\sqrt K$ coupling test.** The defining V&S statement is about *strong* coupling: synaptic weight $\propto J/\sqrt K$, so the recurrent input keeps an $O(1)$ fluctuation as $K \to \infty$. The exact-$K$ connectivity here normalises each synapse by its fan-in (so a fixed nominal weight gives synapse $\propto 1/K$, the classical *weak*-coupling limit whose fluctuation $\propto 1/\sqrt K$ vanishes). The two are one rescaling apart: setting the nominal weight to $w = J\sqrt K$ recovers synapse $= w/K = J/\sqrt K$. So at fixed $N_E = 1024$ we sweep the fan-in $K = 10 \to 160$ under two conventions — **strong** (all recurrent weights scaled $\propto \sqrt K$, and the external drive folded into the balance: rate $\propto K$, per-spike $g \propto 1/\sqrt K$, giving an $O(\sqrt K)$ mean with $O(1)$ fluctuations) and **weak** (everything held fixed) — and read off whether the irregularity survives. (At high $K$ the strong-coupling rate drifts low, so this sweep uses a 3 s trial to keep the ISI count high enough for an unbiased CV.)

6. **Self-generation test (frozen input).** To ask whether the irregularity is *generated* by the network or *inherited* from the Poisson drive, we strip the input of all noise: replace the per-cell Poisson drive with a **quenched DC conductance** — a per-cell offset drawn once and frozen for the trial, so it has no per-timestep fluctuation and cannot itself carry irregularity. We run the balanced four-coupling network and, as a control, a **decoupled** one (recurrent weights ≈ 0) at a matched rate. With a noise-free input, any irregular firing must come from the deterministic dynamics; the decoupled control fixes the baseline (constant current → a clock, CV → 0). A deterministic autonomous system that sustains aperiodic firing is chaotic by elimination — the non-chaotic alternatives (a fixed point or a limit cycle) are both regular.

7. **Direct Lyapunov exponent.** To measure the chaos rather than infer it, we clone the network and run two copies on *identical* quenched-DC input, kicking every membrane voltage by a tiny $\varepsilon$ at $t = 0$ in the second copy, then track the voltage distance $\lVert\Delta V(t)\rVert$ between them. Spiking dynamics *contract* between spikes (leak) and *expand* only at spike-flips, so $\varepsilon$ must be large enough to flip spikes — below that, $\lVert\Delta V\rVert$ merely contracts and reports a spurious $\lambda < 0$; we use $\varepsilon = 0.1$ mV. The slope of $\log\lVert\Delta V\rVert$ in the post-flip, pre-saturation window is the largest Lyapunov exponent, fit for the balanced network and the decoupled control (five seeds each). This is the *initial-growth* estimate: $\lVert\Delta V\rVert$ saturates at the attractor size, and a precise asymptotic $\lambda$ would need a renormalised Benettin scheme — but the sign, positive vs ≈ 0, is the textbook criterion and is unambiguous.

## Next steps

The network is now the full four-coupling Brunel/V&S architecture; Figure 2 shows its signatures and rates are $N$-invariant at fixed $K$, Figure 3 shows the irregularity survives the $K \to \infty$ limit only under strong $J/\sqrt K$ coupling, Figure 4 shows it is self-generated (irregular firing under a noise-free frozen input), and Figure 5 measures a positive Lyapunov exponent — the state is deterministically chaotic. Two refinements remain.

1. **Hold the rate as $K$ grows.** Figure 3's caveat: under strong coupling $r_E$ drifts down with $K$ (17.5 → 4.9 Hz) — the $O(1/\sqrt K)$ finite-$K$ correction to the balance, not a collapse, but it means the operating point is not held. The fix is to retune the external-drive mean per $K$ (a small additive $O(\sqrt K)$ offset solving the balance equations for a target rate) so the state is compared at constant $r_E$. With the rate pinned, the CV curve isolates the coupling effect cleanly and the sweep can push to larger $K$ without starving the spike count.

2. **Pin down the asymptotic $\lambda$.** Figure 5 gives the *sign* unambiguously (positive for the balanced net, ≈ 0 for the decoupled control), but the magnitude is an initial-growth estimate: $\varepsilon$ must be large enough to flip spikes, and $\lVert\Delta V\rVert$ saturates at the attractor size within ~100 ms, so the fit window is short and the value depends on $\varepsilon$. The precise asymptotic exponent needs a *renormalised* Benettin scheme — run the two copies in lockstep, periodically rescale $\Delta V$ back to a small fixed norm (keeping its direction), and average the $\log$-growth over a long trajectory. That dodges both saturation and the $\varepsilon$-dependence, and would also yield the full spectrum (and the attractor dimension) rather than just the leading exponent.
