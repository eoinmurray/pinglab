## Abstract

Cortical neurons fire irregularly — their spike trains look almost like random (Poisson) processes, even though a single neuron driven by steady current fires like clockwork. Vreeswijk & Sompolinsky's 1996 *Science* paper explained why: in a recurrent network where strong excitation and inhibition roughly cancel, what is left over is *fluctuations*, and cells fire when those fluctuations randomly push them over threshold. This entry pushes the [COBANet](/articles/ar003/) all the way into that **canonical balanced asynchronous-irregular (AI) state** — strict sparse fixed fan-in, per-cell independent drive, an $I\!\to\!I$ matrix — and tests V&S's predictions on it, one by one: cancellation of large opposing currents, the *linear* dependence of rates on drive (the paper's signature paradox), fluctuation-driven Poisson firing, asynchrony, deterministic chaos, and invariance under the $1/\sqrt K$ synaptic scaling. There is no PING comparison here — this is the canonical state on its own terms. (The companion entry [nb050](/notebooks/nb050/) contrasts it with gamma.)

## The balance argument

A leaky integrate-and-fire neuron given a constant supra-threshold current is a metronome: it charges to threshold, fires, resets, repeats — perfectly regular, CV (coefficient of variation of inter-spike intervals) = 0. Real cortical neurons have CV ≈ 1, the value you'd get from a Poisson process. Where does the randomness come from? V&S's answer is the **balance of strong recurrent excitation and inhibition**. Here is the argument made quantitative — the part the simulations below put to the test.

**The scaling.** Put $N$ neurons in a sparse recurrent network where each cell receives $K$ excitatory and $K$ inhibitory inputs, $1 \ll K \ll N$. The crucial choice is that each synapse has strength of order $J/\sqrt K$ with $J = O(1)$. A cell's recurrent input is then a sum of $\approx K$ terms each $O(1/\sqrt K)$, so

$$
\text{mean} \;=\; K \cdot \tfrac{J}{\sqrt K}\, r \;=\; J\sqrt K\, r \;=\; O(\sqrt K),
\qquad
\text{std} \;=\; \sqrt K \cdot \tfrac{J}{\sqrt K}\,\sqrt r \;=\; O(1).
$$

The mean recurrent drive is *huge* ($O(\sqrt K)$); the fluctuation around it is merely *order one*.

**The cancellation.** A neuron's firing rate is $O(1)$ — it is neither silent nor saturated — so its *net* mean input must also be $O(1)$. But the excitatory and inhibitory means are each $O(\sqrt K)$. The only way their difference can be small is if they **cancel to leading order**:

$$
\underbrace{(\text{excitatory mean})}_{O(\sqrt K)} \;-\; \underbrace{(\text{inhibitory mean})}_{O(\sqrt K)} \;=\; O(1).
$$

No tuning is required: if excitation gets ahead, rates rise, inhibition grows to match, and the surplus is quenched — the balance is a *stable fixed point* of the rate dynamics. (It exists only when inhibition can dominate; a network without strong enough recurrent inhibition saturates or falls silent instead.)

**The rate law — the surprising part.** Imposing the leading-order cancellation on *both* populations gives two linear *balance equations* for the mean rates, here with external drive $r_0$ and the network's couplings $J_{\cdot\cdot}$ (ours has no E→E, $J_{EE}=0$):

$$
J_{E0}\,r_0 \;-\; J_{EI}\,r_I \;=\; 0,
\qquad
J_{I0}\,r_0 \;+\; J_{IE}\,r_E \;-\; J_{II}\,r_I \;=\; 0.
$$

This $2\times2$ linear system solves to rates that are a **linear function of the external input**, even though every unit is a hard nonlinear threshold device. The balanced network turns nonlinear neurons into a linear input–output map — V&S's signature result, tested directly in [§2](#2-rates-are-linear-in-the-drive).

**Where the irregularity comes from.** After the means cancel, what a cell actually feels is the $O(1)$ residue: a mean parked near threshold plus an $O(1)$ Gaussian fluctuation. Every spike is a chance upward excursion of that noise across threshold. Firing becomes threshold-crossing of noise — approximately Poisson, CV → 1. The same cancellation that linearises the network in the mean makes it *irregular* in time.

**What the theory predicts** — and what the rest of this entry checks, one section each:

1. **Cancellation** — the excitatory and inhibitory mean currents are individually large and nearly equal.
2. **Linear response** — population rates are linear in the external drive.
3. **Fluctuation-driven** — the fluctuation amplitude is comparable to or larger than the gap from mean drive to threshold.
4. **Irregular & asynchronous** — ISI CV → 1; pairwise correlations → 0 (the shared input is cancelled by tracking E/I; Renart et al. 2010).
5. **Chaotic** — a tiny perturbation grows (positive Lyapunov exponent).
6. **Scale-invariant** — because the scaling is $J/\sqrt K$, the state survives $K \to \infty$; its statistics are $K$-invariant.

**From binary to spiking.** V&S used *binary* (0/1) neurons with a stochastic threshold update, but the argument above is only about means and variances of inputs and is indifferent to that choice. Brunel (2000) carried the regime to spiking *LIF* neurons and Renart et al. (2010) to *conductance-based* synapses. We use COBANet — conductance-based LIF — so the per-spike weights play the role of $J/\sqrt K$ and the f-I curve plays the role of V&S's gain function.

## Building the canonical state

The network is [COBANet](/articles/ar003/) ($N_E = 1024$, $N_I = 256$, $\Delta t = 0.25$ ms, $T = 1000$ ms). Three ingredients push it into the strict V&S regime, each mapping onto a V&S assumption:

| Ingredient | Setting | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed fan-in $K \approx 10$ | sparse, exact-$K$ ($s = 0.99$) | every cell draws *exactly* $K$ inputs, so all cells are statistically equivalent — removes the binomial fan-in spread (per-cell rate CV $0.79 \to 0.33$) |
| $I\!\to\!I$ matrix $W^{II}$ | $\mathcal{N}(0.4, 0.12)$ μS | keeps the inhibitory pool from firing in synchronous bursts (which would re-establish a rhythm) |
| Per-cell independent drive | E: 45 Hz × 0.38 μS; I: 8 Hz × 0.25 μS | each cell gets its own private Poisson stream — the uncorrelated external input V&S's cells receive; both populations need their own (driving only E leaves the I cells correlated through $W^{EI}$) |

with recurrent weights $W^{EI} = \mathcal{N}(0.6, 0.18)$ and $W^{IE} = \mathcal{N}(3.0, 0.9)$ μS. The result is the asynchronous-irregular state: $\langle r_E\rangle, \langle r_I\rangle$ ≈ 15, 23 Hz, scattered raster, broadband spectrum (Figure 1).

## Testing V&S's predictions

Each subsection takes one prediction from the balance argument and checks it on the canonical state.

| Prediction | Measured | V&S says |
|---|---|---|
| **Cancellation** — net / excitatory mean current | **0.24** | large opposing means |
| **Linear response** — $r_{E,I}$ vs drive, fit $R^2$ | **0.995 / 0.992** | linear |
| **Fluctuation-driven** — median $\eta$ | **20** | $\gtrsim 1$ (noise) |
| **Irregular** — median ISI CV (E / I) | **1.02 / 1.12** | → 1 |
| **Asynchronous** — peak \|pairwise C(τ)\| | **0.009** | → 0 |
| **Chaotic** — divergence after ε kick | **sustained ≈ 6 cells** | grows ($\lambda > 0$) |
| **$\sqrt K$-invariant** — median CV across $K$ | **0.96–1.02** ($K = 10$–40) | $K$-invariant |

### 1. Excitation and inhibition cancel

The mechanism, made visible. We decompose each E cell's membrane current $I_\text{tot}(t) = g_E(E_e - V) + g_I(E_i - V) + g_L(E_L - V)$ into its excitatory part $I_E = g_E(E_e - V)$ and inhibitory part $I_I = g_I(E_i - V)$. The balance argument predicts $\langle I_E\rangle$ and $|\langle I_I\rangle|$ are each large and nearly equal, leaving a small net that sits near the threshold current $I_\text{th} = g_L(V_\text{th} - E_L) = 0.75$ nA (Figure 2). They are: $\langle I_E\rangle \approx 2.5$ nA, $|\langle I_I\rangle| \approx 2.2$ nA, net $\approx 0.6$ nA — the two big opposing currents cancel to a residue a quarter their size. With $K \approx 10$ the cancellation is partial (V&S's is exact as $K \to \infty$; see [§6](#6-invariance-under-k-scaling)), but the structure is unmistakable. Cancellation of the time-mean is necessary but not sufficient — that the leftover is *genuine noise* is what the fluctuation, asynchrony, and chaos sections establish.

### 2. Rates are linear in the drive

The balance equations predict that population rates are a *linear* function of the external input — the counterintuitive heart of the paper, since the units are sharp nonlinear thresholds. We sweep the external Poisson drive (scaling both the E and I streams together) and read off $r_E$ and $r_I$. Both rise dead straight: $R^2 = 0.995$ ($r_E$) and $0.992$ ($r_I$) across a near-threefold change in drive (Figure 3). Nonlinear neurons, linear network: V&S's signature result, on a spiking conductance-based substrate.

We can go further and *solve* the balance equations for the rates, not just check linearity. We read the network's realized synaptic *current* gains from the conductance traces, combine them with the known external-drive gains, and require the large mean currents to cancel. This pins $r_I$ to within ≈ 20% (predicted slope $0.37$ vs measured $0.30$) — the inhibitory rate is exactly what is needed for recurrent inhibition to cancel the external excitation onto E, V&S's mechanism made quantitative. The excitatory rate is only loosely bounded (predicted slope $0.10$ vs $0.16$): this network has no recurrent E→E excitation, so $r_E$ is not pinned by an E-cell balance — it is the small residual the I-cells need to balance *their* inputs, a delicate difference of large growing currents that the leading-order mean-field under-resolves. That gap is the honest size of the conductance-model correction to the current-based balance theory.

### 3. Fluctuation-driven firing

If the means cancel, firing must be driven by the *fluctuations*. We measure this per cell from the total current $I_\text{tot}(t)$: its time-mean $\mu_I$, its time-std $\sigma_I$, and the ratio

$$
\eta = \frac{\sigma_I}{|I_\text{th} - \mu_I|},
$$

the fluctuation amplitude relative to the gap from the mean to the threshold current. $\eta \gg 1$ means noise dwarfs that gap — fluctuation-driven firing. The canonical state has median $\eta \approx 20$ (Figure 4): every cell sits with its mean drive near threshold and fluctuations many times larger, and here the fluctuations are genuinely random (independent Poisson), which is what makes the firing Poisson.

  Mean drive sits right at threshold ($\mu_I \approx I_\text{th}$) with fluctuations many times larger ($\sigma_I \approx 4$ nA). Median $\eta \approx 20$ — every cell is strongly fluctuation-dominated, and the fluctuations are random, which is what makes the firing Poisson.

### 4. Irregular and asynchronous

These two read directly off the raster page (Figure 1). **Irregularity** is the ISI-CV histogram (panel 4): the E and I CVs both sit at ≈ 1 (1.02 / 1.12) — Poisson firing, not the clockwork of a constant-current integrator. **Asynchrony** is the cross-correlogram (panel 5): it is flat noise around zero, peak magnitude ≈ 0.009 — cells fire essentially independently. The near-zero correlation despite heavy *shared* recurrent input is the active-decorrelation result of Renart et al. (2010): tracking E and I currents cancel the common drive.

### 5. Chaos

The sharpest test, and the one that separates "balanced and noisy" from "balanced and chaotic." Clone the network, perturb every membrane voltage by a tiny ε = 2 mV at $t = 0$, and rerun on *identical frozen input* (same seed). Track $D(t)$ = the number of E cells whose spike differs between the two copies. A chaotic network amplifies the perturbation and never forgets it (Figure 5).

<Figure id="Figure 5" title="Spike-train divergence after an ε = 2 mV perturbation" src="/figures/notebooks/nb058/lyapunov_divergence.png" alt="Line plot of spike-train divergence D(t) vs time, 0–1000 ms, two traces. Red (Poisson drive): rises within about 100 ms to a sustained fluctuating level around 5–6 cells and stays there. Amber (quenched DC drive): sits distinctly higher, around 9 cells, for the full second.">
  Under Poisson drive (red) the perturbation saturates at a sustained ≈ 6-cell divergence; replacing the drive with a frozen quenched DC (amber) lifts it to ≈ 9 sustained and the peak from 16 to 57 cells. Both persist — the signature of positive $\lambda$ (deterministic chaos).
</Figure>

The Poisson-driven divergence saturates at a modest level because the strong shared *fluctuating* input partly *entrains* both copies — strongly-driven neurons fire reliably regardless of network state (the Mainen-Sejnowski effect). Removing that mask is a controlled test: a *quenched* DC drive (a frozen per-cell offset at the same mean conductance, no per-timestep fluctuation) cannot pin spike times, and the divergence climbs to ≈ 9 sustained with a 57-cell peak — the network's autonomous chaos, previously hidden under the entrainment.

### 6. Invariance under √K scaling

The balance argument hangs on one structural choice — synapses $\propto 1/\sqrt K$ — and predicts the state is therefore a well-defined *regime*, not a tuned operating point: its statistics should survive $K \to \infty$. We test this by varying $K$ (through the connection sparsity at fixed $N$) while rescaling every recurrent weight by $1/\sqrt K$. The irregularity holds: median ISI CV stays at ≈ 1 across $K = 10$–40 (0.96–1.02), and pairwise correlations stay flat and low (Figure 6). Only at $K = 5$ does it drift (CV ≈ 1.17) — the finite-$K$ edge, where too few inputs per cell undermine the central-limit argument that makes the leftover Gaussian. The state lives on the balanced manifold the $\sqrt K$ scaling defines, exactly as the theory requires.

### 7. N-invariance with sparse external drive

§6 varied $K$ at fixed $N$ and checked only the CV. The sharper test varies the network size $N$ at fixed $K/N$ (so $K \propto N$) and asks whether the *rates* are invariant. This needs the external drive to scale like the recurrent one: we make the per-cell Poisson stream the superposition of $K_\text{ext} \propto K$ external connections each at strength $\propto 1/\sqrt K$ — amplitude $\propto 1/\sqrt K$, rate $\propto K$ — so its mean is $O(\sqrt K)$ with $O(1)$ fluctuations, matching the recurrent drive.

The result is partial, and the partiality is the point (Figure 7). The CV stays ≈ 1 and $r_I$ is invariant across $N = 1024$–$4096$ ($K = 10$–$41$), with $K = 5$ ($N = 512$) the finite-$K$ edge as in §6 — but $r_E$ drifts steadily upward. The reason is structural: with no recurrent E→E excitation, the E-balance (external excitation cancelled by recurrent inhibition) pins $r_I$, the rate that *does* the cancelling, and leaves $r_E$ as the I-balance residual, which the $\sqrt K$ scaling does not protect — the same loose-$r_E$ story the balance-equation overlay found in [§2](#2-rates-are-linear-in-the-drive). Pinning $r_E$ too would need recurrent excitation (a full Brunel/V&S E–I network with $W^{EE}$). So the balanced manifold is real here for the irregularity and the inhibitory rate; the excitatory rate marks the limit of an inhibition-only-recurrent substrate.

## How faithful is it?

All six predictions of the balance argument reproduce on a conductance-based LIF network — cancellation, linear response, fluctuation-driven Poisson firing, asynchrony, chaos, and $\sqrt K$-invariance. Two honest caveats remain:

- **Invariant CV and $r_I$, drifting $r_E$.** The $N$-sweep holds the CV and the inhibitory rate $N$-invariant, but $r_E$ drifts — this network has no recurrent E→E excitation to pin it. Full rate-invariance would need a $W^{EE}$ network; here the balanced manifold governs the irregularity and $r_I$, not $r_E$.
- **Chaos partly masked by input entrainment.** Under the fluctuating Poisson drive the perturbation can't grow far before the shared input re-entrains both copies. A quenched DC drive lifts the sustained divergence from ≈ 6 to ≈ 9 cells and the peak from 16 to 57 — the autonomous chaos is real and stronger than the Poisson trace alone shows, though still modest at this $N$ and $K$.

Both are sharpenings, not contradictions — the regime is V&S; the remaining work is making it *quantitatively* V&S.

## Next steps

**Recurrent excitation to pin $r_E$.** The recurring limit across §2 and §7 is that $r_E$ is loose — this substrate has no E→E coupling, so no E-balance constrains it. Add a $W^{EE}$ matrix and retune to a balanced point: the full Brunel/V&S E–I architecture, where the E-balance pins $r_E$ and both rates become $N$-invariant.
