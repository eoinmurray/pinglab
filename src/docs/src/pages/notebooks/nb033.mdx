## Abstract

[nb025](/notebooks/nb025/) characterised the PING rate floor empirically: a recruitment cliff where the I-loop engages, gamma at ≈25 Hz, $W^{EI}$ moves the cliff and $W^{IE}$ does not. Both phenomena are bifurcation behaviours; the natural framework is Wilson-Cowan mean-field. The goal here is to derive the recruitment threshold $I_\text{ext}^\star$ and oscillation frequency $\omega^\star$ from the equations alone and check whether they match the spiking simulations.

## Methods

### Derivation

We start with $N_E + N_I$ coupled conductance-based spiking neurons and reduce them, step by step, to four coupled ODEs for the population-mean rates $E(t), I(t)$ and the population-mean conductances $g_e^I(t), g_i^E(t)$. Each step replaces one degree of freedom with a smoother, lower-dimensional approximation; the cost of each is recorded at the end. The full chain is:

> $N$-cell spiking COBA → homogeneous-coupling spike-train integrals → population-mean rates and conductances → driving-force linearisation → relaxational rate dynamics with f-I gain $\Phi$ → final 4D system in $(E, I, g_e^I, g_i^E)$.

**Step 1. Single-cell COBA equations.** Index E cells by $j \in \{1, \ldots, N_E\}$ and I cells by $k \in \{1, \ldots, N_I\}$. From the methods paper [ar009](/articles/ar009/) eq. (1) with the architecture constraints $W^{EE} = W^{II} = 0$ (no recurrent E→E, no I→I) and an external drive $I_{\text{ext},j}$ added on E:

$$
C_m^E\,\dot V_j^E \;=\; -g_L^E(V_j^E - E_L) \;-\; g_{i,j}^E(V_j^E - E_I) \;+\; I_{\text{ext},j}
\tag{D1a}
$$

$$
C_m^I\,\dot V_k^I \;=\; -g_L^I(V_k^I - E_L) \;-\; g_{e,k}^I(V_k^I - E_E)
\tag{D1b}
$$

The E cell has no $g_e^E$ term because the only excitation it receives in this analysis is $I_\text{ext}$ (the feedforward drive); the I cell has no $g_i^I$ because there is no I→I coupling. The conductances $g_{i,j}^E, g_{e,k}^I$ are themselves driven by presynaptic spikes through first-order linear filters:

$$
\tau_\text{AMPA}\,\dot g_{e,k}^I \;=\; -g_{e,k}^I \;+\; \tau_\text{AMPA}\,\sum_j W^{EI}_{kj}\,s_j^E(t)
\tag{D2a}
$$

$$
\tau_\text{GABA}\,\dot g_{i,j}^E \;=\; -g_{i,j}^E \;+\; \tau_\text{GABA}\,\sum_k W^{IE}_{jk}\,s_k^I(t)
\tag{D2b}
$$

with $s_j^E(t) = \sum_n \delta(t - t_{j,n}^E)$ the spike train of E-cell $j$ as a sum of Dirac pulses at its firing times (similarly for $s_k^I$). (D2a–b) are the continuous-time forms of eq. (8) and (7) in the methods backbone — exponential synapses with per-spike weight $W$ and decay $\tau$.

**Step 2. Homogeneous coupling.** The weight matrices in the simulator are random with finite variance. Replace each entry by its population mean: $W^{EI}_{kj} \to w^{EI}$ and $W^{IE}_{jk} \to w^{IE}$ for all $j, k$. The presynaptic-summation terms in (D2a–b) become

$$
\sum_j W^{EI}_{kj}\,s_j^E \;\longrightarrow\; w^{EI} \sum_j s_j^E \;=\; w^{EI}\, N_E\, E(t)
\tag{D3a}
$$

$$
\sum_k W^{IE}_{jk}\,s_k^I \;\longrightarrow\; w^{IE} \sum_k s_k^I \;=\; w^{IE}\, N_I\, I(t)
\tag{D3b}
$$

where we introduce the *population-mean firing rates*

$$
E(t) \;\equiv\; \frac{1}{N_E}\sum_{j=1}^{N_E} s_j^E(t),
\qquad
I(t) \;\equiv\; \frac{1}{N_I}\sum_{k=1}^{N_I} s_k^I(t).
\tag{D3c}
$$

Strictly, $E(t)$ and $I(t)$ as defined are sums of deltas. The mean-field interpretation is that under a *smooth-rate ansatz* — averaging over a short window or assuming a continuous spiking density — we treat $E, I$ as smooth functions, so $w^{EI} N_E E(t)$ becomes an ordinary continuous source term. (D3) drops two things: weight heterogeneity, and **finite-size fluctuations**. The latter is the stochastic noise that survives in any *actual* finite population: even with all weights at their mean values, the population rate $E(t) = N_E^{-1}\sum_j s_j^E(t)$ is a sum of $N_E$ independent point-process samples, so it has shot-noise variance $\mathrm{Var}[E(t)] \propto E(t)/N_E$ over a short window. The mean-field limit takes $N_E, N_I \to \infty$ at fixed $\tilde W^{EI}, \tilde W^{IE}$, in which the law of large numbers makes $E(t), I(t)$ converge to their expected values and the variance vanishes. At finite $N_E = 1024, N_I = 256$ the residual $O(N^{-1/2})$ fluctuations act as a noise term on (D4), which (a) smears the Hopf bifurcation — instead of a sharp $A^2 \propto (I_\text{ext} - I_\text{ext}^\star)$ onset, the spiking simulator shows a rounded shoulder with sub-threshold variance — and (b) maintains a low-amplitude *noisy* gamma oscillation just below criticality where the linearised mean-field predicts strict silence. Both effects reappear in the spiking simulations, with magnitude controlled by $1/\sqrt{N}$.

Because the source terms (D3a) and (D3b) no longer carry a cell index, every E cell sees the same $g_i^E$ and every I cell sees the same $g_e^I$. The per-cell conductance equations collapse to two population-mean equations. Defining lumped couplings

$$
\tilde W^{EI} \;\equiv\; w^{EI} N_E, \qquad \tilde W^{IE} \;\equiv\; w^{IE} N_I
\tag{D3d}
$$

(carries-the-fan-in: each E spike contributes the per-edge weight, and there are $N_E$ presynaptic E cells), the conductance dynamics become

$$
\tau_\text{AMPA}\,\dot g_e^I \;=\; -g_e^I \;+\; \tilde W^{EI}\,E
\tag{D4a}
$$

$$
\tau_\text{GABA}\,\dot g_i^E \;=\; -g_i^E \;+\; \tilde W^{IE}\,I
\tag{D4b}
$$

Two equations down from $N_E + N_I$. (Note: the absolute scale of $\tilde W^{EI}, \tilde W^{IE}$ folds into $W^{EI}, W^{IE}$ used in the final 4D system after Step 5.)

**Step 3. Driving-force linearisation.** The synaptic terms in (D1) still depend on $V$ through factors $(V - E_I)$ and $(V - E_E)$. Take $V$ to its resting value in the synaptic terms only (not in the leak or the threshold-crossing, which we'll handle separately via the f-I curve). With $V_\text{rest} = -65$ mV and the reversal potentials from [ar009](/articles/ar009/),

$$
\Delta V_\text{inh} \;\equiv\; V_\text{rest} - E_I \;=\; -65 - (-80) \;=\; 15~\text{mV}
\tag{D5a}
$$

$$
\Delta V_\text{exc} \;\equiv\; V_\text{rest} - E_E \;=\; -65 - 0 \;=\; -65~\text{mV}
$$

(so $-(V - E_E) \approx -\Delta V_\text{exc} = +65$ mV — depolarising drive on I). The synaptic currents in (D1a–b) become

$$
-g_i^E (V_j^E - E_I) \;\approx\; -g_i^E\,\Delta V_\text{inh}
\tag{D5b}
$$

$$
-g_e^I (V_k^I - E_E) \;\approx\; -g_e^I\,\Delta V_\text{exc} \;=\; +g_e^I \cdot |E_E - V_\text{rest}|
\tag{D5c}
$$

— each synaptic input is now *linear* in conductance, with the cell-population-independent prefactor folded into a constant. This is the crucial reduction: it kills the multiplicative coupling between membrane potential and synaptic conductance that makes COBA so different from CUBA, but only in the *driving force*. Shunting (the effect of $g$ on $\tau_\text{eff}$ and $g_\text{tot}$) is dropped at this step; this is what the table at the end records.

**Step 4. Population rate from an f-I curve.** Under (D5) the membrane equations (D1a–b) have the form $C_m \dot V = -g_L (V - E_L) + I_\text{syn}$ — exactly LIF with a synaptic current. A LIF cell receiving constant net input current $I$ fires at rate $\phi(I)$ where $\phi$ is its f-I curve. Replacing each cell's spike output by its rate response gives

$$
E(t) \;\approx\; \phi_E\!\big(I_\text{eff}^E(t)\big), \qquad
I(t) \;\approx\; \phi_I\!\big(I_\text{eff}^I(t)\big)
\tag{D6a}
$$

with effective input currents (from D1 with D5 substituted)

$$
I_\text{eff}^E(t) \;=\; I_\text{ext}(t) \;-\; g_i^E(t)\,\Delta V_\text{inh}
\tag{D6b}
$$

$$
I_\text{eff}^I(t) \;=\; g_e^I(t)\,|\Delta V_\text{exc}|
\tag{D6c}
$$

(I cells receive only excitation in this architecture; E cells receive external drive minus the GABA shunt.) The instantaneous-rate replacement (D6a) is only valid when input changes are slow compared to the membrane integration time. In reality, $E(t)$ relaxes toward the f-I-curve fixed point on its membrane time constant $\tau_E$. Encode this with an explicit relaxation:

$$
\tau_E\,\dot E \;=\; -E \;+\; \Phi_E\!\big(I_\text{ext} - g_i^E\,\Delta V_\text{inh}\big)
\tag{D7a}
$$

$$
\tau_I\,\dot I \;=\; -I \;+\; \Phi_I\!\big(g_e^I\,|\Delta V_\text{exc}|\big)
\tag{D7b}
$$

where $\Phi_E, \Phi_I$ are the smooth steady-state gain functions (sigmoids in the numerics). Drives that change faster than $\tau_E, \tau_I$ are filtered; the rate doesn't snap to the f-I curve instantaneously. Two more equations down — together with (D4a–b) we now have four equations in $(E, I, g_e^I, g_i^E)$.

**Step 5. Absorb the driving-force constants.** The fixed prefactors $\Delta V_\text{inh}, |\Delta V_\text{exc}|$ in (D7) and the fan-in scalings in (D4) are just multiplicative constants — they don't carry dynamical information. Fold them into the couplings:

$$
W^{EI} \;\equiv\; \tilde W^{EI} \cdot |\Delta V_\text{exc}|, \qquad
W^{IE} \;\equiv\; \tilde W^{IE} \cdot \Delta V_\text{inh}
\tag{D8}
$$

and likewise absorb the $|\Delta V_\text{exc}|$ inside the I-cell argument by redefining $g_e^I \mapsto g_e^I \cdot |\Delta V_\text{exc}|$ (similarly for $g_i^E$). The conductances now have units of input current rather than µS; the f-I curves take their argument directly. This change of variables is pure bookkeeping — no dynamics are lost.

The four equations after (D4) + (D7) + (D8) are exactly the 4D system shown next.

| Step | Approximation | What's lost |
|---|---|---|
| D3 | homogeneous coupling, smooth-rate density | weight heterogeneity, finite-size noise |
| D5 | $V \approx V_\text{rest}$ in driving force | shunting, threshold-crossing nonlinearity |
| D7 | single relaxational mode at $\tau_E, \tau_I$ | sub-membrane dynamics, refractoriness |
| D8 | absorb constants into $W$ and $g$ | nothing (bookkeeping only) |

### The 4D system

After (1)–(5), the mean-field equations are

$$
\tau_E\,\dot E = -E + \Phi_E(I_\text{ext} - g_i^E), \qquad
\tau_I\,\dot I = -I + \Phi_I(g_e^I) \tag{1}
$$

$$
\tau_\text{AMPA}\,\dot g_e^I = -g_e^I + W^{EI}\,E, \qquad
\tau_\text{GABA}\,\dot g_i^E = -g_i^E + W^{IE}\,I \tag{2}
$$

in state $(E, I, g_e^I, g_i^E)$. Whether this 4D state is the *minimal* representation that supports a Hopf — or whether a textbook 2D reduction would also work — is settled later in [#why-2d-reductions-fail](#why-2d-reductions-fail). For now: 4D is necessary because the synaptic phase delays $\tau_\text{AMPA}, \tau_\text{GABA}$ are load-bearing; adiabatically eliminating them removes the destabilising mechanism. We come back to this.

### 4D Jacobian and Hopf criterion

At a fixed point $(E^\star, I^\star, g_e^{I\star}, g_i^{E\star})$:

$$
J = \begin{pmatrix}
-1/\tau_E & 0 & 0 & -\Phi'_E/\tau_E \\
0 & -1/\tau_I & \Phi'_I/\tau_I & 0 \\
W^{EI}/\tau_\text{AMPA} & 0 & -1/\tau_\text{AMPA} & 0 \\
0 & W^{IE}/\tau_\text{GABA} & 0 & -1/\tau_\text{GABA}
\end{pmatrix} \tag{3}
$$

with $\Phi'_E, \Phi'_I$ evaluated at the fixed-point arguments.

## Results

### Why 2D reductions fail

The 4D system in (25)–(26) carries two synaptic-conductance variables alongside the rates $E, I$. The canonical Wilson-Cowan reduction is 2D — could we eliminate the conductances and recover a textbook polynomial ODE? Two attempted reductions both fail, for structurally distinct reasons.

### Naked 2D — adiabatic elimination of $\tau_\text{AMPA}, \tau_\text{GABA}$

Send $\tau_\text{AMPA}, \tau_\text{GABA} \to 0$ in (26): the conductance dynamics collapse instantaneously and $g_e^I \to W^{EI} E$, $g_i^E \to W^{IE} I$. Substituting into (25) gives the textbook 2D Wilson-Cowan system

$$
\tau_E\,\dot E = -E + \Phi_E\!\left(I_\text{ext} - W^{IE} I\right), \qquad
\tau_I\,\dot I = -I + \Phi_I\!\left(W^{EI} E\right). \tag{7a}
$$

The Jacobian of (7a) at any fixed point has

$$
\mathrm{tr}(J_\text{2D}) = -\frac{1}{\tau_E} - \frac{1}{\tau_I} \tag{7b}
$$

— constant in $I_\text{ext}$ and strictly negative. Hopf requires $\mathrm{tr}(J) = 0$, so **the naked 2D reduction with $W^{EE} = W^{II} = 0$ cannot oscillate at any parameters.** Canonical Wilson-Cowan works around this with non-zero $W^{EE}$ (E→E self-excitation), which puts a positive term on the diagonal and lets the trace cross zero. Our Börgers-style architecture sets $W^{EE} = 0$ by construction, so this escape is closed off.

### Cubic-rescue attempt — adding a FitzHugh-Nagumo-style self-suppression

A natural follow-up (raised by Tim): can the missing positive linear feedback be put back by an explicit cubic self-suppression in $E$, à la FitzHugh-Nagumo? Start from (6a) with sigmoid gains $\Phi_{E,I}(x) = r_\text{max}/(1 + e^{-(x-\theta)/k})$. Three reductions get us to a polynomial system.

**Step 1 — Taylor-expand $\Phi_E$ at its inflection.** Let $u = I_\text{ext} - W^{IE} I - \theta_E$ be the drive offset. Sigmoids are odd around the inflection, so even-order terms vanish:

$$
\Phi_E(\theta_E + u) = \Phi_E(\theta_E) + a\,u + \tfrac{1}{6} b\,u^3 + O(u^5) \tag{7}
$$

with $a = \Phi'_E(\theta_E) = r_\text{max}/(4 k_E) > 0$ and $b = \Phi'''_E(\theta_E) < 0$.

**Step 2 — Linearise $\Phi_I$** around the silent state: $\Phi_I(W^{EI} E) \approx \Phi'_I(0)\,W^{EI}\,E$.

**Step 3 — Express the cubic in $E$ rather than $u$.** At leading order the linearised $E$ dynamics give $E^\star \approx a\,u$ at any quasi-steady drive, so $u \approx E/a$. Substituting back into the cubic term, $\tfrac{1}{6} b\,u^3 \to (b/6 a^3)\,E^3 \equiv -\beta\,E^3$ with $\beta \equiv -b/(6 a^3) > 0$.

Folding constants into $I_\text{ext}$ and writing $\alpha \equiv a$, $\gamma \equiv a\,W^{IE}$, $\delta \equiv \Phi'_I(0)\,W^{EI}$ leaves the FitzHugh-Nagumo system:

$$
\tau_E\,\dot E = -E + \alpha\,E - \beta\,E^3 - \gamma\,I + \alpha\,I_\text{ext} \tag{8a}
$$

$$
\tau_I\,\dot I = -I + \delta\,E \tag{8b}
$$

The Jacobian of (8) at a fixed point $E^\star$ is

$$
J = \begin{pmatrix} (-1 + \alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2)/\tau_E & -\gamma/\tau_E \\ \delta/\tau_I & -1/\tau_I \end{pmatrix}, \tag{9}
$$

and a Hopf requires $\text{tr}(J)$ to cross zero. Rearranging $\text{tr}(J) = 0$:

$$
\alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2 = 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I. \tag{10}
$$

The left-hand side is maximised at $E^\star = 0$ where it equals $\alpha$. The right-hand side is bounded below by 1. So a necessary condition for *any* fixed point to lose stability is

$$
\boxed{\;\alpha > 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I.\;} \tag{11}
$$

The cubic enters only through the $-3\beta (E^\star)^2$ term, which *lowers* the left-hand side. It can stabilise an already-destabilised fixed point at finite amplitude (the saturating self-suppression that fixes the limit-cycle radius in a supercritical Hopf), but it cannot raise $\alpha$ past the threshold. Linear destabilisation must come from elsewhere.

The parameters are inherited from the 4D analysis above, so the comparison is on equal footing. Plugging in:

$$
\alpha = \frac{r_\text{max}}{4 k_E} = \frac{0.2}{4 \cdot 0.2} = 0.25, \qquad 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I = 5.
$$

We have $\alpha = 0.25$ where we need $\alpha > 5$ — a factor of twenty off. The trace stays strictly negative for all $I_\text{ext}$ and no Hopf exists. Making the sigmoid steeper ($k_E$ smaller) raises $\alpha$ linearly, but reaching $\alpha = 5$ would require $k_E = 0.01$ — a sigmoid that is essentially a step function, qualitatively wrong as a gain model for an LIF population near rheobase. The deficit is structural, not tuning-limited.

### What's structurally missing

Both routes fail for the same underlying reason. The linear gain $\alpha$ is the strength of positive feedback on $E$. The canonical Wilson-Cowan 1972 model includes an E→E self-coupling $W^{EE}$, which renormalises $\alpha \to W^{EE}\,\Phi'_E$; with $W^{EE} \approx 12$ and a steep sigmoid this comfortably clears the threshold, and the cubic reduction Hopfs by the standard mechanism. Our Börgers-style PING architecture has $W^{EE} = 0$ — there is no E-loop to renormalise $\alpha$, and so neither the naked 2D nor the 2D-cubic route can destabilise the silent fixed point regardless of how the cubic is tuned.

The destabilisation that does drive the 4D Hopf above comes from a different mechanism entirely: the synaptic phase delays $\tau_\text{AMPA}, \tau_\text{GABA}$. These have no analogue in (6a) or (8) — adiabatically eliminating them to get a 2D system kills the very feature that produces the oscillation. A faithful 2D reduction would have to keep the synaptic timescales as explicit delays, yielding a delay-differential equation rather than a polynomial ODE.

### The per-cycle participation gate

The 4D Hopf above gives the cycle *frequency* $\omega^\star$ but not its *amplitude in spikes per cell*. The per-cycle Bernoulli participation $p$ measured spike-resolved in [nb046](/notebooks/nb046/) (median $\approx 0.20$) and probed under $\theta_u$ in [nb025](/notebooks/nb025/) (held constant across the penalty sweep) needs its own mean-field statement. Here it is, as a threshold-crossing argument on the I-population.

Within one cycle the I-population fires when integrated excitatory drive crosses its firing threshold. The derivation below works it out.

Start from the I-cell membrane equation (eq. 1 with the I-cell substitutions and $g_i = 0$, since I receives no inhibition):

$$
C_m \, \frac{dV_I}{dt} \;=\; -g_L(V_I - V_L) \;-\; g_e^I (V_I - E_E)
\tag{9a}
$$

Near rest $V_I \approx V_L$, the leak term is small (its job is to oppose drift on a $\tau_L = C_m/g_L \approx 20$ ms timescale; the cycle plays out on $\tau_\text{AMPA} \approx 2$ ms, an order of magnitude faster). Drop it and linearise $(V_I - E_E) \approx (V_\text{rest} - E_E)$, valid while $V_I$ stays close to rest — i.e. up to the first threshold crossing:

$$
C_m \, \frac{dV_I}{dt} \;\approx\; g_e^I \, (E_E - V_\text{rest})
\tag{9b}
$$

Integrate from cycle start $t = 0$:

$$
V_I(t) - V_\text{rest} \;\approx\; \frac{E_E - V_\text{rest}}{C_m} \int_0^t g_e^I(s)\, ds
\tag{9c}
$$

Now the model for $g_e^I(t)$. The cycle begins with a tight burst of E spikes — the first $K$ E cells to cross their own threshold do so within a window $\Delta t_\text{burst} \ll \tau_\text{AMPA}$ (visible in the rasters of [nb023](/notebooks/nb023/) and [nb025](/notebooks/nb025/) — bursts are sub-millisecond, AMPA decay is 2 ms). Treat the volley as instantaneous at $t = 0$: each of the $K$ spikes adds $W^{EI}$ to $g_e^I$, and the sum then decays exponentially with $\tau_\text{AMPA}$:

$$
g_e^I(t) \;\approx\; K \, W^{EI} \, e^{-t/\tau_\text{AMPA}}
\tag{9d}
$$

Substitute (9d) into (9c) and integrate:

$$
V_I(t) - V_\text{rest} \;\approx\; \frac{K \, W^{EI} \,(E_E - V_\text{rest})\, \tau_\text{AMPA}}{C_m} \,\big(1 - e^{-t/\tau_\text{AMPA}}\big)
\tag{9e}
$$

The bracket saturates at 1 over a few $\tau_\text{AMPA}$ — the I cell either reaches threshold within this window or it doesn't reach threshold at all, because after that $g_e^I$ has decayed away and the leak term we dropped pulls $V_I$ back. So the *peak* depolarisation per cycle is

$$
\Delta V_I^\text{max} \;\approx\; \frac{K \, W^{EI} \,(E_E - V_\text{rest})\, \tau_\text{AMPA}}{C_m}
\tag{9f}
$$

The I-population trips when $\Delta V_I^\text{max} \geq V_\text{th}^I - V_\text{rest}$. Setting equality and solving for the critical count:

$$
K^\star \;\approx\; \frac{C_m \,(V_\text{th}^I - V_\text{rest})}{W^{EI} \,(E_E - V_\text{rest}) \,\tau_\text{AMPA}}
\tag{9}
$$

Two simplifications hide in (9). **The synchronous-volley assumption** ($\Delta t_\text{burst} \ll \tau_\text{AMPA}$) lets us collapse the spike-train sum into a single jump. If the volley is spread over a window comparable to $\tau_\text{AMPA}$, the bracket in (9e) doesn't saturate at 1 — it saturates at some efficiency factor $\eta < 1$, and (9) gets a $\eta$ in the denominator (more spikes needed). **The leak drop** is exact in the limit $\tau_L / \tau_\text{AMPA} \to \infty$ and order-$\tau_\text{AMPA}/\tau_L \approx 0.1$ otherwise; restoring it adds a multiplicative factor $\big(1 - e^{-t/\tau_L}\big)^{-1}$ on the right of (9c) that lowers (9) by ≈ 10% at the cycle peak. Both are bounded corrections — (9) is structurally correct, with prefactor known to within ≈ 20%.

Once I fires, the GABA shunt reaches E after one further AMPA delay; during that window an over-recruitment $\delta$ of additional E cells fire before the shunt arrives. The total fraction firing per cycle is

$$
\boxed{\;p \;\approx\; \frac{K^\star + \delta}{N_E}
\;=\; \frac{C_m \,(V_\text{th}^I - V_\text{rest})}{N_E \, W^{EI} \,(E_E - V_\text{rest}) \,\tau_\text{AMPA}} + \frac{\delta}{N_E}\;}
\tag{10}
$$

Three structural facts fall out of (10):

1. **No $W_\text{in}$.** The trip count $K^\star$ is set by I-cell biophysics and the $E{\to}I$ wiring, not by how hard the cycle is driven. $W_\text{in}$ controls *when* a cycle happens (i.e. $\omega^\star$ from the 4D Hopf), not *who fires* within it.
2. **No $\tau_\text{GABA}$, no $W^{IE}$.** Those terminate the cycle (set the I-shunt strength and recovery time), but they don't enter $K^\star$. So $p$ stays put when $\tau_\text{GABA}$ is swept — exactly what [nb046](/notebooks/nb046/) measures (median $p \approx 0.20$ across the full $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep, even as $f_\gamma$ varies by 3×).
3. **Pinned by the frozen wiring.** $\theta_u$ adjusts $W_\text{in}, W_\text{out}$ only; neither appears in (10). This explains [nb025](/notebooks/nb025/) Figure 5: $p$ stays in 0.19–0.24 across the entire $\theta_u$ sweep while $f_\gamma$ slides from ≈ 37 Hz to ≈ 15 Hz. The optimiser has a temporal lever ($f_\gamma$ via drive) but not a combinatorial one ($p$ via wiring).

Calibrating against the data: $p \approx 0.20$ at $N_E = 1024$ gives $K^\star + \delta \approx 200$ E spikes per cycle. With $W^{EI} \approx 1$ μS, $E_E - V_\text{rest} \approx 65$ mV, $\tau_\text{AMPA} = 2$ ms, $V_\text{th}^I - V_\text{rest} \approx 15$ mV, the leading term gives $K^\star \approx 115$ in normalised units; the remainder is $\delta$ from the AMPA→GABA shunt-arrival lag. Order of magnitude checks out; the exact prefactor depends on the simulator's conductance normalisation.

## Discussion

Two probes anchor on the same wiring. The 4D Hopf (Figure 1) gives $\omega^\star$ from $\tau_\text{AMPA}, \tau_\text{GABA}$, and the coupling matrices. Equation (10) gives $p$ from $V_\text{th}^I, W^{EI}, \tau_\text{AMPA}$, and $N_E$. Their product $r_E = p \cdot f_\gamma$ is the affine law of [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/).
