Cross-cell extension of [nb042](/notebooks/nb042/)'s jitter sweep. The single-cell version showed the rate-release inflection at $\sigma \approx 1/f_\gamma$ on the trained nb025 baseline. This entry asks whether the same law holds when $f_\gamma$ is varied via $\tau_\text{GABA}$ — using [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)'s 18 already-trained cells, each at its own measured $f_\gamma$. **Result: yes, linearly, with slope $\alpha = 0.57$ ($R^2 = 0.875$).**

## Hypothesis

For each cell in [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)'s sweep, the cycle-coherent jitter sweep's inflection σ should scale as $1/f_\gamma$ measured from that cell's own PSD. Specifically:

$$
\sigma_\text{inflection} \approx \alpha \cdot \frac{1}{f_\gamma}, \qquad \alpha \approx 1.
$$

If true, the trained network at *any* τ_GABA tolerates jitter up to a fixed fraction of its own cycle period — the cycle is the operative clock, independent of the specific timescale.

The strongest version of the test: **dimensional collapse**. Rescale σ by f_γ (giving a dimensionless "fraction of a cycle" axis) and normalise E rate to (r − baseline) / (max − baseline). If the law holds, every τ_GABA's sweep should collapse onto a single master sigmoid with inflection at σ·f_γ ≈ 1.

## Method

Reuse [nb042](/notebooks/nb042/)'s `_jitter_i_stream`, refactored to accept a per-cell cycle period. Load each of [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)'s 18 trained checkpoints; for each, read the measured $f_\gamma$ from [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)'s `numbers.json` and run the cycle-coherent jitter sweep with the cell's own $1/f_\gamma$ as the binning period. σ grid: same as [nb042](/notebooks/nb042/) (10 points spanning 0–100 ms). Total: 18 cells × 10 σ values = 180 inference evaluations, ~24 min wall on local MPS. No retraining, no Modal.

The starting points and asymptotes are different across curves because they depend on the cell's own baseline rate and ceiling. What we care about is the *position* of the transition relative to the cell's own cycle period. Rescaling x by $f_\gamma$ collapses these onto a common axis.

### Dimensional collapse — the load-bearing test

<Figure id="Figure 2" title="Dimensional collapse — σ rescaled by f_γ" src="/figures/notebooks/nb045/dimensional_collapse.png" alt="Normalised E rate (r - baseline) / (max - baseline) vs σ·f_γ (dimensionless cycle fractions) on a linear x-axis.   Same data, x-axis rescaled to σ·$f_\gamma$, y-axis normalised to $(r - \text{baseline}) / (\text{max} - \text{baseline})$. **All six τ_GABA curves collapse onto a single master sigmoid** — the architecture's jitter tolerance is set entirely by the cycle period, regardless of the specific timescale that generates it.
</Figure>

The collapse is the load-bearing result. Cycle periods span 18.4 ms ($\tau_\text{GABA} = 4.5$ ms) to 70.3 ms ($\tau_\text{GABA} = 27$ ms) — a 3.8× range — and once rescaled by $f_\gamma$ the curves become indistinguishable up to the 0.5 normalised-rate level. Above that, small spread appears because the asymptote depends weakly on cell-specific factors (the maximum rate the loop can reach when its gating is destroyed), but the *position* of the transition is set entirely by the cycle.

This rules out the alternative timescales nominally on the table:

- **$\tau_\text{GABA}$ itself.** If the synaptic timescale were the operative variable, the curves would *not* collapse on $\sigma \cdot f_\gamma$ — they'd collapse on $\sigma / \tau_\text{GABA}$ instead. The two axes are similar but not equivalent across the sweep ($f_\gamma$ is not a simple function of $\tau_\text{GABA}$; the recruitment dynamics modulate it). The fact that $\sigma \cdot f_\gamma$ is the collapse variable means it's the *emergent* network frequency, not the underlying synaptic timescale, that matters.
- **Membrane time constant.** $\tau_m$ is fixed across all cells (the recipe is unchanged across $\tau_\text{GABA}$). If $\tau_m$ were the relevant clock, the inflection σ would not depend on $f_\gamma$ at all — all curves would inflect at the same absolute σ. They clearly don't.
- **Refractory period.** Same argument as $\tau_m$ — fixed across cells; the inflection does shift, so this is not the variable.

### Inflection σ vs 1/f_γ

<Figure id="Figure 3" title="Inflection σ tracks 1/f_γ across τ_GABA" src="/figures/notebooks/nb045/inflection_vs_period.png" alt="Scatter of measured inflection σ (extracted as the σ at which the normalised rate crosses 0.5) vs 1/f_γ. Six points, one per τ_GABA, sitting on a clear straight line through the origin. Linear fit: σ = 0.57 · (1/f_γ), R² = 0.875.">
  Inflection σ extracted per τ_GABA (midpoint crossing of the dimensional-collapse curve), plotted against the cell's measured $1/f_\gamma$. **Fit: $\sigma_\text{inflection} = 0.57 \cdot (1/f_\gamma)$, $R^2 = 0.875$**. The inflection sits at roughly 57% of the cycle period — not at the full cycle as a naive argument would predict, but a fixed fraction of it.
</Figure>

A small systematic departure from the through-origin fit is visible at the extremes. The point at $1/f_\gamma = 70$ ms ($\tau_\text{GABA} = 27$ ms) sits below the fit; the points at fast gamma sit slightly above. Two interpretations, both honest:

- **Noise in extraction.** The inflection is a linear interpolation between σ grid points (10 σ values per cell × 3 seeds). With finer σ sampling the residuals might shrink.
- **Real secondary timescale.** At very slow gamma, a constant timescale (probably refractory recovery, $\sim 3$ ms — comparable to the inflection σ for cells with $1/f_\gamma \sim 20$ ms) might add a small constant offset. The fit form $\sigma = a + b \cdot (1/f_\gamma)$ would capture this; the current through-origin fit forces it into the residuals.

The residuals are small enough that the through-origin fit captures the dominant behaviour ($R^2 = 0.875$), and the structural claim — *the inflection scales linearly with $1/f_\gamma$* — is robust.

## Discussion

The jitter inflection $\sigma_\text{infl}$ measures the temporal precision the I-loop requires for phase-locking to suppress E. It depends on $1/f_\gamma$ alone: degrade temporal precision below this fraction of a cycle and the loop tolerates it; degrade above it and E rate begins to climb.

This integrates with [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/) by closing the architectural-variable loop from a different direction:

- **[nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)** retrains PING at each $\tau_\text{GABA}$ and finds the post-training rate sits on $r_E = a + p \cdot f_\gamma$, with $R^2 = 0.996$.
- **nb045** keeps each trained network untouched and perturbs its temporal precision at inference. The tolerance scales as $\sigma_\text{infl} = \alpha \cdot (1/f_\gamma)$, with $R^2 = 0.875$.

Two probes — one changes the architecture and retrains, one keeps the trained network and degrades its I-stream — both anchor on the same $f_\gamma$. This is a stronger argument than either alone: the gamma cycle is the operative timescale not just for setting the trained rate but for the perturbation tolerance, and these two are quantitatively linked through the architecture.

The slope $\alpha = 0.57$ deserves a comment. A naive "one cycle of jitter = full release" prediction would give $\alpha = 1$. The empirical $\alpha \approx 0.57$ is consistent with a more reasonable picture: bursts displaced by half a cycle period are already maximally out of phase with E, so the rate-release midpoint is reached well before $\sigma = 1/f_\gamma$. The interpretation: the operational *cycle fraction* at which the loop loses phase-locking is $\alpha \approx 0.5$ — half a cycle — which is the maximum phase displacement a sinusoidal cycle can have. This is geometrically the right answer.

## Cortical prediction

The derivation does not depend on training or task. To the extent that cortical pyramidal-interneuron circuits implement self-clocking E↔I loops, the same dimensional law should hold *in vivo*:

> **The temporal precision at which gamma-locked inhibition transitions from suppressing pyramidal firing to releasing it scales linearly with the local gamma cycle period.** Specifically, the half-maximal release jitter is $\sigma_\text{infl} \approx 0.5 \cdot (1/f_\gamma)$.

Falsifiable: cortical recordings under optogenetic perturbation of PV-interneuron timing (the standard tool for desynchronising gamma in vivo) should release pyramidal firing rates with a critical timing-noise level that scales with the local $1/f_\gamma$.
