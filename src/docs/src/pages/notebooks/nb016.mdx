## Introduction

Sweep the internal E→I→E coupling strength with input held flat (overdrive {scan.fixed_overdrive}×), watch the network walk from async baseline through the onset of gamma, and try to pull a single-frame scalar out of each trace. Three candidates: the population rate (the raw signal), the autocorrelation peak (a candidate score), and a Bayes factor between an async and a ping generative model (a calibrated probability).

### Population rate

Replay the same Poisson input through a fresh ping network at each frame's *ei* value, record the E spike train $s_{i,t}$, and bin at $\Delta_\text{bin} = 2$ ms,

$$
r_k \;=\; \frac{1000}{N_E \, \Delta_\text{bin}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_E} \sum_{t \in \mathrm{bin}_k} s_{i, t}
\quad \text{(Hz)}.
$$

Discard the first {numbers.autocorr_metric?.onset_skip_ms ?? "20"} ms onset transient before the downstream metrics; the full trace is shown in the stacked plot.

### Autocorrelation

The candidate single-frame metric is the **autocorrelation peak** at non-zero lag — async noise has none, a periodic burst train has a tall, sharp one at the gamma period. Let $r_k$ be the post-onset rate ($k = 0, \dots, N-1$), with mean $\bar r$ and standard deviation $\sigma_r$. Z-score and autocorrelate via FFT:

$$
\tilde r_k \;=\; \frac{r_k - \bar r}{\sigma_r}, \qquad
C(\ell) \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k} \tilde r_k \, \tilde r_{k+\ell}.
$$

Z-scoring puts every frame's trace on the same dimensionless scale so the autocorrelation reads out *shape*, not amplitude, and pins $C(0) = 1$. The metric is the *prominence* of the strongest peak in the lag window {numbers.autocorr_metric?.lag_min_ms ?? "5"}–{numbers.autocorr_metric?.lag_max_ms ?? "60"} ms,

$$
\mathrm{prom}(\ell^\star) \;=\; C(\ell^\star) \;-\; \min_{\ell \in [\ell_l, \ell_r]} C(\ell),
$$

with $\ell^\star$ the highest-prominence peak and $[\ell_l, \ell_r]$ the interval bounded by its nearest valleys (scipy's *find_peaks* convention).

### Bayes factor

The two metrics above are descriptive — they summarise the trace but don't say "this is ping with probability $P$". The Bayesian alternative is to fit two generative recipes to each trace and report the log-odds. We work on the raster directly (per-neuron spike times $\{t_{j,1} < \dots < t_{j,n_j}\}$ for $j = 1, \dots, N_E$ over the post-onset window $[0, T]$). Both recipes match the validated step-1a sims rather than the simpler Poisson placeholders.

**Async** is a per-neuron gamma-renewal process. The generative recipe is:

1. Pick parameters: shape $k$, scale $\theta$, so each neuron's mean firing rate is $1/(k\theta)$ and the ISI coefficient of variation is $1/\sqrt{k}$.
2. For each neuron $j = 1, \dots, N_E$, independently:
   - Draw a starting offset $u_j \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, k\theta)$ (random initial phase).
   - Set $t_{j,1} = u_j$.
   - For $i = 2, 3, \dots$, draw $\tau_{j,i} \sim \Gamma(k, \theta)$ and set $t_{j,i} = t_{j,i-1} + \tau_{j,i}$, until $t_{j,i} > T$.

$k = 1$ recovers Poisson; $k = 10$ matches the visible *ei* = 0 stripe pattern (CV $\approx$ 0.32). Conditional on the inner ISIs $\tau_{j,i} = t_{j,i} - t_{j,i-1}$ for $i \ge 2$, the log-likelihood is

$$
\log P(D \mid \text{async}, k, \theta) \;=\; \sum_{j,\, i \ge 2} \big[(k - 1)\log\tau_{j,i} \;-\; \tau_{j,i}/\theta \;-\; k\log\theta \;-\; \log\Gamma(k)\big].
$$

To see where this comes from, start with the gamma probability density of one inter-spike interval,

$$
f_\Gamma(\tau; k, \theta) \;=\; \frac{\tau^{k-1} \, e^{-\tau/\theta}}{\theta^{k} \, \Gamma(k)}, \qquad \tau > 0,
$$

whose logarithm is the bracketed expression in the sum: the first three terms come from the $\tau^{k-1}$, $e^{-\tau/\theta}$, and $\theta^{-k}$ factors of the density; the last is the gamma-function normaliser. The renewal property says that ISIs are independent draws from this density, so across one neuron $j$ the joint log-likelihood of the inner ISIs $\tau_{j,2}, \tau_{j,3}, \dots$ is just the sum $\sum_{i \ge 2} \log f_\Gamma(\tau_{j,i})$. Different neurons are independent renewal processes, so the joint log-likelihood across all $N_E$ neurons sums over $j$ as well, giving the double sum above.

The $i \ge 2$ constraint is deliberate: the *first* spike $t_{j,1}$ is not the end of an ISI but a forward-recurrence time relative to a fictional spike at $t = 0$, so it belongs to a different distribution. Likewise, the post-last-spike interval $T - t_{j, n_j}$ is right-censored — we know the next spike came after $T$ but not when. Boundary terms — first-spike forward-recurrence density and final censored survival — drop out as a per-neuron $O(1)$ offset, small against the $O(\sum_j n_j)$ inner-ISI bulk. At fixed $k$ the MLE for $\theta$ is closed form, $\hat\theta = \bar\tau / k$ with $\bar\tau$ the global mean inner ISI; we then marginalise over $k$ on a grid spanning Poisson to highly regular.

**Ping** is a marked point process driven by a shared periodic burst schedule. The generative recipe is:

1. Pick parameters: frequency $f$ (period $T_p = 1/f$), participation probability $p \in (0, 1]$, jitter $\sigma > 0$.
2. Draw a global phase $b_0 \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, T_p)$, giving the burst schedule $b_n = b_0 + n T_p$ for $n = 0, 1, \dots, N_b - 1$ with $N_b = \lfloor T / T_p \rfloor$.
3. For each $(j, n) \in \{1, \dots, N_E\} \times \{0, \dots, N_b - 1\}$, independently:
   - Draw a participation indicator $z_{j,n} \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(p)$.
   - If $z_{j,n} = 1$, draw $\varepsilon_{j,n} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ and emit a spike at $t = b_n + \varepsilon_{j,n}$ (discarding any spike that falls outside $[0, T]$).

The per-neuron mean rate is $p f$, so matching to a target rate $\lambda$ fixes $p = \lambda / f$. Assigning each observed spike to its nearest burst $n^\star(j, i)$, the log-likelihood factors into a "spike → burst" term and a non-participation term:

$$
\log P(D \mid \text{ping}, b_0, f, p, \sigma) \;=\; \sum_{j,i}\!\Big[\log p \;-\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) \;-\; \tfrac{(t_{j,i} - b_{n^\star})^2}{2\sigma^2}\Big] \;+\; (N_E N_b - n_\text{tot})\log(1 - p),
$$

The derivation runs straight from the recipe. Each $(j, n)$ pair has two outcomes: with probability $1 - p$ the neuron skips that burst (no spike, contribution $1 - p$ to the joint density); with probability $p$ it fires once at $b_n + \varepsilon_{j,n}$, $\varepsilon_{j,n} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, contributing the joint density $p \cdot \mathcal{N}(t_{j,n}; b_n, \sigma^2)$. Because the Gaussian PDF is

$$
\mathcal{N}(t; b_n, \sigma^2) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \, \exp\!\Big[-\tfrac{(t - b_n)^2}{2\sigma^2}\Big],
$$

its log gives the bracketed term: $\log p$ (the participation factor), $-\tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)$ (the normaliser), and the squared-error penalty in $(t - b_n)^2/(2\sigma^2)$.

To get from the per-pair log-likelihoods to the equation above, we partition the $N_E N_b$ pairs into participating and non-participating. A pair is *participating* if there's an observed spike close enough to $b_n$ to have come from it — formalised as "the nearest burst to spike $t_{j,i}$ is $b_{n^\star(j,i)}$, with no other spike of neuron $j$ closer to $b_{n^\star}$". With $\sigma \ll T_p$ this assignment is essentially injective: each spike maps uniquely to one $(j, n)$, so the participating pairs are exactly the $n_\text{tot} = \sum_j n_j$ observed spikes. The remaining $N_E N_b - n_\text{tot}$ pairs are non-participating and each contributes $\log(1 - p)$. Summing the two halves gives the displayed log-likelihood.

With $N_b$ the number of bursts in $[0, T]$ and $n_\text{tot} = \sum_j n_j$ the total spike count, the first sum rewards spikes that fall close to a burst; the second penalises non-participation. MLEs are closed form: $\hat p = n_\text{tot} / (N_E N_b)$ and $\hat\sigma^2 = (1/n_\text{tot}) \sum_{j,i}(t_{j,i} - b_{n^\star})^2$. The discrete nuisance parameters — phase $b_0$ on a fine grid in $[0, T_p)$ and frequency $f$ on a coarser grid spanning the gamma band — get integrated numerically.

The Bayes factor is the ratio of marginalised likelihoods,

$$
\log\mathrm{BF} \;=\; \log P(D \mid \text{ping}) \;-\; \log P(D \mid \text{async}),
$$

each side integrated over its model parameters (Laplace around the MLEs for the continuous scale parameters, grid for the discrete priors). Ping has more degrees of freedom than async, so marginalisation imposes an automatic Occam penalty — ping only beats async when the data demand the burst structure. $\log\mathrm{BF}$ scales linearly with total spike count $\sum_j n_j$, so a 250 ms trace from $N_E$ = 512 neurons at $\sim$10 Hz already carries $\sim$1280 spikes of evidence.

The per-frame output is $\log\mathrm{BF}$ in nats (positive favours ping, zero undecided), converted to $P(\text{ping} \mid D) = \sigma(\log\mathrm{BF})$ under equal prior odds.
