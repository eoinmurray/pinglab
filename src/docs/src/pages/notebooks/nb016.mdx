## Introduction

Sweep the internal E→I→E coupling strength with input held flat (overdrive {scan.fixed_overdrive}×), watch the network walk from async baseline through the onset of gamma, and try to pull a single-frame scalar out of each trace. Three candidates: the population rate (the raw signal), the autocorrelation peak (a candidate score), and a Bayes factor between an async and a ping generative model (a calibrated probability).

### Population rate

Replay the same Poisson input through a fresh ping network at each frame's *ei* value, record the E spike train $s_{i,t}$, and bin at $\Delta_\text{bin} = 2$ ms,

$$
r_k \;=\; \frac{1000}{N_E \, \Delta_\text{bin}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_E} \sum_{t \in \mathrm{bin}_k} s_{i, t}
\quad \text{(Hz)}.
$$

Discard the first {numbers.autocorr_metric?.onset_skip_ms ?? "20"} ms onset transient before the downstream metrics; the full trace is shown in the stacked plot.

### Autocorrelation

The candidate single-frame metric is the **autocorrelation peak** at non-zero lag — async noise has none, a periodic burst train has a tall, sharp one at the gamma period. Let $r_k$ be the post-onset rate ($k = 0, \dots, N-1$), with mean $\bar r$ and standard deviation $\sigma_r$. Z-score and autocorrelate via FFT:

$$
\tilde r_k \;=\; \frac{r_k - \bar r}{\sigma_r}, \qquad
C(\ell) \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k} \tilde r_k \, \tilde r_{k+\ell}.
$$

Z-scoring puts every frame's trace on the same dimensionless scale so the autocorrelation reads out *shape*, not amplitude, and pins $C(0) = 1$. The metric is the *prominence* of the strongest peak in the lag window {numbers.autocorr_metric?.lag_min_ms ?? "5"}–{numbers.autocorr_metric?.lag_max_ms ?? "60"} ms,

$$
\mathrm{prom}(\ell^\star) \;=\; C(\ell^\star) \;-\; \min_{\ell \in [\ell_l, \ell_r]} C(\ell),
$$

with $\ell^\star$ the highest-prominence peak and $[\ell_l, \ell_r]$ the interval bounded by its nearest valleys (scipy's *find_peaks* convention).

### Bayes factor

The two metrics above are descriptive — they summarise the trace but don't say "this is ping with probability $P$". The Bayesian alternative is to fit two generative recipes to each trace and report the log-odds. We work on the raster directly (the pooled E spike times $\{t_1, \dots, t_n\}$ over the post-onset window $[0, T]$), so every spike's exact timing contributes and there's no bin-width choice to defend.

**Async**: homogeneous Poisson with per-neuron rate $\lambda_a$,

$$
\log P(\{t_i\} \mid \text{async}, \lambda_a) \;=\; n \log(N_E \lambda_a) \;-\; N_E \lambda_a T.
$$

**Ping**: same Poisson scaffold but with a periodically modulated rate,

$$
\lambda_p(t) \;=\; \lambda_0 \, \big(1 + A \cos(2\pi f t + \phi)\big),
$$

amplitude $A \in [0, 1]$, gamma frequency $f$, phase $\phi$, baseline $\lambda_0$. $A = 0$ collapses ping to async, so async is nested inside ping. The spike-time log-likelihood is

$$
\log P(\{t_i\} \mid \text{ping}) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log\!\big(N_E \, \lambda_p(t_i)\big) \;-\; N_E \lambda_0 T,
$$

up to end-effect corrections of order $1/(fT)$ in the integral. The sum over spike times is where the rhythm shows up — spikes near the bursts contribute big log-rates, spikes in the troughs contribute small ones.

The Bayes factor is the ratio of marginal likelihoods,

$$
\log\mathrm{BF} \;=\; \log P(\{t_i\} \mid \text{ping}) - \log P(\{t_i\} \mid \text{async}),
$$

with the right-hand side marginalised over nuisance parameters: $\phi$ analytically (a uniform $[0, 2\pi)$ prior gives a Bessel-style closed form), $f$ on a grid across the gamma band, and $A, \lambda_0, \lambda_a$ via Laplace approximation around their MLEs. Marginalisation brings an automatic Occam penalty: ping has more knobs and only beats async when the data demand the structure. $\log\mathrm{BF}$ scales linearly with the total spike count $n \approx N_E \lambda_0 T$ — a 250 ms trace from $N_E$ = 512 neurons at $\sim$10 Hz already carries $\sim$1280 spikes of evidence.

The per-frame output is $\log\mathrm{BF}$ in nats (positive favours ping, zero undecided), converted to $P(\text{ping} \mid D) = \sigma(\log\mathrm{BF})$ under equal prior odds.
