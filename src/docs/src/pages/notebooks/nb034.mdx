## Introduction

[nb033](/notebooks/nb033/) derives a 4D mean-field reduction of PING — rates $E, I$ plus synaptic conductances $g_e^I, g_i^E$ — and finds a Hopf bifurcation at $I_\text{ext}^\star = 1.30$ with $f^\star \approx 24.5$ Hz, matching the gamma rhythm observed in spiking simulations. The textbook 2D Wilson-Cowan reduction with our diagonals ($W^{EE} = W^{II} = 0$) cannot Hopf — its trace is identically negative, so the silent fixed point is always stable.

This raises a natural question, suggested by Tim: can a 2D model recover the Hopf if we add an explicit cubic self-feedback term to $E$, as in the FitzHugh-Nagumo / Wilson-1999 framework? The cubic plays the role that the synaptic phase delays play in 4D: a saturating self-suppression that stabilises an unstable fixed point at finite amplitude. This entry argues that the answer is **no** — not because the cubic is wrong, but because it stabilises something that, in our network, never destabilises in the first place. The negative result pins down which structural feature of the 4D system is load-bearing for the oscillation.

## The 2D-cubic reduction

Taylor-expand the excitatory gain $\Phi_E$ around its inflection, keeping odd terms (even terms vanish by the inflection symmetry):

$$
\Phi_E(\text{drive}) \;\approx\; \Phi_E(0) + a\,\text{drive} + b\,\text{drive}^3 \tag{1}
$$

For a sigmoid $\Phi_E(x) = r_\text{max}/(1 + e^{-(x-\theta)/k})$, one has $a = \Phi'_E(\theta) = r_\text{max}/(4k) > 0$ and $b < 0$ — the cubic *suppresses* the linear response away from the inflection. Substituting drive $= I_\text{ext} - W^{IE} I$ into the rate equation and absorbing constants gives the FitzHugh-Nagumo form

$$
\tau_E\,\dot E = -E + \alpha\,E - \beta\,E^3 - \gamma\,I + \alpha\,I_\text{ext} \tag{2a}
$$

$$
\tau_I\,\dot I = -I + \delta\,E \tag{2b}
$$

with $\alpha = a$, $\beta = -b > 0$, $\gamma = a\,W^{IE}$, $\delta = \Phi'_I(0)\,W^{EI}$.

## When this system Hopfs

The Jacobian at a fixed point $E^\star$ is

$$
J = \begin{pmatrix} (-1 + \alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2)/\tau_E & -\gamma/\tau_E \\ \delta/\tau_I & -1/\tau_I \end{pmatrix} \tag{3}
$$

For the trace to cross zero — the precondition for a Hopf — we need

$$
\frac{-1 + \alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2}{\tau_E} - \frac{1}{\tau_I} = 0
\;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\;
\alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2 \;=\; 1 + \frac{\tau_E}{\tau_I}. \tag{4}
$$

The right-hand side is bounded below by 1 (and strictly larger when $\tau_E > \tau_I$). The left-hand side has a maximum at $E^\star = 0$, where it equals $\alpha$. So a necessary condition for the trace to ever reach zero is

$$
\boxed{\;\alpha > 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I.\;} \tag{5}
$$

The mechanism is intuitive: the silent fixed point must first be linearly destabilised ($\alpha > 1$ buys positive feedback from the E-loop), and only then does the cubic $-3\beta(E^\star)^2$ stabilise the resulting limit cycle at finite amplitude (supercritical Hopf, $A \propto \sqrt{I_\text{ext} - I_\text{ext}^\star}$). Without (5) the trace is negative at *every* fixed point and no oscillation can emerge.

## Why our network fails the condition

The linear gain $\alpha = a = \Phi'_E(\theta)$ depends only on the slope of $\Phi_E$ at the inflection. For our sigmoid (steepness $k_E = 0.2$, $r_\text{max} = 0.2$ ms$^{-1}$) this gives

$$
\alpha = \frac{r_\text{max}}{4 k_E} = \frac{0.2}{4 \cdot 0.2} = 0.25.
$$

With $\tau_E/\tau_I = 4$, the threshold (5) requires $\alpha > 5$. We have $\alpha = 0.25$, a factor of twenty below. The trace stays strictly negative for every $I_\text{ext}$, and no Hopf exists. Crucially, the cubic plays no role in this conclusion — it can only damp oscillations, not create them. The system fails the precondition before $\beta$ enters the picture.

The structural reason is that the linear gain $\alpha$ measures *positive self-feedback on $E$*. In the canonical Wilson-Cowan 1972 model this comes from an E→E self-coupling $W^{EE}$, which renormalises the effective gain to $\alpha_\text{eff} = W^{EE}\,\Phi'_E$ — with $W^{EE} \approx 12$ in their model, $\alpha_\text{eff}$ comfortably exceeds 1 and the 2D Hopf works through the textbook mechanism. Our PING architecture has $W^{EE} = 0$ by construction (Börgers-style: only E→I and I→E recurrence). There is nothing to renormalise $\alpha$ above its bare sigmoid value, and the cubic — which provides amplitude saturation, not linear gain — cannot rescue the trace.

## Conclusion

So the 2D-cubic reduction is mathematically sound but structurally inappropriate for our architecture. The cubic delivers (B) — bounded limit-cycle amplitude — but cannot deliver (A) — linear destabilisation. For our $W^{EE} = 0$ network, the destabilisation comes from elsewhere entirely: the synaptic phase delays $\tau_\text{AMPA}, \tau_\text{GABA}$ in the full 4D system. Eliminating them via the cubic-from-Taylor route loses the very mechanism that produces the oscillation.

This pins the 4D analysis from [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/): synaptic timescales are not a refinement that can be adiabatically eliminated. They are the load-bearing structural feature. A faithful 2D reduction would have to retain them — most naturally as a delay-differential equation — and the resulting system would not be polynomial in $(E, I)$.
