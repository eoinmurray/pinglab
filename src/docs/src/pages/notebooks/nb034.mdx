## Introduction

[nb033](/notebooks/nb033/) develops the 4D mean-field reduction of the PING spiking network — rates $E, I$ plus synaptic conductances $g_e^I, g_i^E$ — and finds a clean Hopf bifurcation at $I_\text{ext}^\star = 1.30$ with $f^\star = 24.5$ Hz, matching the gamma frequency in the spiking simulations. The 4D reduction was needed because the textbook 2D Wilson-Cowan system, with our zero diagonal ($W^{EE} = W^{II} = 0$), has $\text{tr}(J) < 0$ identically and cannot Hopf.

This notebook explores an alternative path back to 2D. The idea — suggested by Tim — is to add an explicit cubic self-feedback term to the E equation. This is the standard FitzHugh-Nagumo / Wilson-1999 / Hindmarsh-Rose move: the cubic provides a saturating self-suppression that can substitute for the phase delays the synaptic timescales introduce in the 4D system. The question is whether the resulting 2D model is faithful to our PING network.

The result is mostly negative — the cubic emerges from sigmoid expansion but at coefficient magnitudes too small to drive a Hopf in our parameter regime, because we don't have the positive self-coupling that the canonical Wilson-Cowan model relies on. That negative result is itself diagnostic: it pins down which structural feature of the 4D system is doing the work.

## Method

### Where the cubic comes from

Taylor-expand the sigmoid gain $\Phi_E$ around the silent fixed point, keeping terms to cubic order:

$$
\Phi_E(\text{drive}) \;\approx\; \Phi_E(0) + a\,\text{drive} + b\,\text{drive}^3 \tag{1}
$$

The even-order terms vanish because $\Phi_E$ is approximately odd around its inflection point ($\Phi''_E = 0$ at the inflection of a symmetric sigmoid). For a sigmoid like $r_\text{max}/(1 + e^{-x/k})$, $a > 0$ and $b < 0$ — the leading correction *suppresses* the response away from the inflection. With drive $= I_\text{ext} - W^{IE} I$, substituting (1) into the rate equation and using self-consistency at the fixed point to re-express the cubic in terms of $E$ itself, the system reduces to the FitzHugh-Nagumo form:

$$
\tau_E\,\dot E = -E + \alpha\,E - \beta\,E^3 - \gamma\,I + \alpha\,I_\text{ext} \tag{2a}
$$

$$
\tau_I\,\dot I = -I + \delta\,E \tag{2b}
$$

with $\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta > 0$ constants read off from the gain expansion: $\alpha$ from the linear part of $\Phi_E$ post-substitution, $\beta$ from the cubic part (positive because of the sign flip on $b$), $\gamma = a \cdot W^{IE}$, and $\delta$ from the linearisation of $\Phi_I$.

### Why the cubic *should* enable Hopf

Compute the Jacobian of (2a)–(2b) at a fixed point $E^\star$:

$$
J = \begin{pmatrix} (-1 + \alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2)/\tau_E & -\gamma/\tau_E \\ \delta/\tau_I & -1/\tau_I \end{pmatrix} \tag{3}
$$

For small $E^\star$ near the silent fixed point, the diagonal $\partial \dot E / \partial E \approx (-1 + \alpha)/\tau_E$, which is **positive when $\alpha > 1$**. The trace becomes

$$
\text{tr}(J) = (-1 + \alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2)/\tau_E - 1/\tau_I
$$

which crosses zero when $\alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2 = 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I$. Past it, the cubic $-3\beta(E^\star)^2$ stabilises the limit cycle at finite amplitude — supercritical Hopf with $A \propto \sqrt{I_\text{ext} - I_\text{ext}^\star}$.

The mechanism: the $E^3$ term provides the saturating self-feedback that, in the 4D system, the synaptic phase delays provide. Both routes give the same near-Hopf normal form.

### Why it doesn't work for our system

The catch is the value of $\alpha$. For our sigmoid $\Phi_E$ with parameters chosen to roughly match the LIF gain (inflection at 1.5, steepness $k_E = 0.2$, $r_\text{max} = 0.2$ ms$^{-1}$), the Taylor coefficient $a = \Phi'_E(\theta) = r_\text{max}/(4k_E) = 0.25$. So $\alpha = 0.25$, and the diagonal entry $(-1 + \alpha)/\tau_E = -0.0375$ — **strongly damped**. The trace can never cross zero. No Hopf.

This is a structural feature of our network: we don't have $W^{EE}$ (E→E self-excitation), which is what makes $\alpha$ large in the canonical Wilson-Cowan model. Wilson and Cowan's $c_1$ is the E→E coupling strength; with $c_1 = 12$ and a steep sigmoid, the effective gain $c_1 \Phi'_E$ at the operating point easily exceeds unity, and the 2D system Hopfs through the standard mechanism. Our $W^{EE} = 0$ removes this path entirely.

The cubic-from-gain-expansion does not rescue the situation. The cubic provides *suppression* (stabilisation), but it doesn't generate the positive feedback needed to destabilise the silent fixed point in the first place. You need both:

1. **Linear destabilisation** of the silent fixed point as $I_\text{ext}$ grows — comes from $\alpha > 1$ (needs $W^{EE}$ or similar).
2. **Cubic stabilisation** at finite amplitude — comes from $\beta > 0$ (from sigmoid expansion).

We have (2) for free; we lack (1).

| coefficient | value | role |

$\alpha$ is well below the $1 + \tau_E/\tau_I = 5$ threshold needed for $\text{tr}(J) = 0$. The Jacobian's trace is always negative, and the diagonal element $(-1 + 0.25)/\tau_E$ shows the silent fixed point is stable to small perturbations regardless of $I_\text{ext}$.

### What would be needed

The 2D-cubic system *could* Hopf if we added one of:

- **A positive linear self-coupling** in the E equation: $+ \alpha_\text{ext} E$ with $\alpha_\text{ext}$ large enough that the total effective linear gain $\alpha + \alpha_\text{ext} > 1$. The most direct way is to allow a non-zero $W^{EE}$ — but our network architecture has $W^{EE} = 0$ by construction (Börgers-style PING).

- **A delay-induced effective self-coupling** from adiabatic elimination of the synaptic dynamics with finite-$\tau$ corrections. The 4D system in [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/) does this implicitly via $\tau_\text{AMPA}$ and $\tau_\text{GABA}$; in the limit where these are small but non-zero, the slow-fast separation can be expanded perturbatively, and the leading correction to the textbook 2D form is *not* a cubic — it's a delayed coupling. This is mathematically more involved than the cubic-from-Taylor route, and recovers the same dynamics as the 4D system to leading order.

So the cubic-in-rate route is mathematically attractive but structurally inappropriate for our architecture. The fact that the cubic is the natural reduction for Wilson and Cowan's 1972 model with $c_1 > 0$ but breaks for our $W^{EE} = 0$ model is itself a useful pin: **the oscillation in our system is genuinely a 4D phenomenon driven by synaptic phase delays, not a 2D phenomenon driven by self-coupling and saturation**.

## Discussion

The negative result here narrows the explanation of PING's bifurcation behaviour:

- The textbook **2D Wilson-Cowan** with our diagonals can't Hopf (trace argument, [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/)).
- Adding a **cubic from sigmoid expansion** doesn't fix it because we lack the positive self-coupling that makes $\alpha > 1$ possible.
- The **4D extension with explicit synaptic dynamics** does Hopf, and the bifurcation frequency matches nb024's measured gamma.

So the synaptic timescales aren't an optional refinement — they're the load-bearing structural feature that produces the oscillation. Any reduction that drops them loses the phenomenon.

A direction for future work: derive the 2D reduction more carefully via singular perturbation theory in the small parameters $\tau_\text{AMPA}/\tau_E, \tau_\text{GABA}/\tau_E$. The result is *not* a polynomial system in $(E, I)$ — it's a delay-differential equation, and the delay is what couples back to give the Hopf. Whether that 2D-with-delay system is cleaner than the 4D-ODE system depends on which tools one wants to use.
