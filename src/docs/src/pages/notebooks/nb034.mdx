## Introduction

[nb033](/notebooks/nb033/) shows that the 4D PING mean-field â€” rates $E, I$ plus synaptic conductances $g_e^I, g_i^E$ â€” Hopfs at $I_\text{ext}^\star = 1.30$ with $f^\star \approx 24.5$ Hz, while the textbook 2D Wilson-Cowan reduction with our zero diagonals ($W^{EE} = W^{II} = 0$) cannot Hopf at all: its trace stays strictly negative. A natural fix (suggested by Tim) is to put back the missing nonlinearity as an explicit cubic self-feedback in $E$, Ă  la FitzHugh-Nagumo. This entry derives that 2D-cubic system from first principles and shows it still cannot Hopf for our network â€” because the cubic supplies amplitude saturation, not the positive linear feedback the architecture lacks.

## Derivation

Start from the 2D Wilson-Cowan equations without self-coupling:

$$
\tau_E\,\dot E = -E + \Phi_E\!\left(I_\text{ext} - W^{IE} I\right) \tag{1a}
$$

$$
\tau_I\,\dot I = -I + \Phi_I\!\left(W^{EI} E\right) \tag{1b}
$$

with sigmoid gains $\Phi_{E,I}(x) = r_\text{max}/(1 + e^{-(x-\theta)/k})$. Two reductions get us to a polynomial system.

**Step 1 â€” Taylor-expand $\Phi_E$ at its inflection.** Let $u = I_\text{ext} - W^{IE} I - \theta_E$ be the drive offset. Sigmoids are odd around the inflection, so even-order terms vanish:

$$
\Phi_E(\theta_E + u) = \Phi_E(\theta_E) + a\,u + \tfrac{1}{6} b\,u^3 + O(u^5) \tag{2}
$$

with $a = \Phi'_E(\theta_E) = r_\text{max}/(4 k_E) > 0$ and $b = \Phi'''_E(\theta_E) < 0$.

**Step 2 â€” Linearise $\Phi_I$** around the silent state: $\Phi_I(W^{EI} E) \approx \Phi'_I(0)\,W^{EI}\,E$.

**Step 3 â€” Express the cubic in $E$ rather than $u$.** At leading order the linearised $E$ dynamics give $E^\star \approx a\,u$ at any quasi-steady drive, so $u \approx E/a$. Substituting back into the cubic term, $\tfrac{1}{6} b\,u^3 \to (b/6 a^3)\,E^3 \equiv -\beta\,E^3$ with $\beta \equiv -b/(6 a^3) > 0$.

Folding constants into $I_\text{ext}$ and writing $\alpha \equiv a$, $\gamma \equiv a\,W^{IE}$, $\delta \equiv \Phi'_I(0)\,W^{EI}$ leaves the FitzHugh-Nagumo system:

$$
\tau_E\,\dot E = -E + \alpha\,E - \beta\,E^3 - \gamma\,I + \alpha\,I_\text{ext} \tag{3a}
$$

$$
\tau_I\,\dot I = -I + \delta\,E \tag{3b}
$$

## Why the cubic cannot save us

The Jacobian of (3) at a fixed point $E^\star$ is

$$
J = \begin{pmatrix} (-1 + \alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2)/\tau_E & -\gamma/\tau_E \\ \delta/\tau_I & -1/\tau_I \end{pmatrix}, \tag{4}
$$

and a Hopf requires $\text{tr}(J)$ to cross zero. Rearranging $\text{tr}(J) = 0$:

$$
\alpha - 3\beta (E^\star)^2 = 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I. \tag{5}
$$

The left-hand side is maximised at $E^\star = 0$ where it equals $\alpha$. The right-hand side is bounded below by 1. So a necessary condition for *any* fixed point to lose stability is

$$
\boxed{\;\alpha > 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I.\;} \tag{6}
$$

The cubic enters only through the $-3\beta (E^\star)^2$ term, which *lowers* the left-hand side. It can stabilise an already-destabilised fixed point at finite amplitude (the saturating self-suppression that fixes the limit-cycle radius in a supercritical Hopf), but it cannot raise $\alpha$ past the threshold. Linear destabilisation must come from elsewhere.

The parameters are inherited verbatim from the 4D analysis in [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/), so the comparison is on equal footing. Membrane time constants $\tau_E = 20$ ms (pyramidal) and $\tau_I = 5$ ms (fast-spiking interneuron) are the standard literature values; $\tau_E/\tau_I = 4$. The sigmoid saturation $r_\text{max} = 0.2$ ms$^{-1}$ caps the population rate at 200 Hz, matching the LIF saturation rate in the spiking simulations. The remaining sigmoid parameters â€” inflection $\theta_E = 1.5$, steepness $k_E = 0.2$ â€” were tuned in nb033 by sweeping until the 4D system Hopfs at a frequency near the empirically measured PING gamma ($\approx 25$ Hz at $I_\text{ext}^\star = 1.30$). They are precisely the values for which the 4D system *does* work; using them here makes the 2D-cubic failure diagnostic rather than a parameter artefact.

Plugging in:

$$
\alpha = \frac{r_\text{max}}{4 k_E} = \frac{0.2}{4 \cdot 0.2} = 0.25, \qquad 1 + \tau_E/\tau_I = 5.
$$

We have $\alpha = 0.25$ where we need $\alpha > 5$ â€” a factor of twenty off. The trace stays strictly negative for all $I_\text{ext}$ and no Hopf exists. Making the sigmoid steeper ($k_E$ smaller) raises $\alpha$ linearly, but reaching $\alpha = 5$ would require $k_E = 0.01$ â€” a sigmoid that is essentially a step function, qualitatively wrong as a gain model for an LIF population near rheobase. The deficit is structural, not tuning-limited.

## What's structurally missing

The linear gain $\alpha$ is the strength of positive feedback on $E$. The canonical Wilson-Cowan 1972 model includes an Eâ†’E self-coupling $W^{EE}$, which renormalises $\alpha \to W^{EE}\,\Phi'_E$; with $W^{EE} \approx 12$ and a steep sigmoid this comfortably clears the threshold, and the cubic reduction Hopfs by the standard mechanism. Our BĂ¶rgers-style PING architecture has $W^{EE} = 0$ â€” there is no E-loop to renormalise $\alpha$, and so the 2D-cubic route is structurally inapplicable regardless of how the cubic is tuned.

The destabilisation that does drive the 4D Hopf in [nb033](/notebooks/nb033/) comes from a different mechanism entirely: the synaptic phase delays $\tau_\text{AMPA}, \tau_\text{GABA}$. These have no analogue in (3) â€” adiabatically eliminating them to get a 2D system kills the very feature that produces the oscillation. A faithful 2D reduction would have to keep the synaptic timescales as explicit delays, yielding a delay-differential equation rather than a polynomial ODE.
