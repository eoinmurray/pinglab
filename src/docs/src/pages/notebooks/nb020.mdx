## Introduction

Sweep an upper-bound spike penalty across the [biophysical ladder](/docs/models/) — *cuba* ([nb010](/notebooks/nb010/)), *coba* ([nb011](/notebooks/nb011/)), *ping* ([nb012](/notebooks/nb012/)) — to draw an accuracy / firing-rate frontier for each architecture. The penalty is the right-hand half of the [Cramer et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1109/TNNLS.2020.3044364) regulariser:

$$
\mathcal{L}_\text{fr} \;=\; \lambda_u \sum_i \mathrm{ReLU}\!\bigl(\bar z_i - \theta_u\bigr)^2,
$$

where $\bar z_i$ is unit *i*'s mean per-trial spike count and $\theta_u$ a per-trial budget. Sweeping $\theta_u$ from "off" (no penalty) downward asks each architecture to do the same task with progressively fewer spikes; the resulting (rate, accuracy) trace is the architecture's compressibility curve.

## Method

Every hyperparameter is a hardcoded literal in *src/pinglab/notebooks/nb020.py* per the [runner contract](/docs/the-notebooks/#runner-contract); the runner shells out to [oscilloscope *train*](/docs/the-oscilloscope/#subcommand-train) once per *(model, $\theta_u$)* cell — 18 cells total — and renders the comparison figures. Each cell uses its model's calibrated recipe from the single-model trainer; the only thing varying across cells is *--fr-reg-upper-theta* and whether the regulariser flag is set at all.

- **fr-reg path was CUBANet-only** until this entry. *coba* and *ping* run through COBANet, which had no *last_spike_counts* hook, so the existing oscilloscope regulariser silently no-op'd for those two models. This entry adds the matching gradient-attached accumulator to *COBANet.forward* (per-layer (B, n_e) tensor, mirrors *CUBANet.rate_counts*) so the penalty applies uniformly across the ladder.
- **same recipes as nb010/11/12** — every other knob (lr, w_in, surrogate slope, readout, batch size, w_out scaling) is identical to each model's calibrated single-model trainer. Differences across cells are attributable to the regulariser alone.
- **$\theta_u$ in spikes per trial**, not Hz. With *T = 200 ms* the conversion is $\theta_u \cdot 5$ for the rate equivalent. The regulariser only fires for units whose mean per-trial spike count exceeds $\theta_u$; populations whose entire mass sits below the budget feel zero gradient pressure.

## Discussion

The frontier in Figure 1 separates the three architectures along an axis no static comparison can show: how cheaply each can be made to be sparse. Both axes are the *final*-epoch state — pairing a final-epoch rate with an earlier best-epoch accuracy would describe a network configuration that never simultaneously existed. Three distinct shapes emerge.

**cuba is the most compressible.** Rate falls from 90.7 Hz to 5.4 Hz (a 17× compression) for only $\sim$6 pp accuracy loss (87.0% → 80.75%), then hits a cliff between $\theta_u = 0.5$ and $0.2$ where rate finally collapses to 0.7 Hz and accuracy crashes to 69.0%. The calibrated recipe was firing 17× more than needed — the penalty just exposes how much spike budget cuba was wasting at its native operating point.

**coba is the least compressible.** Every Hz removed costs more accuracy than it does for cuba: even the loosest budget (25 Hz) costs 5.5 pp (82.0% → 76.5%); at $\theta_u = 1$ Hz coba reaches 7.9 Hz and 70.75%; at $\theta_u = 0.5$ Hz it sags to 65.75% on 2.8 Hz; the most aggressive budget collapses to 61.25% on 0.4 Hz. Conductance synapses scale each spike's drive by $(V - E_\text{rev})$, which is small when $V$ is near rest, so each individual spike contributes less voltage change to the readout than a current-based equivalent. The readout has to integrate more spikes to reach the same logit, so removing them hurts.

**ping is essentially $\theta_u$-invariant.** Final accuracy stays tightly clustered between 80.25% and 82.5% across the entire grid; rate stays near 3 Hz regardless of budget. The cyan cluster of overlapping points in Figure 1 is the punchline: there's no compressibility curve because ping is already past where the curve would land. The I-loop's circuit-level inhibition is enforcing dynamic sparsity gradient-free; the regulariser is asking ping to do something it was already doing.

The bottom panel of Figure 2 makes the mechanism visible. cuba and coba's achieved-rate lines bend smoothly downward as $\theta_u$ tightens — they're actively compressing in response to gradient pressure. ping's line is flat at ~3–4 Hz across the whole grid. cuba sits roughly on the *rate = $\theta_u$* diagonal (the optimiser nails the budget), coba sits above (refuses to compress further once the cost gets too high), ping is below it from the start.

Two of these three architectures have a meaningful frontier; one of them is its own frontier.

## Next steps

1. **Train ping at higher baseline** (lower *ei_strength*) so its $\lambda$ = off rate matches cuba/coba (e.g. ~80 Hz). Re-sweep, ask whether the I-loop still grants sparsity-for-free when the network has been *forced* to fire fast first, or whether ping's flat frontier is just a property of its calibrated working point and not the architecture itself.
2. **Push past the cuba cliff.** The cliff between $\theta_u = 0.5$ and $0.2$ (5.4 → 0.7 Hz) hides where exactly cuba's accuracy collapses. A fine-grained sweep through $\theta_u \in [0.2, 0.5]$ should resolve the knee.
3. **Add a lower-bound penalty** (left half of Cramer 2022, $\mathrm{ReLU}(\theta_l - \bar z_i)^2$) to keep cells from collapsing into silence at the tightest budgets, and report the band-constrained frontier instead of a single-sided one.
