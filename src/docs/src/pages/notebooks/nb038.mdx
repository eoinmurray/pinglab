## Setup

PING and COBA baseline definitions and training recipe are in [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/). This entry runs three inference-only probes on the trained baselines: input-rate sweep (f-I curve), COBA → PING I-loop transfer at eval time, and readout latency.

## Input-rate sweep

The trained PING baseline (seed 42, $\theta_u =$ off) is replayed on MNIST digit 0 with the Poisson input rate swept from 0 to ≈23 Hz in ten steps (2.56 Hz spacing). Spike trains are recorded per panel.

<Figure id="Figure 1" title="Input-rate sweep on trained PING" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/rate_rasters__ping.png" alt="Ten stacked raster panels showing trained PING on MNIST digit 0 at input rates from 0 to about 23 Hz; E spikes in black, I spikes in red. Each panel is labelled with the input rate, the per-cell E rate, and the per-cell I rate. At low input (0–5 Hz) the network is silent (E and I rates both 0). At ≈8 Hz input the I-loop engages (I rate jumps from 0 to ≈10 Hz). Above engagement the E rate saturates near 4–5 Hz while the I rate climbs from ≈10 to ≈25 Hz.">
  Trained PING replayed on MNIST digit 0 across ten input-rate values; E (black) above I (red) per panel.
</Figure>

At rate = 0 the network is silent. Above a recruitment threshold a few E units fire, the I loop catches, and the gamma cadence appears. Once the loop is locked the cycle period is invariant to further increases in input rate across the rest of the sweep.

A subtlety in the just-engaged panels (e.g. input ≈ 5 Hz, E = 1.7 Hz, I = 0): the E spikes look mildly clustered even though the I-loop hasn't fired. This is feedforward — not PING. The Poisson input stream has natural temporal fluctuations, the digit-0 input is spatially correlated, and the trained $W_\text{in}$ has learned to be selective for digit-relevant pixels. So the cells that fire are the input-tuned subset, and they fire together during input-rich windows. The participation pattern is partially preserved from training; the gamma timing is not, because the loop is genuinely off.

Extending the sweep over 0–100 Hz and plotting per-cell mean firing rate against input rate gives the population f-I curve.

<Figure id="Figure 2" title="Trained PING f-I curve (MNIST digit 0)" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/fi_curve__ping.png" alt="Single panel: input Poisson rate (Hz, per channel) on x-axis vs per-cell mean firing rate on y-axis. Two curves — E (black circles) and I (red squares). The network is silent below 5 Hz input; at 5 Hz input E fires at 1.65 Hz but I is still silent. At 10 Hz input I engages (jumps to 15 Hz) and E rises to 3.78 Hz. Above engagement E climbs slowly from ~4 Hz at 10 Hz input to ~10 Hz at 100 Hz input; I rises from 15 Hz to ~50 Hz across the same range.">
  Trained PING (seed 42, $\theta_u =$ off) replayed on MNIST digit 0 with the Poisson input rate swept from 0 to 100 Hz in 5 Hz steps. Per-cell mean E (black) and I (red) firing rates over the 200 ms trial.
</Figure>

Three features. (1) **Recruitment threshold between 5 and 10 Hz input**: I jumps from 0 to 15 Hz across that interval, marking the cliff in classical f-I form. (2) **A narrow sub-recruitment regime** at 5 Hz input where E fires (1.65 Hz) but I does not — the "E spikes, I silent" state from the rate-floor argument made directly visible. (3) **Saturation**: above ≈30 Hz input the I-loop bounds both populations — E plateaus around 8–11 Hz, I around 35–50 Hz, despite 3× more drive. This is gamma physics: cycle period set by $\tau_\text{AMPA} + \tau_\text{GABA}$ limits spikes per cell per trial regardless of how hard the input pushes.

Figure 1 conflates two effects: the network's response to drive, and the readout's selectivity for digit-0-shaped input through trained $W_\text{in}$. To isolate the bare drive-response, repeat the sweep with a **spatially uniform input** — every input channel firing at the same Poisson rate, with no digit structure — and compare PING against COBA on the same axes.

<Figure id="Figure 3" title="Population f-I curves under uniform Poisson input" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/fi_curve_uniform.png" alt="Four-panel figure (2x2). Top row: full input range 0–100 Hz. Top-left (PING): E rate in black, I rate in red. PING engages at 2 Hz input — I jumps from 0 to 13 Hz, E settles near 3-5 Hz. At 100 Hz input E reaches ~27 Hz, I reaches ~125 Hz. Top-right (COBA): E rate in black, I rate flat at zero (no I-loop). E climbs steeply from 0 to ~300 Hz, saturating near the refractory ceiling. Bottom row: 0–10 Hz zoom at 0.1 Hz resolution. Bottom-left (PING): the recruitment cliff resolves into a sharp step at ~1.6–1.8 Hz where I lifts off zero. Bottom-right (COBA): E rises smoothly from 0 to ~130 Hz with no cliff feature.">
  Population f-I curves for trained PING (left) and trained COBA (right) under spatially uniform Poisson input — all 784 input channels firing at the same rate, no MNIST structure. Each panel shows E (black) and I (red) per-cell firing rates. Top row: full range, 26 input rates from 0 to 100 Hz. Bottom row: 0–10 Hz zoom at 0.1 Hz resolution (101 points), exposing the recruitment cliff. All points averaged over 32 trials. COBA's I curve sits at zero because *--ei-strength = 0* disables the I-loop.
</Figure>

Two qualitative responses to the same drive. COBA (top-right) rises sharply across 1–10 Hz input then saturates toward its refractory ceiling (≈300 Hz at 100 Hz input — every E cell firing essentially as fast as it can); the I rate stays at zero because the loop is off. PING (top-left) shows the recruitment cliff at 1–2 Hz input (I jumps from 0 to 13 Hz) and then holds E roughly 10–14× lower than COBA across the entire post-engagement range: at 5 Hz input PING fires at 4.9 Hz vs COBA's 69 Hz; at 100 Hz input PING fires at 26.5 Hz vs COBA's 297 Hz. The I curve sitting above the E curve at every input rate is the visual signature of the loop — I has to fire harder than E to suppress it. The I-loop is doing dynamic-range compression — pulling drive that would saturate the E population into a bounded gamma cadence.

The bottom row zooms into 0–10 Hz at 0.1 Hz resolution. PING (bottom-left) resolves the recruitment cliff into a sharp step: the I rate is identically zero up to ≈1.6 Hz input, then lifts off to ≈12 Hz over a narrow ≈0.5 Hz interval. This is the empirical signature of the saddle-node bifurcation in $f$-space, projected onto the input-rate axis. COBA (bottom-right) shows no cliff feature — its E curve is monotone-smooth across the same range. The recruitment threshold here (≈1.6 Hz input) is lower than in Figure 1 (5–10 Hz) because uniform input drives all channels simultaneously, while MNIST drives only digit-relevant pixels.

## COBA → PING I-loop transfer

Inference-only: the trained COBA baseline (seed 42, $\theta_u =$ off) is loaded and *ei_strength* is overridden at eval time across 11 values from 0 to 1. $W_\text{in}$ and $W_\text{out}$ load from the COBA checkpoint; $W_{ei}$ and $W_{ie}$ are freshly initialised (the COBA checkpoint has these at zero, so skipping the load preserves a functional I-loop). No retraining.

<Figure id="Figure 4" title="COBA → PING I-loop transfer at inference" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/ei_rasters.png" alt="Stacked raster panels showing trained COBA replayed with increasing inference-time ei_strength.">
  Trained COBA replayed at six inference-time *ei_strength* values; same trial, same weights, fresh I-loop each row.
</Figure>

At *ei = 0* the asynchronous-dense COBA pattern persists. By *ei ≈ 0.4* the same feedforward weights produce gamma cycles. The PING dynamics come from the inhibitory architecture, not from training — the same weights that fired at 52 Hz without the loop fire at ≈5 Hz with the loop wired up.

<Figure id="Figure 5" title="ei_strength sweep — accuracy" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/ei_acc_sweep.png" alt="Test accuracy vs inference-time ei_strength on COBA-trained weights, with horizontal lines at the trained COBA baseline and at 10% chance.">
  Test accuracy vs inference-time *ei_strength* on trained COBA weights.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 6" title="ei_strength sweep — E and I rates" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/ei_rates_sweep.png" alt="Mean E (hidden) and I (inhibitory) population firing rates per test pass versus ei_strength.">
  Mean E and I rates across the same *ei_strength* sweep.
</Figure>

Accuracy stays within ≈12 pp of the COBA baseline across the full *ei_strength* range. E rate falls monotonically from ≈52 Hz (*ei = 0*) to ≈5 Hz (*ei = 1*); I rate rises from zero to ≈23 Hz. Suppression is continuous in the loop strength.

## Readout latency

How quickly does each network reach a confident classification during a trial? At each 5 ms checkpoint we compute the running-average readout from the accumulated output-LIF membrane potential up to that point and evaluate test accuracy. Baselines at $\theta_u =$ off, seed 42.

<Figure id="Figure 7" title="Readout latency — accuracy vs time and vs spike count" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/latency.png" alt="Two panels. Left: test accuracy vs trial time (ms) — COBA (red) leads early (73.5% at 20 ms vs PING 68.5%), curves cross near 100 ms, PING finishes ahead (85% vs 83%). Right: test accuracy vs cumulative E spikes per sample — PING's curve is dramatically left-shifted, reaching 81% at ≈530 spikes while COBA needs ≈5,700.">
  Left: test accuracy at each 5 ms checkpoint. Right: the same accuracy plotted against cumulative E spikes per sample at each checkpoint. Baselines, $\theta_u =$ off, seed 42. PING in black, COBA in red. Chance at 10% (dotted).
</Figure>

The left panel shows COBA reaching higher accuracy faster in wall-clock time: 73.5% at 20 ms vs PING's 68.5%. The curves cross near 100 ms and PING finishes ahead (85.0% vs 83.0%). COBA fires asynchronously from timestep 1 at ≈85 Hz, so by 20 ms the readout has ≈1.7 spikes/cell to average over. PING needs the gamma cycle to self-assemble — the first structured burst appears at ≈10-15 ms — so the readout sees little useful signal before then.

The right panel reframes the same data with cumulative E spikes on the x-axis. At the crossover accuracy (≈81%), PING has used ≈530 spikes vs COBA's ≈5,700 — roughly 11× fewer. PING's curve is shifted an order of magnitude to the left. Each gamma burst is a concentrated class-discriminative event, so the readout converges faster per spike than COBA's noisy per-spike average. COBA wins on absolute latency (more spikes available earlier). PING wins on spike efficiency (fewer spikes to reach the same accuracy). If the relevant metric is minimum spikes to decision rather than minimum time to decision, PING is strictly better.
