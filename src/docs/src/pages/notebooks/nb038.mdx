## COBA → PING I-loop transfer

Inference-only: the trained COBA baseline (seed 42, $\theta_u =$ off) is loaded and *ei_strength* is overridden at eval time across 11 values from 0 to 1. $W_\text{in}$ and $W_\text{out}$ load from the COBA checkpoint; $W_{ei}$ and $W_{ie}$ are freshly initialised (the COBA checkpoint has these at zero, so skipping the load preserves a functional I-loop). No retraining.

At *ei = 0* the asynchronous-dense COBA pattern persists. By *ei ≈ 0.4* the same feedforward weights produce gamma cycles. The PING dynamics come from the inhibitory architecture, not from training — the same weights that fired at 52 Hz without the loop fire at ≈5 Hz with the loop wired up.

## Readout latency

How quickly does each network reach a confident classification during a trial? At each 5 ms checkpoint we compute the running-average readout from the accumulated output-LIF membrane potential up to that point and evaluate test accuracy. Baselines at $\theta_u =$ off, seed 42.

<Figure id="Figure 4" title="Readout latency — accuracy vs time and vs spike count" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/latency.png" alt="Two panels. Left: test accuracy vs trial time (ms) — COBA (red) leads early (73.5% at 20 ms vs PING 68.5%), curves cross near 100 ms, PING finishes ahead (85% vs 83%). Right: test accuracy vs cumulative E spikes per sample — PING's curve is dramatically left-shifted, reaching 81% at ≈530 spikes while COBA needs ≈5,700.">
  Left: test accuracy at each 5 ms checkpoint. Right: the same accuracy plotted against cumulative E spikes per sample at each checkpoint. Baselines, $\theta_u =$ off, seed 42. PING in black, COBA in red. Chance at 10% (dotted).
</Figure>

The left panel shows COBA reaching higher accuracy faster in wall-clock time: 73.5% at 20 ms vs PING's 68.5%. The curves cross near 100 ms and PING finishes ahead (85.0% vs 83.0%). COBA fires asynchronously from timestep 1 at ≈85 Hz, so by 20 ms the readout has ≈1.7 spikes/cell to average over. PING needs the gamma cycle to self-assemble — the first structured burst appears at ≈10-15 ms — so the readout sees little useful signal before then.

The right panel reframes the same data with cumulative E spikes on the x-axis. At the crossover accuracy (≈81%), PING has used ≈530 spikes vs COBA's ≈5,700 — roughly 11× fewer. PING's curve is shifted an order of magnitude to the left. Each gamma burst is a concentrated class-discriminative event, so the readout converges faster per spike than COBA's noisy per-spike average. COBA wins on absolute latency (more spikes available earlier). PING wins on spike efficiency (fewer spikes to reach the same accuracy). If the relevant metric is minimum spikes to decision rather than minimum time to decision, PING is strictly better.
