## Setup

[nb040](/notebooks/nb040/) showed that CUBA-PING's I-loop architecturally clamps the hidden-E rate near 1 Hz, while the CUBA-no-PING control sits at tens of Hz. [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) showed that the COBA-PING baselines walk the same rate–accuracy frontier via the explicit firing-rate regulariser $L_\text{rate}$. This entry transplants the nb035 sweep to the instant-synapse CUBA family — adding the spike-budget penalty $s_u \cdot \text{relu}(\bar{z} - \theta_u)^2$ to CE loss and sweeping $\theta_u$ on both arms — to ask the same two questions:

1. **Does the penalty walk the no-PING control onto a sparse-spike frontier comparable to PING's?** (the *penalty alone* path to low firing rates)
2. **Does the I-loop architecture stay quiet under the penalty, sitting at its natural floor?** (the *architectural floor* should make the penalty redundant on PING)

Same recipe as nb040 — `cuba-ping` and `cuba-noping` via the oscilloscope, $\Delta t = 1$ ms, $T = 200$ ms, hard reset, no refractory period, no synaptic state, mem-mean output readout, TBPTT $K = 10$, Adam at lr $2 \times 10^{-3}$, batch 64, 5000 training samples, 40 epochs. The penalty is applied to the population-mean spike count $\bar{z} = \mathbb{E}_{i, \text{batch}}[z_i]$ — a single scalar across all hidden neurons and the batch — with $s_u = 10^{-3}$ matching nb036. The grid $\theta_u \in \{\text{off}, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2\}$ spikes-per-trial is identical to nb035/036 so the frontier is directly cross-comparable. At $T = 200$ ms, $\theta_u$ in spk/trial × 5 = Hz, i.e. $\theta_u = 0.2$ corresponds to a 1 Hz target.

## Interpretation

Both architectural and explicit knobs work, but they do different things.

**PING + penalty is flat across the sweep.** For $\theta_u \geq 0.5$ every cell lands at exactly 84.7% / 1.37 Hz / 39.7 Hz I — bit-identical to PING-off. The reason is mechanical: PING's population mean E-rate (≈0.27 spk/trial) sits below every threshold from 0.5 up, so $\text{relu}(\bar{z} - \theta_u) = 0$ exactly, the penalty term vanishes, and the optimisation reduces to the nb040 PING-off problem. The I-loop is already doing all the rate-control work.

The exception is $\theta_u = 0.2$, where the threshold is *below* the population mean. The penalty engages but the optimisation doesn't converge to a low-rate solution — it lands at 6.25 Hz / 82.6%, *higher* than PING-off's rate and slightly lower accuracy. Figure 4's bottom-right panel makes the mechanism visible: the network jumps to a qualitatively different operating regime — dense E firing across the full population, no gamma cycles, I firing every step rather than in bursts. See [bistability](#bistability) below for the trajectory. So the I-loop's natural floor (≈1.4 Hz) is a hard wall — pushing below it with the penalty doesn't help.

**no-PING + penalty walks the frontier cleanly.** From the rate-coded baseline (39 Hz / 90%) down to PING territory (1.28 Hz / 88%) is **30× rate compression at 2.6 pp accuracy cost**. The frontier is monotone, the trajectory is stable on every cell, and at every matched rate against PING the accuracy is comparable or higher. The explicit penalty is doing for the E-only network what the I-loop architecture does for PING — driving the rate down without losing the digit signal.

This reproduces nb035 Figure 5 for the instant-synapse CUBA family. The I-loop and the explicit penalty are *substitutes*: either alone is enough to land the network in the sparse-spike regime at high accuracy. Combining them (PING + tight penalty) doesn't help beyond what the architecture already does, and pushing below the architectural floor with the penalty pays a real cost.

## Bistability

The $\theta_u = 0.2$ PING outlier (Figure 1; Figure 4 bottom-right) traces a clean bifurcation through training. The trajectory has two distinct phases:

- **Phase 1 (epochs 1–28)**: trajectory is *identical* to PING-off. Loss 2.30 → 0.65, accuracy 11% → 82%, E rate climbs to 1.88 Hz, I rate to 66 Hz, gradient norm ≈0.09. The population mean of ≈0.38 spk/trial is only fractionally above $\theta_u = 0.2$, and the gradient on each of 1024 neurons is ≈$3 \times 10^{-7}$ — negligible.
- **Bifurcation at epoch 29**: a one-epoch discontinuity. E rate jumps 1.88 → 4.31 Hz, I rate 66 → 172 Hz, gradient norm 0.090 → 6.45 (a 70× jump in a single epoch).
- **Phase 2 (epochs 29–40)**: settles at a *different attractor*. E rate ≈9 Hz, I rate ≈250 Hz, gradient norm ≈30, accuracy holds at 82–83%, but loss climbs slowly from 0.70 to 1.81.

### Penalty as perturbation, not rate control

The counter-intuitive observation is that the penalty's *intent* is to reduce rate, yet the rate ends up *higher* than PING-off. Mechanically the penalty is doing exactly what's asked of it — at epoch 29+ the gradient norm is ≈30 with almost all of it from the penalty term pulling the population mean down — but the rate refuses to drop. The reason is that the penalty isn't a rate control. It's a *persistent perturbation to the optimisation trajectory in $W_\text{in}$ space*.

Walk through what's happening:

1. **Phase 1 — drift under negligible pressure.** The penalty term contributes ≈$3 \times 10^{-7}$ per neuron — dominated 1000× by the CE gradient. The trajectory tracks PING-off's basin of attraction. But the penalty contribution is *directionally consistent*: every step pushes $W_\text{in}$ slightly away from where pure-CE would put it. Over ~2200 steps the cumulative drift is real.
2. **Bifurcation.** The accumulated drift carries $W_\text{in}$ across a *separatrix* in the recurrent dynamics phase plane. PING's E↔I loop admits at least two stable digit-classifying fixed points: the low-rate gamma-burst attractor (≈1.4 Hz / 40 Hz, what PING-off occupies) and the high-rate dense-firing attractor (≈9 Hz / 250 Hz, Figure 4 bottom-right). One epoch is enough to flip basins.
3. **Trapped in a worse minimum.** Once in the high-rate basin, the penalty becomes massively active (mean ≈1.84 spk/trial, well above $\theta_u = 0.2$; gn ≈30 pulling hard downward). The recurrent dynamics holding the high-rate fixed point are stable against weight changes of any reasonable size — the basin is too deep for gradient descent to climb out. Critically, **the high-rate basin has *worse* CE loss** (1.8 vs 0.5 at PING-off) for the same top-1 accuracy. The optimiser has been nudged out of a good local minimum into a worse one — and the very gradient that nudged it there is now too weak to bring it back.

Accuracy holding at 83% across the bifurcation is a side note: at the new operating point the readout sees many more spike events per trial, so the digit signal is still encoded — just in a denser representation. The *loss* climbs because the logit distribution becomes more extreme (confidently wrong on a few hard samples) while top-1 accuracy is robust to that.

### A generic failure mode for regularised recurrent SNN training

The general pattern, stripped of nb042-specifics:

1. **The forward dynamics have multiple stable fixed points** that all solve the task to similar quality. (Generic for recurrent networks: PING circuits, attractor networks, RNNs with saturating non-linearities, anything with feedback.)
2. **A regulariser adds a small persistent gradient** that biases the optimisation trajectory in some weight direction. The bias is too small per-step to do meaningful work, but it's consistent.
3. **Cumulative drift crosses a separatrix.** Weight space and dynamics-attractor space are linked: small changes in weights can move the system across boundaries between qualitatively different forward-dynamics regimes.
4. **The new attractor has worse loss but is gradient-stable.** The regulariser activates strongly (now the network is firing outside the desired regime), but gradient descent through stable recurrent dynamics can't escape — the gradient passes through but the forward attractor refuses to budge.

The smell of this failure mode in a training log is the one we have here: many epochs of clean convergence, a one-epoch discontinuity, post-discontinuity the regulariser term dominates the gradient but its target metric doesn't move, loss climbs while accuracy holds.

Practical implications:

- **Don't trust monotone loss as a stability indicator.** PING + $\theta_u = 0.2$ has monotonically decreasing loss through ep 28 and looks identical to PING-off. The pathology is invisible until the bifurcation fires.
- **Watch the rate target itself, not just the gradient norm.** If `gn` is large but the regulariser's controlled quantity isn't moving, the gradient is hitting a basin wall, not changing the operating point.
- **Penalty annealing or basin-shaping (e.g. spectral normalisation of $W^{EI}, W^{IE}$) are the principled fixes** — they either avoid creating the separatrix crossing condition or remove the alternative attractor entirely. Adding more aggressive penalty strength makes the problem *worse*, not better, because the perturbation becomes stronger and crosses the separatrix earlier.
- **No-PING avoids this** because its forward dynamics has a single attractor (no recurrent feedback to host multiple fixed points). The penalty becomes a true rate control because there's only one rate-vs-accuracy frontier to walk along.

So the $\theta_u = 0.2$ PING outlier is concrete evidence of a generic failure mode: *regulariser as perturbation, bistable forward dynamics, basin trap*. Worth carrying forward as a warning for any future work that combines an explicit penalty with a recurrent SNN architecture.

### Ways to avoid the bifurcation

A few candidate formulations, ordered from fundamentally preventing the separatrix crossing to practical workarounds that just delay it past the training budget.

**Architectural — no separatrix to cross.**
- **Spectral-norm regulariser on $W^{EI} W^{IE}$.** Instead of penalising the spike count statistic (an output of the dynamics), penalise the recurrent gain itself ($\lambda \|W^{EI} W^{IE}\|_2$). The recurrent gain is what sets the natural firing-rate floor (see [nb040 Section 4](/notebooks/nb040/#bptt-stability)), so lowering it shifts the single attractor continuously rather than pushing the optimiser to escape it. Single attractor, no separatrix, no possible bifurcation. Requires $W^{EI}/W^{IE}$ to become trainable, but that's a one-line change in `CubaPingNet`.
- **Output-side regularisation.** Penalise something on the acyclic readout path — e.g. $\lambda \|W_\text{out}\|_1$. The decoder graph has no recurrent feedback, so no separatrix. Doesn't control rate directly but indirectly encourages sparse-spike solutions (small $W_\text{out}$ → readout needs more spike events to classify → optimiser doesn't push rate up).

**Curriculum — delay the bifurcation past the training budget.**
- **$\theta_u$ annealing.** Start with $\theta_u = \infty$ (off), gradually lower over training. The penalty only switches on after the network has settled into PING-off's low-rate basin; the remaining question is whether the late, weak gradient can still escape — and at small $s_u$ it usually can't.
- **lr annealing post-warmup.** Pre-train without penalty for $K$ epochs to land in the low-rate basin, then drop the learning rate 10× and turn on the penalty. Cumulative drift per epoch shrinks 10×, so you need 10× as many epochs to cross the same separatrix. For bounded training budgets, that prevents the crossing in practice.
- **Cumulative-drift early stopping.** Track $\|W_\text{in}(t) - W_\text{in}(0)\|$ during training and freeze the weights (or drop lr to zero) once it crosses a precharacterised threshold. Detection-based; doesn't prevent the bifurcation in principle but stops it before it fires.

**Surgical gradient interventions.**
- **Detach the penalty's backward through the recurrent loop.** Compute the spike-count summary used by the penalty on a stop-gradient path that doesn't flow through $W^{EI}/W^{IE}$. The penalty's gradient only reaches $W_\text{in}$ via the direct input→V→spike pathway, not via the recurrent Jacobian. Theoretically prevents the kind of cumulative drift that crosses a separatrix in the recurrent phase plane. Implementation is fiddly with autograd and may weaken the penalty's effectiveness.

**What probably won't work.**
- **Smoothing the relu kink** (softplus, huber, smooth-l1) — the kink isn't the problem; the cumulative drift is. Smoothing changes gradient magnitude near the threshold but not its direction.
- **Larger $s_u$** — makes the perturbation stronger and crosses the separatrix sooner. *Gentler* penalty is safer.
- **Lower-bound term** ($\text{relu}(\theta_l - \bar{z})^2$) — homogenises the firing distribution, breaking PING's selective code rather than preserving it. Ends up in a no-PING-like regime.

The honest summary is that the cleanest fix is *architectural*: switch the rate-control knob from output statistics ($\theta_u$ on $\bar{z}$) to recurrent spectral norm. That's a different experiment and probably its own notebook entry — option **PING-spectral vs PING-penalty** as parallel arms of "architectural rate control" comparison. The cheapest empirical confirmation that the bifurcation *can* be avoided without changing the formulation would be a $\theta_u$ annealing run on a single PING cell — same recipe, $\theta_u(\text{epoch}) = \max(0.2,\, 5 \exp(-\text{epoch}/10))$ or similar.

## Findings

The architectural rate-control (I-loop) and the explicit rate-control (spike-budget penalty) are interchangeable in the instant-synapse CUBA family, just as they are in the COBA-PING family from [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/). On no-PING the penalty pulls rate by 30× for ≈3 pp accuracy; on PING the architectural floor already does the work and the penalty is a no-op until $\theta_u$ drops below the natural floor.

The natural follow-ups:

1. **Spectral-norm regulariser on $W^{EI}, W^{IE}$.** The I-loop's natural floor is set by the operator norm of the recurrent product; controlling that directly is the architectural analogue of $\theta_u$. Combined with the penalty it would give a continuous knob on the floor itself.
2. **Lower-bound regulariser $\theta_l$ (Cramer et al. 2022).** Constrains $\bar{z}$ from below so the optimiser doesn't drive cells silent on hard-to-classify samples. Useful for the tightest-$\theta_u$ no-PING cell where some accuracy is being lost to silence.
3. **Scale to full MNIST.** The 5000-sample / 40-epoch run lands no-PING at 90% accuracy; with full data, both arms should reach the ≈98% regime PING-off does in the COBA-PING baselines.
