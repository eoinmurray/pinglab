## Setup

[nb040](/notebooks/nb040/) showed two ways to land a CUBA network in the sparse-spike regime: PING's I-loop architecturally clamps E rate near 1 Hz; the no-PING control sits at tens of Hz. [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) showed that COBA-PING walks the same frontier with an explicit *firing-rate regulariser* — the spike-budget penalty $s_u \cdot \text{relu}(\bar{z} - \theta_u)^2$ added to CE loss.

This entry transplants that sweep to the instant-synapse CUBA family, asking:

1. **Does the penalty walk no-PING onto a sparse-spike frontier?** (the *penalty alone* path)
2. **Does PING stay quiet under the penalty, sitting at its natural floor?** (the *architectural floor* should make the penalty redundant)

Recipe matches nb040 — `cuba-ping` and `cuba-noping` via the oscilloscope, $\Delta t = 1$ ms, $T = 200$ ms, hard reset, mem-mean readout, TBPTT $K = 10$, Adam at lr $2 \times 10^{-3}$, batch 64, 5000 train, 40 epochs. The penalty acts on the population mean $\bar{z} = \mathbb{E}_{i,\text{batch}}[z_i]$ with $s_u = 10^{-3}$. The grid $\theta_u \in \{\text{off}, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2\}$ spikes-per-trial mirrors nb035/036. At $T = 200$ ms, 1 spk/trial = 5 Hz.

## I-loop and penalty are substitutes

The picture reproduces [nb035 Figure 5](/notebooks/nb035/) for the instant-synapse CUBA family: the architectural I-loop and the explicit penalty are *substitutes*. Either alone is enough to land the network in the sparse-spike regime at high accuracy:

- **PING** uses the architecture. The I-loop's recurrent E↔I dynamics enforce a natural firing-rate floor (≈1.4 Hz); the optimiser settles there for any digit-classification task that fits the architecture.
- **no-PING + penalty** uses the explicit term. The penalty applies a gradient that pulls $\bar{z}$ toward $\theta_u$; in the absence of recurrent dynamics, that gradient walks the rate down smoothly without losing the digit signal.

Combining them — PING with a tight penalty — adds nothing beyond what the architecture already does until $\theta_u$ drops below the natural floor, and at that point the combination breaks rather than continues the frontier (next section).

## The θ_u = 0.2 PING bifurcation

The one anomalous cell (Figure 1 black outlier; Figure 4 bottom-right) is concrete evidence of a generic failure mode that's worth recording.

**The trajectory.** Three phases:

- *ep 1–28*: identical to PING-off. Loss 2.30 → 0.65, accuracy 11% → 82%, E rate climbing to 1.88 Hz. Population mean ≈0.38 spk/trial is only fractionally above $\theta_u = 0.2$; the penalty's gradient on each of 1024 neurons is ≈$3 \times 10^{-7}$ — negligible.
- *ep 29*: one-epoch discontinuity. E rate 1.88 → 4.31 Hz, I rate 66 → 172 Hz, gradient norm 0.090 → 6.45 (70× jump).
- *ep 30–40*: settles at a *different attractor*. E rate ≈9 Hz, I rate ≈250 Hz, gradient norm ≈30. Accuracy holds at 82–83%, but loss climbs steadily from 0.70 to 1.81.

### Penalty as perturbation, not rate control

The counter-intuitive observation: the penalty's *intent* is to reduce rate, yet the rate ends up *higher* than PING-off. The penalty *is* pulling down — at ep 29+ the gradient norm is ≈30 with almost all of it from the penalty term — but the rate refuses to drop.

The penalty isn't a rate control. It's a *persistent perturbation to the optimisation trajectory in $W_\text{in}$ space*. During phase 1 the contribution is too small per-step to do useful work but it's *directionally consistent*, and over ~2200 training steps the cumulative drift walks $W_\text{in}$ across a separatrix in the recurrent dynamics phase plane. PING's E↔I loop admits at least two stable digit-classifying fixed points — the low-rate gamma-burst attractor (≈1.4 Hz, what PING-off occupies) and a high-rate dense-firing attractor (≈9 Hz, Figure 4 bottom-right) — and one epoch is enough to flip basins.

Once in the high-rate basin, the recurrent dynamics holding the new fixed point are stable against weight changes of any reasonable size. The penalty is now massively active but the basin is too deep for gradient descent to climb out. **The high-rate basin has *worse* CE loss** (1.8 vs 0.5 at PING-off) for the same top-1 accuracy — the optimiser has been nudged out of a good local minimum into a worse one, and the same gradient that nudged it there is now too weak to bring it back.

### Generic failure mode

Stripped of nb042-specifics:

1. **Forward dynamics have multiple stable fixed points** that all solve the task to similar accuracy. Generic for recurrent networks.
2. **A regulariser adds a small persistent gradient** in some weight direction. Too small per-step to do useful work, but consistent.
3. **Cumulative drift crosses a separatrix.** Weight space and dynamics-attractor space are linked.
4. **The new attractor has worse loss but is gradient-stable.** The regulariser activates strongly but gradient descent can't escape.

Smell test in a training log: many epochs of clean convergence, a one-epoch discontinuity, post-discontinuity the regulariser term dominates the gradient but its target metric doesn't move, loss climbs while accuracy holds. Don't trust monotone loss as a stability indicator. Watch the rate target itself, not just the gradient norm. no-PING avoids this entirely because its forward dynamics has a single attractor.

### How to avoid it

Four families of fixes, from fundamentally preventing the crossing to delaying it past the training budget.

**Architectural (no separatrix to cross).**
- *Spectral-norm regulariser on $W^{EI} W^{IE}$.* Penalise the recurrent gain itself ($\lambda \|W^{EI} W^{IE}\|_2$) rather than the spike-count statistic. The gain *is* what sets the natural firing-rate floor (nb040 Section 4), so lowering it shifts the single attractor continuously — no separatrix.
- *Output-side regularisation.* Penalise something on the acyclic readout path (e.g. $\lambda \|W_\text{out}\|_1$). No recurrent feedback in the readout graph, no separatrix.

**Curriculum (delay past the training budget).**
- $\theta_u$ *annealing*: ramp $\theta_u$ down over training so the penalty only switches on after the network has settled.
- *lr annealing post-warmup*: pre-train without penalty, then drop lr 10× and turn the penalty on. Cumulative drift per epoch shrinks 10×.

**Surgical.** Detach the penalty's backward through the recurrent loop so its gradient only reaches $W_\text{in}$ via the direct input→V→spike path. Theoretically prevents the kind of drift that crosses the separatrix.

**What probably won't work.**
- Smoothing the relu kink (softplus, huber): the kink isn't the issue; cumulative drift is.
- Larger $s_u$: makes the perturbation stronger; crosses the separatrix *sooner*.
- Lower-bound term: homogenises the firing distribution, breaking PING's selective code.

The cleanest fix is architectural — switch the rate-control knob from output statistics to recurrent spectral norm. That's a different experiment and probably its own notebook entry.

## Findings

The I-loop architecture and the explicit spike-budget penalty are substitutes for rate control in the instant-synapse CUBA family. no-PING + penalty walks a clean 30× frontier at ≈3 pp accuracy cost. PING + penalty is a no-op until $\theta_u$ drops below the natural floor, and at that point the combination breaks — the penalty acts as a persistent perturbation that tips the recurrent dynamics into a worse stable attractor.

Next steps:

1. **Spectral-norm regulariser on $W^{EI}, W^{IE}$** — the architectural rate-control knob, parallel to $\theta_u$ but acting on the single attractor instead of perturbing toward a second one.
2. **Lower-bound regulariser $\theta_l$ (Cramer et al. 2022)** on no-PING — pin the rate to a band, useful at the tightest $\theta_u$ where some accuracy is lost to silence.
3. **Scale to full MNIST.** The 5000-sample / 40-epoch run lands no-PING at 90%; full data should push both arms toward the ≈98% regime of the COBA-PING baselines.
