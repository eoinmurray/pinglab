## Setup

[nb040](/notebooks/nb040/) showed that CUBA-PING's I-loop architecturally clamps the hidden-E rate near 1 Hz, while the CUBA-no-PING control sits at tens of Hz. [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) showed that the COBA-PING baselines walk the same rate–accuracy frontier via the explicit firing-rate regulariser $L_\text{rate}$ — the *spike-budget* penalty $s_u \cdot \sum_i \text{relu}(\langle z_i\rangle - \theta_u)^2$ added to the CE loss, which pulls per-neuron trial spike counts down toward the threshold $\theta_u$.

This entry asks the natural question those two leave open: how do the two knobs — the I-loop architecture and the explicit penalty — interact in the CUBA family? Two sub-questions:

1. **Can penalty alone recover PING-style sparsity on the no-PING control?** If yes, the I-loop is sufficient but not necessary for the sub-Hz regime; if no, the architectural clamp is doing something the penalty can't.
2. **Does the penalty buy anything on top of PING?** PING is already at ≈0.4 spikes per trial at the nb040 baseline ($\theta_u =$ off). Pushing further with the penalty either tightens the rate at no accuracy cost (free), or pays an extra accuracy price, or simply does nothing because the cells already fire below threshold.

Same recipe as nb040 — `cuba-ping` and `cuba-noping` via the oscilloscope, $\Delta t = 1$ ms, $T = 200$ ms, hard reset, no refractory period, no synaptic state, mem-mean output readout, TBPTT $K = 10$, Adam at lr $2 \times 10^{-3}$, batch 64. The only addition is the regulariser, with $s_u = 10^{-3}$ matching nb036:

$$
L = \text{CE}(\text{logits}, y) + s_u \sum_{i} \text{relu}\!\left(\frac{1}{T}\sum_t s_i^E(t) - \theta_u\right)^2.
$$

The grid $\theta_u \in \{\text{off}, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2\}$ spikes-per-trial matches nb035/036, so the frontier here is directly comparable. At $T = 200$ ms, $\theta_u$ in spikes-per-trial × 5 = Hz, i.e. $\theta_u = 0.2$ spk/trial is a 1 Hz target.

## Interpretation

The frontier figure says the two knobs do *not* compose the way the COBA-PING picture in [nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) would predict.

**No-PING + penalty walks a clean frontier (red).** From the rate-coded baseline at $\theta_u = $ off (37 Hz / 88.7%) down to $\theta_u = 0.2$ (1.5 Hz / 80.6%), the penalty pulls the E rate by **25×** while costing only **≈8 pp** of accuracy. The slope is gentle and monotone; every $\theta_u$ value lands on the curve. The spike-budget term is doing exactly what it's designed for — slide along the rate–accuracy axis with a continuous knob, no architectural change required.

**PING + penalty splits into two regimes (black).** Figure 3 separates them cleanly:

- *Loose targets* ($\theta_u = 5, 2$): loss diverges upward over training (2.0 → 3.4 for $\theta_u = 5$ across the 15-epoch run), accuracy plateaus near 55%, and the **E rate climbs *into* the threshold instead of being pulled down by it** (0.9 → 2.8 Hz). The I rate explodes alongside (28 → 100+ Hz). These cells aren't under-converged — they're *actively unstable*; more epochs would make things worse.
- *Tight targets* ($\theta_u \leq 1$): loss is still slowly decreasing at ep 15, accuracy is still climbing, no instability visible. These cells are *under-converged* in the medium-tier budget; more epochs would likely close most of the gap to the PING-off baseline (78%).

The diverging $\theta_u = 5, 2$ cells aren't a convergence artefact and are the qualitative finding of this entry. The mechanism is mechanical and reproducible.

**At matched rates, no-PING + penalty wins on the medium budget.** Reading off the frontier at ≈1 Hz: no-PING + penalty sits at 80.6% / 1.5 Hz, while PING off sits at 78.2% / 0.9 Hz — comparable rate, **+2 pp accuracy for no-PING**. The under-converged PING $\theta_u \leq 1$ cells in Figure 3 may close this gap at higher epoch budgets, but the diverging $\theta_u = 5, 2$ cells will not, so the headline conclusion stands: in the instant-synapse CUBA family the explicit spike-budget penalty is the better way to walk the frontier — at least until the per-neuron formulation is replaced with one that doesn't fight the I-loop's winner-take-most code.

This contradicts what one would have predicted from the [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/) Figure 5 frontier overlay, where COBA-PING and PING-PING (both with $\theta_u$ sweep) land on similar frontiers. The instability has a clean mechanistic explanation.

### Why the penalty bites the wrong cells

Replaying the trained PING-off network on a 64-sample MNIST test batch and dumping the per-neuron mean spike count per trial gives the distribution below (and the no-PING control alongside):

| Cell | mean | median | p90 | p99 | max |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| PING off | 0.37 | **0.00** | **0.00** | 15.3 | 20.1 |
| PING $\theta_u = 5$ | 0.19 | 0.00 | 0.00 | 8.3 | 10.8 |
| no-PING off | 13.5 | 13.6 | 16.3 | 18.7 | 20.1 |
| no-PING $\theta_u = 5$ | 7.78 | 7.69 | 9.3 | 10.7 | 12.1 |

PING produces an **extremely sparse, selective code** — the *median* neuron never fires on any trial, but the *top percentile* fires ≈15 spikes per trial. The per-neuron penalty $\text{relu}(\langle z_i\rangle - \theta_u)^2$ activates *only* on those high-firing selective cells when $\theta_u = 5$, while 99% of the population sees zero gradient. So even though the population-mean rate (0.37 spk/trial) sits an order of magnitude below the threshold, the penalty applies non-trivially — and to exactly the cells that carry the digit-classification signal.

no-PING is the opposite: median ≈ mean ≈ 13.6 spk/trial, every cell firing at roughly the same rate. The penalty distributes uniformly across the population with no single signal channel singled out, and the optimiser walks the frontier smoothly.

The same penalty walks the COBA-PING frontier in [nb035 Figure 5](/notebooks/nb035/) because the conductance synaptic filter spreads activity across more cells — there's no sub-1% sparse selective subpopulation for the per-neuron term to concentrate on. In CUBA-PING with instant synapses, the I-loop drives the population straight to a winner-take-most code, and the per-neuron penalty becomes a *selective signal-cell suppressor*.

## Findings

In the instant-synapse CUBA family, the spike-budget penalty as currently formulated (per-neuron upper bound) does *not* compose with the I-loop architecture — the I-loop induces a winner-take-most code where 1% of cells carry the signal, and the per-neuron penalty selectively suppresses those signal-carrying cells. The architectural clamp and the explicit penalty don't compose because they're tuned for different population-rate distributions: the penalty assumes uniform activity (true in no-PING), the I-loop produces selective activity (true in PING).

For a sparse-spike CUBA recipe the simplest path is **no-PING + penalty** at the desired $\theta_u$ — at ≈1 Hz this beats PING-off in accuracy too, so the I-loop isn't earning the optimisation complexity it adds.

The natural follow-ups:

1. **Population-mean penalty.** Replace per-neuron $\text{relu}(\langle z_i\rangle - \theta_u)^2$ with a single scalar penalty on the grand mean across all $i$. Loses the per-cell selectivity that lets the regulariser drive *individual* runaway cells silent, but doesn't damage the sparse selective code PING produces. Should let PING + penalty compose.
2. **Top-quantile-only penalty.** Apply the penalty to the top 5% of cells (sorted by mean rate) rather than all cells above $\theta_u$. Threshold becomes a *quantile* not a rate. Targets the runaway-cell case without crushing the signal-carrying few in PING.
3. **Re-test with COBA synapses.** Replace the instant E→I and I→E projections with the COBANet exponential filter at $\tau_\text{GABA} = 9$ ms, $\tau_\text{AMPA} = 2$ ms, and rerun the same sweep. If COBA-PING in [nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) walks a clean frontier with the same per-neuron penalty, the synaptic filter is what spreads activity wide enough that the per-neuron formulation behaves like a population formulation.
4. **Scale to full MNIST + more epochs.** Figure 3 shows the tight-$\theta_u$ PING cells ($\theta_u \leq 1$) are under-converged in the 15-epoch budget — the loss is still decreasing at the final epoch. A longer run would resolve whether those cells recover toward the PING-off baseline (in which case the I-loop *is* doing useful work below 1 Hz) or plateau where they are (in which case the per-neuron penalty is also damaging the signal at tight thresholds, just more slowly than at loose ones).
