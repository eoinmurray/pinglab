## Setup

[nb040](/notebooks/nb040/) showed that CUBA-PING's I-loop architecturally clamps the hidden-E rate near 1 Hz, while the CUBA-no-PING control sits at tens of Hz. [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/)–[nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) showed that the COBA-PING baselines walk the same rate–accuracy frontier via the explicit firing-rate regulariser $L_\text{rate}$ — the *spike-budget* penalty $s_u \cdot \sum_i \text{relu}(\langle z_i\rangle - \theta_u)^2$ added to the CE loss, which pulls per-neuron trial spike counts down toward the threshold $\theta_u$.

This entry asks the natural question those two leave open: how do the two knobs — the I-loop architecture and the explicit penalty — interact in the CUBA family? Two sub-questions:

1. **Can penalty alone recover PING-style sparsity on the no-PING control?** If yes, the I-loop is sufficient but not necessary for the sub-Hz regime; if no, the architectural clamp is doing something the penalty can't.
2. **Does the penalty buy anything on top of PING?** PING is already at ≈0.4 spikes per trial at the nb040 baseline ($\theta_u =$ off). Pushing further with the penalty either tightens the rate at no accuracy cost (free), or pays an extra accuracy price, or simply does nothing because the cells already fire below threshold.

Same recipe as nb040 — `cuba-ping` and `cuba-noping` via the oscilloscope, $\Delta t = 1$ ms, $T = 200$ ms, hard reset, no refractory period, no synaptic state, mem-mean output readout, TBPTT $K = 10$, Adam at lr $2 \times 10^{-3}$, batch 64. The only addition is the regulariser, with $s_u = 10^{-3}$ matching nb036:

$$
L = \text{CE}(\text{logits}, y) + s_u \sum_{i} \text{relu}\!\left(\frac{1}{T}\sum_t s_i^E(t) - \theta_u\right)^2.
$$

The grid $\theta_u \in \{\text{off}, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2\}$ spikes-per-trial matches nb035/036, so the frontier here is directly comparable. At $T = 200$ ms, $\theta_u$ in spikes-per-trial × 5 = Hz, i.e. $\theta_u = 0.2$ spk/trial is a 1 Hz target.

## Interpretation

The frontier figure says the two knobs do *not* compose the way the COBA-PING picture in [nb036](/notebooks/nb036/) would predict.

**No-PING + penalty walks a clean frontier (red).** From the rate-coded baseline at $\theta_u = $ off (37 Hz / 88.7%) down to $\theta_u = 0.2$ (1.5 Hz / 80.6%), the penalty pulls the E rate by **25×** while costing only **≈8 pp** of accuracy. The slope is gentle and monotone; every $\theta_u$ value lands on the curve. The spike-budget term is doing exactly what it's designed for — slide along the rate–accuracy axis with a continuous knob, no architectural change required.

**PING + penalty destabilises (black).** The baseline at $\theta_u =$ off sits at 0.9 Hz / 78.2% — the nb040 number — but every cell with a *finite* $\theta_u$ collapses 15–25 pp of accuracy. Worse, the disruption is non-monotone: $\theta_u = 5$ (a loose target, well above PING's natural rate) drops accuracy to 55% and *raises* the I rate to 58 Hz; $\theta_u = 1$ recovers partially to 63%; the tightest two cells settle near 52%. The penalty doesn't ride on top of the I-loop — it interferes with it. Two plausible mechanisms: the penalty gradient on $s^E$ propagates back through the I-loop's recurrent Jacobian and adds to the already-marginal TBPTT signal, or the penalty pushes $W_\text{in}$ into a regime where the recruitment cliff that drives PING dynamics never triggers.

**At matched rates, no-PING + penalty wins.** Reading off the frontier at ≈1 Hz: no-PING + penalty sits at 80.6% / 1.5 Hz, while PING off sits at 78.2% / 0.9 Hz — comparable rate, **+2 pp accuracy for no-PING**. At ≈0.3 Hz, PING + $\theta_u = 1$ reaches 63%; no-PING never gets that low in this sweep but its trajectory extrapolates well above 63% at 0.3 Hz. So in the instant-synapse CUBA family, the explicit spike-budget penalty is *the better way* to walk the frontier — the I-loop architecture doesn't offer a free lunch over it.

This contradicts what one would have predicted from the [nb035](/notebooks/nb035/) Figure 5 frontier overlay, where COBA-PING and PING-PING (both with $\theta_u$ sweep) land on similar frontiers. The difference here is the architecture: in COBA-PING the I-loop is filtered (exponential GABA), giving it a separation of timescales the penalty can act independently of. In CUBA-PING the I-loop is *instant* — the suppression hits every step, and combining it with the per-step regulariser gradient seems to over-constrain the optimisation problem.

## Findings

In the instant-synapse CUBA family, the spike-budget penalty is the cheaper of the two rate-control knobs. The I-loop architecture by itself is a competitive operating point (the nb040 baseline), but combining it with the penalty makes things *worse*, not better — the architectural clamp and the explicit penalty don't compose. For a sparse-spike CUBA recipe the simplest path is no-PING + penalty at the desired $\theta_u$.

The natural follow-ups:

1. **Diagnose the PING + penalty instability.** Is it gradient interference (the penalty term inflating the I-loop's TBPTT signal past stability) or an optimisation-basin issue (penalty deforming the recruitment cliff)? A clean diagnostic would be turning off the penalty's gradient through $s^E$ — applying it only to the readout — and seeing whether PING + penalty recovers.
2. **Re-test with COBA synapses.** Replace the instant E→I and I→E projections with the COBANet exponential filter at $\tau_\text{GABA} = 9$ ms, $\tau_\text{AMPA} = 2$ ms, and rerun the same sweep. If the COBA-PING in nb036 walks a clean frontier with the penalty, the synaptic filter is what lets the two knobs compose.
3. **Scale to full MNIST.** At large tier the medium gaps may compress; the no-PING + penalty advantage is small enough that full-data training could close it.
