## Introduction

Stub entry for the [Spiking Heidelberg Digits](https://zenkelab.org/resources/spiking-heidelberg-datasets-shd/) dataset — 20 classes of spoken digits (English + German) encoded as cochleogram spike trains over 700 input channels and $\sim$1 s per trial. Picked up after entry [004](/notebook/nb004/) parked sMNIST: SHD is natively event-based, so the dt / discretisation / encoding questions the earlier notebooks studied apply directly rather than being mediated by an artificial row-by-row encoding.

## Method

First-cell baseline to validate the pipeline: one [*cuba*](/models/#cuba) model with two hidden layers (128, 128), trained on SHD at *medium* tier (2000 samples × 10 epochs). Trials run for 1000 ms at *dt* = 1 ms (1000 steps). Same cuba init-scale recipe as nb004 (*init_scale_weight* = 3.0, *init_scale_bias* = $1/(1-\beta)$). Two readouts are compared against the same training setup: (a) **rate** — cumulative last-hidden spike counts projected once at the final timestep, the default shared ladder decoder; (b) **li** — a non-spiking leaky-integrator neuron per class with $\tau = \tau_\text{mem} = $ 10 ms and max-over-time as the logit, the field-standard SHD readout.

Both readouts train cleanly end-to-end, but the field-standard li readout **underperforms** the rate baseline at matched init and training budget:

| readout | best acc | final acc | final loss | duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| rate | 36.5% | 36.0% | 0.34 | 1m 47s |
| li   | 21.75% | 21.0% | 0.72 | 2m 28s |

This is the opposite of the field expectation and worth debugging before drawing any conclusion about readouts. Two plausible causes stand out. (i) *Init-scale bleed into the output layer.* The *init_scale_weight* = 3.0 / *init_scale_bias* = $1/(1-\beta) \approx 10.5$ recipe was tuned for the hidden-layer drive in cuba; applied uniformly it also multiplies the output weights and biases. The rate readout absorbs a large $W_\text{out}$ because it's a single linear projection on a single cumulative vector, but the li readout integrates $W_\text{out} \cdot s_t$ step-by-step into a non-spiking neuron that can saturate well before the informative spikes arrive. (ii) *Readout* $\tau$ *too short.* The li neuron currently reuses $\tau_\text{mem} = $ 10 ms; for $\sim$1 s SHD trials that's a very leaky integrator, and the max-over-time collapses to whichever burst happened to arrive strongest rather than integrated evidence. Zenke-style SHD pipelines typically use a longer readout $\tau$ (often $\sim$20 ms) and/or a separate synaptic $\tau$.

## Implications

The readout swap is not free — it has its own init + time-constant tuning, and blindly wrapping the hidden-layer init recipe around the output layer makes the li path worse, not better. The rate baseline at 36.5% stays as the honest pre-tuning number; the li path needs its own init + $\tau$ pass before a fair readout-vs-readout comparison.

## Next steps

1. Separate the readout init from the hidden init: add an *init-scale-readout* knob (default 1.0) so the 3.0× hidden scaling doesn't bleed into $W_\text{out}$, and rerun *cuba* + li at *medium*.
2. Expose the li readout's time constant as a separate param (e.g. $\tau_\text{out}$), default $\sim$20 ms to match Zenke-style SHD pipelines, and sweep it ($\tau_\text{out} \in $ \{10, 20, 40\} ms) at the best init from step 1.
3. Once li is at or above rate, broaden the cell matrix across the four model families ([*cuba*](/models/#cuba), [*standard-snn*](/models/#standard-snn), [*ping*](/models/#ping), [*snntorch-library*](/models/#snntorch-library)) at matched init.
4. Sweep training *dt* (0.25 / 0.5 / 1.0 ms) to reprise the nb003 discretisation comparison on a natively-spiking task.
