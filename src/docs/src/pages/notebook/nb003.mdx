## Introduction

Train an SNN at one simulation timestep *dt*, run it at another — does it still work? Depends on how the LIF ODE gets discretised.

[snnTorch](https://snntorch.readthedocs.io)'s [Tutorial 3 § 1.2](https://snntorch.readthedocs.io/en/latest/tutorials/tutorial_3.html#the-decay-rate-beta) sets $\Delta t = 1$ and absorbs the $(1-\beta)$ prefactor into the weight, giving the clean $U_{t+1} = \beta\,U_t + W\,s_t$ update — pragmatic for fixed-*dt*, but *dt* drops out of the spec. This entry sweeps eval-*dt* anyway, and compares against a version that keeps *dt* in the update.

**Models** (see [the ladder](/models/)):

- [*standard-snn*](/models/#standard-snn) — pinglab's reimplementation of snnTorch's *snn.Leaky*. Tutorial-3 rule, *dt*-free by design.
- [*snntorch-library*](/models/#snntorch-library) — snnTorch itself. Parity probe against *standard-snn*.
- [*cuba*](/models/#cuba) — same LIF, ZOH-integrated over a step without the $\Delta t = 1$ substitution, so *dt* appears explicitly.

[Parthasarathy et al.](/references/#temporal-discretisation) document the same failure for the Tutorial-3 rule and propose a regulariser (§2.5) plus a ZOH weight-rescaling transport (App. 1C, Eq 27). nb003 takes the complementary route: bake the ZOH prefactors into the update rule itself — *cuba*'s $(1-\beta)/dt$ on drive and $(1-\beta)$ on bias, matching Paper Eq 25 — so no rescaling is needed.

## Method

1. **Train** each model at two timesteps: *dt* = {dt1} ms (fine) and *dt* = {dt2} ms (coarse).
2. **Freeze weights** and **evaluate** at a grid of *dt* values: \{{(cfg.dt_sweep ?? []).join(", ")}\} ms.
3. Plot accuracy and hidden-layer firing rate vs eval-*dt*. A *dt*-stable model stays flat; an unstable one sags as eval-*dt* departs from train-*dt*.

**Controls.** (i) *Matched init*: *standard-snn* and *cuba* start from bit-identical random weights; *cuba* gets a one-shot rescaling that cancels its per-step drive factors at train-*dt* so both models produce the same initial firing rate (otherwise *cuba* would start silent). (ii) *Frozen input + paper-matched transports*: the MNIST Poisson encoding is generated once at train-*dt* and transported to each sweep *dt* via one of the three transports the paper defines — **upsample** (zero-pad to finer eval-*dt*, §2.1 Fig 1B), **downsample** (sum-pool to coarser eval-*dt*, §2.3), or **resample** (fresh Poisson at the target *dt*, §2.1 alternative). All three are run per regime; see the [encoder-mode audit](#4-encoder-mode-does-not-change-the-picture). (iii) All three models share surrogate-gradient slope 1 (overriding snnTorch's slope-25 default) so *standard-snn* vs *snntorch-library* is a pure update-rule + init parity probe.

### 4. Encoder mode does not change the picture

Each eval-*dt* point needs an input spike train at that *dt*. We try three ways to produce it, one per paper-named case (all from [Parthasarathy et al.](/references/#temporal-discretisation) §2.1 and §2.3):

- **upsample** (§2.1 Fig 1B) — generate the spike train once at train-*dt*, then *zero-pad* to finer eval-*dt*: each reference spike lands at the first fine sub-step of its block, the rest of the block is zero. Count-preserving; per-ms rate preserved. Paper's primary transport for fine eval-*dt*. Only makes sense for eval-*dt* ≤ train-*dt*.
- **downsample** (§2.3) — generate the spike train once at train-*dt*, then *sum-pool* into integer bins at coarser eval-*dt*. Count-preserving (up to trimmed leftover steps); per-ms rate preserved; output is non-binary (integer counts per bin). Paper's primary transport for coarse eval-*dt*. Only makes sense for eval-*dt* ≥ train-*dt*.
- **resample** — draw a fresh Poisson spike train at the target *dt* with the same per-ms rate. Covers both directions but re-introduces sampling noise.

The three rows of Figure 8 are these three transports. If the sag were really about how the input gets rebinned, the rows would disagree. They don't — here is how much test accuracy moves across each mode's valid eval-*dt* range (max − min) for each model:

## Discussion

**Parity rules out a pinglab bug.** In Results §2 and §3 the *standard-snn* and *snntorch-library* curves overlap in accuracy and share shape in rate. That matches enough of the reference implementation to attribute the sag to the Tutorial-3 update rule rather than something local to pinglab.

**The input transport is not the driver.** The encoder-mode table (Results §4) shows snnTorch sags and *cuba* stays flat under all three paper-named transports — count-preserving zero-pad (upsample), count-preserving sum-pool (downsample), and rate-preserving fresh Poisson (resample). Whatever carries the *dt*-dependence, it lives in the LIF update, not in how spikes are reshaped to the target *dt*.

**Training-budget is not the driver either.** All three models reach comparable best-acc per regime (Results §1), so the eval-*dt* sweep gap is a frozen-weight *dt*-stability effect, not a capacity or budget artefact.

**A partial mechanism: the bias term.** A steady-state analysis explains the *magnitude* of the snnTorch fan but not its *direction*. Under the snnTorch update $\text{mem} = \beta \cdot \text{mem} + W{\cdot}s + b$ with $\beta = e^{-dt/\tau}$ and Poisson drive of rate $r$,

$$\text{mem}_{ss} = \frac{W \cdot \mathbb{E}[s_{step}]}{1-\beta} + \frac{b}{1-\beta}.$$

With frozen inputs $\mathbb{E}[s_{step}] \approx r\,dt$ and $1-\beta \approx dt/\tau$ for small $dt/\tau$, the spike contribution is $W\,r\,\tau$ — *dt*-invariant — while the bias contribution scales as $b\,\tau/dt$, i.e. **1/*dt***. The rate-ratio column in Results §3 matches this magnitude (30–100× for snnTorch, consistent with 40×), but Figure 9 shows the fan flips *direction* between train-*dt* regimes, which a bias-only story can't explain. Each regime solves a different optimisation problem (drive/step, $\beta$, bias steady-state, and BPTT depth all change with train-*dt*) and lands on its own operating point; the bias term is the piece of that point whose sensitivity is easiest to read off.

**Why *cuba* is flat.** *cuba*'s update $\text{mem} = \beta \cdot \text{mem} + \tfrac{1-\beta}{dt} W{\cdot}s + (1-\beta)\, b$ cancels both $1/(1-\beta)$ terms in steady state — bias → $b$, spike → $W\,r\,\tau$, both *dt*-invariant — which is the formal explanation for the direction-insensitive asymmetric cliff (Results §2 (iii)) showing up only in the snnTorch paths.

**Practical upshot.** Fixed-*dt* research: Tutorial-3 is a fine, battle-tested choice. Cross-*dt* transfer — sampling-rate changes, hardware with a different clock, comparison to continuous-time biology — reintroduce the prefactors and the problem goes away. Every later rung of the [ladder](/models/) inherits CUBA's ODE-derived discretisation, so *dt*-stability is a **ladder invariant from CUBA onwards** — any rung that breaks it is a rung defect, not biophysics.

## Success criteria

- ✅ ***cuba* rate-flat across eval-*dt***. Ratio $\sim${ratioFmt(rateRatio(cuba1Cp), 2)}× / $\sim${ratioFmt(rateRatio(cuba2Cp), 2)}× (criterion ≤ 2×).
- ✅ **snnTorch paths sag**. Rate ratios $\sim${ratioFmt(rateRatio(snn1Cp))}–{ratioFmt(rateRatio(lib2Cp))}× (criterion ≥ 10×, the 1/*dt* signature).
- ✅ **Pinglab parity with snnTorch**. *standard-snn* tracks *snntorch-library* — sag is the update rule, not a bug.
- ✅ **Encoder-mode neutrality**. Qualitative picture unchanged under either transport.
