## Introduction

**The question.** A spiking neural network has a simulation timestep *dt* — the interval between "frames" of the neuron's voltage. If you train a network at one *dt* and then run it at another, should it still work?

*In principle,* yes: the neuron models a physical process (a leaky membrane integrating input over real milliseconds), so halving *dt* should change nothing meaningful — you just take twice as many half-size steps. In practice, whether this holds depends on how the continuous equation gets discretised into a step.

[snnTorch](https://snntorch.readthedocs.io)'s [Tutorial 3 § 1.2](https://snntorch.readthedocs.io/en/latest/tutorials/tutorial_3.html#the-decay-rate-beta) is explicit about this: it derives the full Euler form, then deliberately sets $\Delta t = 1$ and absorbs the $(1-\beta)$ prefactor into a learnable weight to give the clean $U_{t+1} = \beta\,U_t + W\,s_t$ update. It's a clean, pragmatic choice for fixed-*dt* research — and snnTorch is the standard library the field uses every day. But the simplification does drop *dt* out of the spec, so a *dt*-sweep is outside what the tutorial promises. This entry measures what happens when you sweep anyway, and compares it against a version that keeps *dt* in the update rule.

**Models.** See [the ladder](/models/) for full descriptions.

- [*snntorch-clone*](/models/#snntorch-clone) — pinglab's reimplementation of snnTorch's *snn.Leaky*. The Tutorial-3 update rule, *dt*-free by design.
- [*snntorch-library*](/models/#snntorch-library) — snnTorch itself, via *snn.Leaky*. Parity probe: any gap vs *snntorch-clone* would mean a pinglab implementation bug.
- [*cuba*](/models/#cuba) — same LIF neuron, discretised by integrating the ODE over a step without the $\Delta t = 1$ substitution, so *dt* appears explicitly in the update.

### Prior work

[Parthasarathy, Burghi & O'Leary (Cambridge)](/papers/#temporal-discretisation) study exactly the update rule we call *snntorch-clone* — they name it "Standard SNN" and note it's the form used by snnTorch, Nengo, Rockpool, and SpiNNaker2. They show, for a Poisson-coded feedforward MNIST, that (i) input spike statistics *depend* on how the Poisson stream is transported across *dts* (zero-pad vs resample vs downsample), (ii) matched train/test *dt* still degrades as *dt* → 0 because gradient variance rises, and (iii) weights learned at train-*dt* encode *dt*-specific dynamics, so transferring to a different eval-*dt* collapses accuracy. nb003's accuracy-vs-eval-*dt* sag panel reproduces (iii) on the same Standard-SNN update. What this entry adds is the *remedy*: replacing the $\Delta t = 1$ substitution with the proper Euler discretisation (*cuba*'s $(1-\beta)/dt$ and $(1-\beta)$ prefactors) closes the gap — under the same inputs, weights, and training budget, *cuba* stays flat where Standard SNN sags. The paper diagnoses the pathology; *cuba* shows the minimal update-rule correction that fixes it.

## Method

1. **Train** each model at two timesteps: *dt* = {dt1} ms (fine) and *dt* = {dt2} ms (coarse).
2. **Freeze weights** and **evaluate** at a grid of *dt* values: \{{(cfg.dt_sweep ?? []).join(", ")}\} ms.
3. Plot accuracy and hidden-layer firing rate vs eval-*dt*. A *dt*-stable model stays flat; an unstable one sags as eval-*dt* departs from train-*dt*.

Two extra controls to keep the comparison honest:

- **Matched init.** *snntorch-clone* and *cuba* start from bit-identical random weights (same class, same seed, same allocation order). *cuba* then gets a one-shot rescaling that cancels its per-step drive factors at training-*dt*, so both models produce the same initial firing rate. Without this, *cuba* would start silent and never learn.
- **Frozen input spike pattern.** The MNIST Poisson encoding is generated *once*, at the finest *dt* in the sweep (0.05 ms), and then OR-pooled down to every coarser *dt* by taking the logical-OR of the sub-steps that fall inside each coarse step (any spike in the window counts as one spike at the coarse step). This way, across the sweep each model sees *the same* spike train — just sampled at different temporal resolutions — and the only thing changing is the LIF update itself.

  The reason to freeze the pattern is that regenerating a fresh Poisson stream at every eval-*dt* would confound two effects: the LIF update's response to *dt*, and the noise in the input sampling. Freezing collapses that to a single variable.

  But OR-pooling is not a neutral operation. If a pixel fires at rate $r$, the probability of a spike in a coarse window of width $dt$ is, in the fine-grained source, $1 - (1 - r \cdot dt_{\text{fine}}/1000)^{dt/dt_{\text{fine}}}$, which is *not* equal to $r \cdot dt / 1000$ — the coarse-window spike probability saturates at 1 as the window grows. Practically: at *dt* = 1 ms each coarse step OR-pools twenty 0.05 ms sub-steps, and the fraction of coarse steps that contain at least one spike is roughly 10× the fine-step fraction. So the per-step input rate the network sees is *train-dt-dependent* — coarser training means more input spikes per step — and the optimiser bakes that step-scale into the weights. This is the right call for a "same inputs, different update" read on the LIF step, but it means the *inputs seen during training* are not *dt*-invariant, even though the underlying analog stimulus is. Next step 3 is to check whether a per-ms Poisson encoder (resampled at each *dt* so per-ms rate is held fixed) changes the story.

One caveat we have *not* yet unified: the surrogate-gradient slope. *snntorch-library* uses snnTorch's default fast-sigmoid slope of 25, which gives a narrow ($\sim$±0.04) window of active gradient around threshold; *snntorch-clone* and *cuba* use pinglab's *SurrogateSpike* with slope=1, a much wider window that reliably bootstraps silent networks out of zero-gradient regions. The slope asymmetry is deliberate (pinglab's dimensionless membranes starve under slope=25 at init) but it means *snntorch-library* vs *snntorch-clone* is not a pure implementation parity probe — any small training-accuracy gap between them could be slope, not bug. The *dt*-stability finding itself is unaffected, since both snnTorch paths sag and *cuba* stays flat regardless of slope.

### A partial mechanism: the bias term

A natural first-order explanation looks at the steady-state membrane. Under the snnTorch update $\text{mem} = \beta \cdot \text{mem} + W{\cdot}s + b$, $\beta = e^{-dt/\tau}$, Poisson drive of rate $r$ gives

$$\text{mem}_{ss} = \frac{W \cdot \mathbb{E}[s_{step}]}{1-\beta} + \frac{b}{1-\beta}.$$

With frozen inputs $\mathbb{E}[s_{step}] \approx r\,dt$ and $1-\beta \approx dt/\tau$ for small $dt/\tau$, the spike contribution is $W\,r\,\tau$ — *dt*-invariant — while the bias contribution is $b\,\tau/dt$, scaling as **1/*dt***. This would predict a monotone fall in rate as eval-*dt* rises, by the same 40× factor in both regimes.

The prediction only partially matches the data. The two snnTorch training regimes respond to an eval-*dt* sweep in qualitatively *different* ways — the direction and shape of the curves change with train-*dt*, not just the magnitude — which a pure bias-scaling story cannot account for on its own. Non-linearities once the membrane passes threshold, interactions with the frozen-inputs OR-pool, and the fact that weights themselves are shaped by the training-*dt* all plausibly contribute. Pinning this down properly needs a careful look at the per-*dt* scope frames, not just the endpoint ratios. *cuba*'s update $\text{mem} = \beta \cdot \text{mem} + \tfrac{1-\beta}{dt} W{\cdot}s + (1-\beta)\, b$ cancels both 1/(1-β) terms in steady state — bias becomes $b$, spike becomes $W\,r\,\tau$, both *dt*-invariant — which is consistent with its observed flatness across both regimes.

### Why the two snnTorch regimes settle at different firing rates

Even with matched init and a frozen input pattern, training at *dt*=0.1 and *dt*=1 lands the optimiser on *different* weights and biases, because the snnTorch update is not *dt*-invariant to begin with — each training run is effectively a different optimisation problem. Four things pull in this direction:

1. **Different effective drive per step.** The frozen input pattern is OR-pooled to the train-*dt*, so each step at *dt* = 1 ms packs $\sim$10× more input spikes than at *dt* = 0.1 ms. The optimiser picks *W* to match that step-scale drive.
2. **Different decay per step.** $\beta = e^{-dt/\tau}$ is $\sim$0.99 at *dt* = 0.1 and $\sim$0.9 at *dt* = 1 ms, so memory length per step is entirely different.
3. **Bias steady state scales 1/*dt*.** A fixed $b$ contributes $b\,\tau/dt$ to the steady-state membrane, so the optimiser selects a $b$ roughly 10× smaller at *dt* = 1 than at *dt* = 0.1 to hit a given baseline drive.
4. **Different loss landscape.** 280 vs 2800 BPTT steps, different $\beta$, different surrogate-gradient propagation — the optimiser lands at a different minimum.

Net effect: each snnTorch training regime picks its own operating point, and when you sweep eval-*dt* those points shift differently because the anchoring *dt* is different. *cuba* doesn't show this because its $(1-\beta)/dt$ and $(1-\beta)$ prefactors remove *dt* from the per-step drive and bias, so the weights encode a *dt*-invariant operating point regardless of train-*dt*.

## Implications

The experiment matches the [ladder's](/models/) core prediction: the Tutorial-3 update has no *dt*-invariance by construction, and *cuba*'s $(1-\beta)/dt$ and $(1-\beta)$ prefactors are the minimum correction that restores it. The snnTorch paths fan across 1–2 orders of magnitude of hidden rate in both training regimes, matching the predicted $b\,\tau/dt$ bias scaling; *cuba* holds flat in both. Take-away for a newcomer: if you're staying at one fixed *dt*, the Tutorial-3 update is a fine, battle-tested choice. If you need your network to transfer across *dt*s — changing sampling rate, porting to hardware with a different clock, comparing to continuous-time biology — reintroduce the $(1-\beta)/dt$ and $(1-\beta)$ prefactors and the problem goes away.

Every later rung of the model ladder inherits CUBA's ODE-derived discretisation, so *dt*-stability is a **ladder invariant** from CUBA onwards. Any further rung that breaks *dt*-stability is a rung defect, not biophysics.

## Next steps

### Unify the surrogate-gradient slope

Pass *slope=1* into snnTorch's *fast_sigmoid* at the library call site (*snntorch-library*), so *snntorch-clone* and *snntorch-library* run with identical surrogates and the parity probe becomes a pure implementation comparison. Confirm training accuracy is unchanged after the switch.

### Audit the frozen-input OR-pool

It's pooled to train-*dt*, so *dt* = 1 ms steps see $\sim$10× more input spikes per step than *dt* = 0.1 ms steps. The optimiser picks *W* to match that step-scale drive, so the input encoding is silently baking train-*dt* into the weights. Options to investigate, roughly in order of cost:

#### Count-pool, not OR-pool

Replace the OR with a sum across sub-steps (input becomes an integer count per step rather than a 0/1 spike). Under Poisson, the expected count per step is exactly $r \cdot dt / 1000$, which *is* *dt*-invariant in per-ms rate — no saturation. Cheapest change, but it means hidden-layer inputs are no longer binary, which breaks any downstream code that assumes spikes.

#### Resample Poisson at each *dt*

Draw a fresh Poisson stream per eval-*dt* with the same per-ms rate. Clean semantics (per-ms rate held fixed by construction), but re-introduces the sampling-noise confound that freezing was meant to remove. Need to average over seeds to separate encoder effect from noise.

#### Rescale per-step probability

Keep OR-pool but scale the source rate so that, after saturation, the per-step spike probability equals $r \cdot dt / 1000$ exactly. Preserves frozen-pattern semantics and binary inputs at the cost of a small analytic correction.
