## Introduction

First rung-to-rung ablation on the [CUBA ladder](/models/#the-ladder). *snntorch-clone* and *cuba* differ by a single knob: *cuba* scales the input drive by $(1-\beta)/dt$ so each kick integrates to a fixed per-ms quantity; *snntorch-clone* deliberately does not, carrying the canonical tutorial discretisation. *snntorch-library* sits alongside as an external parity reference — the actual snnTorch *snn.Leaky* cell with the same canonical discretisation — so the *snntorch-clone ↔ snntorch-library* pair tells us whether the Δt-stability gap we see is a property of the canonical update rule or a pinglab implementation quirk.

At training *dt* the canonical and continuous update rules are numerically close — the difference is a scaling factor that equals 1 when $dt = \tau_\text{mem}(1 - 1/e)$. Away from that point the update rules diverge: halving *dt* doubles the number of kicks per ms in the canonical path, so the effective drive grows; *cuba*'s $(1-\beta)/dt$ factor keeps the per-ms drive invariant. Δt-stability is the sharpest test of that invariance.

## Method

All three models are trained twice — once at a fine training *dt* = {dt1} ms, once at a coarse training *dt* = {dt2} ms — then for each regime we run inference across *dt* ∈ \{{(cfg.dt_sweep ?? []).join(", ")}\} ms with the trained weights frozen. The two regimes let us separate *absolute* eval-*dt* effects from *ratio* (eval/train) effects: if the Δt-stability story is driven by the ratio, the canonical curves should shift horizontally with train-*dt* while *cuba* stays flat in both panels.

**Matched init.** *snntorch-clone* and *cuba* share the *SNNTorchNet* class and differ only in a runtime *discretisation* flag, so with matched seed they allocate every parameter in the same order. *cuba* additionally receives a one-shot init-scale (*W* × $dt/(1-\beta)$, *b* × $1/(1-\beta)$) that cancels the per-step drive factors at training *dt* — a different scale per regime, recorded in *numbers.json*. *snntorch-library* wraps *nn.Linear* with *snnTorch*'s own *kaiming_uniform_* convention, so its init is independent — parity probe on the discretisation, not a bit-match. The preflight asserts bit-equal pre-scale init across the *SNNTorchNet* family.

**Frozen inputs.** The spike pattern is encoded once at the finest *dt* and OR-pooled down to coarser ones, so the only thing changing between sweep points is the LIF update at eval time — not the Poisson realisation.

### Why the canonical rate drops as *dt* grows

The sag is almost entirely the **bias term**. Under the canonical update

$$\text{mem} = \beta \cdot \text{mem} + W{\cdot}s + b, \quad \beta = e^{-dt/\tau}$$

the steady-state membrane under a Poisson drive of rate $r$ is

$$\text{mem}_{ss} = \frac{W \cdot \mathbb{E}[s_{step}]}{1-\beta} + \frac{b}{1-\beta}.$$

With frozen inputs $\mathbb{E}[s_{step}] \approx r\,dt$ (Poisson binning at matched rate) and for small $dt/\tau$, $1-\beta \approx dt/\tau$. So the spike contribution is $W\,r\,\tau$ — *dt-invariant* — while the bias contribution is $b\,\tau/dt$, scaling as **1/dt**. The spike drive is already dt-stable; the bias drive is not. Biases were trained to push membrane to threshold *at training-dt*; at coarser eval-*dt* they contribute proportionally less per ms, at finer eval-*dt* proportionally more. The ratio is the same regardless of the training *dt* — eval-*dt* sweeping over 0.05–2.0 ms spans a 40× range, so the 1/*dt* prediction is 40× in both regimes.

Both canonical paths come in above the pure 1/*dt* prediction. The excess comes from the LIF threshold nonlinearity: once membrane depolarises past threshold, spike rate rises super-linearly with drive, amplifying the 1/*dt* bias swing.

*cuba*'s two scaling factors are specifically designed to kill these 1/*dt* terms. Its update is

$$\text{mem} = \beta \cdot \text{mem} + \frac{1-\beta}{dt} W{\cdot}s + (1-\beta)\, b,$$

so the $(1-\beta)$ prefactor on $b$ cancels the $1/(1-\beta)$ in steady-state — bias contribution becomes just $b$, *dt*-invariant. The $(1-\beta)/dt$ prefactor on $W{\cdot}s$ does the same for the spike term. That's the entire mechanism, and it applies identically whether the network was trained at {dt1} ms or {dt2} ms.

## Implications

The canonical snnTorch discretisation is fundamentally tied to the *dt* it trained under: moving eval-*dt* changes the effective per-ms bias drive by a factor of *dt_train*/*dt_eval*, regardless of whether training happened at {dt1} ms or {dt2} ms. Networks — having learned to balance against their training-time drive — spike across 1–2 orders of magnitude of rates across the 40× eval-*dt* range, and this pattern reproduces in both regimes for the canonical paths. The $(1-\beta)/dt$ and $(1-\beta)$ scalings in *cuba*'s update are the minimum correction to un-break this: both are required, one per drive term. At train-*dt* = {dt1} ms *cuba* learns and holds a flat rate across the sweep — the steady-state membrane is *dt*-invariant as predicted. At train-*dt* = {dt2} ms the **extra small** tier is too thin for *cuba* to train at all (see *Next steps*), so the dt={dt2} panel presently tests only the canonical paths.

For the CUBA ladder, this matters because every subsequent rung (cuba-exp, coba, ping) inherits the continuous discretisation. The Δt-stability property is therefore a *ladder invariant* — each rung should retain it. If a later rung breaks it, that is a defect in the rung, not a property of biophysics.

## Next steps

- **cuba → cuba-exp**: add an exponential synaptic kernel and repeat the same Δt-stability sweep across both regimes. Expected: flat rate and accuracy, matching *cuba*.
- **cuba-exp → coba**: swap current-based drive for conductance-based. Biggest single jump in biophysical realism; also the first point where E/I separation enters.
- **coba → ping**: add recurrence + I-to-E delays to produce γ oscillations. Δt-stability test becomes: does the oscillation frequency shift with eval-*dt*?
