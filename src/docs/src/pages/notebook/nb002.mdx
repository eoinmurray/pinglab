**PING** — Pyramidal-Interneuron Network Gamma — is a rhythm that emerges when a population of excitatory (**E**) neurons and inhibitory (**I**) neurons drive each other: E fires, recruits I, I silences E, I decays, E fires again. The cycle repeats at $\sim$30–80 Hz ("gamma"). But it only runs when E gets enough external drive; below threshold the network sits in uncorrelated background firing.

## *scan.mp4*

This entry maps that threshold. The input is a single MNIST image (digit {input.digit}, sample {input.sample}) Poisson-encoded into the input layer: each pixel fires at a rate proportional to its intensity. Each frame of the video below is a fresh {cfg.sim_ms} ms simulation. The "stim window" is {cfg.step_on_ms}–{cfg.step_off_ms} ms — outside it the input fires at a baseline rate ({input.base_rate_hz} Hz max); inside it, that rate is multiplied by the **overdrive** factor shown on the frame. The sweep walks overdrive from {scan.min}× (no boost) to {scan.max}× (strong boost) over {scan.frames} frames.

{hasScan && <video controls loop muted playsinline width="100%" src="/figures/notebook/nb002/scan.mp4" />}

What to watch: the top raster is E spikes, the smaller raster below is I spikes. At low overdrive the stim window looks the same as the rest of the trial. As overdrive grows past threshold, vertical bands appear inside the window — that's gamma: E firing in near-synchrony, I firing in the gaps between E bursts.

At the canonical overdrive of {cfg.canonical_overdrive}×, E fires at {fmtHz(r.pre.e)} Hz before the window with I silent. Inside the window I wakes to {fmtHz(r.stim.i)} Hz and E's time-averaged rate drops to {fmtHz(r.stim.e)} Hz — the bursts are higher, but I-dominated gaps pull the mean down. After the window: {fmtHz(r.post.e)} / {fmtHz(r.post.i)} Hz. The onset is sharp: below threshold nothing happens, above it the rhythm snaps on.

## *scan_dt.mp4*

Gamma has a period of $\sim$15–30 ms; if the integration step *dt* is too coarse, the simulator can't resolve the E→I→E round-trip and the rhythm distorts or diverges. Here overdrive is held at {dtScan.fixed_overdrive}× and *dt* is swept from {dtScan.min} ms to {dtScan.max} ms over {dtScan.frames} frames.

{hasScanDt && <video controls loop muted playsinline width="100%" src="/figures/notebook/nb002/scan_dt.mp4" />}

At fine *dt* the rhythm is clean. As *dt* grows past a few tenths of a millisecond the gamma bands smear, then the sim becomes unstable. This is the upper bound on how coarsely you can step the solver while still getting PING — a practical rule for picking *dt* when scaling up the network.

## *scan_ei.mp4*

The overdrive sweep above shows that *external drive* is one knob controlling PING. The other knob is internal: the strength of the E→I→E feedback loop. To isolate it, this sweep turns the first knob off — overdrive is held at {eiScan.fixed_overdrive}×, so the input rate is flat through the whole trial with no stim-window boost — and walks the **E→I coupling strength** from {eiScan.min} (E and I decoupled — no feedback, no rhythm) up to {eiScan.max} (strongly coupled) over {eiScan.frames} frames.

{hasScanEi && <video controls loop muted playsinline width="100%" src="/figures/notebook/nb002/scan_ei.mp4" />}

At strength 0 the I-cells can't hear the E-cells: E just fires asynchronously at its input-driven rate, no bands, no peak in the PSD. As the coupling rises, I-cells start to fire in response to E bursts and punch gaps back into E's spike train; vertical bands appear across the whole trial and a peak lifts out of the PSD noise floor. At the top of the sweep E's time-averaged rate has collapsed (inhibition-suppressed sparse firing) while the rhythm is visible in the raster. Without any stim-window boost the peak sits below gamma — the feedback loop can close on its own, but it needs external drive to push the cycle into the 30–80 Hz band.
