# References

Reading list for this project. PDFs themselves are third-party copyrighted material and are not redistributed; follow the links to fetch them. Citation keys match the filenames under *src/papers/*. Each entry carries a short TL;DR and, where applicable, the pages in this project that cite it.

## Temporal discretisation

- **parthasarathy-et-al** — Parthasarathy, Burghi & O'Leary, *Temporal Discretisation Shapes Optimisation Landscape in Spiking Neural Networks*, Univ. of Cambridge (2024). Shows that the Standard-SNN update used by snnTorch / Nengo / Rockpool / SpiNNaker2 learns a *dt*-dependent discrete dynamical system: at fixed mean input rate, finer *dt* densifies population drive and raises postsynaptic firing, while coarser *dt* makes the input Bernoulli-like — so a network trained at one *dt* and evaluated at another collapses. Taxonomises three ways to carry a Poisson input across *dt*s (zero-pad, resample, integer-bin downsample) and gives a zero-order-hold (ZOH) weight rescaling (Eq 27) plus an average-loss regulariser that recover *dt*-invariant training. Used in: [dt-stability](/notebooks/dt-stability/), [Models § CUBA](/models/#cuba), [The Oscilloscope § Input flags](/the-oscilloscope/#input-flags-shared).

## Surrogate-gradient SNN training

- **eshraghian-et-al** — Eshraghian et al., *Training Spiking Neural Networks Using Lessons From Deep Learning*, Proc. IEEE 2023. [arXiv:2109.12894](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.12894). The snnTorch reference paper: surveys how ANN deep-learning recipes (optimisers, init, regularisation, batchnorm variants, losses) transfer — or don't — to surrogate-gradient SNNs, and documents the snnTorch library's canonical update forms. The shape of our *snnTorch-library* and *standard-snn* models, and the fast-sigmoid surrogate default, follow this paper. Used in: [Models](/models/), [Training § Surrogate gradients](/training/#surrogate-gradients).
- **neftci-et-al** — Neftci, Mostafa & Zenke, *Surrogate Gradient Learning in Spiking Neural Networks*, IEEE Signal Processing Magazine 2019. [arXiv:1901.09948](https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.09948). The canonical statement of the surrogate-gradient method: replace the non-differentiable spike with a smooth pseudo-derivative only in the backward pass, leaving the forward dynamics binary. Establishes the fast-sigmoid / box / SuperSpike family and gives the stability argument for why a slope around 1 works where slope 40 (Cramer) is only stable under specific update forms. Used in: [Training § Surrogate gradients](/training/#surrogate-gradients), [Models](/models/).
- **burghi-et-al** — Burghi et al., costate-based SNN training (2026 draft). Introduces a learnable controller $K_\theta(t, x)$ directly into the adjoint (costate) equations; by choosing $K_\theta$ to make the costate dynamics exponentially contracting, they bound the gradient matrices uniformly while preserving the global optimum — a principled replacement for our current constant adjoint-scaling factor. Used in: [Training § The training loop](/training/#the-training-loop).

## PING / gamma rhythms

- **borgers-neural-computation** — Börgers, *An Introduction to Modeling Neuronal Dynamics*, Texts in Applied Mathematics 66, Springer 2017. Textbook on reduced neuron and network models: single-cell dynamics (LIF, QIF, Izhikevich, HH), synapse kinetics, and population phenomena including PING. Source of the tonic-drive step-plus-heterogeneity recipe, the 4:1 E:I ratio, $\tau_m^E/\tau_m^I = 4$, and the "PING needs no E→E" initialisation used throughout pinglab. Used in: [Models § PING](/models/#ping), [Training § Tasks and inputs](/training/#tasks-and-inputs).
- **chapter-30-ping** — Kopell & Börgers, PING chapter in Börgers (2017). Focused derivation of pyramidal-interneuron gamma: an E population tonically driven just above threshold, coupled E→I→E with delayed inhibition on a GABA timescale, synchronises at gamma frequency even with zero E→E coupling. Gives the frequency-vs-drive and frequency-vs-$\tau_\text{GABA}$ relationships we rely on for the gamma regime. Used in: [Models § PING](/models/#ping).
- **buzsaki-and-wang** — Buzsáki & Wang, *Mechanisms of gamma oscillations*, Annu. Rev. Neurosci. 2012. [doi:10.1146/annurev-neuro-062111-150444](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-neuro-062111-150444). Review of gamma generation in cortex and hippocampus: compares ING (interneuron-only) and PING (pyramidal–interneuron) mechanisms, catalogues the biophysical constants — in particular the 8–12 ms GABA$_\text{A}$ decay window pinglab rounds to 9 ms — and frames gamma as a substrate for cortical communication and gating. Used in: [Models § PING](/models/#ping).
- **fries** — Fries, *A mechanism for cognitive dynamics: neuronal communication through neuronal coherence*, Trends in Cognitive Sciences 2005. [doi:10.1016/j.tics.2005.08.011](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2005.08.011). Proposes Communication Through Coherence (CTC): only neural groups whose oscillations are phase-aligned exchange information effectively, because the post-synaptic target's excitability window repeats at the rhythm's period. The functional motivation for studying gamma rhythms in a trainable network, rather than treating them as incidental.
- **kopelletal-et-al** — Kopell et al., gamma/theta rhythm theory. Mathematical analysis of nested gamma-within-theta rhythms and the role of interneuron subpopulations (fast-spiking vs low-threshold-spiking) in multiplexing frequency bands — background on why PING sits inside a larger hierarchy of cortical rhythms.
- **viriyopase-et-al** — Viriyopase, Memmesheimer & Tiesinga, comparative analysis of ING and PING gamma mechanisms. Source of the 2–3× I→E vs E→I weight-ratio rule used as the pinglab default ($W^{IE} \approx 3$, $W^{EI} \approx 1$): shows that PING's frequency, robustness to heterogeneity, and lock-in range all depend on this asymmetry. Used in: [Models § PING](/models/#ping).
- **segneri-et-al** — Segneri et al., PING network dynamics. Numerical study of PING in larger balanced networks, characterising how sparseness and input heterogeneity push the regime between synchrony, splay states, and chaos — sets expectations for the kind of regime transitions we should see as pinglab scales up hidden sizes.

## Balanced / asynchronous state

- **renart** — Renart et al., *The asynchronous state in cortical circuits*, Science 2010. [doi:10.1126/science.1179850](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1179850). Shows that tightly balanced E/I networks produce a genuine asynchronous irregular state with near-zero cross-correlations despite dense shared input, resolving the apparent contradiction with Shadlen & Newsome. The counterpoint to PING: gamma-locked activity is not the only plausible cortical regime, and balanced-network pretraining is a natural ablation target for pinglab.
- **shadlen** — Shadlen & Newsome, *The variable discharge of cortical neurons*, J. Neurosci. 1998. Argues that cortical spike trains can be Poisson-like with $\text{CV} \approx 1$ only if excitation and inhibition are approximately balanced — the classical motivation for the balanced-network literature, and for why pinglab tracks $\text{CV}$ and firing rate alongside accuracy.

## Spiking MNIST / benchmarks

- **cramer-et-al** — Cramer, Stradmann, Schemmel & Zenke, *The Heidelberg spiking datasets for the systematic evaluation of spiking neural networks*, IEEE TNNLS 2022 (preprint Oct 2020). [arXiv:1910.07407](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.07407). Introduces SHD (10,420 recordings of spoken digits 0–9 in English and German) and SSC, each converted through a biologically-motivated cochlea model into 700-channel spike rasters of about one second per trial. Also fixes the reference SNN recipe most subsequent work measures against: current-based LIF, exponential synapse, LI (non-spiking) readout, full-BPTT with fast-sigmoid surrogate ($\beta = 40$), two-sided firing-rate regulariser, Adamax. Headline numbers: 48.1–48.6% feedforward SNN, 71.4% RSNN. Used in: [shd](/notebooks/shd/), [The Oscilloscope § Subcommand train](/the-oscilloscope/#subcommand-train).
- **yan-et-al** — Yan et al., sequential-MNIST SNN architectures (2025). Depth precedent for sMNIST: shows that the 28-input bottleneck makes single-hidden-layer SNNs underperform badly and that 2+ hidden layers are needed for competitive accuracy — the finding that prompted pinglab's deep-sMNIST configuration.
- **yan-et-al-supplementary** — Supplementary material for the Yan et al. paper above: extended ablations on hidden size, depth, and readout choices for sequential MNIST.
- **xing-et-al** — Xing et al., SNN classification benchmarks. Comparative study of surrogate-gradient architectures on standard static and event-based image benchmarks; useful as a sanity-check table for where a given depth / width / timestep budget should land.
- **lee-et-al** — Lee et al., surrogate-gradient MNIST. Early demonstration that surrogate-gradient BPTT can close most of the gap between SNNs and ANNs on MNIST under matched capacity — one of the baselines pinglab implicitly compares against on the static-image tasks.
- **nguyen-et-al** — Nguyen et al., SNN training tricks. Collects practical training tricks (initialisation scales, regularisation, learning-rate schedules, gradient-clip ranges) that stabilise deep SNN training; the grab-bag of heuristics pinglab draws on when a run diverges.
- **pes-et-al** — Pes et al., PES learning rule. Prescribed Error Sensitivity — an online, biologically-plausible, local learning rule for SNNs used in Nengo-style networks. Reference point for local / bio-plausible alternatives to the full-BPTT training loop pinglab currently uses.
- **nandi-et-al** — Nandi et al., neuromorphic benchmarks. Benchmarks SNN workloads on neuromorphic hardware, quantifying the latency / energy / accuracy trade-offs that constrain what a trained pinglab model would cost to deploy.
