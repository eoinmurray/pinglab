A position piece, not an experiment. The claim: the same recurrent E↔I loop that this project trains for spike economy could double as the *timing reference* for a neuromorphic substrate — a clock that the network grows for itself, instead of one piped in from a crystal and a clock tree. If that holds, the rhythm we already pay for (Claims 1–2) is not just a gate on the excitatory rate; it is the missing low-power clock.

## The clock tax

Neuromorphic hardware sells itself on energy. The pitch — spikes are sparse, events are rare, so a chip that only does work when a spike arrives should sip power where a GPU gulps it. The problem is that "doing work only on events" still needs a shared sense of *when*, and in conventional digital design that sense of time is expensive.

In a synchronous chip the clock is distributed to every register through a balanced clock-distribution network — the clock tree. It toggles every cycle, everywhere, whether or not the logic underneath it is computing. Its dynamic power is

$$
P_\text{clk} = \alpha\, C_\text{clk}\, V_\text{dd}^2\, f
$$

- $P_\text{clk}$ — dynamic power burned in the clock network;
- $\alpha$ — activity factor (for the clock itself, $\alpha = 1$: it switches every cycle by definition);
- $C_\text{clk}$ — total switched capacitance of the clock tree (buffers, wires, register clock pins);
- $V_\text{dd}$ — supply voltage;
- $f$ — clock frequency.

Because the clock's activity factor is pinned at one while the logic's is far below one, the clock network is routinely one of the single largest power consumers on a synchronous die — commonly reported in the range of a fifth to nearly half of total dynamic power. That is the tax: a constant energy draw whose only product is *synchrony*.

## Event-driven hardware does not abolish the clock — it hides it

The usual rebuttal is that neuromorphic chips are asynchronous and event-driven, so the clock tax does not apply. That is half true. TrueNorth (Merolla et al. 2014) is genuinely event-driven and famously low-power; Loihi (Davies et al. 2018) and SpiNNaker (Furber et al. 2014) lean on asynchronous fabrics and message passing rather than one global edge. But every one of them still needs a notion of time — synaptic delays, membrane leak, refractory periods, and the discrete *timestep* that a digital spiking model integrates over are all defined against a tick. In practice the global edge is replaced by a global timestep barrier: neurons advance one $\Delta t$, synchronize, advance again. That barrier is a clock by another name, and keeping a large array coherent against it costs energy and, worse, *scales badly* — the synchronization overhead grows with the number of cores you must hold in lockstep.

So the honest framing is not "neuromorphic chips have no clock" but "neuromorphic chips spend energy manufacturing a shared sense of time, and that spend is one of the things standing between them and their promised efficiency."

## The proposal: let the loop tell the time

This project's central object is a minimal pyramidal–interneuron loop that, switched on, turns asynchronous excitatory firing into a gamma rhythm (Claim 1) and does so smoothly and predictably across the coupling plane via a supercritical Hopf onset (Claim 2). The rhythm is *architecture-generated* — it falls out of the E↔I dynamics, it is not injected as a prior, and gradient descent does not have to discover it.

Read that as a hardware primitive rather than a neuroscience result and the proposal writes itself: **use the PING rhythm as the timing reference.** The gamma cycle of period

$$
T_\gamma = \frac{1}{f_\gamma}
$$

- $T_\gamma$ — duration of one gamma cycle, the candidate "clock period";
- $f_\gamma$ — the architecture-generated gamma frequency (≈ 25 Hz at the project's default operating point, tunable through $\tau_\text{GABA}$ and the drive, per Claims 2 and 6),

becomes the frame within which computation happens. Claim 5 is what makes this more than analogy: in the trained network the excitatory rate is pinned to the clock at roughly one spike per cell per cycle, $r_E = a + p\,f_\gamma$. The rhythm does not merely coexist with the spikes — it *quantizes* them into discrete volleys, one per frame. A discretizer that emits a clean periodic edge and aligns the computation to it is, functionally, a clock.

The attraction is that this clock is not piped in. It is generated by the same neurons that compute, on the same wires, from the same supply. There is no separate distribution network with $\alpha = 1$ burning power across the die for synchrony alone; the synchrony is a *byproduct* of the recurrent inhibition the network runs anyway. A self-organized clock pays no clock-tree tax because it has no clock tree.

## Why a rhythm buys reliability, not just timing

The second half of the claim — "reliable spiking computation" — is the more interesting one. Analog or mixed-signal spiking neurons are noisy: device mismatch, thermal noise, and integration drift mean that two nominally identical neurons given the same input fire at slightly different times, and that jitter *accumulates* over a free-running computation. Without something to re-reference against, an asynchronous spiking array slowly loses coherence.

A rhythm fixes this for free. Each gamma cycle the inhibitory volley silences the excitatory pool and re-opens the window; spikes that would have smeared across time get herded toward the rising phase of the next cycle. The rhythm acts as a periodic *resynchronization*, bounding accumulated jitter to within one cycle rather than letting it grow without limit. This is exactly the role gamma is argued to play biologically — framing and gating communication so that what arrives in-phase is read and what arrives out-of-phase is suppressed (Buzsáki & Wang 2012; Fries 2015). The brain runs reliable computation on noisy, slow, leaky components without a quartz crystal; it appears to use self-organized rhythms to do it. That is an existence proof for the engineering claim.

## What this project already supplies, and what it does not

Three of the existing claims are load-bearing for this idea, and it is worth being precise about which:

- **The clock exists and is cheap to make** — Claim 1 (the loop generates gamma from minimal structure) and Claim 2 (the onset is a smooth, predictable Hopf, not a fragile knife-edge). A timing reference you cannot reliably bring up is no use; the supercritical onset says you can.
- **The clock actually quantizes computation** — Claim 5 (the E rate is pinned to the cycle at ≈ one spike per cell per cycle). This is what distinguishes a clock from a mere oscillation in a power trace.
- **The clock is robust to the implementation** — Claim 6 (the dynamics are reported in physical Hz and are invariant to the integration step $\Delta t$). A timing reference whose behaviour changed with the simulator's timestep would not survive contact with hardware.

What the project does *not* yet supply is anything addressing the hardware claim directly. There is no energy model of the loop as a clock, no comparison against a clock-tree baseline, and no test of whether downstream computation latched to the gamma phase is actually more reliable than the same computation run free. Those are experiments, not corollaries.

## The honest caveats

This is not a free lunch, and a few objections are strong enough to state plainly:

- **A self-organized clock drifts.** $f_\gamma$ moves with drive, with $\tau_\text{GABA}$, and on real silicon it would move with temperature and mismatch. A crystal gives you parts-per-million stability; gamma gives you a frame whose *absolute* frequency wanders. This is fine for computation that cares about relative timing and ordering *within* a cycle, and useless as a frequency standard. The honest pitch is "framing reference," not "replacement for the system clock that times your I/O."
- **The clock is not actually free — you pay for it in inhibitory spikes.** The rhythm is generated by the I population's synchronous volleys, and this project already insists on accounting spikes as E + I for exactly this reason. The bet is that the I-spike budget is far smaller than a clock tree's constant draw, but that is a quantitative claim that needs measuring, not asserting.
- **One loop is one local clock.** A single PING pool synchronizes the neurons coupled into it. Tiling a whole die means *coupled* oscillators, which generically gives phase gradients and traveling waves rather than one global phase. That is plausibly a feature — biological cortex uses traveling gamma — but it means "global synchrony" is precisely the thing you would have to give up, and any architecture built on this has to want local frames, not one die-wide edge.
- **Jitter is bounded, not eliminated.** Resynchronization caps accumulated jitter at roughly one cycle; it does not make spike times exact. Whether that bound is tight enough for a given computation is, again, an empirical question.

## A concrete first experiment

The cheapest test that would move this from essay to result: take a trained network, inject per-neuron timing noise (membrane noise, mismatch in $\tau_\text{GABA}$), and measure a task-relevant reliability metric — say, the trial-to-trial variance of the readout — as a function of noise amplitude, with the loop on versus off. The prediction is that the loop-on network degrades far more gracefully, because each cycle re-references the population. Pair that with a back-of-the-envelope energy comparison — total I-spike count to maintain the rhythm versus the modelled switched capacitance of a clock tree distributing the same effective frame rate across an equivalent array — and the two halves of the claim, *reliability* and *energy*, each get a number. Until then this is a hypothesis with good priors, no more.
