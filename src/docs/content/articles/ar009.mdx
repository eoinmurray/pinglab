## Introduction

- Gamma in the brain: where it appears (cortex, hippocampus), the 30–80 Hz band, and the cognitive functions it is tied to — attention, binding, and gating (Buzsáki & Wang 2012; Fries 2015), with the original empirical attention–gamma link in visual cortex (Fries et al. 2001).
- The "communication through coherence" hypothesis and its critics — why phase, not just rate, may carry information (Fries 2015; Akam & Kullmann 2012), against the temporal-binding skeptics (Shadlen & Movshon 1999).

- The PING mechanism: gamma as an emergent property of a recurrent excitatory↔inhibitory loop, not a pacemaker (Börgers 2017; Börgers & Kopell 2003, 2005) — backed by causal experimental evidence that fast-spiking interneurons generate the rhythm (Whittington et al. 1995 in vitro; Cardin et al. 2009 and Sohal et al. 2009 via optogenetics), and by the interneuron synaptic mechanisms that synchronise it (Bartos et al. 2007).

- State of the art in *modelling* gamma: biophysical PING networks (Kopell et al. 2010; Viriyopase et al. 2016) and next-generation neural-mass / mean-field reductions (Wilson & Cowan 1972; Segneri et al. 2020; Nandi et al. 2024) — all descriptive, none trained on a task.

- State of the art in *training* spiking networks: surrogate-gradient descent makes SNNs trainable end-to-end (Neftci et al. 2019; Eshraghian et al. 2023), but the trained networks are not rhythmic.

- The gap: gamma is either modelled-but-not-trained or trained-but-not-rhythmic. The recent oscillation-modulated SNN work (Yan et al. 2025) injects oscillations as a prior rather than letting an E↔I loop generate them.

- Why spikes are costly in the brain: the cortical energy budget is dominated by the metabolic cost of action potentials and excitatory synaptic transmission (Attwell & Laughlin 2001; Howarth et al. 2012), so a low firing rate is a metabolic imperative for cortex, not a convenience.

- The biological puzzle we address: cortical pyramidal cells fire at strikingly low rates (often ≪ 10 Hz) despite strong, dense excitatory drive (Barth & Poulet 2012) — what holds them down? We offer architecture-generated gamma gating as one concrete, mechanistic answer.

- The rate-vs-timing framing: classical views treat rate and spike timing as competitors; we show a regime where the rhythm *sets* the rate, so the two are one quantity (Ainsworth et al. 2012).

- This work: we train a PING network on a task for the first time, and show the architecture-generated gamma rhythm *gates* the excitatory population to an order-of-magnitude-lower spike rate at matched accuracy.

- The headline claim in one line: gamma gating buys a per-spike economy that is supplied by the architecture, not learned — and it operates online, on continuous input streams, as cortical processing must.

- Roadmap of the paper: build the model (Claim 1), locate the onset in theory + data (Claim 2), show the trained economy (Claim 3), prove it is architectural not learned (Claim 4), pin the rate to the clock (Claim 5), test the dynamics for robustness (Claim 6), and demonstrate streaming use (Claim 7).

## Methods

- Single-neuron model: the LIF/conductance dynamics, membrane time constant, threshold and reset, define every term ($V$, $g_E$, $g_I$, $E_E$, $E_I$, $\tau_m$, $\tau_\text{GABA}$).
- Synapse model: conductance-based (COBA), the canonical decay-then-add update $g \leftarrow g\,\text{decay} + W\,\text{spike}$, so a spike's full weight is the peak conductance.
- Network architecture: input → hidden E population, with the optional recurrent loop E→I ($W_{ei}$) and I→E ($W_{ie}$); readout $W_\text{out}$ over the E layer. State that COBA is the loop-off limit ($W_{ei} = W_{ie} = 0$).
- Modelling choice — no $W_{ee}$, no $W_{ii}$: keep the loop minimal so any rhythm is unambiguously PING (E↔I), not ING (I↔I) or recurrent-E driven; cite the PING/ING distinction (Börgers 2017; Viriyopase et al. 2016).
- Modelling choice — fixed (untrained) $W_{ei}$, $W_{ie}$: the loop is a structural prior, not a learned feature; defended empirically by Claim 4 (Figures 4–5) where unfreezing the weights prunes the loop away. Dale's-law clamp keeps E and I signs fixed.
- Rhythmicity metric: the lobe–trough contrast (scored 0–1, nb054), with a short literature note on how it relates to spectral-peak and population-coherence measures (Atallah & Scanziani 2009; Xing et al. 2012).
- Mean-field reduction: the 4D conductance mean-field used in Claim 2, calibrated from the biophysics with no fitted scale; the Hopf-bifurcation framing (Wilson & Cowan 1972; Segneri et al. 2020).
- Training: surrogate-gradient descent on MNIST, optimiser, epochs, seeds; the spike-budget penalty $\theta_u$ that traces the accuracy–rate frontier (Neftci et al. 2019; Eshraghian et al. 2023).
- Gradient damping for the PING case: the method that stabilises backprop through the recurrent loop, why the stiff E↔I dynamics need it, and what it changes vs vanilla surrogate gradients.
- Surrogate-gradient choice: the surrogate function and its width, and how the spike nonlinearity is handled in the backward pass (Neftci et al. 2019).
- Dale's-law clamp: how E and I weight signs are enforced through training, and why this matters for the prune-the-loop result (Claim 4).
- Default parameters table: the canonical $\tau_\text{GABA}$, $W^{IE}$, pool sizes $N_E$/$N_I$, $\Delta t$, and drive — the operating point all "default" runs use, with the swept ranges noted per claim.
- Integration scheme: the ODE solver and timestep $\Delta t$, with the invariance check (Claim 6, Figure 11) showing results are reported in physical Hz not per-step.
- Rate and frequency measurement: how mean E/I rate is computed, how $f_\gamma$ is extracted (Welch PSD peak), and the (cell, cycle) binning used for the spikes-per-cycle statistic (Figure 7).
- Spike accounting: report both the mean E rate and the total population spike count (E + I), so the economy is stated net of the I pool. Note qualitatively that excitatory spikes are the costly currency in cortex (Attwell & Laughlin 2001; Howarth et al. 2012) — we do not build a network-specific metabolic model.
- Reproducibility: seeds per condition, the size tiers used for each run, and pointers to the per-notebook repro scripts.
- Software and implementation: the model, training, and analysis are implemented in Python (≥ 3.10). Spiking dynamics and surrogate-gradient training use PyTorch (2.11; with snnTorch 0.9 for baseline spiking primitives); numerical analysis uses NumPy (2.2) and SciPy (1.15); all figures are produced with Matplotlib (3.10). Each result has a standalone notebook script and each manuscript figure is regenerated, at print quality, by a single render pipeline driven from those scripts.
- Code and data availability: all source, notebook scripts, trained-weight artefacts, and the figure-render pipeline are in the project repository (link in end-matter); the standard datasets (MNIST; SHD for the classification benchmark) are obtained from their public distributors.
- Datasets and evaluation: MNIST presentation protocol (duration, input rate), the streaming protocol for Claim 7 (sliding leaky integrator over $W_\text{out}$), and the metrics reported (test accuracy, mean E/I rate, $f_\gamma$).

### Claim 4. The sparsity is supplied by the architecture, not learned

<Figure id="Figure 4" title="The I-loop is a post-hoc sparsity knob" src="/figures/notebooks/nb038/loop_transfer_compound.png" alt="Two-by-two figure. Top: single-trial rasters of a trained COBA replayed at ei=0 (dense asynchronous, I silent) and ei=1 (gamma bands, synchronous I bursts in red above E). Bottom left: mean E (black) and I (red) firing rate vs inference E→I strength — E falls from ≈60 to ≈5 Hz, I rises to ≈28 Hz. Bottom right: test accuracy vs ei strength, falling from the ≈87% COBA baseline to ≈63% at ei=1.">
  Switch the recurrent loop on *at inference* on a trained COBA, with no retraining ([nb038](/notebooks/nb038/)). **Top** — the same feedforward weights fire densely/asynchronously at $ei=0$ and in gamma bands at $ei=1$. **Bottom left** — E rate falls ≈ 10× (≈ 60 → 5 Hz) as the loop engages. **Bottom right** — accuracy *degrades* without retraining (≈ 87% → 63%, a ≈ 24 pp cost). The reading: the rate-gating is **architectural** (it appears the instant the loop is wired in), but the accuracy must be **learned** by training with the loop (Claim 3). Together with the converse — unfreeze the recurrent weights and gradient descent *prunes* the loop away rather than discovering it ([nb049](/notebooks/nb049/), Dale's-law clamp; Figure 5) — the loop is a structural prior the architecture imposes, not one training finds.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 5" title="Gradient descent prunes the loop — it does not discover PING" src="/figures/notebooks/nb049/training_curves.png" alt="Four panels (2×2) of per-epoch training curves over 100 epochs, coloured by initial condition: PING init (black), zero init (red), small-seed init (amber), and the frozen-PING control (grey dashed); mean over three seeds with the across-seed envelope shaded. A: test accuracy — all conditions overlap at ≈88–90%. B: E firing rate — trainable runs climb (zero init ≈55 Hz, small init ≈45, PING init ≈25) while the frozen control stays gated at ≈8 Hz. C: I firing rate — every trainable run collapses to ≈0 while the frozen control rises to ≈45 Hz.   The converse test ([nb049](/notebooks/nb049/)): unfreeze the recurrent weights $W^{EI}, W^{IE}$ and let Adam train them, tracked per epoch. **A** — accuracy is identical across all conditions (≈ 88–90%); pruning the loop costs nothing. **B–C** — from *any* initialisation (canonical PING, zero, or small-seed) the **I rate collapses to ≈ 0 within a few epochs** while the E rate climbs, as the Dale's-law clamp turns the pruned $W^{EI}$ into structural zeros; only the frozen control keeps the loop (I ≈ 45 Hz, E gated near 8 Hz). **D** — the rhythmicity (nb054 lobe–trough contrast) tells the same story: the frozen control holds at ≈ 0.9 while every trainable init drains to near zero. Gradient descent does not rediscover PING; the loop is a structural prior the **freeze imposes**. Read with Figure 4, the two halves close the claim: the architecture supplies the gating, and training neither needs it nor finds it.
</Figure>

### Claim 5. The E rate is set by the gamma clock: one spike per cell per cycle

<Figure id="Figure 6" title="The E rate is set by the gamma clock: r_E = a + p·f_γ" src="/figures/notebooks/nb041/rate_vs_fgamma.png" alt="Top panel: scatter of mean post-training E rate (Hz) vs measured gamma frequency f_γ (Hz), six clusters one per τ_GABA value (4.5 to 27 ms), three seeds each, error bars in both axes; an affine fit r_E = 0.76 + 0.216·f_γ passes through every error bar with R² = 0.997. Bottom panel: test accuracy vs the same x-axis, flat at 81–89% across the whole f_γ range.">
  Retrain the network at each $\tau_\text{GABA}$ ([nb041](/notebooks/nb041/)) to sweep the gamma frequency $f_\gamma$, and the post-training E rate tracks it **affinely**: $r_E = 0.76 + 0.216\,f_\gamma$ ($R^2 = 0.997$, three seeds per point). The slope $p \approx 0.2$ is the per-cycle E-cell participation; the intercept is a small non-rhythmic baseline. **Bottom** — accuracy holds at 81–89% across the whole range, so the cycle clock constrains the rate without costing accuracy. The E rate is not a free parameter: it is set by the rhythm — roughly one spike per cell every few gamma cycles.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 7" title="One spike per cell per cycle — the rate law's microscopic cause" src="/figures/notebooks/nb046/spikes_per_cycle_distribution.png" alt="Six bar-chart panels, one per τ_GABA value. Each panel shows the fraction of (E cell, gamma cycle) pairs emitting 0, 1, 2, or ≥3 spikes within the cycle window. Across all panels P(0) ≈ 77%, P(1) ≈ 22%, P(2) ≈ 0.5%, P(≥3) ≈ 0.04%; the ≥2 fraction grows mildly with τ_GABA but stays below 3%.">
  Why is the rate affine in $f_\gamma$? Because each E cell fires **at most once per gamma cycle** ([nb046](/notebooks/nb046/)). Resolving the spikes into (cell, cycle) pairs, $99.45\%$ emit $\leq 1$ spike: across the whole $\tau_\text{GABA}$ sweep $P(0) \approx 77\%$, $P(1) \approx 22\%$, and multi-spike events ($\geq 2$) stay below $\sim 3\%$. A cell either skips the cycle or contributes a single spike — the I-volley resets the population before a second can fire. So the E rate is just the per-cycle participation fraction times the cycle frequency, which is exactly the affine law of Figure 6.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 8" title="The rate knob is the per-synapse weight W^IE, not the I pool size" src="/figures/notebooks/nb047/rate_vs_w_ie.png" alt="Two side-by-side panels of per-cell firing rate versus the per-synapse I-to-E weight W^IE in microsiemens, from 0.5 to 16. Left: E rate. Right: I rate. Each panel has three lines, one per pool size N_I = 16, 64, 256, and the three lines lie on top of one another. The E rate falls from ≈ 14 Hz at W^IE = 0.5 to ≈ 9.6 Hz at W^IE = 16; the I rate falls from ≈ 42 Hz to ≈ 27 Hz. A dotted vertical line marks the default W^IE = 2.">
  What sets the suppression strength? The *per-synapse* I→E weight $W^{IE}$, not how many interneurons there are ([nb047](/notebooks/nb047/)). Sweeping $W^{IE}$ across three I-pool sizes ($N_I = 16, 64, 256$), the E and I rate curves **collapse onto a single line** — the three pool sizes are indistinguishable. The rate is governed by the strength of each inhibitory contact, so the loop scales by synaptic gain rather than headcount: a handful of strong interneurons gate the E population as effectively as a large pool.
</Figure>

### Claim 6. The gating is dynamical and robust

<Figure id="Figure 9" title="The gating is robust to dropped spikes but fragile to added ones" src="/figures/notebooks/nb037/perturbation_curves.png" alt="Two-panel accuracy plot, both axes in percentage units. Left: accuracy vs % of emitted spikes dropped (0–100%). PING (black) holds ≈ 82% through 80% drop then collapses to chance by 100%; COBA (red) tracks slightly below, collapsing similarly. Right: accuracy vs added Poisson noise as % of each population's baseline rate (0–100%). PING falls off a sharp cliff between 15% and 50% relative noise, reaching chance by 65%. COBA holds 82–88% through 50% then drifts down to 75% at 100% noise.">
  Perturbing a trained PING's spikes at inference reveals a sharp **drop/add asymmetry** ([nb037](/notebooks/nb037/)). **Left** — dropping emitted spikes is benign: PING holds ≈ 82% accuracy through an *80%* drop, only collapsing as the network goes fully silent. The code is robust to deletion because the surviving gamma cycles still carry the digit. **Right** — added Poisson spikes are corrosive: PING falls off a cliff by ≈ 15% relative noise and reaches chance by 65%, because off-phase spikes drive the I-pool at arbitrary phase and dissolve the rhythm. COBA, having no rhythm to protect, is the mirror image — it tolerates added noise but has no structure to exploit. The gating lives in spike *timing*: silence is information, but noise breaks the clock.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 10" title="The suppression is the rhythm, not mean inhibition" src="/figures/notebooks/nb042/rhythm_compound.png" alt="Two-by-two figure. Top row, two single-trial PING rasters, E black below and I red above. Left: per-I-cell jitter σ = 5 ms, I smeared into continuous scatter, E silent (E ≈ 0 Hz, I ≈ 40 Hz). Right: cycle-coherent jitter σ = 100 ms, bursts displaced but crisp, E firing densely (E ≈ 44 Hz, I ≈ 35 Hz). Bottom row, E rate (black) and accuracy (red) vs σ. Bottom left: per-I-cell jitter, E rate falls to ≈ 0 Hz, accuracy to chance. Bottom right: cycle-coherent jitter, E rate rises from ≈ 8 to ≈ 50 Hz, accuracy ≈ 80%.">
  The rate gap cannot be explained away as "the I-loop just delivers more inhibition." Two inference-time perturbations of the trained PING I-stream — **both holding mean per-cell I rate fixed** ([nb042](/notebooks/nb042/)) — drive the E rate in *opposite* directions. **Left** — per-I-cell jitter smears each burst into a continuous shunt at the same mean rate; the E rate *falls to zero* and accuracy collapses to chance. **Right** — cycle-coherent jitter displaces each gamma burst while preserving its within-burst synchrony; gaps open and the E rate *rises* from ≈ 8 Hz toward ≈ 50 Hz, accuracy holding near 80%. Identical mean inhibition, opposite outcome: the gate is the *timing* of inhibition, not its average.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 11" title="The rate is a physical-Hz property, invariant across a 20× Δt sweep" src="/figures/notebooks/nb044/dt_sweep.png" alt="Two-axis plot. Log x-axis: integration timestep Δt from 0.05 to 1.0 ms. Left y-axis (black diamonds, 0–50 Hz scale): post-training hidden E rate sits in an 8.4–12 Hz band across the whole sweep, non-monotonic in Δt. Right y-axis (red squares): test accuracy holds essentially flat at 86.8–89.5% across the entire sweep, a span of 2.7 percentage points.">
  Is the sparse rate a real biophysical quantity or a discretisation artefact? Retraining the PING across a **20× span of integration timestep** ($\Delta t = 0.05 \to 1.0$ ms) leaves it intact ([nb044](/notebooks/nb044/)): the post-training E rate stays in a tight $8.4$–$12$ Hz band — non-monotonic in $\Delta t$, so finer stepping does not buy a lower rate — and accuracy holds flat within $2.7$ pp. The bursts keep the same physical cadence (≈ 30 ms period) regardless of $\Delta t$. The rate is measured in spikes per second, not spikes per step: a property of the dynamics, not of the solver.
</Figure>

### Claim 7. The economy is usable: real-time streaming classification

The economy would be academic if it only worked on static, one-digit-at-a-time presentations. It does not: a PING trained on single MNIST digits classifies a *continuous stream* with no retraining and no segmentation cues ([nb048](/notebooks/nb048/)). The readout is a sliding leaky integrator over the same trained $W_\text{out}$; the network re-identifies each new digit within ≈ 1 gamma cycle of the switch.

<Figure id="Figure 12" title="Robust to per-digit changes in duration and drive within one stream" src="/figures/notebooks/nb048/varying_headline_stream.png" alt="Four-panel column figure. Top: five MNIST digits across 450 ms, each segment labelled with its own (τ, rate) and a prediction badge; thumbnail opacity scales with input rate. Segments: (200 ms, 10 Hz)→5; (50 ms, 100 Hz)→3; (100 ms, 25 Hz)→4; (25 ms, 200 Hz)→1; (75 ms, 15 Hz)→7. All five correct. Hidden E and I rasters show gamma cycles throughout, sparser under weak drive and denser under strong drive; readout traces identify each segment cleanly.">
  A harder test: each digit gets its *own* duration (25–200 ms) and input rate (10–200 Hz) within a single stream, the sliding window using each segment's own τ. The gamma clock persists under both weak and strong drive, firing sparsely or densely to match, and every digit is still read out cleanly. **5/5 correct** — the streaming economy is not tuned to one presentation rate.
</Figure>

<Figure id="Figure 13" title="The gamma cycle is the temporal quantum of classification" src="/figures/notebooks/nb048/acc_grid_tau_rate.png" alt="Heatmap of per-segment accuracy across τ ∈ {10, 15, 25, 40, 50, 75, 100, 200} ms (x-axis) and input rate ∈ {5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 200} Hz (y-axis), 48 cells, averaged over 3 seeds × 1200 segments per cell, magma colormap 0–100%.   Per-segment accuracy over the full (τ, input-rate) grid (3 seeds × 1200 segments per cell). Three readings. **(1) Sub-cycle failure below τ ≈ 15 ms** — at τ = 10 ms even 200 Hz input reaches only 59%; the network cannot classify in less than one cycle whatever the drive, so the **gamma cycle is the temporal quantum** of its ability. **(2) Above one cycle, accuracy ≈ f(τ · rate)** — iso-accuracy contours run diagonal, so duration and drive substitute for one another. **(3) The trained operating point** (200 ms, 25 Hz) sits at 88%, interior to a broad plateau — the corners add only ≈ 3 pp, so there is headroom in both directions. The economy is usable across a wide envelope of stream timings, bounded below only by the cycle itself.

## Discussion

- What the results add up to: a single E↔I loop simultaneously generates gamma and clamps the E rate ≈10× at matched accuracy, and the gating is architectural (Claims 3–4) rather than learned.
- Why timing, not mean inhibition: the σ-jitter result (Figure 10) rules out the "more inhibition" explanation — identical mean per-cell I rate, opposite E-rate outcome — so the gate is the *phase structure* of inhibition (Schaefer et al. 2006; Ainsworth et al. 2012).
- The rate law as a coding statement: $r_E = a + p\,f_\gamma$ with ≤1 spike/cell/cycle (Figures 6–7) means the gamma cycle is the unit of computation — a concrete instance of rhythm-organised rate coding (Ainsworth et al. 2012; Fries 2015).
- Robustness asymmetry (Figure 9): tolerant to dropped spikes, fragile to added ones — connect to perturbation-sensitivity and rate-vs-timing debates (London et al. 2010; Renart et al. 2010), and to the claim that oscillations sharpen spike precision (Schaefer et al. 2006).
- Relation to the asynchronous balanced state: PING is a structured alternative to the asynchronous irregular regime (van Vreeswijk & Sompolinsky 1996; Renart et al. 2010); the loop trades chaos for a clock.
- Relation to inhibitory plasticity / E–I balance: we *fix* the loop where others *learn* balance (Vogels et al. 2011) — and show gradient descent actively prunes it, a result worth dwelling on.
- A functional role for gamma in cortex: beyond communication-through-coherence and binding, the rhythm may act as a metabolic gate that holds pyramidal firing rates low — a sparsity mechanism the microcircuit gets for free from its E↔I structure, requiring no learned tuning (contrast Yan et al. 2025, who impose oscillations rather than let them emerge from the loop).
- Is the economy net of the I population? PING trades a high E rate for a fast but small I pool, so by raw spike count the saving is ≈ 5× rather than ≈ 10×. But excitatory glutamatergic signaling dominates the cortical energy budget — an excitatory spike is far more expensive than an inhibitory one (Attwell & Laughlin 2001; Howarth et al. 2012) — so weighting spikes by cost recovers the order-of-magnitude figure. We do not attempt a detailed metabolic model: our network differs from cortex in too many ways (no $W_{ee}$/$W_{ii}$, no realistic synapse counts) for that to mean anything. The robust statement needs no model — even counting every spike equally the economy is still ≈ 5×.
- Limitations: single dataset (MNIST), single readout, fixed loop topology; the rhythmicity metric is one of several; mean-field calibrated but reduced.
- Biological caveats: minimal PING vs real cortical microcircuits (no $W_{ee}$/$W_{ii}$, no conduction delays, no heterogeneity); what would change with richer connectivity (Kopell et al. 2010; Nguyen & Rubchinsky 2021).
- The Hopf framing as explanation, not just description: because onset is a supercritical-reversible bifurcation (Claim 2), the rate-gate has no hysteretic dead-zone — it can be dialled smoothly, which is what makes the inference-time knob of Claim 4 possible.
- Why drop-tolerant but add-fragile is the *expected* signature of a clock: missing spikes leave the phase intact, off-phase spikes corrupt it — a falsifiable prediction for biological gamma circuits (London et al. 2010).
- The gamma cycle as a temporal quantum (Figure 13): a computational reading of "communication through coherence" — the cycle bounds how fast a decision can form, independent of drive (Fries 2015; Akam & Kullmann 2012).
- Capacity question raised but not answered: how many digits/classes can one gamma cycle carry, and how does that scale with assembly size (Börgers et al. 2012)?
- Honest negative: accuracy on MNIST is modest (≈83–90%); the contribution is the *mechanism and economy*, not a benchmark number — say so plainly.
- Generality caveat: we have not shown the gate survives on temporally structured inputs where timing carries the signal — that is exactly where it might help most, or break.

## Conclusion and Future Directions

- Restate the core finding: architecture-generated gamma is a usable, learned-into computation that gates spikes ≈10× at matched accuracy.
- The economy is real and physical: invariant across a 20× $\Delta t$ sweep (Figure 11), governed by per-synapse $W^{IE}$ not pool size (Figure 8), and usable for streaming classification (Figures 12–13).
- Future: harder datasets (SHD as the canonical classification benchmark), deeper / multi-layer PING stacks, and the planned independent 2D $\alpha_{EI} \times \alpha_{IE}$ sweep.
- Future: theta-nested gamma and multi-rhythm extensions (Segneri et al. 2020; Nandi et al. 2024); minimal-assembly size and capacity limits (Börgers et al. 2012).
- Future: closing the empirical $W$-plane / cliff picture (nb036) to fully match the mean-field bifurcation story.
- Future: temporally structured tasks (SHD, streaming sequences) where the gamma clock should help rather than just cost — the real test of timing-coding.
- Future: learned-but-constrained loops — relax the freeze with a structural regulariser so training can keep PING instead of pruning it, bridging our result with inhibitory-plasticity work (Vogels et al. 2011).
- Future: testable in vivo predictions — if gamma sets the pyramidal rate (Claim 5), manipulating interneuron timing (e.g. optogenetically) should move the E rate without changing mean inhibition, the in-circuit analogue of Figure 10.
- Closing line: gamma is not decoration — it is a candidate mechanism by which the cortical microcircuit computes at a sparse, metabolically cheap firing rate.
