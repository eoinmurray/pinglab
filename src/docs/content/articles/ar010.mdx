#### 1. Buzsáki & Wang (2012) — [*Mechanisms of Gamma Oscillations*](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-neuro-062111-150444)

*Summary.* This canonical review lays out the cellular-synaptic basis of gamma oscillations (defined as 30-90 Hz), arguing that gamma-band rhythmogenesis is "inextricably tied to perisomatic inhibition" via GABA_A synapses. It distinguishes the two mechanistic classes still used today: the interneuron-network (I-I / ING) model, where mutual inhibition among fast-spiking interneurons sets frequency by IPSC decay kinetics, and the excitatory-inhibitory-loop (E-I / PING) model, where pyramidal cells drive interneurons via AMPA and receive delayed GABA_A feedback so that fast excitation and delayed inhibition alternate. In the E-I model, axon-conduction and synaptic delays impose a characteristic phase lag (approximately 5 ms, up to ~90°) between pyramidal and interneuron spikes, and these delays set the gamma frequency; gamma coincides with irregular single-cell firing.

*Relevance.* This is the foundational citation defining the PING (E-I loop) mechanism our manuscript builds its entire architecture on: a fixed pyramidal-interneuron reciprocal loop with no E-E or I-I connections, so any rhythm is unambiguously PING by Buzsáki-Wang's taxonomy. It grounds our gamma frequency band (our ~25-44 Hz sits within their 30-90 Hz definition), our claim that gamma timing is set by the E-I loop and inhibitory kinetics rather than mean drive, and the E-to-I spike phase-lag signature that motivates our phase/timing-gate results (finding 6). It also legitimizes our conductance-based GABA_A perisomatic inhibition as the biophysical substrate of the gate, and frames gamma as a structural/architectural property of the E-I circuit rather than a learned one.

*Used for.* The opening of the Introduction (§1.1), in the sentence associating the gamma band with attention, binding, and gating in cortex — establishing at the outset that gamma is functionally implicated in cortical computation.

#### 2. Fries (2015) — [*Rhythms for Cognition: Communication through Coherence*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2015.09.034)

*Summary.* Fries's 2015 Neuron review lays out the Communication through Coherence (CTC) hypothesis: activated neuronal groups oscillate, and rhythmic inhibition creates recurring windows of high and low excitability, so effective communication between two groups requires their gamma rhythms to be coherent with the right phase lag — spikes from a presynaptic group must arrive in the postsynaptic group's window of maximal excitability to have impact. He argues gamma-band synchronization (roughly 30-90 Hz) is fast enough that a volley of excitation escapes the trailing inhibition and drives postsynaptic cells, while phase relationships select which inputs are heard. The updated framework adds a directional division of labor: predominantly bottom-up/feedforward influences are carried in gamma, and predominantly top-down/feedback control is exerted in the alpha-beta band (roughly 8-20 Hz).

*Relevance.* This is a canonical citation for the premise that gamma rhythms are functional rather than epiphenomenal — that the phase/timing structure of rhythmic inhibition, not its mean level, gates communication. It directly grounds our finding (6) that the gate lives in the timing/phase structure of inhibition (jitter that holds mean I-rate fixed still moves E-rate; the drop-tolerant/add-fragile asymmetry), which is essentially a CTC-style claim operationalized inside a trainable PING network. It also motivates why a single unambiguous PING E-to-I loop is worth isolating: CTC treats the PING gamma as the substrate for windowed excitability, and our work reframes that same windowing as a metabolic constraint that clamps excitatory firing rates roughly an order of magnitude below a non-rhythmic baseline.

*Used for.* Two places: §1.1, among the works linking gamma to attention, binding, and gating; and §3.3, where we read the gamma cycle as the computational unit for classification in the spirit of communication-through-coherence.

#### 3. Fries, Reynolds, Rorie & Desimone (2001) — [*Modulation of Oscillatory Neuronal Synchronization by Selective Visual Attention*](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1055465)

*Summary.* Fries, Reynolds, Rorie & Desimone recorded from macaque area V4 while monkeys attended one stimulus and ignored a nearby distracter. Neurons driven by the attended stimulus showed increased gamma-band (35–90 Hz) synchronization together with reduced low-frequency (&lt;17 Hz) synchronization relative to neurons at nearby sites driven by distracters. They argue that, because postsynaptic integration windows are short, this localized rise in gamma synchrony amplifies the impact of behaviorally relevant signals downstream — a landmark demonstration that attention modulates the temporal (phase) structure of firing, not just mean rate.

*Relevance.* This is a keystone empirical anchor for our claim that gamma's computational role lives in the timing/phase structure of activity rather than its mean level (finding 6). Fries et al. show attention selectively boosts gamma synchrony while their short-integration-window argument mirrors our result that a PING-trained network integrates a concatenated digit stream over roughly one gamma cycle (finding 7). It also grounds the broader framing that gamma is behaviorally functional and worth building into a spiking architecture, motivating our use of a fixed PING loop as a metabolic/structural gate rather than treating the rhythm as epiphenomenal.

*Used for.* §1.1, grouped with the citations tying gamma-band synchrony to attentional selection at the start of the Introduction.

#### 4. Gray, König, Engel & Singer (1989) — [*Oscillatory Responses in Cat Visual Cortex Exhibit Inter-Columnar Synchronization Which Reflects Global Stimulus Properties*](https://doi.org/10.1038/338334a0)

*Summary.* Gray, König, Engel and Singer recorded from cat striate cortex and showed that stimulus-driven neuronal responses oscillate in the gamma band (~40–60 Hz), and that oscillations in spatially separated cortical columns synchronize with near-zero phase lag. Critically, this inter-columnar synchronization is not fixed wiring but reflects global stimulus properties: two spatially separate cell groups synchronize strongly when driven by a single long continuous moving bar, but weakly or not at all when driven by two independent bars moving in opposite directions. This is the foundational empirical evidence for the "binding-by-synchrony" hypothesis — that gamma-band temporal coordination, rather than firing rate alone, carries relational structure.

*Relevance.* This is the canonical experimental grounding for treating cortical gamma as a real, functionally meaningful rhythm rather than an epiphenomenon — it anchors our claim that a PING E↔I loop producing a ~25–44 Hz gamma rhythm is biologically motivated. It most directly supports our finding (6) that the computational content of gamma lives in the timing/phase structure of activity, not its mean level, since Gray et al. show it is the temporal coordination (synchrony), not the rate, that encodes the global "belongs-together" relation. It also motivates the biophysical, non-trained E↔I coupling in our model: gamma here is a structural feature of cortical dynamics with a demonstrated stimulus-dependent role, consistent with our interpretation of gamma as an architectural constraint.

*Used for.* §1.1, cited as the original visual-cortex observation of stimulus-driven gamma.

#### 5. Whittington, Traub, Kopell, Ermentrout & Buhl (2000) — [*Inhibition-Based Rhythms: Experimental and Mathematical Observations on Network Dynamics*](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0167-8760(00)00173-2)

*Summary.* A foundational review synthesizing experiment, large-scale simulation, and mathematical analysis to argue that CNS oscillations spanning ~12-80 Hz (beta and gamma bands) are generated by, or inextricably linked to, the dynamics of GABA_A-ergic inhibitory interneurons. It sets out the two canonical circuit motifs for these rhythms — interneuron-network gamma (ING), where mutually coupled inhibitory cells synchronize on their own, and pyramidal-interneuron network gamma (PING), where the reciprocal excitatory-inhibitory feedback loop paces the rhythm — and shows the oscillation frequency is set by the decay kinetics of GABA_A inhibition, with each cell tending to fire at most once per cycle. It pairs pharmacology (anaesthetics, sedatives, ketamine/opiates acting on inhibition) with formal analysis of coupled-oscillator synchronization to explain how coherent network rhythms emerge as an collective property.

*Relevance.* This is the paper that names and defines the PING mechanism our architecture instantiates — a reciprocal E-to-I / I-to-E loop with no E-to-E or I-to-I coupling — so it grounds our claim that the rhythm in our network is unambiguously PING rather than ING. It also supplies the biophysical rationale for our central firing-rate results: the near-one-spike-per-gamma-cycle discipline it describes is exactly the mechanism behind our affine rate law r_E = a + p·f_gamma and the order-of-magnitude excitatory-rate clamp, while its Kopell/Ermentrout mathematical-analysis lineage is the tradition our 4D conductance mean-field / Hopf-bifurcation reduction extends.

*Used for.* §1.1, in the sentence distinguishing the two canonical generation mechanisms, ING and PING — the taxonomy that makes our pure E↔I loop unambiguously PING.

#### 6. Williams et al. (2026) — [*Fast Spiking Interneurons Autonomously Generate Fast Gamma Oscillations in the Medial Entorhinal Cortex with Excitation Strength Tuning ING-PING Transitions*](https://doi.org/10.1523/ENEURO.0452-25.2026)

*Summary.* Combining whole-cell patch-clamp with optogenetics (Thy1-ChR2 and PV-ChR2 mice) and NetPyNE network simulations (100 PV+ interneurons, 400 stellate cells), Williams et al. show that fast-spiking PV+ interneurons in medial entorhinal cortex autonomously generate fast gamma (interneuron-network gamma, ING) at ≈113–144 Hz — persisting with reduced power after AMPA-receptor blockade (DNQX), proving it does not require excitatory pacing. Crucially, their model demonstrates that the strength of excitatory drive onto interneurons is the tuning parameter for the ING↔PING transition: weak drive yields fast ING (≈100–140 Hz), while strengthening it shifts the network to slower PING-dominated oscillations (≈60–100 Hz).

*Relevance.* This directly grounds our manuscript's central mechanistic commitment: we build a network with a pure E↔I loop (no E→E or I→I) so that any rhythm is unambiguously PING, and this paper establishes the experimental/modeling distinction between ING (mutual inhibition) and PING (E↔I) that makes our architectural choice meaningful. Their finding that E→I coupling strength tunes the ING↔PING regime is the biological counterpart to our finding (2) that gamma onset across the W^EI × W^IE coupling plane is a Hopf bifurcation — both frame E→I synaptic strength as the control knob for whether and how gamma emerges. It supports our claim that a single E↔I loop is a legitimate, physiologically grounded PING generator rather than an artifact.

*Used for.* §1.1, alongside [5], for the ING-versus-PING distinction that motivates isolating a pure E↔I loop.

#### 7. Börgers (2017) — [*The PING Model of Gamma Rhythms*](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-51171-9_30)

*Summary.* Börgers' textbook chapter (Ch. 30 of "An Introduction to Modeling Neuronal Dynamics," Springer 2017) is a didactic derivation of the pyramidal–interneuron network gamma (PING) mechanism: RTM excitatory cells drive Wang–Buzsáki interneurons, whose feedback inhibition silences the E-cells until it decays and firing resumes, producing a population rhythm of about 30–80 Hz (≈45 Hz emerges within ≈50 ms of asynchronous onset). He shows the gamma period is a small multiple of the ≈10 ms GABA_A decay but depends far more sensitively on external drive than on inhibitory conductance or decay time (Table 30.1: a 1% drive reduction shifts the period 0.66%, versus 0.10% for g_IE and 0.14% for τ_d,I). He also establishes that PING needs neither E→E nor I→I connectivity — recurrent excitation actually destroys the rhythm (Fig. 30.12) — and that a "suppression boundary" separates rhythmic from non-rhythmic states, requiring drive to E-cells to be strong enough and drive to I-cells weak enough that interneurons fire only when prompted by an E-cell volley.

*Relevance.* This is the canonical reference for the exact circuit motif our manuscript builds on: a fixed E↔I loop with no E→E or I→I connections, so any rhythm is unambiguously PING — a point Börgers makes explicitly by showing recurrent excitation is unnecessary and even harmful. It grounds our finding (2) that gamma arises from the E↔I loop and finding (5) that the E-rate is set by the per-synapse I→E weight rather than pool size, since Börgers scales conductances by expected input count and shows synaptic strengths, not neuron numbers, govern the rhythm. His "at most one I-cell response per E-cell volley" picture and the suppression-boundary/drive-dependence results underpin our claims that the gamma cycle imposes a ≈1-spike-per-cycle ceiling on excitatory firing (finding 5) and that inhibitory timing, not mean level, gates the rate (finding 6).

*Used for.* The load-bearing PING citation and source of the fixed loop-weight values: §1.1 (PING arises from an E↔I loop), §2.4 (the loop weights are held at the canonical biophysical values of [7]), §3.1 (the inductive bias is paid at design time via these weights), and Methods §5.1–§5.2 (parameters and the no-E→E / no-I→I architecture).

#### 8. Cardin, Carlén, Meletis, Knoblich, Zhang, Deisseroth, Tsai & Moore (2009) — [*Driving Fast-Spiking Cells Induces Gamma Rhythm and Controls Sensory Responses*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature08002)

*Summary.* Using optogenetics in mouse barrel cortex in vivo, Cardin et al. drove channelrhodopsin-expressing fast-spiking PV+ interneurons at a range of frequencies (8–200 Hz) and found that this selectively amplified LFP power in the gamma band (~20–80 Hz), whereas driving regular-spiking pyramidal cells amplified only lower frequencies (8–24 Hz) — a double dissociation. They further showed that the phase of a whisker (sensory) input relative to the light-driven 40 Hz gamma cycle controlled the amplitude, timing, and precision of the evoked response, because the rhythmic FS-cell IPSP suppresses transmission at its peak and permits it after decay, sharpening sensory responses.

*Relevance.* This is the canonical causal demonstration that fast-spiking inhibitory interneurons generate gamma and that the gate lives in the timing of inhibition, not its mean level — grounding our core claims that a fixed E↔I (PING) loop produces gamma (~25–44 Hz) and that the firing-rate gate resides in the phase structure of inhibition (our jitter and drop/add-asymmetry findings). Our manuscript cites it as biological evidence for PING-generated gamma and phase-based control of excitatory transmission, motivating why we hold the E↔I loop fixed and why gamma acts as a structural/architectural constraint on excitatory firing rather than a learned one.

*Used for.* §1.1, for the optogenetic demonstration that driving fast-spiking interneurons generates gamma in vivo; and §4.3, where perturbing inhibitory timing optogenetically is proposed as a follow-up experiment.

#### 9. Sohal, Zhang, Yizhar & Deisseroth (2009) — [*Parvalbumin Neurons and Gamma Rhythms Enhance Cortical Circuit Performance*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature07991)

*Summary.* Sohal et al. used optogenetics in mouse neocortex to establish the causal role of fast-spiking parvalbumin (PV) interneurons in gamma (30-80 Hz) rhythms: halorhodopsin-mediated inhibition of PV cells selectively reduced gamma power (~20%, P&lt;0.001) while sparing other bands, whereas channelrhodopsin-2 driving of PV cells was sufficient to generate emergent gamma even from non-rhythmic principal-cell input. Dynamic-clamp experiments injecting simulated currents at 40 Hz versus 8 Hz or non-rhythmic patterns showed gamma-timescale modulation increased pyramidal-cell gain and boosted information transmission (from 0.43 to 0.62 bits per gamma cycle, P&lt;0.001), improving signal-to-noise by reducing noise entropy without raising response entropy.

*Relevance.* This is the canonical experimental grounding for two of our load-bearing claims: that gamma arises from a pyramidal-interneuron (PING-style) E-I feedback loop, and that the loop is generated/gated by the inhibitory cell population rather than E-E connectivity — mirroring our fixed E↔I architecture with no E-E or I-I coupling. It also supplies the functional rationale for our metabolic-gate framing (gamma as a structural constraint that improves circuit performance), letting us cite an in-vivo demonstration that gamma reorganizes excitatory firing and enhances signal transmission, which we contrast with our finding that in a trained SNN the same loop clamps excitatory rate roughly an order of magnitude while matching accuracy.

*Used for.* §1.1 (optogenetic control of gamma by fast-spiking interneurons) and §4.3 (as an in-vivo timing-perturbation precedent for future work).

#### 10. Phensy et al. (2026) — [*Prefrontal Gamma Oscillations Engage Dynamic Cell Type-Specific Configurations to Support Flexible Behavior*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2026.05.002)

*Summary.* Using dual-color voltage imaging and optogenetics in mouse prefrontal cortex, Phensy et al. show that parvalbumin (PV) interneurons dynamically entrain mediodorsal-thalamus-projecting pyramidal neurons at ~40 Hz gamma, both locally and across hemispheres. Rather than a single global rhythm, gamma synchrony forms multiple distinct, circuit-specific configurations that are recruited in a behavior-specific manner, and disrupting this cell-type-specific synchronization impairs cognitive flexibility. The work casts prefrontal gamma as a reconfigurable, PV-generated coordination mechanism linking specific projection neuron ensembles to specific behavioral demands.

*Relevance.* This is an in-vivo, cell-type-resolved demonstration of exactly the pyramidal–PV interneuron gamma loop our PING network abstracts: it grounds our claim that a fixed E↔I loop generating ~25–44 Hz gamma is the biologically canonical substrate, and that the interneuron pool acts on excitatory pyramidal cells through timed inhibition. It supports our framing of gamma as a structural gate/router for excitatory activity (rather than a byproduct), paralleling our finding that the gate lives in the timing/phase of inhibition and that PING coordinates which excitatory cells fire when. It is background/biological-plausibility support rather than a methodological or quantitative point of comparison for our firing-rate-economy results.

*Used for.* §1.1 (optogenetic gamma generation) and §4.3 (in-vivo inhibitory-timing perturbation as a proposed experiment).

#### 11. Offermanns, Pöpplau & Hanganu-Opatz (2026) — [*Developmental Embedding of Parvalbumin Interneurons Drives Local and Crosshemispheric Prefrontal Gamma Synchrony*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pneurobio.2025.102866)

*Summary.* Using bilateral extracellular recordings plus cell-type-selective optogenetic manipulation of parvalbumin-positive (PV+) and somatostatin-positive (SOM+) interneurons in the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) of non-anesthetized postnatal-day-16-to-60 mice, the authors show that the inhibitory efficacy and functional network integration of PV+ (but not SOM+) interneurons increases with age. This age-dependent embedding of PV+ cells causally controls the protracted emergence of local prefrontal gamma power and of crosshemispheric gamma synchrony, which strengthens over development in step with rising local gamma. The paper thus assigns the developmental generation of adult-like prefrontal gamma specifically to PV+ interneuron maturation rather than to SOM+ circuits.

*Relevance.* Our PING mechanism makes the interneuron population the gamma pacemaker of the E↔I loop, and this paper provides the in vivo, causal, cell-type-resolved evidence that the relevant interneurons are PV+ fast-spiking cells — grounding the biological identity of the I-population in our loop and the claim that a dedicated E↔I inhibitory circuit generates gamma. The SOM+-negative dissociation supports our design choice of a single PV-like E↔I loop as the sufficient gamma generator, and the finding that PV+ integration, not merely its presence, sets gamma strength parallels our result that the rhythm (and its firing-rate clamp) is architectural — a property of how inhibition is wired into the loop rather than of any single learned weight. It is background/support for our motivation, not a competing model.

*Used for.* §1.1, in the cluster establishing that activating fast-spiking interneurons drives gamma in intact cortex.

#### 12. Whittington, Traub & Jefferys (1995) — [*Synchronized Oscillations in Interneuron Networks Driven by Metabotropic Glutamate Receptor Activation*](https://doi.org/10.1038/373612a0)

*Summary.* In rat hippocampal and neocortical slices, activating metabotropic glutamate receptors evokes an emergent ~40 Hz gamma oscillation carried by networks of GABA-A-coupled inhibitory interneurons (an interneuron-network gamma, ING, mechanism), and these synchronized IPSP oscillations can entrain pyramidal-cell discharge. The oscillation frequency is set jointly by the net tonic excitatory drive to the interneurons and by the kinetics (decay time constant) of the GABA-A IPSPs between them, establishing inhibition as the timing element of cortical gamma.

*Relevance.* This is a foundational citation for our central premise that a recurrent E-I loop with GABA-A inhibition generates gamma and that the rhythm's frequency is governed by inhibitory synaptic weight and kinetics rather than excitatory drive alone; our finding (5) that the post-training E-rate is affine in f_gamma and set by the per-synapse W^IE echoes their "frequency set by IPSP kinetics and interneuron excitation" result. It also grounds finding (6) that the gate lives in the timing/phase structure of inhibition, and usefully contrasts with our architecture: they demonstrate pure ING (interneuron-only synchronization), whereas we deliberately isolate PING with no I-to-I connectivity, so citing it sharpens the claim that our rhythm is unambiguously E-I-loop-driven.

*Used for.* §1.1, cited for the earlier in-vitro recordings of interneuron-network gamma.

#### 13. Wang & Buzsáki (1996) — [*Gamma Oscillation by Synaptic Inhibition in a Hippocampal Interneuronal Network Model*](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.16-20-06402.1996)

*Summary.* Wang & Buzsáki show, via computer simulation of a sparsely and randomly connected network of GABAergic fast-spiking interneurons, that mutual synaptic inhibition alone (an I-I loop, no excitation) can generate coherent gamma-band oscillations (20-80 Hz, peaking near 30-50 Hz) even though individual cells span 0-400 Hz. The rhythm is set by the GABA-A decay time constant (≈10 ms, giving an optimal τ_syn/T ratio near 0.2), and network-wide synchrony requires a critical minimum of roughly 60 synaptic contacts per cell that does not scale with network size, while it is readily degraded by heterogeneity in the drive.

*Relevance.* This is the canonical demonstration that an inhibition-based loop imposes a gamma clock whose frequency is dictated by inhibitory synaptic kinetics rather than intrinsic single-cell properties, grounding our claim that a fixed E-I (PING) loop produces a rhythm in the ~25-44 Hz band. It provides the ING counterpoint to our PING mechanism and supports our finding that the gate lives in the timing/phase structure of inhibition and that the excitatory rate is affine in f_γ (each cell firing at most once per gamma cycle), since Wang-Buzsáki likewise tie the period to the GABA-A decay constant. It also anchors the biophysical values we clamp for the recurrent inhibitory weights and the conductance-based COBA LIF formalism used throughout our model and mean-field reduction.

*Used for.* §1.1, cited for the biophysical interneuron-network model of gamma.

#### 14. Bartos, Vida & Jonas (2007) — [*Synaptic Mechanisms of Synchronized Gamma Oscillations in Inhibitory Interneuron Networks*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn2044)

*Summary.* Bartos, Vida & Jonas review the synaptic basis of gamma-frequency (roughly 30-90 Hz) network oscillations, arguing that fast-spiking, parvalbumin-expressing, soma-targeting interneurons are the key generators. Combining experimental data (hippocampus/neocortex) with computational modeling, they show that mutual fast GABA-A inhibition among these interneurons — the "interneuron network gamma" (ING) mechanism — is sufficient to synchronize the pool, and that specialized synaptic features (fast, large, proximally located IPSCs, short latencies, plus electrical coupling via gap junctions) turn the interneuron network into a robust and frequency-tunable gamma oscillator. The rhythm's frequency is set by the kinetics and strength of recurrent inhibition rather than by cell-intrinsic properties.

*Relevance.* This is a canonical reference grounding the biophysical premise of our PING loop: that gamma is generated by fast GABA-A inhibition from PV-type interneurons and that its frequency and synchrony are controlled by inhibitory synaptic kinetics/weights. It supports our mechanistic claims that the gate resides in the timing/phase structure of inhibition rather than its mean level (finding 6) and that the post-training excitatory rate is set by the per-synapse inhibitory weight W^IE, not the interneuron pool size (finding 5). It also situates our fixed, biophysically-valued E&lt;->I loop within the established inhibition-based-oscillation literature, and its ING framing provides the natural contrast to our explicitly PING (E&lt;->I, no I->I) architecture in which inhibition alone does not close the loop. *(partially verified — spot-check.)*

*Used for.* §1.1, for the synaptic mechanisms that synchronize the interneuron pool.

#### 15. Kopell, Börgers, Pervouchine, Malerba & Tort (2010) — [*Gamma and Theta Rhythms in Biophysical Models of Hippocampal Circuits*](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4419-0996-1_15)

*Summary.* A canonical review chapter (in Hippocampal Microcircuits, Springer Series in Computational Neuroscience) surveying how biophysical single-compartment models of CA1/CA3 circuits — pyramidal cells plus fast-spiking basket and O-LM interneurons with Na/K, AMPA and GABA-A kinetics — generate gamma (30-90 Hz) and theta (4-12 Hz) rhythms and their interaction. It lays out the two gamma mechanisms, PING (pyramidal-interneuron network gamma, where E-cell volleys recruit I-cells that then impose a rhythmic inhibitory window pacing the next E-volley) and ING, and shows that PING requires the drive to I-cells to be weak relative to E-cells; a sharp boundary in parameter space separates the PING regime from a "suppression" regime where over-driven interneurons fire autonomously and silence the pyramidal cells, so small excitability changes can toggle the network between rhythmic activity and suppression.

*Relevance.* This is a foundational touchstone for our core mechanistic claim: that a fixed E↔I (PING) loop generates gamma and clamps excitatory firing via the timing of inhibition. It grounds our definition of PING as an E→I→E loop (no E→E or I→I), our observation that the gate lives in the phase/timing structure of inhibition rather than its mean level (their inhibitory-window pacing), and our finding that each E-cell fires ≈once per gamma cycle. Their sharp PING-to-suppression boundary in the drive/coupling plane is the biological analogue of the supercritical Hopf boundary we map across the W^EI × W^IE plane and of the ~10× excitatory-rate reduction we attribute to architecture rather than learning.

*Used for.* §1.2, among the biophysical PING studies; and §3.7, where the implications for microcircuits with richer connectivity are flagged as open.

#### 16. Viriyopase, Memmesheimer & Gielen (2016) — [*Cooperation and Competition of Gamma Oscillation Mechanisms*](https://doi.org/10.1152/jn.00493.2015)

*Summary.* A computational study (Journal of Neurophysiology, 2016) of networks with excitatory pyramidal cells and inhibitory interneurons coupled by chemical and electrical (gap-junction) synapses, asking how the two gamma-generating mechanisms — interneuron gamma (ING, from mutual inhibition) and pyramidal-interneuron gamma (PING, from the E to I to E loop) — interact. The central result is competitive: when a network can support both, the mechanism producing the HIGHER frequency "wins," setting the network rhythm (30–80 Hz gamma) and suppressing the slower mechanism; gap junctions and inhibition can either aid or hinder oscillations depending on interneuron type (I vs II) and coupling strength. It also notes that pyramidal firing rates stay well below the oscillation frequency during PING, a previously unexplained feature.

*Relevance.* Grounds our manuscript's core PING vocabulary and the claim that a pyramidal-interneuron E to I loop is a canonical, well-characterized gamma generator distinct from interneuron-only (ING) mechanisms — supporting our design choice to isolate PING by removing E to E and I to I connectivity. Most directly, its observation that pyramidal cells fire far below the gamma frequency prefigures our finding (5) that trained excitatory cells emit at most ~1 spike per gamma cycle, making the E-rate affine in f_gamma; we cite it as prior mechanistic grounding for the low-rate, cycle-locked firing that underlies our metabolic-gate interpretation.

*Used for.* §1.2, among the biophysical-model PING literature that our fixed loop abstracts.

#### 17. Brunel & Wang (2003) — [*What Determines the Frequency of Fast Network Oscillations with Irregular Neural Discharges? I. Synaptic Dynamics and Excitation-Inhibition Balance*](https://doi.org/10.1152/jn.01095.2002)

*Summary.* Brunel & Wang derive, via mean-field analysis of sparsely connected leaky integrate-and-fire networks with realistic synaptic kinetics, what sets the frequency of fast network oscillations when individual neurons fire irregularly and sparsely. They identify two regimes: a fast interneuron-driven (ING-type) rhythm governed by inhibitory kinetics that can reach ~200 Hz ripple frequencies, and a slower excitatory-inhibitory feedback loop (PING-type) rhythm in the gamma range (~40 Hz). Frequency depends much more strongly on the shortest synaptic time constants (delay and rise time) than on the slower decay; faster excitation than inhibition, or a higher E/I ratio, favors the feedback loop and a slower, gamma-range oscillation.

*Relevance.* This is a foundational theory reference for our core claim that a fixed E↔I loop with no E→E or I→I connectivity produces gamma unambiguously via feedback (PING), and it grounds our mean-field / Hopf-bifurcation treatment of gamma onset across the W^EI × W^IE plane — Brunel & Wang provide the canonical mean-field prediction of oscillation frequency for exactly this class of sparse LIF E-I networks. It also directly underpins finding (5), that the excitatory rate is affine in gamma frequency: their result that the frequency is set by feedback dynamics and short synaptic timescales licenses our reading of f_γ as a controllable, mechanistically-determined quantity rather than an incidental one. Their gamma-vs-ripple regime distinction (E/I ratio and relative E/I kinetics selecting feedback-loop gamma) frames why our biophysically-clamped loop sits in the gamma band.

*Used for.* §1.2, in the same list of biophysical PING studies.

#### 18. Wilson & Cowan (1972) — [*Excitatory and Inhibitory Interactions in Localized Populations of Model Neurons*](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0006-3495(72)86068-5)

*Summary.* Wilson & Cowan (1972, Biophys. J. 12:1-24) derive coupled nonlinear differential equations for the mean activity of spatially localized populations of interacting excitatory and inhibitory model neurons, and analyze them with phase-plane methods and numerical integration. They show that the E-I population supports limit-cycle oscillations whose frequency is a monotonic increasing function of stimulus intensity, alongside simple and multiple hysteresis and bistability; they further prove that the existence of limit-cycle dynamics under one class of stimuli implies multiple stable states and hysteresis under another. This is the founding two-population neural-mass (firing-rate) model of E-I interaction.

*Relevance.* This is a canonical empirical anchor for our PING loop: it establishes that an E→I→E feedback motif with delayed, dominant inhibition is what sets the gamma period, and that gamma frequency is governed by the balance between excitation and the counterbalancing inhibition — precisely the W^EI × W^IE coupling our fixed loop implements. It directly grounds our finding (5) that the post-training excitatory rate is affine in f_γ with ≈1 spike per gamma cycle, since Atallah & Scanziani show excitatory drive is metered out one E-I wave per cycle and its amount tunes the interval to the next cycle. It also supports our framing of gamma as a phase/timing gate on excitation (finding 6): they show inhibition tracks excitation on a per-cycle basis, so the rhythm's period — not the mean inhibitory level — is the controlled variable.

*Used for.* §5.5, among the spectral-peak and population-coherence measures used to characterize the rhythm.

#### 56. Xing, Shen, Burns, Yeh, Shapley & Li (2012) — [*Stochastic Generation of Gamma-Band Activity in Primary Visual Cortex of Awake and Anesthetized Monkeys*](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.5644-11.2012)

*Summary.* Xing et al. recorded V1 LFPs in awake and anesthetized monkeys and used Gabor-transform spectrograms to show that visually-evoked gamma-band activity (peak ≈60 Hz awake, ≈40 Hz anesthetized) comes in short, irregular bursts — corrected mean duration ≈62–65 ms, fewer than 4 cycles per burst, with peak-frequency SD ≈9 Hz within trials — whose statistics are indistinguishable from phase-randomized surrogates. They argue gamma is not a periodic clock but stochastic, noise-driven output, and reproduce the peaked spectrum with a two-node excitatory–inhibitory (Wilson–Cowan-style) recurrent network driven by Poisson/random input, where gamma emerges as network filtering of broadband activity rather than a sustained limit-cycle oscillation.

*Relevance.* This grounds two of our claims. First, its E–I recurrent network driven by noise, producing a peaked-but-broad gamma spectrum, is the canonical picture behind our supercritical-Hopf finding (2): near the Hopf boundary the deterministic system has a damped resonance that noise excites into intermittent bursts rather than a rigid rhythm, matching our Welch PSD peaks and the ≈25–44 Hz band we report. Second, it directly supports our timing-over-clock reading (6–7): gamma being "too random to serve as a clock" yet still functionally organizing E-firing motivates why our gate lives in the phase/timing structure of inhibition (≈1 spike per cycle, integration over ≈one gamma cycle) rather than requiring a metronomic oscillator. It is a physiological anchor for the PING mechanism, not a firing-rate-economy result per se.

*Used for.* §5.5, alongside [55], for the coherence and spectral measures our gamma quantification follows.

#### 57. Rotter & Diesmann (1999) — [*Exact Digital Simulation of Time-Invariant Linear Systems with Applications to Neuronal Modeling*](https://doi.org/10.1007/s004220050570)

*Summary.* Rotter and Diesmann present a method for the exact digital simulation of time-invariant linear systems (Biol Cybern 81:381-402, 1999). Writing the subthreshold dynamics as dy/dt = Ay, they use the matrix exponential exp(A·h) to build a propagator that advances the state exactly across a fixed time step h, and they incorporate a broad class of inputs — including trains of delta-pulses (spikes) — into this exact scheme. Applied to networks of integrate-and-fire neurons with linear subthreshold integration and nonlinear spike generation, "Exact Integration" yields the smallest error in both subthreshold trajectory and spike timing versus general-purpose ODE solvers, especially at moderate step sizes.

*Relevance.* This is the methodological anchor for our exponential-Euler / exact-exponential integration of the COBA-LIF dynamics: for the linear subthreshold and conductance decay terms, the propagator exp(A·h) is exact regardless of step size, which is exactly the property our timestep-invariance result (finding 6) leans on when we argue the gamma gate is a genuine dynamical feature rather than a discretization artifact. It grounds our claim that spike-driven synaptic conductances and delta-pulse inputs are handled exactly on the integration grid, so that measured f_gamma (Welch PSD) and E-firing rates are not sensitive to dt. We cite it as the standard justification for the exponential integrator underlying the network simulation.

*Used for.* §5.6, as the exact-exponential (exponential-Euler) integration scheme for the COBA-LIF dynamics, underpinning the timestep-invariance result.

#### 58. LeCun, Bottou, Bengio & Haffner (1998) — [*Gradient-Based Learning Applied to Document Recognition*](https://doi.org/10.1109/5.726791)

*Summary.* This foundational paper introduces LeNet-5, a gradient-based convolutional neural network trained end-to-end by backpropagation for handwritten-digit recognition, and establishes the MNIST dataset as its benchmark; LeNet-5 reaches roughly 0.95% test error on MNIST (dropping to about 0.8% with input distortions), beating the fully-connected nets and template/feature-based classifiers of the era. *Relevance.* Our manuscript trains its PING and COBA spiking networks on MNIST, so this is the canonical origin citation for both the dataset and the gradient-based (surrogate-gradient BPTT) learning paradigm we build on. It grounds the choice of MNIST as the classification task on which we compare accuracy at matched excitatory-spike budgets, and anchors the convolutional/end-to-end-trainable lineage that our surrogate-gradient training of a biophysically-constrained SNN extends into the spiking, Dale's-law-clamped regime.

*Used for.* §5.7, as the origin citation for MNIST, the classification task on which we compare PING and COBA at matched spike budgets.
