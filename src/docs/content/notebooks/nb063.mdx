## Abstract

A pedagogical entry, not a result. The Poisson process is the "completely random at a fixed rate" model, and it sits underneath everything this collection does: the network's inputs are Poisson spike trains, and the firing statistics we measure (CV, Fano factor) are defined *relative* to it. This note builds the distribution from the ground up — coin flips → binomial → Poisson — shows that this limit is exactly how the simulator encodes inputs, and explains why a Poisson process is the yardstick against which structured spiking is interesting.

## From coin flips to Poisson

Start with the simplest random event: a coin that lands heads with probability $p$. Flip it once per timestep for $n$ steps, and the number of heads follows the **binomial** distribution. Now do two things at once — **bias the coin harder** (shrink $p$ toward zero, so each flip rarely scores) and **flip it more often** (grow $n$) — while holding the expected number of heads $\lambda = np$ fixed. Each flip becomes vanishingly unlikely, but you take so many that the average yield doesn't move. In that limit the binomial becomes the **Poisson** distribution:

$$P(k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$$

- $k$ — the number of events (heads, or spikes) observed
- $\lambda$ — the expected number of events over the whole window
- $P(k)$ — the probability of seeing exactly $k$

## One number sets everything

The Poisson distribution has a single parameter, $\lambda$, and it controls both the centre and the spread. Its defining fingerprint is that the **mean equals the variance**:

$$\mathbb{E}[k] = \operatorname{Var}[k] = \lambda$$

This is what makes "Poisson" a testable claim about real data rather than a vague synonym for "random": measure the mean and variance of a spike count, and if they match, the process is consistent with Poisson.

## Spike trains in continuous time

Take the timestep to zero and the count process becomes a **point process** in continuous time. Two facts make it the canonical spike-train model. The intervals between consecutive events — the **inter-spike intervals (ISIs)** — are exponentially distributed, and the process is **memoryless**: the time to the next spike doesn't depend on when the last one fired. A clean way to summarise irregularity is the coefficient of variation of the ISIs:

$$\mathrm{CV} = \frac{\sigma_{\text{ISI}}}{\mu_{\text{ISI}}}$$

- $\mu_{\text{ISI}}$, $\sigma_{\text{ISI}}$ — the mean and standard deviation of the inter-spike intervals

For a Poisson process $\mathrm{CV} = 1$ exactly. Regular, clock-like firing gives $\mathrm{CV} \to 0$; bursty firing gives $\mathrm{CV} > 1$.

## This is the simulator's encoder

None of this is incidental to the project: it *is* the input layer. The canonical encoder (*encode_images_poisson*) turns a pixel into spikes with one line — a per-timestep Bernoulli draw, $\text{spike if } u < \text{intensity}\cdot r\,\Delta t/1000$, with $u$ uniform on $[0,1]$. That is precisely the coin-flip construction above, so the spike count over a trial is Poisson with

$$\lambda = \text{intensity} \times r \times \frac{T}{1000}$$

- $r$ — the maximum input rate (Hz) at full pixel intensity
- $T$ — the trial length (ms); $\Delta t$ — the integration timestep (ms)

The bias is extreme: at full intensity with $r = 50$ Hz and $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms, the per-step coin lands "spike" just $p = 0.005$ of the time, flipped $T/\Delta t = 2000$ times a trial for $\lambda = 10$ — a heavily biased coin, flipped a lot, exactly the regime above.

Two consequences fall straight out, and the encoder confirms both. Pixel intensity scales $\lambda$ linearly, and the result is **invariant to $\Delta t$** — halving the step doubles the number of draws but halves each draw's probability, leaving $\lambda$ unchanged. Running the real encoder, the spike-count Fano factor (variance/mean) comes out at **≈ 1** across intensities, as a Poisson count must.

## Why Poisson is the yardstick

Cortical spiking is famously "Poisson-like" — irregular, with $\mathrm{CV} \approx 1$ and Fano $\approx 1$ — so a Poisson process is the natural null model for a spiking network. That makes the *interesting* results the **departures** from it. When the PING loop imposes a gamma rhythm, spikes cluster into cycles and the within-cycle statistics go **sub-Poisson** (regular, $\mathrm{CV} < 1$) — which is exactly the structure [nb024](/notebooks/nb024/) reads off the firing rate's CV and [nb046](/notebooks/nb046/) reads off the spikes-per-cycle count. Poisson is the baseline; gamma-gated sparsity is the deviation worth measuring.
