## Abstract

Granting a clean PING gamma rhythm, can the self-generated tick be *used* as a timing reference? Reusing a competent, already-trained PING (the [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/) tg6 cell — GABA decay 6 ms, ≈90% on MNIST, gamma on the task), two in-network coincidence-detector neurons read the gamma volley — one off the inhibitory pool, one off the excitatory pool — each emitting one spike per cycle: the clock edge that *samples* the output readout. We compare these against the always-on readout, a fixed external clock, and random sampling. Both self-clocks match the always-on readout — 88% (I tap) and 87% (E tap) against 89% — while reading only ≈8 samples per trial instead of 2000, a ≈250× reduction, and both beat random sampling (75%) and a best-phase fixed external clock (77%). A fixed crystal falls short because the rhythm's rate varies per input, from 24 to 54 Hz, so no single period lands on the volleys. Two conclusions: the gamma tick is *good enough to replace* an always-on readout, and a fixed external clock is *not good enough to replace* the gamma tick.

## Background

Neuromorphic hardware sells itself on energy: spikes are sparse, events are rare, so a chip that only works when a spike arrives should sip power where a GPU gulps. But event-driven logic still needs a shared sense of *when* — synaptic delays, refractory periods, the discrete timestep an integrator advances over — and in a synchronous chip that sense of time is distributed by a clock tree that toggles every cycle, everywhere, whether or not the logic underneath is computing. Its dynamic power is routinely one of the largest single draws on the die. The clock is a constant energy tax whose only product is synchrony.

The PING loop generates a gamma rhythm for free, out of its own excitatory–inhibitory dynamics. The question here is whether that architecture-generated rhythm is good enough to *be* the timing reference — to replace the piped-in tick — rather than something the network merely happens to do. The most basic thing a clock does is gate a downstream operation on its edge; here the downstream operation is the classifier's own readout, and the edge is the inhibitory volley. This entry tests usability in the clean regime where the rhythm is intact; whether the rhythm survives per-cell noise is a separate reliability question and not the subject here.

## Method

**Reused network.** We do not retrain. The rhythm is a property of a trained network, so we load the [nb041](/notebooks/nb041/) tg6 cell — a PING with GABA decay 6 ms that reaches ≈90% on MNIST and oscillates in gamma during the task — and run inference only. The cell's GABA time constant is wired back into the synaptic decay on load so the rhythm matches the one it was trained with.

**Readout schemes.** The trained readout is a leaky membrane average (mem-mean): the class logits are the time-average of the output membrane over the whole trial. What the simulator stores each step is that running average, $o(t)$ — the mean so far of the per-step output contributions $v_\text{out}(s)$. To sample the readout at chosen instants we need the per-step contribution itself, and a running mean changes from one step to the next exactly in proportion to the new term, so we recover it by differencing consecutive stored values:

$$
\underbrace{o(t) = \tfrac{1}{T}\textstyle\sum_{s\le t} v_\text{out}(s)}_{\text{recorded each step}}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\underbrace{v_\text{out}(t) = T \cdot \big(o(t) - o(t-1)\big)}_{\text{recovered by differencing}}
\tag{1}
$$

- $o(t)$ — the running mem-mean the simulator records up to step $t$ (the cumulative average of the output membrane);
- $v_\text{out}(t)$ — the single-step output contribution at step $t$, per class — the quantity we sample;
- $T$ — integration steps per trial (2000 at $\Delta t = 0.1$ ms over 200 ms).

Class logits are then the average of $v_\text{out}$ over a chosen set of sample times $\mathcal{S}$:

$$
z_\mathcal{S} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}|}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{S}} v_\text{out}(t)
\tag{2}
$$

Four choices of $\mathcal{S}$, all on the same spikes and weights:

- **continuous** — every timestep ($\mathcal{S} = \{1,\dots,T\}$): the trained readout, the always-on baseline;
- **self-clock** — the gamma volley times: the self-grown tick;
- **fixed-clock** — a fixed period equal to the median gamma period, an external crystal. We sweep its phase across the cycle and report its *best* phase, to steelman it;
- **shuffled** — random times, matched in count to the self-clock: the floor control.

**The tick is emitted in-network.** The clock is not detected by an offline signal-processing pass — it is produced by a coincidence-detector neuron wired into the network. A fast leaky integrate-and-fire unit reads a population's firing fraction,

$$
u(t) = e^{-\Delta t/\tau_C}\,u(t-1) + \frac{1}{N}\sum_i s_i(t),
\qquad \text{fire when } u(t)\ge\theta
\tag{3}
$$

- $u(t)$ — the detector's membrane, a leaky integral of the fraction of the pool firing;
- $\tau_C = 1.5$ ms — a short window, so it integrates *coincidences* (a synchronous volley) rather than rate;
- $\theta$ — threshold on that fraction (0.15 for the inhibitory pool, 0.05 for the larger, less-synchronous excitatory pool);
- $N$ — pool size,

then resets and holds a refractory lockout of 12 ms (under one gamma cycle) so each volley makes exactly one tick. The unit is read-only — it taps existing spikes with no feedback into the loop — so it needs no training, and it is causal: it fires on the rising synchrony, using only past and present spikes. It reproduces an offline peak-finder's volley times to within the sampling step (median gap 0.0 ms), confirming the tick is genuinely online-realisable.

We instantiate two such detectors and compare them: an **I tap** reading the inhibitory pool and an **E tap** reading the excitatory pool. The difference is a sign convention. A spike is a sign-free event, so the I tap simply weights inhibitory spikes positively — legitimate on event-routed neuromorphic hardware (the receiver assigns the sign), but a Dale's-law violation in a biological substrate, where an inhibitory cell cannot excite the detector. The E tap is Dale's-law-clean: the excitatory volley genuinely excites the detector. Per-trial gamma frequency is the inverse of the median inter-tick interval (I tap).

**Logic.** This is a *non-inferiority* test, not a superiority one. The self-clock is good enough to replace the always-on readout if it falls within a small margin (3 points) of continuous while using far fewer samples; it must beat shuffled or the timing carries no information; and the best-phase fixed clock is the external standard we ask whether a crystal could match.

## Results

The rhythm is real gamma at this operating point — sharp inhibitory volleys and a clean spectral peak at $f_\gamma \approx 39$ Hz, ≈8 cycles in the 200 ms trial.

The self-clock works because the readout itself is volley-locked: the output membrane bursts once per gamma cycle, and sampling on the volley catches each burst.

Sampling only on the tick recovers essentially all of the accuracy at a fraction of the cost, and beats both controls.

<Figure id="Figure 3" title="Self-clock matches the always-on readout" src="/figures/notebooks/nb061/accuracy_bars.png" alt="Bar chart: continuous 89, self-clock 88, fixed-clock 77, shuffled 70 percent, with samples per trial.">
Test accuracy across the four schemes, with samples per trial annotated; the shaded band is the non-inferiority margin below continuous. Self-clock sits inside it.

| scheme | accuracy | samples / trial |
|---|---|---|
| continuous | 89.0% | 2000 |
| self-clock (I tap) | 88.3% | ≈8 |
| self-clock (E tap) | 86.7% | ≈8 |
| fixed-clock (best phase) | 77.0% | 8 |
| shuffled | 75.0% | ≈8 |

Both taps land inside the non-inferiority band; the I tap edges the E tap (88.3 vs 86.7%). The gap is pure timing: the E volley *leads* the I volley by 1.2 ms — the E→I conduction delay, since the excitatory burst drives the inhibitory one — so the E-tap tick fires on the output's rising edge rather than its peak, sampling a touch early. The inhibitory tick, firing with the volley, lands on the peak. A small fixed latch latency on the E tap would close the gap; left uncorrected, the I tap is the better-aligned clock.

The reason the fixed clock falls short is that the rhythm's rate is not fixed: across the test set the per-trial gamma frequency spans 24 to 54 Hz (median 39, interquartile 35–43). A single external period cannot land on the volleys of every trial.

Per-trial gamma frequency across the evaluation set, with the single rate a fixed external clock must commit to (dashed). The rhythm self-organises per input, so the crystal is off-beat on most trials — only a clock derived from the network's own activity tracks it.

## Discussion

**Good enough to replace the always-on readout.** Self-clock 88% against continuous 89% is within the non-inferiority margin, at ≈250× fewer samples. The readout no longer needs to integrate every timestep; it can latch once per gamma cycle and lose almost nothing. That is the event-driven energy argument made concrete — work is done on the tick, not continuously.

**A fixed external clock is not good enough to replace the gamma tick.** Even steelmanned at its best phase, the crystal reaches only 77%, eleven points short, and Figure 4 says why: the rhythm's frequency rides the input, so a fixed period samples off the volleys. The stronger reading is not merely that the self-clock is as cheap as a crystal, but that a crystal cannot do this job — the informative moments are tied to a rhythm only the network itself defines.

**I tap vs E tap.** The hardware-legitimate I tap (88.3%) slightly beats the Dale's-law-clean E tap (86.7%), purely on alignment — the E volley leads by 1.2 ms, so its tick samples early. Neither choice changes the headline: both replace the always-on readout and both beat the crystal. Which to prefer is a substrate question. Event-routed neuromorphic hardware can use either, and the I tap is marginally better-aligned. A biological substrate must use the E tap (or build a post-inhibitory rebound detector on I) and absorb the small latency cost. That the clock can be read off *either* population is itself reassuring: the tick is a property of the loop, not of one cell type.

**Honest limits.** Shuffled is 75%, not chance: the mem-mean readout integrates, so even random samples recover a good deal, and the clock's marginal value is the 13-point gap over shuffled and the 11-point margin over the crystal — a solid effect, not a knockout. The result is one cell (tg6, seed 42) at the clean operating point ($\sigma = 0$). It grants an intact rhythm; under per-cell membrane noise the rhythm is fragile, so the usability shown here is real but conditional on a regime that noise can spoil. Usable, but only while the rhythm holds.

## Success criteria

Evaluated on the I tap, the primary clock (the E tap is within a point on all three).

- Self-clock within 3 points of continuous — **met** (88 vs 89): good enough to replace the always-on readout.
- Self-clock above shuffled — **met** (88 vs 75): the timing carries information.
- Self-clock above the best-phase fixed clock — **met** (88 vs 77): a fixed crystal cannot stand in.
