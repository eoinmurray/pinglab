# Exploding gradients in conductance-based LIF networks

## The problem in one sentence

The voltage-dependent driving force $(E_e - V)$ in conductance-based
synapses amplifies gradients by $\approx 65\times$ per timestep, making
backpropagation through time (BPTT) numerically unstable for any window
longer than a few steps.

## The forward dynamics

Our conductance-based LIF neuron updates voltage as (Euler discretization
with $dt = 1$ ms, $C_m = 1$):

$$
V_{t+1} = V_t + \frac{dt}{C_m}\bigl[g_L(E_L - V_t) + g_e(E_e - V_t) + g_i(E_i - V_t) + I_\text{ext}\bigr]
$$

Expanding:

$$
V_{t+1} = V_t + dt\bigl[g_L E_L + g_e E_e + g_i E_i + I_\text{ext} - (g_L + g_e + g_i)V_t\bigr] / C_m
$$

The excitatory conductance $g_e$ evolves as:

$$
g_{e,t+1} = g_{e,t} \cdot e^{-dt/\tau_\text{ampa}} + \sum_j w_{ij}\, s_j(t - d)
$$

where $s_j$ is the spike indicator of presynaptic neuron $j$ and $d$ is
the synaptic delay.

Spikes are generated by a surrogate step function:

$$
s_t = H_\sigma(V_t - V_{th}), \quad H_\sigma'(u) = \frac{1}{(1 + |u|)^2}
$$

## The Jacobian

BPTT requires the Jacobian $\partial V_{t+1} / \partial V_t$ at each
timestep. Differentiating the voltage update:

$$
\frac{\partial V_{t+1}}{\partial V_t} = 1 - \frac{dt}{C_m}(g_L + g_e + g_i)
$$

With our parameters ($dt = 1$, $C_m = 1$, $g_L = 0.05$, typical
$g_e \approx 0.1$, $g_i \approx 0.05$):

$$
\frac{\partial V_{t+1}}{\partial V_t} \approx 1 - 0.2 = 0.8
$$

This term alone is fine — it's a contraction. The problem comes from the
**cross-derivative** $\partial V_{t+1} / \partial g_e$:

$$
\frac{\partial V_{t+1}}{\partial g_e} = \frac{dt}{C_m}(E_e - V_t) = \frac{1.0 \times (0 - (-65))}{1.0} = 65
$$

This is the driving force of excitatory synapses. It enters the gradient
chain whenever $g_e$ depends on upstream quantities (weights, spikes from
earlier layers) through the temporal unrolling.

## Why it explodes

Consider the gradient of the loss $\mathcal{L}$ with respect to a weight
$w$ at layer $\ell$ that affects $g_e$ at timestep $t_0$. The chain rule
through $T$ subsequent timesteps involves:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial V_T} \prod_{t=t_0}^{T-1} \frac{\partial V_{t+1}}{\partial V_t} \cdot \frac{\partial V_{t_0+1}}{\partial g_e} \cdot \frac{\partial g_e}{\partial w}
$$

The full temporal Jacobian is not simply $(\partial V_{t+1}/\partial V_t)^T$
because the spike mechanism couples $V$ back into $g_e$ at subsequent
timesteps. When a neuron spikes, it increments downstream $g_e$ by $w$,
and each such increment carries the $\partial V/\partial g_e = 65$
multiplier. The effective per-step amplification along paths that pass
through spikes is:

$$
\left|\frac{\partial V_{t+1}}{\partial g_e}\right| \cdot \left|\frac{\partial g_e}{\partial s_t}\right| \cdot |H_\sigma'| \approx 65 \cdot w \cdot H_\sigma'(u)
$$

For typical weights $w \sim 0.1$–$0.3$ and surrogate gradient values
$H_\sigma' \sim 0.1$–$1.0$, the per-step gain through a spiking path is
$\sim 0.65$–$19.5$. When multiple neurons spike per timestep (as in an
active network), these paths compound:

$$
\|\nabla_w \mathcal{L}\| \sim \mathcal{O}\!\left(\left(\frac{dt \cdot (E_e - V)}{C_m}\right)^k\right) = \mathcal{O}(65^k)
$$

where $k$ is the effective number of spike-mediated gradient hops.
Empirically:

| BPTT window | B=4 random data | B=64 MNIST |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5 steps | $\|\nabla\| \sim 10^3$ | Inf/NaN |
| 10 steps | $\|\nabla\| \sim 10^6$ | Inf/NaN |
| 50 steps | $\|\nabla\| \sim 10^9$ | Inf/NaN |
| Full (200 steps) | $\|\nabla\| \sim 10^{12}$ | Inf/NaN |

Even aggressive gradient clipping + `nan_to_num` cannot salvage learning:
the gradients are so large that after clipping, all directional
information is destroyed. Networks trained with sanitized gradients
converge to chance accuracy (~10% on MNIST).

## The detachment solution and its cost

The current implementation detaches spikes before writing them to the
delay buffer:

```
spiked_bool = spiked.detach().bool()
```

This severs the autograd graph at every spike emission, so gradient
cannot flow backward through time via the spike→conductance→voltage
chain. The result:

### What receives gradient

At readout time, the loss depends on E\_out voltages and spikes. The
gradient for the last weight matrix traces:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_{ee}^{(\text{hid→out})}} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial g_e^{(\text{out})}} \cdot s_\text{hid}^{(\text{detached})}
$$

This is a simple matrix multiply — no temporal unrolling, no explosive
driving force. Gradient flows to $W_{ee}$ but not through $s_\text{hid}$.

### What is structurally unreachable

For gradient to reach $W_{ee}^{(\text{in→hid})}$, it would need to
backpropagate through:

$$
\mathcal{L} \xrightarrow{\partial} V_\text{out} \xrightarrow{\partial} g_e^\text{(out)} \xrightarrow{\partial} \underbrace{s_\text{hid}}_{\text{detached}} \not\xrightarrow{\partial} V_\text{hid} \xrightarrow{\partial} g_e^\text{(hid)} \xrightarrow{\partial} s_\text{in} \cdot W_{ee}^{(\text{in→hid})}
$$

The detachment at $s_\text{hid}$ blocks the chain. Similarly, the PING
inhibitory loop (W\_ei, W\_ie) is doubly blocked — gradient would need
to cross two detached spike boundaries:

$$
\mathcal{L} \not\xrightarrow{\partial} s_\text{hid} \xrightarrow{W_{ei}} g_e^\text{(inh)} \xrightarrow{\partial} \underbrace{s_\text{inh}}_{\text{detached}} \not\xrightarrow{\partial} \ldots
$$

The cost: only 2,560 of 1,102,500 parameters (0.23%) receive gradient.
The network operates as a random feature extractor with a trained linear
readout.

## Why current-based LIF is not an option

Current-based LIF replaces the multiplicative synaptic current:

$$
I_\text{syn} = g_e(E_e - V) + g_i(E_i - V) \quad \text{(conductance-based)}
$$

with an additive current:

$$
I_\text{syn} = g_e \cdot w_e + g_i \cdot w_i \quad \text{(current-based)}
$$

The Jacobian $\partial V/\partial g_e = dt \cdot w_e / C_m$ is now a
small constant ($\sim 0.01$–$0.1$), not the voltage-dependent $65$.
BPTT would be stable.

However, the $g(E - V)$ term is precisely what creates **shunting
inhibition** — the mechanism by which inhibitory conductances divisively
reduce excitatory drive rather than simply subtracting from it. This
voltage-dependent interaction is essential for **PING (Pyramidal-Interneuron
Network Gamma) oscillations**: the excitatory-inhibitory feedback loop
that produces gamma-band rhythms. Removing it collapses the oscillatory
dynamics this project studies.

Mathematically, the shunting effect appears in the steady-state voltage:

$$
V_\infty = \frac{g_L E_L + g_e E_e + g_i E_i}{g_L + g_e + g_i}
$$

This is a conductance-weighted average of reversal potentials — $g_i$
*divides* the influence of $g_e$. In current-based models, inhibition is
purely subtractive and cannot produce this divisive normalization.

## Possible solutions

### 1. Auxiliary per-layer losses

Attach a local classifier to each hidden layer and compute a separate
loss:

$$
\mathcal{L}_\text{total} = \mathcal{L}_\text{readout} + \beta \sum_\ell \mathcal{L}_\ell^\text{aux}
$$

where $\mathcal{L}_\ell^\text{aux}$ is a cross-entropy loss on layer
$\ell$'s spike counts or mean voltage, projected through a small linear
head $W_\ell^\text{aux} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_\ell \times C}$:

$$
\hat{y}_\ell = W_\ell^\text{aux} \cdot \bar{s}_\ell, \quad \bar{s}_\ell = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t s_\ell(t)
$$

Gradient for $W_{ee}^{(\text{in→hid})}$ flows through the auxiliary head
without crossing any spike buffer:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_\text{hid}^\text{aux}}{\partial W_{ee}^{(\text{in→hid})}} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_\text{hid}^\text{aux}}{\partial \bar{s}_\text{hid}} \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{s}_\text{hid}}{\partial g_e^\text{(hid)}} \cdot s_\text{in}^{(\text{detached})}
$$

The detached input spikes still block temporal backprop, but gradient
reaches $W_{ee}^{(\text{in→hid})}$ via the auxiliary head at E\_hid.

**Complexity:** Low — a few lines of code. Add a `nn.Linear(N_hid, C)`,
compute loss, sum.

**Limitation:** Each layer trains to be independently linearly separable
for the *same* class labels. The hidden layer doesn't learn features
specifically useful for the output layer — just generically discriminative
features.

### 2. e-prop (eligibility traces)

Replace BPTT entirely with a three-factor learning rule. Each synapse
$w_{ij}$ maintains an **eligibility trace** $e_{ij}(t)$ that tracks the
local contribution of the synapse to postsynaptic activity:

$$
e_{ij}(t+1) = \alpha \, e_{ij}(t) + \frac{\partial s_j(t)}{\partial w_{ij}}\bigg|_\text{local}
$$

where $\alpha = e^{-dt/\tau_e}$ is the trace decay and the partial
derivative is computed using only local quantities (pre/postsynaptic
voltage, spike history) — no backprop through time.

The weight update combines the trace with a global **learning signal**
$L(t)$ derived from the output error:

$$
\Delta w_{ij} = \eta \sum_t L(t) \cdot e_{ij}(t)
$$

For a classification task, $L(t)$ can be the broadcast error
$\hat{y} - y$ at the final timestep, applied retroactively to the
accumulated traces.

The key insight: e-prop approximates BPTT by replacing the exact
temporal Jacobian with a forward-running trace. It never computes
$\partial V_{t+1}/\partial g_e = 65$ because it doesn't differentiate
through the LIF dynamics at all.

**Mathematical relationship to BPTT:** Bellec et al. (2020) showed that
e-prop computes the same gradient as BPTT for a linear readout, and
approximates it for recurrent connections. The approximation error comes
from ignoring future-to-past dependencies (e-prop is causal; BPTT is
acausal).

**Complexity:** Moderate. Need to define eligibility traces for
conductance-based synapses (most e-prop literature uses current-based
LIF). The trace equation for conductance-based models would be:

$$
e_{ij}(t) = \sum_{t' < t} \alpha^{t-t'} \cdot H_\sigma'(V_j(t') - V_{th}) \cdot \frac{dt(E_e - V_i(t'))}{C_m} \cdot s_i(t' - d)
$$

Note the $(E_e - V_i)$ term appears in the trace but is evaluated at a
*specific* timestep (not chained multiplicatively), so it doesn't
compound.

### 3. DECOLLE (Deep Continuous Local Learning)

Similar to auxiliary losses but operates at every timestep rather than
only at the end of simulation. Each layer $\ell$ has a local readout
$W_\ell^\text{local}$ and a per-timestep loss:

$$
\mathcal{L}_\ell = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t \text{CE}\!\left(W_\ell^\text{local} \cdot \sigma(V_\ell(t)),\; y\right)
$$

where $\sigma$ is a surrogate function applied to the membrane voltage.
Weight updates use single-timestep gradients:

$$
\Delta W_\ell = -\eta \frac{1}{T}\sum_t \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_\ell(t)}{\partial W_\ell}
$$

No temporal backprop is needed because each $\mathcal{L}_\ell(t)$
depends only on quantities at time $t$.

**Complexity:** Moderate. Similar to auxiliary losses but with
per-timestep supervision. Original paper (Kaiser et al., 2020) uses
current-based LIF; adaptation to conductance-based requires care with
the surrogate applied to $V$.

### 4. Feedback alignment

Replace the transpose weight matrix $W^T$ used in backpropagation with
a fixed random matrix $B$:

$$
\delta_\ell = B_\ell \cdot \delta_{\ell+1} \quad \text{instead of} \quad \delta_\ell = W_{\ell+1}^T \cdot \delta_{\ell+1}
$$

This bypasses the spike buffer entirely — the error signal is projected
directly from the output to each layer without tracing through the
forward dynamics.

Lillicrap et al. (2016) showed that random $B$ works because the forward
weights $W$ learn to align with $B$ over training (the "feedback
alignment" property). The key condition is that $B$ preserves the
**sign** of the true gradient on average:

$$
\mathbb{E}\left[\text{sign}(B \cdot \delta) \cdot \text{sign}(W^T \cdot \delta)\right] > 0
$$

**Direct feedback alignment** (DFA) goes further by projecting the
output error directly to every layer, skipping intermediate layers
entirely:

$$
\delta_\ell = B_\ell \cdot (\hat{y} - y)
$$

**Complexity:** Low. Replace the backward pass with random projections.
But unclear how well this interacts with PING temporal dynamics.

### 5. Conductance rescaling (partial BPTT)

Rescale the problematic Jacobian entry without changing the forward
dynamics. During the backward pass only, replace:

$$
\frac{\partial V_{t+1}}{\partial g_e} = \frac{dt(E_e - V_t)}{C_m} \quad \longrightarrow \quad \frac{\partial V_{t+1}}{\partial g_e}\bigg|_\text{surrogate} = \frac{dt \cdot \lambda}{C_m}
$$

where $\lambda \ll |E_e - V|$ is a small constant (e.g., $\lambda = 1$).
This is a **surrogate Jacobian** analogous to the surrogate gradient for
spikes — the forward pass sees the true driving force, but the backward
pass uses a tamed version.

With $\lambda = 1$, the per-step gradient amplification drops from
$65\times$ to $1\times$, and BPTT over the full 200 steps becomes
numerically feasible.

**Complexity:** Low — a custom autograd function wrapping the LIF update.

**Risk:** The surrogate Jacobian may point in a different direction than
the true gradient. Whether learning still converges depends on sign
agreement (similar to feedback alignment). This is not well studied for
conductance-based models.

## Comparison

| Method | Trains E\_in→E\_hid | Trains PING loop | Temporal credit | Complexity | Established |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Auxiliary losses | Yes | No | None | Low | Yes |
| e-prop | Yes | Yes | Forward traces | Moderate | Yes (current-based) |
| DECOLLE | Yes | No | Per-timestep | Moderate | Yes (current-based) |
| Feedback alignment | Yes | Partially | Via random projection | Low | Yes |
| Conductance rescaling | Yes | Yes | Surrogate BPTT | Low | No |

## Recommended path

1. **Auxiliary losses first** — minimal code change, immediately tests
   whether E\_in→E\_hid can learn useful features. If accuracy jumps
   significantly, the random projection was the bottleneck and more
   sophisticated methods are worth pursuing.

2. **Conductance rescaling** — if auxiliary losses help but we want
   temporal credit assignment, this is the lowest-effort path to partial
   BPTT. Requires empirical validation that the surrogate Jacobian
   preserves enough gradient information.

3. **e-prop** — the principled long-term solution. No BPTT at all,
   naturally handles conductance-based dynamics, biologically motivated.
   Requires deriving eligibility traces for our specific neuron model.
