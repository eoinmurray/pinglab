## Overview

We test whether oscillatory phase and spike-time precision jointly gate
communication into a receiver network. The implementation lives in
`experiments/coupled-networks/` and replaces a full sender network with a
controlled spike-packet generator. The core question is whether identical input
packets have different efficacy depending only on arrival phase and timing
jitter.

## Receiver model

The receiver is a fixed PING regime with E and I populations and conductance-
based LIF dynamics. A baseline tonic drive produces a stable gamma oscillation.
We use the population mean inhibitory conductance onto E neurons,
`g_i_mean_E(t)`, as an LFP proxy.

The receiver dynamics are summarized by

$$
C_m \frac{dV}{dt} = g_L(E_L - V) + g_e(E_e - V) + g_i(E_i - V) + I_{ext}(t)
$$

where $I_{ext}(t)$ is the external input constructed from the spike packets.

## Input interface (fiber projection)

We define an input fiber population of size $N_{in}$. Each fiber $f$ targets a
fixed subset $\mathcal{T}_f$ of E neurons. This is a sparse random projection
with $|\mathcal{T}_f| = M$ targets per fiber. When fiber $f$ emits a spike, we
add a brief conductance/current pulse of amplitude $w$ to all neurons in
$\mathcal{T}_f$.

Formally, the external input to neuron $i$ is

$$
I_{ext,i}(t) = I_{tonic} + \sum_{f=1}^{N_{in}} \mathbf{1}[i \in \mathcal{T}_f]
\sum_{k} w \cdot \mathbf{1}[t \in (t_{f,k}, t_{f,k}+\Delta t_{pulse})]
$$

where $t_{f,k}$ are spike times for fiber $f$.

## Packet generator

A packet is defined a base time $t_0$, width $W$, mean spike count per fiber
$K$, and jitter $\sigma$. For each fiber $f$ we sample a spike count
$k_f \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(K)$ and then sample spike times uniformly within the
packet window, then add Gaussian jitter:

$$
\tau_{f,j} \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(t_0, t_0 + W), \quad
t_{f,j} = \tau_{f,j} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2).
$$

Packets are delivered at a set of times $\{t_0^m\}$ across the trial.

## Phase estimation

We estimate instantaneous phase from the LFP proxy $x(t) = g_i\_\mathrm{mean\_E}(t)$.
After mean subtraction and smoothing, we compute the analytic signal

$$
\tilde{x}(t) = x(t) + i\,\mathcal{H}[x](t),
$$

and define phase as

$$
\phi(t) = \arg\,\tilde{x}(t).
$$

For visualization, we also plot the unwrapped phase to avoid $\pm\pi$ wrapping
artifacts.

## Phase control (delay sweep)

We keep packet times fixed and apply an additional delay $d$ to all packet
spikes. This sweeps packet arrival phase without changing the packet generator.
Delays $d$ span one gamma cycle, so the arrival phase is

$$
\phi(t_0^m + d).
$$

## Readout metric

We measure the evoked response in the **targeted E neurons only**. For each
packet arrival time $t_a$, we compute a baseline-subtracted spike count:

$$
R_{\mathrm{evoked}}(t_a) =
\#\,\mathrm{spikes}_{\mathrm{target}}[t_a, t_a+\Delta] -
\#\,\mathrm{spikes}_{\mathrm{target}}^{\mathrm{baseline}}[t_a, t_a+\Delta].
$$

We then average across packet times and trials to obtain
$\bar{R}(d, \sigma)$, the mean evoked response as a function of delay and jitter.

## Expected signature

This minimal Fries-style prediction is:

1. **Phase dependence:** $\bar{R}(d, \sigma)$ is modulated across delays, with a
   preferred delay where packets land on a high-excitability phase.
2. **Precision dependence:** increasing jitter $\sigma$ reduces peak response and
   flattens phase modulation.

The LFP proxy should look like a clean, nearly periodic waveform. This indicates
the receiver is in a stable oscillatory regime rather than drifting into
asynchrony. If this trace is noisy or nonperiodic, phase becomes ambiguous and
any delay sweep will fail to produce systematic modulation.

The unwrapped phase should rise smoothly with time, with a roughly constant
slope that reflects the oscillation frequency. Deviations in slope correspond
to transient changes in frequency or coherence. A stable ramp confirms that the
phase estimate can be used as a timing reference for packet delivery.

### Phase- and precision-gated response

<Gallery
  title="Response vs delay"
  path={`${path}`}
  globs={["response_vs_delay*"]}
  caption="Mean evoked response vs delay for each jitter level. Lower jitter preserves phase modulation; higher jitter reduces response magnitude and phase contrast."

This plot is the core test. We expect a **phase window**: a preferred delay
where evoked response peaks and an anti-preferred delay where response troughs.
As jitter increases, the peak should drop and the trough should fill in, because
timing noise smears packets across both excitable and inexcitable phases. A
flattened curve at high jitter is the hallmark of precision loss.

## Practical notes

The full sweep is computationally heavy (delays x jitters x trials). For quick
validation, reduce the number of delays, packets, and trials in
`experiments/coupled-networks/config.yaml`.
