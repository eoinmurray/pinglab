The brain can be described as a dynamical system operating under tight energetic
and stability constraints. It represents structure in sensory input, adapts
through learning, and exhibits behavior that is often well described by Bayesian
models, without implying that Bayesian inference is implemented explicitly at
the level of individual neurons.

A number of theoretical frameworks aim to explain different aspects of brain
function. These frameworks are largely complementary and typically leave open
the question of the specific circuit mechanisms that enable stable, efficient
computation. Understanding such mechanisms is necessary to connect theory to
neural implementation.

## Gamma Oscillations and PING

Gamma-band activity (approximately 30–80 Hz) is a prominent feature of cortical
recordings. It is commonly observed in local field potential (LFP) measurements,
which reflect the summed synaptic and membrane activity of local neural
populations.

One well-supported mechanism for cortical gamma is the Pyramidal–Interneuron
Network Gamma (PING) model. In this mechanism, excitatory pyramidal neurons
drive inhibitory interneurons, which in turn suppress pyramidal activity. The
resulting excitatory–inhibitory feedback loop can produce rhythmic population
activity in the gamma range.

## PING as a Circuit-Level Control Mechanism

From a computational perspective, PING can be viewed as a circuit-level
mechanism that supports stable and flexible neural dynamics under biological
constraints.

Key properties include:

* **Fast negative feedback** Excitatory–inhibitory loops stabilize recurrent
  excitation on short timescales.

* **Gain regulation** Inhibitory activity scales with excitation, supporting
  sensitivity across varying input strengths.

* **Temporal structuring** Oscillatory cycles create time windows that influence
  when spikes are most effective.

* **Phase-dependent communication** Relative phase relationships can modulate
  effective connectivity between populations.

* **Control of spike-time variability** Excitatory–inhibitory timing affects the
  precision of spiking responses.

* **Energetic efficiency** A greater proportion of computation occurs in
  subthreshold dynamics, with spikes used selectively.

* **Flexible regime transitions** Circuits can transition between asynchronous
  and oscillatory states without structural changes.

* **Robustness to perturbation** Changes in parameters typically lead to gradual
  degradation of timing and coherence.

## Relevance of PING

Many high-level theories of brain function implicitly assume mechanisms that
regulate gain, stabilize recurrent activity, structure time, and coordinate
distributed neural populations. PING provides a biologically plausible circuit
mechanism that can support these requirements.

In this sense, PING is not a theory of cognition, but a candidate control
mechanism through which cortical circuits may implement and constrain
higher-level computational principles.

### Core circuit primitives implied by major brain theories

**1. Gain control / normalization**
Needed by efficient coding, Bayesian inference, predictive processing.
Controls sensitivity without saturation.
→ Implemented via E/I balance, divisive inhibition.

**2. Fast negative feedback (stability)**
Needed by control theory, dynamical systems, predictive processing.
Prevents runaway excitation; enables tight operating regimes.
→ PING, inhibitory loops, balanced networks.

**3. Temporal discretization / windowing**
Needed by communication, binding, sequencing, routing.
Defines *when* spikes matter.
→ PING, ING, theta–gamma nesting.

**4. Phase-dependent gating (effective connectivity)**
Needed by communication-through-coherence, attention, routing.
Controls *who talks to whom* without rewiring or rate explosions.
→ PING + delays + coherence.

**5. Noise shaping / variability control**
Needed by Bayesian behavior, sampling views, robustness.
Manages trial-to-trial variability without destroying signal.
→ Balanced E/I, oscillatory locking, stochastic resonance regimes.

**6. Regime switching**
Needed by control, active inference, task flexibility.
Fast transitions between async ↔ oscillatory ↔ suppressed states.
→ Neuromodulation + PING-like control knobs.

**7. Credit assignment locality**
Needed by learning theories (RL, predictive coding).
Allows local plasticity to reflect global objectives.
→ Eligibility traces, spike timing, modulatory signals.

**8. Energy-efficient computation**
Needed by *biology*, whether theorists admit it or not.
Maximize computation per spike.
→ Subthreshold dynamics, sparse spiking, oscillatory gating.

**9. Robust population coordination**
Needed by dynamical systems and motor control.
Aligns distributed populations without central clocks.
→ Oscillations, phase locking, delay structure.

**10. Representational segregation**
Needed to avoid interference (often ignored).
Prevents everything synchronizing into useless mush.
→ Partial coherence, multiplexed rhythms, controlled desynchrony.

### Where PING sits (and where it doesn’t)

PING is **not** a theory of inference, learning, or cognition.
It is a **control primitive** that cleanly implements **1–6, 8–9**.

If a theory assumes:

* gain control,
* temporal structure,
* stable recurrence,
* selective communication,

…but can’t point to *something like PING*, it’s incomplete.

If it claims PING is *the explanation*, it’s also wrong.

PING is closer to a **clocked logic primitive** than a belief system.
