## Overview

This document defines the **core metrics used to characterise network state**
throughout the experimental programme. The narrative thread is simple: we first
verify that the circuit is in a stable regime, then quantify timing structure,
variability, and energy so later experiments can be interpreted without
confounds.

The aim is not to privilege any single measure, but to provide a **redundant,
cross-checking set of diagnostics** that jointly identify dynamical regime,
stability, variability, and timing structure. All subsequent experiments
(phase-dependent gating, perturbation response, communication, and energy
tradeoffs) are interpreted relative to these metrics.

To read the page sequentially: start with firing rates (matching constraints),
then timing/variability (ISI CV, correlations, E–I lag), then spectral and
synchrony summaries, and finally conductance and energy proxies that anchor the
story to subthreshold dynamics and cost.

## Mean Population Firing Rates

We begin with firing rates because they are the primary matching constraint
across regimes. If rates drift, downstream timing differences are harder to
interpret.

- **`mean_rate_E`**: Mean firing rate of the excitatory population (Hz).  
- **`mean_rate_I`**: Mean firing rate of the inhibitory population (Hz).

**Role:**  
Mean rates are tracked primarily to enable **rate-matched comparisons** across
regimes. Differences in timing, variability, or information transfer are
interpreted only when mean firing rates are comparable.

## Inter-Spike Interval Variability (ISI CV)

Once rates are matched, we quantify temporal irregularity. ISI CV captures
whether spikes are Poisson-like or locked to a rhythm.

- **`cv_pop_E`, `cv_pop_I`**: CV computed from pooled ISIs within each population.  
- **`cv_mean_E`, `cv_mean_I`**: Mean CV across individual neurons.

**Role:**  
ISI CV distinguishes asynchronous irregular activity (CV ≈ 1) from more regular,
oscillation-locked firing. Population-pooled and neuron-averaged measures are
reported separately to avoid conflating heterogeneity with regularity.

## Pairwise Spike Count Correlations

Correlations indicate shared variability beyond rate. They diagnose whether the
network is drifting into over-coherence that could confound later comparisons.

- **`corr_EE_mean`**: Mean E–E spike count correlation.  
- **`corr_II_mean`**: Mean I–I spike count correlation.  
- **`corr_EI_mean`**: Mean E–I spike count correlation.

**Role:**  
These correlations quantify shared variability and synchrony beyond firing rate.
They are particularly relevant for assessing over-coherence and representational
interference in later experiments.

## Excitatory–Inhibitory Timing (E–I Lag)

The E→I→E loop is the mechanism behind PING. We measure the timing offset to
confirm the loop is intact and stable.

- **`lag_EI_mean_ms`**: Mean lag of inhibitory spikes relative to excitatory spikes (ms).  
- **`lag_EI_std_ms`**: Variability of this lag.

**Role:**  
A short, consistent E→I lag is a defining signature of PING dynamics. Deviations
from this range signal loss of coherent oscillatory structure or destabilisation
of feedback control.

## Spectral (Gamma) Metrics

Spectral summaries compress oscillatory structure into a few interpretable
numbers. They are used to compare oscillation strength across sweeps.

- **`gamma_peak_freq`**: Frequency of the dominant gamma-band peak (Hz).  
- **`gamma_peak_power`**: Power at the peak frequency.  
- **`gamma_Q`**: Quality factor (peak sharpness).

**Role:**  
Spectral metrics provide a compact summary of oscillatory strength and coherence.
The quality factor is particularly useful for distinguishing weak rhythmicity
from narrowband, strongly phase-locked oscillations.

## Population Fano Factor

We next quantify trial-to-trial variability. This is the reliability counterpart
to ISI CV.

- **`fano_E`, `fano_I`**: Fano factor of spike counts across trials or windows.

**Role:**  
The Fano factor captures trial-to-trial variability in population output. It is
used later to assess whether oscillatory regimes improve or degrade response
reliability under noise.

## Synchrony Index

Synchrony is a coarse global summary. We treat it as a supporting signal rather
than a primary measure.

- **`synchrony`**: Scalar measure of overall spike-time alignment.

**Role:**  
This provides a coarse summary of collective timing. It is intentionally paired
with correlation and spectral measures to avoid over-interpreting a single index
of synchrony.

## Conductance Statistics

To connect spiking to subthreshold dynamics, we track mean conductances and their
fluctuations. This becomes important when interpreting energy and excitability.

- **`g_e_mean`, `g_i_mean`**: Mean excitatory and inhibitory conductances.  
- **`g_ei_ratio`**: Ratio of mean excitation to inhibition.  
- **`g_e_cv`, `g_i_cv`**: Temporal variability of conductances.

**Role:**
These measures track **subthreshold dynamics**, providing an energy-relevant view
of network state that complements spike-based metrics.

## Energy Metrics

Energy proxies let us compare regimes on cost, not just activity. They are used
for energy-matched analyses in later communication and routing experiments.

- **`energy_conductance`**: Time-integrated total conductance $\int(g_e + g_i)\,dt$.
- **`energy_spikes`**: Total spike count (E + I).
- **`energy_total`**: Combined energy proxy (conductance integral + spikes).
- **`energy_per_spike`**: Efficiency ratio (spikes / energy_total).

**Role:**
Energy metrics enable **energy-matched comparisons** across regimes. Differences
in communication or gating are only meaningful when both rate and energy are
controlled.

## Regime Classification

Finally, we summarize the joint metric profile into a regime label. The label is
useful for navigation but never replaces the metrics above.

- **`regime`**: Discrete classification (e.g. asynchronous, oscillatory) derived
  from the joint metric set.

**Role:**  
Regime labels are not treated as ground truth but as **summary descriptors**
derived from multiple diagnostics. All claims about phase dependence, routing, or
control are evaluated relative to these regime assignments.
