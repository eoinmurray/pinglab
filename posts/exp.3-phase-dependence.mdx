## Overview

Having identified asynchronous and oscillatory regimes in the **Parameter
Sweeps** experiment, we now ask whether oscillatory dynamics confer
**phase-dependent control over input processing**.

Specifically, we test whether identical, brief inputs delivered at different
phases of an ongoing gamma oscillation produce systematically different
responses. This experiment operationalizes the idea that oscillations structure
time into alternating windows of high and low excitability, without changing
mean firing rates or synaptic architecture.

The experiment proceeds in two stages:

1. A single-trial phase sweep to visualize phase-dependent gain.
2. A multi-trial protocol (GPP-1) to quantify gain, reliability, and temporal
   precision as a function of phase.

The narrative flow is: establish a clean oscillation, probe it with identical
inputs at different phases, then quantify gain and precision across trials.

## Baseline Oscillatory Regime

### Purpose

Before delivering any inputs, we verify that the network is operating in a
stable oscillatory regime consistent with PING dynamics, as established in
**Parameter Sweeps**.

### Setup

We simulate a canonical PING network with:
- $N_E = 800$, $N_I = 200$
- tonic input: $I_E = 1.2$, $I_I = 0.9$
- noise: $\sigma = 0.4$
- coupling: $g_{ei} = 1.5$, $g_{ie} = 2.5$
- synaptic time constants: $\tau_{AMPA} = 2$ ms, $\tau_{GABA} = 6.5$ ms
- timestep: $dt = 0.1$ ms
- duration: $T = 1000$ ms

We record population-averaged membrane potentials and synaptic conductances.
Mean inhibitory conductance onto E neurons serves as an LFP proxy and phase
reference.

### Observation

The network exhibits sustained gamma-band oscillations with the characteristic
E→I→E timing loop. Inhibitory conductance shows strong rhythmic modulation,
creating alternating periods of higher and lower excitability.

## Phase-Dependent Responses to Identical Inputs

### Design

We deliver brief current pulses to a fixed subset of E neurons while holding all
network parameters constant.

- Target population: 10% of E neurons
- Pulse width: 5 ms
- Pulse amplitude: 0.5 (arbitrary units)
- Pulse times: 100 evenly spaced points spanning 500–700 ms

For visualization, we display raster plots for every tenth phase.

### Question

Does the timing of an input relative to the ongoing oscillation affect whether
and how strongly it is transmitted through the network?

### Observation

Inputs arriving near inhibitory troughs evoke robust spiking responses in the
target population, while inputs delivered near inhibitory peaks are strongly
attenuated. The same physical input thus produces different network responses
depending solely on its timing.

This step is descriptive: it shows phase gating in a single sweep without yet
testing reliability.

## Phase–Gain Relationship

### Quantification

For each pulse time, we compute the change in spike count (Δ-spikes) in the
target population:

- pre-window: 20 ms before pulse
- post-window: 100 ms after pulse

We overlay Δ-spikes with the normalized inhibitory conductance from the baseline
simulation to relate response magnitude to oscillatory phase.

### Result

Δ-spikes varies systematically with phase and is anti-correlated with inhibitory
conductance. Maximal responses occur during periods of low inhibition; minimal
responses occur during periods of high inhibition.

This gives a compact gain curve we can compare against the trial-averaged
protocol below.

## Gamma Phase Precision (GPP-1)

### Motivation

Phase-dependent gain alone does not imply improved computation. We therefore ask
whether oscillatory phase also affects **response reliability and temporal
precision** under noise.

### Design

For each of the 100 pulse phases, we run 30 trials with independent noise
realizations. For each phase we compute:

- **Gain:** Δ-spikes rate (spikes/ms)
- **Reliability:** Fano factor of spike counts across trials
- **Temporal precision:** standard deviation of first-spike latencies

Metrics are saved for downstream analysis.

### Results

- Gain reproduces the phase dependence observed in the single-trial sweep.
- Reliability and latency precision vary with phase, indicating that oscillatory
  timing modulates not only response magnitude but also trial-to-trial
  variability.
- Periods of low inhibition tend to support more temporally precise responses,
  though the relationship is not strictly monotonic.

## Interpretation

These results show that, once an oscillatory regime is established, PING
dynamics impose a **temporal structure on input processing**. Identical inputs
are differentially gated based on their timing relative to the E–I cycle, without
changes in mean drive or synaptic parameters.

This supports the view of gamma oscillations as a **control mechanism** that
structures when inputs are effective, rather than as a representational code in
their own right. Subsequent experiments examine whether such phase-dependent
gating can support selective communication, routing, and rapid regime switching.
