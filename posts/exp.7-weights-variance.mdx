## Goal

Explore how mean and variance in synaptic weights affects the emergence PING rhythms.

We will do this by creating a heatmap of $\mu$ vs $\sigma$ vs PINGness metrics.

## Prior work

> Variance in the strength of I → E synapses has little effect on the resulting
> rhythm, while E → I variance reduces coherence by a factor of 0.5. In some
> cases, strong variance in both sets of synapses degrades the rhythm entirely.
> — Byrne 2024, p. 5.

Rory Byrne's report *"Stable and flexible computation in rhythmic spiking
networks"* found that variance in the $I \to E$ weights had little effect on the
PING rhythms, while variance in the $E \to I$ weights had a stronger effect.
Thus for our initial pass we will focus on $E \to I$ weights. In future we can
repeat the study on all weight types.

## Neuron Selection

For now we will use MQIF neurons, we have as of yet not done a full study of
which neuron type (LIF, MQIF, QIF, AdEx, HH) is best for PING rhythms. MQIF is a
reasonable starting point, given it’s proposed in Thomas Burgers thesis in the
context of PING networks.

## De-counfounding

I believe we need to firing rate match all scans. This is because increasing
variance in weights can affect firing rates, which in turn affects oscillation
metrics. To isolate the effect of weight variance on oscillation quality, we
need to ensure firing rates are held constant.

We will do this by adjusting the external drive (input current) to the E and I
populations such that their firing rates remain at a target level. 

## Metrics

### 1. Gamma PSD SNR (signal-to-noise ratio)

$$
\rm{Gamma\ SNR} = \frac{ \sum_{f \in \Tau } PSD(f) df }{ \sum_{f \notin \Tau} PSD(f) df } \ with \ \Tau = [30, 100] Hz
$$

By comparing power in the gamma band (30–100 Hz) to power outside the gamma
band, the gamma signal-to-noise ratio of the population E-rate (or synaptic
current) answers the question: *Does gamma-band activity stand out from
background fluctuations?*

**Strengths:**

* Normalises against broadband power, reducing firing-rate confounds.
* Detects oscillations that are spectrally distinct, not just strong.
* Retains information about dominant frequency via the gamma band peak.

**Weaknesses:**

* Insensitive to cycle-to-cycle regularity.
* Does not capture spike timing or phase structure.

**Fails at catching:** Unstable or bursty rhythms with strong but irregular
gamma power.

### 2. Autocorrelation Rhythmicity Index (RI)

Definition as in [exp.3-rhythmicity-metric](./exp.3-rhythmicity-metric.mdx).

By taking the ratio of largest non-zero-lag peak (in gamma-lag window) to
zero-lag peak from the autocorrelation of the mean-subtracted population rate we
answer the question: *Does the rhythm repeat cleanly from cycle to cycle?*

**Strengths:**

* Captures temporal regularity.
* Detects stable oscillators vs noisy bursts.

**Weaknesses:**

* Sensitive to rate and silence if not normalized.
* Population-level only.

**Fails at catching:**

* Bursty, unstable “fake gamma”.

### 3. Phase-Locking Value (PLV)

Definition as in [exp.5-plv-metric](./exp.5-plv-metric.mdx).

By extracting instantaneous phase of population rhythm (Hilbert) and computing
vector strength of spike phases we answer the question *are spikes actually
riding the rhythm?*

**Strengths:**

* Rate-independent spike timing structure.
* Distinguishing true coordination from rate bumps.

**Weaknesses:**

* Noisy at very low spike counts.
* Needs decent phase estimation.

**Failure mode it catches:**

* High PSD / RI with uncoordinated spikes.

### 4. How the metrics work together

| Case | Gamma SNR | RI | PLV | Interpretation                        |
| ---- | --------- | -- | --- | ------------------------------------- |
| 1    | ↑         | ↑  | ↑   | **True PING rhythm**                  |
| 2    | ↑         | ↓  | ↓   | Broadband / burst-driven gamma        |
| 3    | ↑         | ↑  | ↓   | Population rhythm, weak spike locking |
| 4    | ↓         | ↓  | ↓   | Asynchronous regime                   |
| 5    | ↓         | ↑  | ↓   | Pathological bursting / silence       |

**Gamma SNR** tells us whether gamma-band activity stands out from background
fluctuations, **RI** tells us how stable the rhythm is across cycles, and
**PLV** tells us whether spikes are actually coordinated with the rhythm.

What raster looks like in each case:

1. **↑ SNR, ↑ RI, ↑ PLV:** tight, repeating spike bands with clear phase locking.
2. **↑ SNR, ↓ RI, ↓ PLV:** bursty packets with a PSD peak but jittered timing.
3. **↑ SNR, ↑ RI, ↓ PLV:** clean population rhythm but diffuse spike phases.
4. **↓ SNR, ↓ RI, ↓ PLV:** scattered spikes with no clear banding.
5. **↓ SNR, ↑ RI, ↓ PLV:** sparse or locked bursts with large silent gaps.

### 5. Metric notes

PSD (which is used for Gamma SNR) and autocorrelation are Fourier pairs, so in
theory they contain the same information, but they highlight different
structures. PSD tells us whether there is oscillatory energy, while
autocorrelation tells us whether the rhythm repeats cleanly in time.

## Pseudocode outline

```python
for mu in mu_values:
  for sigma in sigma_values:
    # Generate weights
    g_ei_weights = sample_weights(mu, sigma, size=(N_e, N_i))
    # Rate match external drive
    I_ext_e, I_ext_i = rate_match(g_ei_weights, target_rates)
    # Run simulation
    sim_data = run_simulation(g_ei_weights, I_ext_e, I_ext_i, other_params)
    # Compute metrics
    gamma_snr = compute_gamma_snr(sim_data)
    ri = compute_rhythmicity_index(sim_data)
    plv = compute_phase_locking_value(sim_data)
    # Plot results
    plot_results(mu, sigma, gamma_snr, ri, plv, sim_data)
```
