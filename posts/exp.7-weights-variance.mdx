# TL'DR
We explore how mean and variance in synaptic weights affects the emergence of PING
rhythms. We create a heatmap of $\mu_{g_{ei}}$ vs $\sigma_{g_{ei}}$ vs our Rhythmicity
metric, using MQIF neurons with conductance-based synapses and weight
heterogeneity. We find that increasing variance in E→I weights generally decreases rhythmicity,
especially at higher mean weights, while firing rates are partially controlled via
external drive adjustments.

# Goal

Explore how mean and variance in synaptic weights affects the emergence PING
rhythms. To do this we create a heatmap of $\mu_{g_{ei}}$ vs $\sigma_{g_{ei}}$ vs
our Rhythmicity metric, once we have settled on an analysis we will do the same
treatment for the other weight matrices ($g_{ee}$, $g_{ie}$, $g_{ii}$).

# Prior work

> Variance in the strength of I → E synapses has little effect on the resulting
> rhythm, while E → I variance reduces coherence by a factor of 0.5. In some
> cases, strong variance in both sets of synapses degrades the rhythm entirely.
> — Byrne 2024, p. 5.

Rory Byrne's report *"Stable and flexible computation in rhythmic spiking
networks"* found that variance in the $I \to E$ weights had little effect on the
PING rhythms, while variance in the $E \to I$ weights had a stronger effect.
Thus for our initial pass we will focus on $E \to I$ weights. In future we can
repeat the study on all weight types.

# Neuron Parameters and Connectivity

For now we will use **MQIF neurons**, we have as of yet not done a full study of
which neuron type (LIF, MQIF, QIF, AdEx, HH) is best for PING rhythms. MQIF is a
reasonable starting point given it’s proposition in Thomas Burgers thesis in the
context of PING networks.

**MQIF dynamics (implemented form).** In our code, MQIF is implemented with a
configurable number of quadratic voltage terms and a configurable number of
slow variables. The exact update is:

$$
C_m \, \dot V
  = g_L (E_L - V)
  + \sum_{k=1}^{K} a_k (V - V_{r,k})^2
  - \sum_{s=1}^{S} w_s
  + I_{syn} + I_{ext},
$$

$$
\tau_s \, \dot w_s = b_s (V - V_{w,s})^2 - w_s.
$$

with synaptic currents defined below. Here $K = \text{len}(\text{mqif\_a})$ and
$S = \text{len}(\text{mqif\_w\_a})$. For this experiment, `mqif_a: [0.02]` and
`mqif_w_a: [0.02]`, so **$K=1$ quadratic term** and **$S=1$ slow variable**
with `mqif_w_tau: [100.0]` ms. When $V$ reaches a threshold $V_{th}$, a spike
is emitted, $V$ is reset to $V_{reset}$, and a refractory period $t_{ref}$ is
applied. 

**Conductance‑based synapses.** For each synapse type $x \in \{E,I\}$ the
synaptic current into neuron $i$ is:

$$
I_{syn,i} = \sum_{j} g_{ij}^{(E)} (E_E - V_i) + \sum_{j} g_{ij}^{(I)} (E_I - V_i),
$$

with conductances updated on presynaptic spikes and decaying exponentially:

$$
\tau_x \, \dot g_{ij}^{(x)} = -g_{ij}^{(x)} + w_{ij}^{(x)} \sum_k \delta(t - t_k^{(j)}).
$$

Here $w_{ij}^{(x)}$ is the synaptic weight (peak conductance increment) from
presynaptic neuron $j$ to postsynaptic neuron $i$.

**Weights × connection probability.** For each block (E→E, E→I, I→E, I→I) we
sample a dense weight matrix $G$ from a Gaussian with mean $\mu$ and standard
deviation $\sigma$, then apply a Bernoulli mask $A$ with connection
probability $p$:

$$
W = A \odot G,\quad A_{ij} \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p),
$$

where $\odot$ denotes the element‑wise (Hadamard) product.

This is equivalent to drawing $W_{ij}=0$ with probability $1-p$ and
$W_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma)$ with probability $p$ (then clipped to
be non‑negative). The expected effective weight is $E[W_{ij}] = p \mu$, and
the total synaptic drive therefore scales with both $\mu$ and $p$.

If we dont apply the Bernoulli mask to create sparse connectivity then the
network blows up, and firing rates become extreme.

**Neuron heterogeneity.** To avoid unrealistically identical neurons we add
independent Gaussian heterogeneity to select parameters per neuron. In the
sim, each neuron draws

$$
V_{th,i} \sim \mathcal{N}(V_{th}, \sigma_{V_{th}})
$$
$$
g_{L,i} \sim \mathcal{N}(g_L, \sigma_{g_L})
$$
$$
C_{m,i} \sim \mathcal{N}(C_m, \sigma_{C_m})
$$
$$
t_{ref,i} \sim \mathcal{N}(t_{ref}, \sigma_{t_{ref}})
$$

with separate E/I baselines ($g_L$, $C_m$, $t_{ref}$) and shared standard
deviations. For this experiment we use
`V_th_heterogeneity_sd = 1.2`, `g_L_heterogeneity_sd = 0.15`,
`C_m_heterogeneity_sd = 0.1`, `t_ref_heterogeneity_sd = 0.3`.

Here $t_{ref}$ is the absolute refractory period applied after a spike: once
$V$ crosses $V_{th}$ and is reset, the neuron is held non‑spiking for
$t_{ref}$ ms (implemented as a per‑neuron countdown that masks spikes and holds
$V$ at $V_{reset}$ during the refractory window). This is why $t_{ref}$ appears
in the heterogeneity list even though it is not part of the continuous‑time
voltage ODE.

# Autocorrelation Rhythmicity Index (RI)

We measure rhythmicity using the same autocorrelation-based index from
[exp.5-rhythmicity-metric](./exp.5-rhythmicity-metric.mdx). In short:

1. Run the simulation for $T=1000$ ms.
2. Discard the first 200 ms (burn-in); analyze the remaining 800 ms.
3. Bin excitatory spikes into 5 ms bins to form a population rate $r(t)$.
4. Normalize the rate to zero mean and unit variance:
   $x_n=(r_n-\bar{r})/\sigma_r$.
5. Compute the normalized autocorrelation over positive lags.
6. Search lags $\tau \in [5,200]$ ms and take the largest peak.

Formally, with bin width $\Delta t = 5$ ms and discrete samples $x_n$:

$$ C_k = \frac{1}{n - k} \sum_{m=0}^{n-k-1} x_m \, x_{m+k}, $$

where $k$ maps to $\tau = k \Delta t$ and $n$ is the number of bins. The
rhythmicity index is

$$ \rho = \max_{\tau \in [\tau_{min}, \tau_{max}]} C(\tau), $$

with $\tau_{min}=5$ ms and $\tau_{max}=200$ ms. If the rate series is empty or
has zero variance, we set $\rho=0$.

**Note:** In this experiment we compute RI using only the final 500 ms of the
simulation (i.e., we slice spikes to $[T-500,\,T]$ ms before forming the rate),
so the metric reflects the settled dynamics rather than early transients.

# Firing rate matching

Changing $g_{ei}$ without rate-matching confounds rhythmicity because the metric
depends on firing rate and population activity level. As $g_{ei}$ shifts
inhibition strength, it can suppress or boost E‑rates, which changes
autocorrelation amplitude, PSD power, and apparent coherence. So differences
in “rhythmicity” may mostly reflect rate changes (and altered spike density)
rather than variance effects on oscillatory structure.

We should control firing rate across conditions. The most defensible approach is
explicit rate matching by adjusting external drive $I_E$ (holding $I_I$ fixed)
so that each $(\mu_{g_{ei}}, \sigma_{g_{ei}})$ run hits a common target E-rate.
That keeps spike density and baseline activity comparable, letting the scan
reflect rhythm quality rather than simple gain changes.

Mathematically, for each condition $\theta = (\mu_{g_{ei}}, \sigma_{g_{ei}})$
we choose $I_E$ to minimize the squared error between the observed E‑rate and a
target rate $r^*$:

$$
I_E^*(\theta) = \arg\min_{I_E \in [I_{\min}, I_{\max}]} \left(r_E(I_E, \theta) - r^*\right)^2.
$$

Here $r_E(I_E, \theta)$ is the mean E‑rate measured over a fixed window
after burn‑in in a short simulation (e.g., 300 ms total, analyze a window
[$t_0, t_1$]). We estimate $r_E$ from spike counts:

$$
r_E = \frac{1}{N_E (t_1 - t_0)} \sum_{i=1}^{N_E} \sum_k \mathbf{1}\{t_0 \le t_k^{(i)} < t_1\}.
$$

In practice: (1) coarse grid search over $I_E$ to locate the minimum, then
(2) a small refinement sweep around the best value. The matched $I_E^*(\theta)$
is then used for the full simulation that produces rasters, PSDs, and rhythmicity.
We do this for every raster (every $\theta$).

Target: fix E around a chosen rate (e.g., 20Hz) and let I vary naturally.

# Pseudocode outline

```python
for mu in mu_values:
  for sigma in sigma_values:
    # Generate weights
    g_ei_weights = sample_weights(mu, sigma, size=(N_e, N_i))
    # Rate-match: search I_E to hit target E-rate (short sims)
    I_E_star = argmin_I_E(
        lambda I_E: (rate_E(run_short_sim(I_E, g_ei_weights)) - r_target)**2
    )
    # Run full simulation with matched drive (I_I fixed)
    sim_data = run_simulation(g_ei_weights, I_E_star, I_I_default, other_params)
    # Compute metrics (RI on last window)
    ri = compute_rhythmicity_index(sim_data, window="last_500ms")
    # Plot results
    plot_results(mu, sigma, ri, sim_data)
```

**Interpreting the variance heatmap**

Two intuitive ways RI changes across the $(\mu_{g_{ei}}, \sigma_{g_{ei}})$ scan:

**As $\mu_{g_{ei}}$ increases (mean E→I strength):** Mean rate of E cells
decreases while I rate stays roughly (very roughly) constant. This is because we
need less E cells overall to drive the same I activity due to higher E→I
weights. I cells mush out because they are driven harder by fewer E cells.

**As $\sigma_{g_{ei}}$ increases (variance in E→I strength):** Similar arguments
apply. Higher variance means some E→I synapses are very strong, so fewer E cells
are needed to drive I activity. This again leads to lower E rates and higher I
rates, which degrades rhythmicity.

The bands of rhythmic and unrhythmic activity as seen in the appendix are
unexplored for now.

The variance in the RI values for similar rasters is disturbing for now, and
needs to be explored, this might be done by running multiple sims per
parameter set and averaging the RI values, or by modifying the RI metric to be
more robust. We could run the same sim with different random seeds to see how
much the RI varies for the same parameters.
