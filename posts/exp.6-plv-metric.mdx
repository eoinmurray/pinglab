# Phase-locking value (PLV)

This experiment introduces PLV and computes it over a sweep of input current.
PLV answers a simple question: do spikes arrive at a consistent phase of the
population rhythm?

In a PING regime, a high PLV means excitatory spikes are tightly locked to the
population oscillation (clear, repeatable E–I timing). A low PLV means spikes
are spread across the cycle, which aligns with more irregular or weakly
coherent dynamics.

## Definition

We estimate an oscillatory phase $\phi(t)$ from the population firing rate and
sample it at each spike time $t_k$. The phase-locking value is the vector
strength of those spike phases:

$$
\mathrm{PLV} = \left| \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} e^{i \phi(t_k)} \right|.
$$

PLV is in $[0, 1]$. A value near 1 means spikes cluster at one phase; near 0
means spikes are spread across phases.

## How we compute PLV

1. **Bin spikes into a population rate.** We compute the excitatory population
   rate in bins of `bin_ms`.

<VeslxGallery
  path={`${path}`}
  globs={["plv_steps_rate_I_E_*.png"]}
/>

2. **Bandpass filter the rate.** We filter the rate between `fmin` and `fmax`
   (gamma band by default) to isolate the oscillatory component.

Let $r(t)$ be the binned population rate. We apply a bandpass filter with
passband $[f_{min}, f_{max}]$:

$$
\tilde{r}(t) = (h * r)(t),
$$

where $h$ is the bandpass filter impulse response.
In practice we implement this by filtering the discrete rate series
$r_n = r(n \Delta t)$ at sampling rate $f_s = 1000 / \Delta t$ Hz.
The filter is designed to pass frequencies between `fmin` and `fmax` and
attenuate everything else. We then apply it to $r_n$ to get a filtered series
$\tilde{r}_n$, which is the narrowband signal used for phase extraction. Because
the mean (DC component) is removed by the filter, $\tilde{r}_n$ oscillates around
zero and can take negative values.

Think of this as keeping only the rhythm you care about. The raw rate has slow
wandering and fast jitter; a bandpass keeps the “middle” oscillation range and
suppresses the rest. That way the phase you extract later is tied to a clear,
single oscillation rather than a messy mixture of timescales.

  globs={["plv_steps_filtered_I_E_*.png"]}

This plot is the bandpass-filtered version of the raw rate in step 1. It has the

so peaks and troughs align with the dominant rhythm in the original $r(t)$.

3. **Extract phase.** We take the analytic signal via the Hilbert transform and
   use its angle as the instantaneous phase $\phi(t)$.

Define the analytic signal of the filtered rate:

$$
z(t) = \tilde{r}(t) + i\,\mathcal{H}\{\tilde{r}(t)\},
$$

and the instantaneous phase

$$
\phi(t) = \arg z(t).
$$

The phase is defined for each time bin of the filtered rate.

<VeslxGallery
  path={`${path}`}
  globs={["plv_steps_phase_I_E_*.png"]}
/>
  
4. **Sample phases at spike times.** We interpolate $\phi(t)$ at each spike
   time $t_k$.

For each spike time $t_k$ we compute a phase by interpolation:

$$
\phi_k = \phi(t_k).
$$

This yields a set of spike phases for that sweep point.

<VeslxGallery
  path={`${path}`}
  globs={["plv_steps_spike_phases_I_E_*.png"]}
/>

5. **Vector strength.** We compute the PLV using the complex average above.

With spike phases $\{\phi_k\}_{k=1}^K$ we compute

$$
\mathrm{PLV} = \left| \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} e^{i \phi_k} \right|.
$$

## Hilbert transform (quick intro)

The Hilbert transform turns a real signal into a complex **analytic signal**
that separates amplitude and phase.

Given a real signal $x(t)$, its Hilbert transform is $\mathcal{H}\{x(t)\}$ and
the analytic signal is:

$$
z(t) = x(t) + i\,\mathcal{H}\{x(t)\}.
$$

From $z(t)$ we get:

- **Instantaneous amplitude:** $A(t) = |z(t)|$
- **Instantaneous phase:** $\phi(t) = \arg(z(t))$

Why we bandpass first: the Hilbert phase is meaningful when $x(t)$ is narrowband
(dominated by one oscillatory component). Bandpassing to `[fmin, fmax]` isolates
the rhythm so the phase is stable and interpretable.
