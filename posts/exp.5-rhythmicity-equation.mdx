# Definitions

This section explains, in plain language, how we turn spikes into a single
rhythmicity number for each simulation. The equations below match the code, but
the bullets are the easiest way to follow the steps.

## Plain-language recipe

1. Run the simulation for 1000 ms.
2. Ignore the first 200 ms (burn-in). Analyze the remaining 800 ms.
3. Count excitatory spikes in 5 ms bins to make a population firing-rate trace.
4. Normalize that trace to zero mean and unit variance so the autocorrelation
   reflects rhythmic structure rather than absolute firing rate.
5. Compute how similar the trace is to itself at different time shifts
   (the autocorrelation).

6. Look only at lags between 5 ms and 200 ms to skip the trivial zero-lag peak
   and focus on plausible oscillation periods.
7. Rhythmicity is the largest autocorrelation value in that lag window.

We define rhythmicity from spike times by (1) converting spikes to a binned
population rate, (2) normalizing the rate, (3) computing its autocorrelation,
and (4) taking the first positive-lag peak within a specified lag window. The
window avoids the zero-lag peak and keeps the search in a plausible period range.
All steps below match the implementation used to produce the artifacts.

All sweeps below scan tonic input $I_E$ from 0.6 to 1.5 (50 steps).

## Spikes to population rate

We analyze only the excitatory population over a fixed window after burn-in.
In this experiment, we discard the first $200$ ms (burn-in) and analyze the
remaining $800$ ms of the $T = 1000$ ms simulation. We also include small
parameter heterogeneity across neurons:
$\sigma_{V_{th}} = 0.5$ mV, $\sigma_{g_L} = 0.05$,
$\sigma_{C_m} = 0.05$, $\sigma_{t_{ref}} = 0.1$ ms.
Let neuron index be $i = 1..N$ and spike times be $t_k^{(i)}$.
For a bin width $\Delta t$ and time bin starting at $t$,

$$
r(t) = \frac{1}{N \Delta t} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_k \mathbf{1}\{t \le t_k^{(i)} < t + \Delta t\}.
$$

In practice we use rectangular bins with $\Delta t = \text{bin\_ms} = 5$ ms and
convert spikes into a discrete rate series $r_n = r(n \Delta t)$.
If the analysis window has duration $T$, then we have
$n = \lceil T / \Delta t \rceil$ bins.

## Normalized rate

Let $\bar{r}$ be the mean of $r_n$ over the analysis window, and $\sigma_r$ its
standard deviation. Define the normalized rate:

$$
x_n = \frac{r_n - \bar{r}}{\sigma_r}.
$$

If the rate series is empty or $\sigma_r = 0$ (no variability), we define
rhythmicity as zero.

## Autocorrelation

For lag $\tau \ge 0$, the normalized autocorrelation is

$$
C(\tau) = \frac{1}{T - \tau} \int_0^{T-\tau} x(t) \, x(t + \tau) \, dt,
$$

where $T$ is the analysis window length. Here we use $T = 800$ ms and evaluate
lags over $\tau \in [5, 200]$ ms. For discrete bins, replace the integral by a sum:

$$
C_k = \frac{1}{n - k} \sum_{m=0}^{n-k-1} x_m \, x_{m+k},
$$

with lag index $k$ corresponding to $\tau = k \Delta t$. This matches the
implementation: compute the full correlation of $x$ with itself, take the
nonnegative lags, and divide by $n-k$ to correct for the decreasing overlap.

## Rhythmicity and period

Let $\tau_{min}$ avoid refractory effects and $\tau_{max}$ bound the plausible period range.
Here, $\tau_{min} = 5$ ms and $\tau_{max} = 200$ ms.
Define the first positive-lag peak

$$
\tau^* = \arg\max_{\tau \in [\tau_{min}, \tau_{max}]} C(\tau),
$$

and the rhythmicity score as the peak height, where a larger peak indicates a
more consistent rhythmic pattern at that lag.

$$
\rho = C(\tau^*).
$$

In code, the lag window is converted to indices
$k_{min} = \lceil \tau_{min} / \Delta t \rceil$ and
$k_{max} = \lfloor \tau_{max} / \Delta t \rfloor$. If the window is invalid
($k_{max} < k_{min}$), rhythmicity is set to zero.

## Neuron model equations

All models use conductance-based synaptic currents with the same additive
structure. The equations below match the implementations in `pinglab/lib`.

### LIF

$$
C_m \frac{dV}{dt} =
g_L (E_L - V) + g_e (E_e - V) + g_i (E_i - V) + I_{ext}.
$$

### HH

Voltage:

$$
C_m \frac{dV}{dt} =
I_{ext} + g_e (E_e - V) + g_i (E_i - V) - I_{Na} - I_K - I_L,
$$

with

$$
I_{Na} = g_{Na} m^3 h (V - E_{Na}), \quad
I_K = g_K n^4 (V - E_K), \quad
I_L = g_L (V - E_L).
$$

Gating variables:

$$
\frac{dm}{dt} = \alpha_m(V) (1 - m) - \beta_m(V) m,
$$

$$
\frac{dh}{dt} = \alpha_h(V) (1 - h) - \beta_h(V) h,
$$

$$
\frac{dn}{dt} = \alpha_n(V) (1 - n) - \beta_n(V) n,
$$

with

$$
\alpha_m(V) = 0.1 \frac{V + 40}{1 - e^{-(V + 40)/10}}, \quad
\beta_m(V) = 4 e^{-(V + 65)/18},
$$

$$
\alpha_h(V) = 0.07 e^{-(V + 65)/20}, \quad
\beta_h(V) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(V + 35)/10}},
$$

$$
\alpha_n(V) = 0.01 \frac{V + 55}{1 - e^{-(V + 55)/10}}, \quad
\beta_n(V) = 0.125 e^{-(V + 65)/80}.
$$

### QIF

$$
C_m \frac{dV}{dt} =
g_L (E_L - V) + a (V - V_r)(V - V_t) + I_{ext}
 + g_e (E_e - V) + g_i (E_i - V).
$$

### MQIF

$$
C_m \frac{dV}{dt} =
g_L (E_L - V) + \sum_j a_j (V - V_{r,j})^2 + I_{ext}
 + g_e (E_e - V) + g_i (E_i - V).
$$
