# Minimal validation of a PING Spiking Neural Network

## AI-OSC Transition as a function of E→I synaptic conductance $g_{ei}$.

### What this shows

Raster plots demonstrating the AI-to-OSC transition as the excitatory-to-inhibitory synaptic conductance ($g_{ei}$) increases. Stronger E→I coupling enables more effective recruitment of inhibitory neurons, which is necessary for PING oscillations.

### How we created it

We simulate the PING network with fixed $I_E = 1.2$ while sweeping $g_{ei}$ from 1.3 to 1.8 nS (3 values). For each parameter value, we run a 1000 ms simulation and extract spike rasters from the 500–1000 ms window.

### What we expect to see

At low $g_{ei}$, E neurons cannot effectively recruit I neurons, leading to asynchronous firing. As $g_{ei}$ increases, E→I coupling strengthens sufficiently to engage the inhibitory population, which then provides synchronized feedback inhibition. This creates the PING mechanism: E fires → recruits I → I inhibits E → E recovers → cycle repeats.

## AI-OSC Transition as a function of input current to E neurons $I_E$.

### What this shows

Raster plots demonstrating the transition from asynchronous irregular (AI) to oscillatory (OSC) activity as external drive to excitatory neurons increases. Each dot represents a single spike, with E neurons (blue) and I neurons (orange) shown separately.

### How we created it

We simulate the PING network with fixed $g_{ei} = 1.6$ nS while sweeping $I_E$ from 0.8 to 1.3 (3 values). For each parameter value, we run a 1000 ms simulation and extract spike rasters from the 500–1000 ms window to exclude transients.

### What we expect to see

At low $I_E$, the network exhibits asynchronous irregular firing with spikes scattered randomly across time. As $I_E$ increases, the network transitions to synchronized oscillatory activity—spikes become clustered into rhythmic volleys at gamma frequencies (~30–80 Hz). The E population fires first in each cycle, recruiting I neurons which then inhibit E neurons, creating the characteristic PING rhythm.

## F-I curves for E and I populations.

### What this shows

Frequency-input (F-I) curves characterizing the mean firing rate of E and I populations as a function of external drive $I_E$. These curves reveal the input-output relationship of the network and help validate that neurons respond appropriately to input.

### How we created it

We run two sweeps with fixed $g_{ei} = 1.4$:
- **Large range**: $I_E$ from 0 to 100 (40 values) to capture the full dynamic range
- **Small range**: $I_E$ from 0 to 2 (40 values) to examine the transition region in detail

For each simulation, we compute the mean firing rate of E and I populations over the full 1000 ms simulation.

### What we expect to see

Both populations should show monotonically increasing firing rates with $I_E$. The E population receives direct external input, so its F-I curve should be steeper. The I population is driven indirectly through E→I synapses, so it follows E but with lower rates initially. At high $I_E$, both populations should saturate as refractory periods limit maximum firing rates.

## PSD

### What this shows

Power Spectral Density (PSD) plots of the E population firing rate, quantifying the frequency content of network activity. A peak in the gamma band (30–80 Hz) indicates oscillatory activity, while a flat spectrum indicates asynchronous firing.

### How we created it

We sweep $I_E$ from 0.5 to 2.0 (3 values) with fixed $g_{ei} = 1.4$. For each simulation:
1. Compute the instantaneous population rate of E neurons using 1 ms bins
2. Apply Gaussian smoothing ($\sigma$ = 2 ms) to reduce noise
3. Compute the PSD using Welch's method
4. Display frequencies from 5–150 Hz, normalized to peak power

Each plot shows both the PSD (top) and corresponding raster (bottom) for direct comparison.

### What we expect to see

At low $I_E$, the PSD should be relatively flat (broadband noise from asynchronous firing). As $I_E$ increases past the AI-OSC transition, a clear peak should emerge in the gamma range (~40–60 Hz), with the peak becoming sharper and taller as oscillations strengthen. The raster below each PSD should visually confirm the presence or absence of rhythmic activity.

## Cross-Correlogram

### What this shows

The cross-correlogram between E and I populations reveals the temporal relationship between excitatory and inhibitory firing. In PING networks, E neurons should lead I neurons by a characteristic delay determined by synaptic and membrane time constants.

### How we created it

We run a single simulation with $g_{ei} = 1.4$ and $I_E = 1.5$ (parameters chosen to produce clear oscillations). We then compute the spike-time cross-correlation between all E-I neuron pairs:
1. For each E spike, find all I spikes within ±50 ms
2. Histogram the time differences (I spike time − E spike time)
3. Normalize to peak value

### What we expect to see

A clear peak at positive lag (I lags E), typically around 2–5 ms. This reflects the PING mechanism: E neurons fire first, their spikes propagate through E→I synapses with some delay, and I neurons respond. The peak width reflects the jitter in this timing. A secondary structure may appear at multiples of the oscillation period (~15–25 ms for gamma).

## Population ISI CV

### What this shows

The coefficient of variation (CV) of inter-spike intervals (ISI) is a classic measure of firing regularity. For a Poisson process, CV = 1. Values below 1 indicate more regular (periodic) firing, while values above 1 indicate bursty firing. This metric helps quantify the AI-to-OSC transition.

### How we created it

We sweep $I_E$ from 0.5 to 2.0 (40 values) with fixed $g_{ei} = 1.4$. For each simulation:
1. Extract spike times for each neuron
2. Compute ISI for each neuron with ≥2 spikes
3. Calculate $\text{CV} = \text{std}(\text{ISI}) / \text{mean}(\text{ISI})$ for each neuron
4. Average across all neurons in E and I populations separately

### What we expect to see

At low $I_E$, neurons fire irregularly and CV should approach 1 (Poisson-like). As $I_E$ increases and oscillations emerge, neurons become entrained to the rhythm and fire more regularly, driving CV below 1. Notably, even at weak drive the network shows some synchrony (CV < 1), which highlights — "the network oscillates a little even at weak drive."
